Screening Plaintiffs and Selecting Defendants in Medical Malpractice Litigation: Evidence from Illinois and Indiana
  • Authors: Mohammad Rahmati, David A. Hyman, Bernard Black, Jing Liu, Charles Silver.
    Published: July 2017
    Link: https://ssrn.com/abstract=2465092
knowledge test
  • - What percentage of medical malpractice claims result in a payout?
    - What is the definition of a 'serious' case in this context?
    - What were the success rates for serious cases in Illinois and Indiana?
    - How many defendants are typically sued in medical malpractice cases?
    - What is the impact of defense costs on the perceived seriousness of a case?

Introduction
  • - Physicians view many med mal claims as frivolous.
    - Plaintiffs' lawyers assert they screen cases carefully.
    - Study analyzes claims from Illinois (2000-2010) and Indiana (1980-2015).

Key Findings
  • - Many claims are non-serious; often dropped before lawsuits.
    - Success rates vary significantly based on definitions (claims vs. cases).
    - Serious cases show higher success rates (43% in Illinois; 44% in Indiana).
Non-serious Cases
  • - Defined as cases with < $5k in defense costs unless payout > $25k.
    - Account for a significant portion of claims but minimal payouts.
    - Many are abandoned or never filed.

Success Rates
  • - Success rates for serious cases: 43% in Illinois; 44% in Indiana.
    - Higher rates for institutional defendants (58% in Illinois; 68% in Indiana).
    - Claim-level success rates are much lower (20% in Illinois; 14% in Indiana).


Screening Process
  • - Screening is an ongoing process; many cases are dropped after initial acceptance.
    - Plaintiffs' lawyers often reject cases based on insufficient damages or liability issues.
    - Defense costs are used as a proxy for case seriousness.
Defense Costs
  • - Cases with defense costs > $5k are considered serious.
    - A significant percentage of cases close without payment, indicating non-serious claims.
    - Defense spending correlates with higher success rates.


Claim-Level vs. Case-Level Analysis
  • - Claim-level analysis shows low success rates due to inclusion of non-serious cases.
    - Case-level analysis provides a more accurate picture of success rates.
    - Excluding pro se cases significantly alters success rate calculations.

Defendant Selection
  • - Plaintiffs' lawyers are selective about whom to sue.
    - Average number of defendants per case: 1.5 in Illinois; 1.8 in Indiana.
    - Multi-defendant cases are more common in Indiana.
Defendant Types
  • - Cases against institutions show higher success rates compared to those against individual physicians.
    - Institutions are more likely to settle cases quickly.
    - The presence of multiple defendants can complicate cases but may also increase payouts.


Qualitative Insights from Lawyer Interviews
  • - Interviews with six plaintiffs' lawyers reveal extensive screening processes.
    - Lawyers emphasize the importance of selecting cases with high potential payouts.
    - Many cases are abandoned if they do not meet merit criteria.
Lawyer Perspectives
  • - Lawyers report that successful case selection is crucial for maintaining a viable practice.
    - They often invest significant resources upfront to evaluate cases.
    - The decision to drop a case can occur at multiple points in the process.


Conclusion
  • - Med mal litigation success rates vary greatly depending on perspective.
    - Case-level analysis provides a more favorable view of plaintiffs' success.
    - Ongoing research is needed to further understand the dynamics of med mal litigation.
