Large Language Model for Interpreting the Paris Classification of Colorectal Polyps
  • - Journal: Endosc Int Open 2025; 13: a27030209
  • - Publication Date: 2025-10-09
  • - DOI: 10.1055/a-2703-0209
Knowledge Test
  • - What was the M-LLM's accuracy in differentiating sessile from pedunculated lesions?
  • - What was the M-LLM's specificity for the same task (differentiating sessile from pedunculated lesions)?
  • - What percentage of pedunculated lesions were misclassified by the M-LLM as sessile?
  • - What was the Fleiss' κ for interobserver agreement among expert endoscopists?
  • - What was the primary reason suggested for the M-LLM's failure to identify pedunculated morphology?

Background & Objective
  • - The Paris classification is used for clinically relevant morphological definition of colorectal neoplasia.
  • - However, its application suffers from significant interobserver variability, especially for non-polypoid lesions.
  • - This study aimed to evaluate a Multimodal Large Language Model (M-LLM), GPT-4o, in classifying colorectal polyps according to the Paris classification compared to human endoscopists.

Study Design & Cohort (SUN Database)
  • - The study utilized the SUN database from Showa University Northern Yokohama Hospital, Japan.
  • - It included colonoscopy videos of 100 unique polyps from 99 patients.
  • - An M-LLM (GPT-4o) and six endoscopists (3 expert, 3 non-expert) classified the polyps based on five representative frames per lesion.
SUN Database Characteristics (Table 1)
  • - Median Polyp Size: 5mm (IQR 3–7 mm).
  • - Diminutive Polyps (≤5mm): 60% of the dataset.
  • - Histology: 87% were adenomas.
  • - Polypoid/Non-polypoid Ratio: 66/34.
Paris Classification Distribution
  • - Is (Sessile): 49
  • - IIa (Superficial elevated): 34
  • - Isp (Semi-pedunculated): 9
  • - Ip (Pedunculated): 8

Protruded Lesions Breakdown
  • - Pedunculated (Ip/Isp): 17 lesions.
  • - Sessile (Is): 49 lesions.



Primary Outcome: Differentiating Non-Polypoid vs. Polypoid Lesions
  • - This task involved distinguishing non-polypoid lesions (Paris IIa, IIc) from polypoid/protruded lesions (Paris Is, Ip, Isp).
  • - Performance metrics are detailed in Table 2 and Fig 1 & 2.
M-LLM Performance
  • - Accuracy: 73% (95% CI 63%-81%)
  • - Sensitivity: 35% (95% CI 20%-54%) ---> Low ability to correctly identify non-polypoid lesions.
  • - Specificity: 92% (95% CI 83%-97%) ---> High ability to correctly identify polypoid lesions.

Human Endoscopist Performance
  • - Experts: Accuracy 75%, Sensitivity 53%, Specificity 86%.
  • - Non-Experts: Accuracy 77%, Sensitivity 56%, Specificity 88%.

Conclusion: Comparable Performance
  • - M-LLM performance was statistically comparable to both experts (P=0.84) and non-experts (P=0.52).
  • - Key Issue: All groups showed high specificity but low sensitivity, meaning non-polypoid lesions were frequently misclassified as polypoid.
  • - This may be due to the inherent ambiguity of the Paris classification for small lesions.


Secondary Outcome: Differentiating Sessile vs. Pedunculated Lesions
  • - This task, performed within the subset of protruded lesions, involved distinguishing sessile (Is) from pedunculated (Ip, Isp) morphologies.
  • - Performance metrics are detailed in Table 2 and Fig 1 & 2.
M-LLM Performance: Major Failure
  • - Accuracy: 55% (95% CI 42%-67%)
  • - Sensitivity: 69% (95% CI 55%-82%)
  • - Specificity: Extremely poor at 12% (95% CI 1%-36%) ---> Failed to correctly identify pedunculated lesions.
Misclassification Details
  • - The M-LLM misclassified 15 out of 17 (88%) pedunculated polyps as sessile (Is).


Human Endoscopist Performance
  • - Experts: Accuracy 76%, Specificity 82%.
  • - Non-Experts: Accuracy 77%, Specificity 88%.

Conclusion: M-LLM Underperformed Significantly
  • - M-LLM accuracy was significantly lower than experts (P=0.02) and non-experts (P=0.01).
  • - The failure was driven by the abysmal specificity (P < 0.01 vs. both human groups).


Interobserver Agreement
  • - Agreement was measured to assess consistency within each group of raters.
M-LLM (3 context-reset runs)
  • - Fleiss’ κ: 0.522
  • - Mean Pair-wise Agreement: 0.767
  • - Showed the highest internal consistency.

Expert Endoscopists
  • - Fleiss’ κ: 0.500 (Moderate concordance)
  • - Mean Pair-wise Agreement: 0.673

Non-Expert Endoscopists
  • - Fleiss’ κ: 0.462
  • - Mean Pair-wise Agreement: 0.640


Explaining M-LLM Pitfalls: Generative AI Insights
  • - To understand why the M-LLM failed to identify pedunculated lesions, its generative capabilities were used to visualize its 'understanding' of polyp morphology (Fig. 3).
Key Finding: Flawed Internal Representation
  • - The M-LLM has an oversimplified and non-physiological internal representation of a polyp stalk.

Characteristics of Generated Images
  • - Stalks were depicted as geometrical, rigid, and unnaturally cylindrical.
  • - The images lacked realistic physical properties like gravity and tissue pliability.
  • - This contrasts with real endoscopic images where stalks often bend or lie along the mucosal surface.


Study Limitations
  • - Paris Classification Ambiguity: Inherent complexity and subtlety of the classification, especially for small polyps.
  • - Small Cohort: Modest, single-center dataset of 100 polyps limits generalizability.
  • - Exploratory Design: No multiplicity correction was applied, risking type-I error.
  • - No Height Estimation Tool: Lack of biopsy forceps for scale, a limitation that affects both AI and human raters in clinical practice.

Clinical Implications & Future Directions
  • - M-LLMs show potential for endoscopic assessment despite current limitations, particularly their failure with underrepresented morphologies.
Potential Clinical Applications
  • - Real-time Support: Could run with CADe systems to highlight subtle non-polypoid features.
  • - Automated Reporting: Could write the corresponding Paris code directly into structured reports.
  • - Quality Assurance: Post-procedure mining of videos to populate morphology-based quality dashboards.

Required Future Improvements
  • - Targeted Fine-Tuning: M-LLMs need further training on balanced, domain-specific, annotated datasets.
  • - Focus on Underrepresented Features: Special emphasis is needed on features like stalk morphology to improve performance on less common polyp types.
