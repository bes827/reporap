Positioning and sequencing of advanced therapies in inflammatory bowel disease: A guide for clinical practice

Introduction & Core Concepts
  • - Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), including Crohn's Disease (CD) and Ulcerative Colitis (UC), are incurable immune-mediated disorders requiring advanced therapies.
  • - The therapeutic arsenal has expanded beyond anti-TNF-α agents to include multiple new classes.
Key Terminology
  • - Positioning: Selecting the optimal first-line advanced therapy based on patient profile, disease activity, and drug attributes.
  • - Sequencing: Strategic selection of subsequent therapies after the failure of the initial treatment (due to non-response, loss of response, or adverse events).

Expanded Therapeutic Armamentarium
  • - Anti-integrin agents (e.g., vedolizumab)
  • - Interleukin (IL) inhibitors (IL-12/23 and IL-23p19)
  • - Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitors (e.g., tofacitinib, upadacitinib)
  • - Sphingosine 1-phosphate (S1P) receptor modulators (e.g., ozanimod, etrasimod)


General Principles of Therapy Selection
  • - Decisions are guided by evidence (RCTs, NMAs, RWE), clinical expertise, and shared decision-making.
  • - Early initiation of biologics in CD, ideally within the first 12 months of diagnosis, improves outcomes and enhances transmural healing rates.
Fundamental Principles (Table 2)
  • - Start early: Initiate effective drugs as soon as possible.
  • - First is best: The first advanced therapy chosen is often the most effective, especially in CD.
  • - Avoid steroid cycles: The need for steroids signals the necessity for steroid-sparing therapies.
  • - Think ahead: Always consider which agent could be an effective second-line treatment.

Factors Influencing Choice
  • - Patient-specific: Age, frailty, comorbidities, extraintestinal manifestations (EIMs), preferences (oral vs. IV/SC).
  • - Disease-specific: Severity, phenotype (e.g., fistulizing CD), inflammatory load, hypoalbuminemia.
  • - Drug-specific: Speed of onset, efficacy, safety profile, route of administration, cost, and access.

Timeframe for Assessing Effectiveness (Table 1)
  • - A structured approach is needed to avoid premature discontinuation or delays in switching ineffective therapies.
  • - Most effective therapies show response within 2-4 weeks, but delayed responses can occur.
Specific Regimens & Timeframes
  • - Anti-TNF: Assess after 2-3 loading doses and the first maintenance dose.
  • - Vedolizumab (Anti-integrin): Assess after 3 loading doses and 1-3 maintenance doses.
  • - JAK Inhibitors:
  • - - Tofacitinib: 10 mg PO BID for 8-16 weeks.
  • - - Upadacitinib (UC): 45 mg PO QD for 8-16 weeks.
  • - - Upadacitinib (CD): 45 mg PO QD for 12 weeks.
  • - S1P Modulators:
  • - - Ozanimod: 0.92 mg/day for 10 weeks.
  • - - Etrasimod: 2 mg PO QD for 12 weeks.



Positioning & Sequencing in Ulcerative Colitis (UC)
  • - The initial therapeutic choice can impact the effectiveness of future treatments.
  • - Achieving steroid-free remission and mucosal healing remains challenging, especially in therapy-experienced patients.
First-Line Therapy (Biologic-Naive)
  • - The VARSITY study was the first head-to-head trial comparing two biologic classes in UC.
VARSITY Trial: Vedolizumab vs. Adalimumab
  • - At week 52, vedolizumab was superior to adalimumab for:
  • - - Clinical Remission: 31.3% vs 22.5% (p=0.0061).
  • - - Mucosal Healing: 39.7% vs 27.7% (p=0.0005).

Network Meta-Analysis (NMA) Insights
  • - Upadacitinib is ranked as the most effective therapy for both biologic-naive and experienced populations.
  • - When JAK inhibitors are excluded, infliximab, ozanimod, risankizumab, and guselkumab show greater benefit than adalimumab and mirikizumab.

Speed of Onset
  • - Rapid onset is crucial for patients needing urgent symptom control.
  • - JAK inhibitors: Upadacitinib provides relief as early as day 1; Tofacitinib shows significant symptom reduction by day 3.
  • - Infliximab (IFX): Demonstrates early reductions in stool frequency and rectal bleeding.

Patient Preference
  • - A nationwide study found oral therapy was most preferred (Acceptability Score = 8.68), followed by SC injections and IV infusions.
  • - SC therapies with extended intervals (≥ 8 weeks) were highly acceptable.


Management After Biologic Failure
  • - No direct head-to-head trials exist specifically for anti-TNF refractory patients, so decisions rely on RWE and NMAs.
Real-World Evidence (RWE)
  • - Tofacitinib vs. Ustekinumab: Comparable corticosteroid-free remission at week 16 (37.8% vs 35.6%).
  • - Tofacitinib vs. Vedolizumab: Tofacitinib showed significantly higher remission rates at week 52 (47.1% vs 23.5%).
  • - EFFICACI Trial (post-SC anti-TNF failure): Vedolizumab was superior to infliximab for steroid-free clinical remission at week 14 (34.6% vs 19.2%).

Meta-Analysis Findings
  • - Lymphocyte trafficking inhibitors (anti-integrins, S1P modulators) are more effective in TNF-naive vs. TNF-exposed patients.
  • - JAK inhibitors may be less effective in TNF-naive than in TNF-exposed patients.


Management of Multirefractory UC
  • - This population fails multiple lines of therapy (anti-TNF, integrin inhibitors, JAK inhibitors, etc.).
  • - Newer agents have been studied in more heavily pre-treated populations.
IL-23p19 Inhibitors
  • - Mirikizumab (LUCENT trials): Showed efficacy in patients where 40% had failed prior biologics.
  • - Risankizumab: Showed meaningful remission in a population where ~50% had failed prior advanced therapies.
  • - Guselkumab (QUASAR program): Effective in patients with multiple prior biologic failures, including anti-TNF, vedolizumab, and tofacitinib.

S1P Receptor Modulators
  • - Etrasimod (ELEVATE trials): Effective in biologic-experienced patients, with higher remission rates in those with only one prior biologic/JAK inhibitor failure vs. multiple failures.


Acute Severe UC (ASUC)
  • - Standard rescue therapy for steroid-refractory ASUC is infliximab or cyclosporine.
  • - JAK inhibitors are promising options due to rapid onset, especially after IFX failure.
Tofacitinib in ASUC
  • - Systematic review: Associated with a 90-day colectomy-free survival rate of 86% in refractory patients.
  • - TACOS study (RCT): Combining hydrocortisone with tofacitinib significantly increased response rates (83.01% vs 58.82% with placebo).

Upadacitinib in ASUC
  • - RWE suggests efficacy and safety comparable to infliximab, with no significant difference in colectomy rates.



Positioning & Sequencing in Crohn's Disease (CD)
  • - Management goals include rapid remission, maintaining steroid-free remission, and targeting deeper outcomes like endoscopic and transmural healing.
Key Management Strategies
  • - Early Intervention: A 'top-down' approach (early combined immunosuppression) is linked to better long-term outcomes.
  • - PROFILE study: Top-down approach resulted in significantly higher sustained steroid- and surgery-free remission (79% vs 15% for accelerated step-up).

Comparative Studies & Meta-Analyses
  • - Head-to-head trials and NMAs are crucial for guiding therapy choices.
Head-to-Head Trials
  • - SONIC study: Combination therapy with infliximab + azathioprine was superior to either monotherapy in biologic-naive patients.
  • - SEAVUE trial: Ustekinumab vs. Adalimumab (biologic-naive) showed similar clinical remission rates at week 52 (64.9% vs 61.0%).
  • - SEQUENCE study: Risankizumab vs. Ustekinumab (anti-TNF failure) showed risankizumab was non-inferior for clinical remission at week 24 and superior for endoscopic remission at week 48 (31.8% vs 16.2%).

Network Meta-Analysis (NMA) Insights
  • - Biologic-Naive: Infliximab and adalimumab ranked highest for inducing clinical remission.
  • - Biologic-Exposed: Risankizumab was the most effective agent.
  • - Maintenance: Upadacitinib 30 mg ranked highest.
  • - Fistulizing CD: Infliximab remains the preferred first-line therapy.


Real-World Evidence (RWE)
  • - ROTARY study: First-line treatment with infliximab or vedolizumab was associated with significantly lower rates of switching or discontinuation compared to adalimumab.
  • - Vedolizumab was found to be more effective when used as a first-line agent.

Postoperative Recurrence
  • - Prophylactic therapy is crucial for high-risk patients (e.g., smokers, penetrating disease, prior resection).
  • - Endoscopic recurrence (Rutgeerts score ≥ i2) within 6-12 months predicts clinical relapse.
REPREVIO Trial
  • - Vedolizumab significantly reduced severe endoscopic recurrence at week 26 compared to placebo (23.3% vs 62.2%).
  • - This suggests VDZ is a viable option for preventing postoperative recurrence.

Treatment of Recurrence
  • - If recurrence occurs despite prophylaxis, first step is to optimize existing therapy (e.g., dose escalate).
  • - For those without prophylaxis, anti-TNF agents are a common first-line choice. Ustekinumab is an effective option after prior biologic failure.



Managing Secondary Loss of Response (LOR)
  • - A personalized approach is required, ideally guided by Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM).
TDM-Guided Approach for Anti-TNF
  • - Subtherapeutic drug levels (no antibodies) ---> Benefit from dose escalation.
  • - High anti-drug antibody levels ---> Switch to another anti-TNF or a different class.
  • - Adequate drug levels (pharmacodynamic failure) ---> Switch to a different class.
  • - Do not discontinue anti-TNF therapy before reaching adequate trough levels (e.g., IFX/ADA > 10-15 μg/mL).

LOR to Non-Anti-TNF Therapies
  • - The role of TDM for agents like vedolizumab and ustekinumab is less established.
  • - Emerging data suggests TDM can be beneficial; dose escalation of ustekinumab resulted in response/remission in 69% of cases with LOR.


Positioning & Sequencing in Special Populations
  • - Treatment strategies must be individualized to balance efficacy and safety in specific subgroups.
Pregnant & Lactating Patients
  • - Goal: Maintain remission while minimizing fetal/maternal risk.
Safe / Generally Safe Therapies
  • - 5-ASA: Generally safe. Sulfasalazine requires folic acid supplementation of at least 2 mg/day.
  • - Thiopurines: Considered safe despite FDA category D.
  • - Anti-TNF agents: Generally safe. Certolizumab has the lowest placental transfer.
  • - Anti-integrin & Anti-ILs: Emerging data suggest safety, especially for vedolizumab and ustekinumab.

Contraindicated Therapies
  • - Absolute Contraindication: Methotrexate (teratogenic); must be discontinued at least 3 months prior to conception.
  • - Relative Contraindication: JAK inhibitors (teratogenic in animal studies); must be discontinued at least 1 month prior.


History of Malignancy or Active Cancer
  • - Thiopurines: Avoid in patients with history of EBV-related lymphoma or HPV-related carcinomas.
  • - JAK inhibitors: Malignancy risk appears concentrated in high-risk individuals (older, smokers, etc.).
  • - Safer Options: Vedolizumab and anti-IL agents have not been associated with increased cancer risk and are preferred options.

Patients with Comorbidities
  • - Cardiovascular Disease: TNF inhibitors are relatively contraindicated in NYHA class III-IV heart failure. Vedolizumab and anti-ILs have safer profiles.
  • - Demyelinating Diseases (e.g., Multiple Sclerosis): Anti-TNF agents are contraindicated. Vedolizumab and anti-ILs are preferred.
  • - - Ozanimod (S1P modulator) may be a first-line choice for UC patients with multiple sclerosis.

Older Individuals (≥65 years)
  • - Increased risk of infections and adverse events.
  • - JAK inhibitors: Should be avoided in individuals ≥ 65 years unless no other options exist, due to cardiovascular and thromboembolic risks.
  • - Preferred First-Line: Vedolizumab and anti-IL agents are considered first-line options due to their favorable safety profiles.


IBD with Extraintestinal Manifestations (EIMs)
  • - Management requires addressing both intestinal inflammation and specific EIMs.
  • - Collaboration with specialists (rheumatology, dermatology, ophthalmology) is essential.
Therapy Effectiveness for EIMs (See Table 5)
  • - Anti-TNF agents: Broadly effective for musculoskeletal, cutaneous, and ocular EIMs. Can cause paradoxical psoriasis.
  • - Vedolizumab: Effective for EIMs associated with luminal activity (e.g., erythema nodosum, type 1 peripheral arthritis) but not for independent EIMs (e.g., axial spondyloarthritis).
  • - Anti-IL agents: Particularly effective for psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis.
  • - JAK inhibitors: Effective for various arthritis types (rheumatoid, psoriatic, axial spondyloarthritis).


Conclusion & Future Directions
  • - IBD management is entering an era of complexity and opportunity, requiring individualized, context-sensitive strategies.
  • - Future research in personalized medicine (genomics, proteomics, microbiome) may allow for predictive models to guide therapy selection from the outset.
