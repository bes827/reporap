  • - It shows how our minds (System 1) substitute difficult questions (like probability) with easier ones (like similarity).

üß© The Tom W Puzzle: Base Rates vs. Representativeness
  • - A puzzle designed to show the conflict between statistical base rates and judgments based on stereotypes (representativeness).
Task 1: Judging by Base Rate
  • - Question: Rank nine fields of study by the likelihood that a random graduate student, Tom W, is in that field.
  • - Correct approach: Use the base rate, which is the proportion of students in each field.
  • - Result: People correctly identify that fields with more students (e.g., humanities) are more likely choices when no other information is given.

Task 2: Judging by Similarity (Stereotype)
  • - A personality sketch of Tom W is provided, describing him as intelligent, needing order, dull, and with little sympathy for others.
  • - Question: Rank the nine fields by how similar Tom W is to the typical student in that field.
  • - This task activates System 1 to create a stereotype.
Typical Ranking by Similarity
  • - People consistently rank fields that match the 'nerd' stereotype highest.
  • - 1. Computer Science
  • - 2. Engineering
  • - 9. Social Science and Social Work
  • - The description was intentionally written to fit the stereotype of small fields and be a poor fit for large fields.


Task 3: The Critical Prediction
  • - Question: Knowing the base rates and the (unreliable) personality sketch, rank the fields by the likelihood that Tom W is a student in them.
  • - This is a difficult System 2 probability question.
The Common Error: Substitution and Neglect
  • - Finding: People's rankings for probability were identical to their rankings for similarity.
  • - Mechanism: They substituted the difficult probability question with the easier similarity question.
  • - This is called the representativeness heuristic.
  • - They completely ignored two critical facts:
  • - 1. The base rates of enrollment in each field.
  • - 2. The stated low quality of the evidence (the sketch's validity was 'uncertain').



üö´ The Sins of Representativeness
  • - Relying only on representativeness leads to serious errors in statistical logic, even if it's sometimes accurate.
Sin 1: Excessive Willingness to Predict Unlikely Events
  • - People predict rare (low base-rate) events if they match a stereotype.
  • - Example: Guessing if a person reading the NY Times on the subway has a PhD or no college degree.
  • - Representativeness suggests PhD.
  • - Base rates suggest no college degree, because there are vastly more non-graduates than PhDs on the subway.
  • - Rule: When evidence is weak, stick closer to the base rates.

Sin 2: Insensitivity to the Quality of Evidence
  • - System 1's rule is WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is).
  • - It automatically processes available information as if it were true, ignoring doubts about its quality.
  • - In the Tom W problem, people trusted the personality sketch even though they were told it was of uncertain validity.
  • - Rule: Worthless information should be treated like no information. Your judgment should stay close to the base rate.

Overcoming the Sins
  • - The failure is due to both System 1 (suggesting the wrong intuition) and System 2 (lazily endorsing it).
  • - System 2's failure can be from ignorance (not knowing base rates matter) or laziness (not focusing enough to apply the knowledge).
Activating System 2
  • - An experiment found that activating System 2 improved accuracy.
  • - Students told to frown during the task showed sensitivity to base rates.
  • - Students with a neutral expression (puffing cheeks) ignored base rates.
  • - This suggests laziness is a key reason for base-rate neglect, as frowning increases cognitive vigilance.



üß† How to Discipline Intuition: Bayesian Reasoning
  • - To be useful, our beliefs about probability must follow logic.
  • - Bayes's Rule specifies how to combine prior beliefs (base rates) with the strength of new evidence.
Two Key Ideas of Bayesian Reasoning
  • - 1. Base rates matter, even when you have specific evidence about a case.
  • - 2. Intuitive impressions of evidence are often exaggerated. We believe our own stories too much due to WYSIATI.

Practical Steps for Disciplined Thinking
  • - 1. Anchor your judgment on a plausible base rate.
  • - 2. Question the diagnostic value of your evidence. (How much should this evidence really change my belief from the base rate?)
Bayesian Example: Tom W
  • - Base rate: 3% of grad students are in computer science.
  • - Evidence: The description is 4 times more likely for a computer science student than others.
  • - Bayesian result: The probability Tom W is a computer scientist is now 11% (not the 80-90% intuition might suggest).



üë© Linda Problem: The Conjunction Fallacy
  • - An experiment showing a conflict between the intuition of representativeness and the logic of probability.
The Setup
  • - A description of Linda is given: 31, single, outspoken, bright, philosophy major, concerned with social justice.
  • - Participants are asked to rank scenarios by probability.

The Critical Items & The Fallacy
  • - Two key scenarios:
  • - A. Linda is a bank teller.
  • - B. Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement.
  • - The Conjunction Fallacy: People judge the conjunction of two events (B) to be more probable than one of the events alone (A).
Why It's a Fallacy
  • - The set of 'feminist bank tellers' is a subset of the set of 'bank tellers'.
  • - Therefore, the probability of B must be lower than the probability of A.
  • - Adding detail can only lower a probability.

Why It Happens: Plausibility vs. Probability
  • - System 1 substitutes probability with plausibility or coherence.
  • - The 'feminist bank teller' scenario creates a more coherent and plausible story that fits the description of Linda.
  • - The stereotype of a bank teller does not fit Linda well.


Experimental Results
  • - The fallacy is extremely robust, even when people can directly compare the options.
Widespread Error
  • - In a direct comparison, 89% of undergraduates violated the logic of probability.
  • - Even 85% of doctoral students in decision science with advanced statistics training made the same error.

The Power of Intuition
  • - The fallacy remains attractive even when you know it's wrong.
  • - Stephen Jay Gould described a "little homunculus in my head" (System 1) insisting that Linda couldn't *just* be a bank teller.


Reducing the Fallacy
  • - The fallacy can be reduced by changing the question's format.
Frequency Representation
  • - The error rate dropped from 65% to 25% with a simple change:
  • - Bad phrasing: "What percentage of people will have a heart attack?"
  • - Good phrasing: "How many of the 100 participants will have a heart attack?"
  • - Reason: The "how many?" format makes people think of individuals in a group, making the subset relationship (e.g., heart attack victims under 55 are a part of all heart attack victims) visually and logically obvious.



üçΩÔ∏è Less is More: Averaging vs. Adding
  • - The conjunction fallacy is an example of a broader 'less is more' pattern where System 1 averages attributes instead of adding their values.
The Dinnerware Experiment
  • - People were asked to price dinnerware sets.
The Sets
  • - Set A: 40 pieces, including 8 good plates, 8 good bowls, 8 good dessert plates, but also 2 broken cups and 7 broken saucers.
  • - Set B: 24 pieces, including the same 8 good plates, 8 good bowls, and 8 good dessert plates (all items from Set A that are not broken).

The Results
  • - Joint Evaluation (seeing both sets): People correctly value Set A higher ($32) than Set B ($30). Logic rules because more items add value.
  • - Single Evaluation (seeing only one set): People value Set B much higher ($33) than Set A ($23).
  • - Reason: In single evaluation, System 1 averages the value of the items. The broken dishes in Set A bring down the average value, making it seem less attractive.


Connection to Linda Problem
  • - Probability is a sum-like variable, just like economic value.
  • - P(Bank Teller) = P(Feminist Bank Teller) + P(Non-Feminist Bank Teller)
  • - When people evaluate the 'bank teller' option, the non-feminist tellers (who don't fit the description) act like 'broken dishes', lowering the average plausibility.
  • - Removing them (by specifying 'feminist bank teller') makes the story better, increasing subjective probability.


üöï Causes Trump Statistics
  • - We treat two types of base rates very differently depending on whether they fit into a causal story.
The Cab Problem
  • - A cab was in a hit-and-run. A witness identified it as Blue. The witness is 80% reliable.
Version 1: Statistical Base Rate
  • - Data: 85% of cabs in the city are Green, 15% are Blue.
  • - This is a purely statistical fact about the city's cab population.
  • - Result: People tend to underweight or ignore this base rate and focus on the witness testimony.
  • - The Bayesian answer is that the probability the cab was Blue is 41%.

Version 2: Causal Base Rate
  • - Data: The two companies have equal numbers of cabs, but Green cabs are involved in 85% of accidents.
  • - This is mathematically the same, but psychologically different.
  • - Result: People give this base rate significant weight.
  • - Reason: It creates a causal story and a stereotype: "Green drivers are reckless." This information is seen as being about the individual driver, not just the group.


Two Types of Base Rates
  • - Statistical Base Rates: Facts about a population. Generally underweighted when specific information is available.
  • - Causal Base Rates: Change your view of how the individual case came to be. Treated as information about the individual and easily combined with other facts.

Stereotypes
  • - Stereotypes are how System 1 represents social categories.
  • - In the cab problem, stereotyping the Green drivers as reckless improves judgment accuracy.
  • - This highlights a conflict: resisting stereotypes is a laudable moral position for a better society, but neglecting valid stereotypes can lead to suboptimal judgments.


üéì Can Psychology Be Taught? The Helping Experiment
  • - A classic experiment showing that people often fail to learn from surprising statistical facts about human behavior.
The Experiment Setup
  • - Participants in booths hear a confederate have a (fake) seizure over an intercom and ask for help.
  • - The key factor is that they know other people are also listening.
The Surprising Result
  • - Only 4 of the 15 participants responded immediately.
  • - Most people did not help because of diffusion of responsibility.
  • - This result is surprising because most of us believe we (and other decent people) would rush to help.


The Learning Test (Nisbett & Borgida)
  • - Goal: To see if students' beliefs about human nature actually change after learning about the experiment's results.
The Failure of Statistical Teaching
  • - Students were told the procedure and the statistical results (only 4 of 15 helped).
  • - They were then shown videos of two participants and asked to predict if those individuals helped.
  • - Result: They predicted the individuals would be helpful, completely ignoring the base rate they had just learned.
  • - Conclusion: Students "quietly exempt themselves" from the conclusions of surprising experiments. They learn the fact, but it doesn't change their underlying beliefs.

The Success of Individual Case Teaching
  • - A different group was shown the videos first.
  • - They were told that these two specific, nice individuals had NOT helped.
  • - They were then asked to guess the overall group result.
  • - Result: Their guesses were now extremely accurate.
  • - Conclusion: We are more willing to infer the general from the particular than to deduce the particular from the general.


The Main Takeaway
  • - The test of learning is whether your understanding of situations has changed, not whether you have learned a new fact.
  • - Surprising individual cases have a more powerful impact on changing our beliefs than surprising statistics.
