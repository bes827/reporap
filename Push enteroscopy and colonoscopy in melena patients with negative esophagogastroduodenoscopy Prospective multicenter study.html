Push enteroscopy and colonoscopy in melena patients with negative esophagogastroduodenoscopy: Prospective multicenter study
  • Info about the article:
  • Authors: Kotchakon Maipang, Julajak Limsrivilai, et al.
  • Journal: Endosc Int Open
  • Publication Date: 2025
  • DOI: 10.1055/a-2676-3957
  • Study Design: Prospective, multicenter cohort study
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of patients with melena have a non-diagnostic EGD?
  • 2. What is the reported diagnostic yield of colonoscopy in melena patients with negative EGD in this study?
  • 3. What is the diagnostic yield of push enteroscopy in melena patients with negative EGD in this study?
  • 4. What is the combined diagnostic yield of push enteroscopy and colonoscopy in this study?
  • 5. What are the limitations of VCE?
Introduction
  • Melena often indicates upper GI bleeding.
  • EGD is the initial test.
  • EGD fails to find the source in ≈25% of patients.
Colonoscopy Recommendations
  • Guidelines suggest colonoscopy after negative EGD.
  • Based on older studies showing 23%-35% detection rates.
  • Recent large study found only 4.7% yield.
Small Bowel Evaluation
  • 4%-15% of overt GI bleeds remain unexplained after EGD & colonoscopy.
  • Considered potential small bowel bleeding.
  • VCE is recommended due to high yield & non-invasiveness.
VCE Limitations
  • Misses proximal lesions due to rapid transit.
  • Cannot perform therapeutic interventions.
Push Enteroscopy (PE)
  • Valuable for obscure GI bleeding.
  • Straightforward technique.
  • Retrospective study showed 40% diagnostic yield in melena.
PE Benefits
  • May avoid unnecessary colonoscopy & VCE.
  • Potentially reduces treatment costs.
Study Aim
  • Evaluate diagnostic yields of PE & colonoscopy in melena patients w/ negative EGD.
Patients and Methods
  • Prospective, multicenter cohort study.
  • July 2019 – October 2022.
  • Four referral centers in Thailand.
Patient Selection
  • Inclusion: Melena (black, tarry stools), age ≥18, negative EGD.
  • Exclusion: Unstable hemodynamics (BP <90/60 mmHg despite inotropes), respiratory compromise, uncorrectable bleeding disorders, pregnancy.
Data Collection
  • Demographics, comorbidities, bleeding characteristics.
  • PE followed by colonoscopy.
  • Time to each procedure & findings documented.
Culprit Lesions
  • Ulcers, angiodysplasia, Dieulafoy’s lesions, tumors, diverticula, other abnormalities.
  • Colonoscopy not done if PE found culprit lesion & high risk of colonoscopy complications.
  • Colonoscopy presumed negative in these cases.
Follow-up
  • 3-month follow-up w/o rebleeding to confirm absence of additional bleeding sources after PE.
  • Further investigations at physician's discretion if both PE & colonoscopy negative.
  • Diagnostic & therapeutic yields compared.
PE Procedure
  • PCF-Q180AL or PCF-HQ190 L/I pediatric colonoscopes.
  • 6-8 hour fast.
  • IV propofol sedation.
  • Hyoscine butyl-bromide for motility reduction if needed.
PE Technique
  • Oral insertion, advanced to proximal small intestine.
  • Minimal insufflation (air).
  • Careful inspection during withdrawal.
  • Monitored recovery.
Statistical Analysis
  • Descriptive statistics.
  • T-test or Wilcoxon rank-sum test for continuous data.
  • Chi-square or Fisher exact test for categorical data.
  • McNemar test for diagnostic yield differences.
Significance
  • P < 0.05 considered statistically significant.
  • SAS OnDemand for Academics.
Ethical Considerations
  • Written informed consent.
  • Approved by local IRB (Si-484/2019).
  • Good clinical practices.
  • Registered w/ ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT06574542).
Results
  • 221 patients w/ melena & no hematemesis underwent EGD.
  • EGD identified bleeding sources in 141 (63.8%).
  • 3 excluded due to respiratory compromise.
Study Population
  • 77 patients (34.8%) w/ nondiagnostic EGD included.
  • Mean age: 67.8 years.
  • 51.9% men.
Comorbidities
  • Hypertension (64.9%).
  • Diabetes mellitus (33.8%).
  • Chronic kidney disease (33.8%).
  • Atrial fibrillation/valvular heart disease (26.0%).
  • Coronary artery disease (19.5%).
Medications
  • NSAID use (9.1%).
  • Antiplatelet use (27.3%).
  • Anticoagulant use (24.7%).
  • Warfarin overdose (6.5%).
Previous Bleeding
  • 24.7% had a history of previous bleeding.
  • No patients w/ surgically altered upper GI anatomy.
Endoscopic Procedures Timing
  • All 77 patients underwent PE, 59 underwent colonoscopy.
  • Median time from presentation to EGD: 3 days (IQR 1-5).
  • Median time to PE: 3 days (IQR 2-6).
  • Median time to colonoscopy: 5 days (IQR 3-7).
Procedure Duration & Complications
  • Mean PE time: 27.9 ± 13.9 minutes.
  • Mean colonoscopy time: 31.4 ± 28.7 minutes.
  • No serious complications (bleeding/perforation).
Diagnostic Yield
  • PE: 27/77 (35.0%) had culprit lesion identified.
  • Colonoscopy: 10/59 (12.9%) had bleeding source identified.
  • PE yield significantly greater than colonoscopy (P = 0.005).
PE Lesion Types
  • Ulcers (25.9%).
  • Angiodysplasia (29.6%).
  • Dieulafoy’s lesion (11.1%).
  • Tumor (14.8%).
  • Polyps (3.7%).
  • Bleeding diverticulum (3.7%).
  • Other (11.1%).
PE Lesion Locations
  • Duodenum reached by EGD: 5 patients.
  • Duodenum not reached by EGD: 2 patients.
  • Jejunum: 22 patients.
Lesions Missed by EGD
  • 5 patients (18.5%) had lesions missed during EGD.
  • Duodenal ulcers, duodenal angiodysplasia, hemosuccus pancreaticus.
Colonoscopy Lesion Types
  • Ulcers (3 patients).
  • Dieulafoy’s lesions (3 patients).
  • Cancer (1 patient).
  • Polyp (1 patient).
  • Diverticulum (1 patient).
  • Colitis (1 patient).
Combined Diagnostic Yield
  • PE + Colonoscopy: 48% (37 patients).
  • Significantly greater than PE alone (P = 0.002) or colonoscopy alone (P < 0.0001).
Predictive Factors
  • Univariate analysis: No significant associations between age, sex, comorbidities, medications, BUN/Cr ratio & positive PE findings.
Therapeutic Interventions
  • PE: 13 patients (16.9%), successful in 12 (15.6%).
  • Colonoscopy: 5 patients (6.5%), successful in all 5 (6.5%).
  • Combined PE & colonoscopy significantly ↑ successful therapeutic intervention rate.
Rebleeding Rate
  • 30-day rebleeding rate: 3.7% for PE, 0% for colonoscopy.
Further Investigations
  • VCE (10 patients).
  • CT (6 patients).
  • BAE (4 patients).
Definitive Diagnosis
  • Definitive source identified in 53 patients (68.8%) via initial procedures & further investigations.
Discussion
  • Melena implies upper GI bleeding → EGD first-line.
  • Study supports this: 64% of patients had bleeding sources found via EGD.
  • Previous study: ≈75% had lesions detected on EGD.
Colonoscopy After Negative EGD
  • Guidelines recommend colonoscopy before small bowel evaluation.
  • This study: diagnostic yield of 14%, lower than previous retrospective reports (23%-35%).
  • 5%-10% of lesions detected were colon cancers.
Colonoscopy Benefits
  • Cancer detection.
  • Therapeutic interventions (successful in 5/10 lesions).
  • Accessibility.
VCE
  • Recommended after negative EGD & colonoscopy (potential small bowel bleeding).
  • High diagnostic yield (55%-65%) for overall potential small bowel bleeding.
  • Limited data for melena specifically.
VCE Limitations
  • Misses proximal lesions (major papilla visualized in only 10.4%-43.6%).
  • Cannot perform therapeutic interventions.
VCE in Melena
  • Mussetto et al.: VCE yield in melena & negative EGD = 83.3% (10/12 patients).
Early Direct Enteroscopy
  • Could be beneficial for vascular lesions (Dieulafoy's, angiodysplasia) that may not be actively bleeding during VCE.
  • Small bowel angiodysplasia often in proximal small bowel (67% in duodenum/ligament of Treitz).
Push Enteroscopy Role
  • Substantial possibility of proximal small bowel bleeding in melena.
  • Limited evidence on PE in melena.
  • Previous trials included melena/hematochezia: diagnostic yield 19%-35%.
Lepère et al. Study
  • Retrospective study.
  • Overall PE yield = 34%.
  • Melena: 40%.
  • Hematochezia: 17%.
Current Study Findings
  • First prospective analysis of PE in melena w/ negative EGD.
  • Diagnostic yield = 35%.
PE Benefits
  • Therapeutic interventions (13/27 patients, 12 successful).
  • Safe (no serious complications).
  • Simple technique, most endoscopists can perform.
ACG Guidelines
  • Recommend PE as option in GI bleeding w/ negative EGD & colonoscopy before VCE.
PE Advantage
  • May reduce need for bowel preparation (required for colonoscopy).
  • Relevant for hospitalized patients (bowel prep often suboptimal).
PE Drawback
  • Limited reach (≈0.6 meters distal to duodenojejunal junction).
  • Melena can originate from more distal bleeding.
Predictors for Proximal Bleeding
  • Lepère et al.: Chronic renal failure associated w/ positive PE findings.
  • Current study: No significant predictive factors identified (age, sex, comorbidities, medications, BUN/Cr ratio).
BAE
  • High diagnostic yield (comparable to VCE) in potential small bowel bleeding.
  • Deeper insertion → greater diagnostic yield.
  • Can perform therapeutic interventions.
BAE Limitations
  • Complications: sedation-related, bleeding, perforation, pancreatitis.
  • Unavailable at many centers, requires skilled endoscopists.
Proposed Approach
  • PE should be performed in melena w/ negative EGD.
  • Consider before colonoscopy.
  • PE & colonoscopy complement each other.
Combined Approach
  • PE + Colonoscopy: ↑ diagnostic yield to 48%.
  • ↑ therapeutic intervention rates.
  • If PE negative → colonoscopy.
  • After negative PE & colonoscopy → VCE, consider BAE based on VCE findings.
Study Strengths
  • First prospective investigation of consecutive melena patients w/ negative EGD.
  • Assessed diagnostic & therapeutic yields of PE & colonoscopy.
  • Timely treatment (interventions in same/nearly same session).
Study Limitations
  • Colonoscopy not performed in every patient due to safety concerns.
  • May have missed additional lesions.
  • Addressed w/ 3-month follow-up for recurrent bleeding.
Referral Bias
  • Significant proportion from large tertiary referral centers.
  • May not reflect typical case mix in general/community hospitals.
  • Diagnostic yields may not be fully generalizable.
Adjunctive Tool
  • PE may serve as valuable adjunctive tool, enhancing diagnostic & therapeutic outcomes, especially where available.
Conclusions
  • EGD is essential in evaluating melena patients.
  • If EGD negative, consider PE before/with colonoscopy.
  • Future studies w/ larger sample sizes needed.
