AGA Clinical Practice Update on the Role of Intestinal Ultrasound in Inflammatory Bowel Disease: Commentary
Clinical practice guidance for IUS in IBD care
  • - Journal: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology; Volume 22, Issue 9; September 2024
  • - Topic: role of intestinal ultrasound (IUS) in IBD care and guidance for implementation
  • - Emphasis: noninvasive, real-time assessment to guide treat-to-target decisions and reduce reliance on invasive imaging
knowledge test

  • - In Crohn's disease, a post-treatment BWT reduction of at least 18% at 4–8 weeks predicts endoscopic response.
  • - In UC treated with tofacitinib, an absolute BWT of 2.8 mm predicted week 8 endoscopic remission.
  • - In acute severe UC, > 20% reduction in BWT within 48 ± 24 hours after IV steroids predicts endoscopic response.
  • - In pregnancy, IUS shows sensitivity 84% and specificity 98% for correlating with clinical activity

Overview
  • - Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) includes Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC); persistent inflammation risks obstruction, fistula, and cancer.
  • - The primary goal is to reverse endoscopic inflammation to sustain long-term clinical remission and prevent complications.
  • - IUS offers a noninvasive, radiation-free method to assess disease activity and guide treatment decisions at the point of care

Intestinal Ultrasound Technique
  • - Equipment: high-resolution ultrasound system with a low-frequency convex transducer and a high-frequency linear transducer capable of color Doppler assessment.
  • - Procedure: start with global bowel assessment using the convex probe, then targeted evaluation with the linear probe; no bowel prep required.
  • - Primary activity measures: BWT and bowel wall color Doppler hyperemia; inflammatory mesenteric fat, loss of wall stratification, and lymphadenopathy indicate more severe disease

Comparison with Imaging Modalities
  • - IUS vs MRE/CTE: comparable accuracy for ileal activity; advantages include no oral/IV contrast, no radiation, and real-time, clinic-based assessment; supports shared decision-making
  • - Interobserver reliability: moderate to excellent with proper training; improves with standardized techniques
  • - Limitations: while IUS can detect complications comparably to MRE/CTE, it has limited visualization of proximal small bowel and rectum; see Table 1

Applications of IUS in Clinical Care
  • - Screening tool to rule out IBD in appropriate contexts; positive findings can expedite ileocolonoscopy for confirmation
  • - Monitoring disease activity and guiding treatment adjustments; early changes in BWT/Doppler can signal response or failure
  • - Special populations: valuable in pregnancy, patients with comorbidities, and obesity where radiation or sedation risks are higher

Crohn's Disease
  • - Treatment response is often detectable within ~3 months via IUS improvements or resolution of findings
  • - Higher baseline BWT predicts lower likelihood of transmural healing at 1 year, regardless of therapy type
  • - In ustekinumab, sonographic response (BWT reduction ≥25%) at 48 weeks occurred in 46.3%; transmural healing in 24.1% of patients

Ulcerative Colitis
  • - IUS can indicate treatment response, with normalization of BWT sometimes seen within 2 weeks of therapy change
  • - In UC treated with tofacitinib, absolute BWT < 2.8 mm predicted week 8 endoscopic remission; 32% BWT reduction predicted week 8 endoscopic response
  • - In hospitalized acute severe UC, ≥20% BWT reduction within 48 hours of IV corticosteroids predicts endoscopic response and reduces need for biologic rescue

Pregnancy & Special Populations
  • - IUS is particularly valuable in pregnancy to avoid radiation and sedation; sensitivity 84% and specificity 98% for correlating with clinical activity
  • - Visualization of colon and terminal ileum in the third trimester is possible in up to 92% of cases
  • - Obesity may limit image quality but does not outright preclude IUS findings; obesity alone should not exclude use

Training & Implementation
  • - Establish institutional IUS programs with appropriate equipment selection, storage, and workflow; billing guidance varies; codes like 76705 may be used where applicable
  • - IBUS offers structured training and certification; modules progress from introductory to advanced hands-on training
  • - Future maintenance of certification and broader integration of IUS into practice will require ongoing training and demonstration of competency

Limitations
  • - Limited visualization for proximal small bowel and rectum in some patients; bowel gas and body habitus can impede image quality
  • - IUS is not ideal for dysplasia surveillance; endoscopy remains the standard for mucosal assessment and biopsies
  • - Operator dependence; requires standardized training and experience for reliable interpretation

IUS & Environmental Sustainability
  • - IUS reduces healthcare waste compared with colonoscopy or cross-sectional imaging due to fewer disposables and no radiation
  • - Adoption supports sustainable care, but requires training and workflow integration to maximize impact
  • - Potential reductions in patient travel and resource use by enabling point-of-care decisions

Patient Experience & Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion
  • - Patients prefer noninvasive monitoring when feasible; IUS increases confidence in management and willingness to engage in care decisions
  • - Point-of-care IUS can improve access for rural or underserved populations by reducing need for multiple appointments
  • - Training and implementation should prioritize equitable access and culturally competent care
