Diagnosis and Treatment of Hemochromatosis
  • Authors: Paul C. Adams, John D. Ryan
  • Journal: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology
  • Publication Date: 2025
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the estimated risk for a male C282Y homozygote to develop severe liver disease if left untreated?
  • 2. A serum ferritin >1000 mg/L is a critical threshold. What is the approximate risk of advanced liver fibrosis (F3/F4) in C282Y homozygotes who exceed this level?
  • 3. What is the negative predictive value (NPV) of having both a normal serum ferritin and a transferrin saturation <45% for ruling out hemochromatosis?
  • 4. Beyond the liver, male C282Y homozygotes have a significantly increased risk for another common condition. What is the odds ratio for osteoporosis in this group?
  • 5. What is the most common cause of an elevated serum ferritin in the general population?
Introduction & Overview
  • Hemochromatosis: a common genetic disease of iron overload.
  • Focus on C282Y homozygotes in the HFE gene.
  • Emphasizes need for earlier diagnosis & treatment to prevent complications.
Historical Context
  • Genetic variants identified in 4000-year-old human fossils in North Western Europe.
  • Postulated to promote iron absorption as a survival benefit.
Clinical Significance
  • Excess iron absorption → serious complications.
  • Early diagnosis & treatment can prevent morbidity & mortality.
Key Complications
  • Arthritis
  • Liver fibrosis & cirrhosis
  • Primary liver cancer (HCC)
  • Diabetes
Epidemiology
  • Most common genetic disease in persons of European ancestry.
Prevalence of C282Y Homozygosity
  • North America (HEIRS study): 1 in 227
  • Northern England (UK Biobank): 1 in 156
  • Ireland: ≈1% (highest in the world)
Clinical Penetrance
  • Not absolute, but significant morbidity risk.
  • UK Biobank data:
  • - 1 in 5 males develop morbidity.
  • - 1 in 10 females develop morbidity.
Severe Liver Disease Risk
  • 1 in 10 males will develop severe liver disease if not identified early & treated.
Diagnosis
  • Challenging due to non-specific signs & symptoms.
  • Diagnosis often delayed until 40s in men or 50s in women.
  • Relies heavily on biochemical and genetic testing.
Clinical Presentation
  • Symptoms are non-specific & often absent in early stages.
  • Diagnosis is frequently incidental.
Common Symptoms
  • Fatigue & arthralgias of the knuckles: affect >50% of individuals.
Diagnostic Triggers
  • Family history of hemochromatosis.
  • Incidental finding of ↑ serum ferritin or ↑ transferrin saturation.
  • Incidental MRI finding of a 'black liver' due to iron overload.
The Underdiagnosis Problem
  • UK Biobank study: only 12% of male & 3.4% of female C282Y homozygotes had a prior diagnosis.
  • Average age at diagnosis: 58.5 years (women), 61 years (men).
Interpretation of Iron Studies
  • Diagnostic Paradox: Most cases are missed (missed diagnosis), yet hemochromatosis is also frequently over-diagnosed (misdiagnosis) in patients with elevated ferritin from other causes.
Serum Ferritin
  • Non-specific test; elevation is extremely common.
  • HEIRS study: 13% of participants had elevated ferritin.
  • Probability of C282Y homozygosity ↑ as ferritin level ↑.
Common Causes of Hyperferritinemia
  • Metabolic hyperferritinemia is the most common cause.
  • - Metabolic syndrome
  • - Fatty liver
  • - Daily alcohol use
  • - Obesity
  • - Inflammation
Transferrin Saturation
  • Reflects daily iron absorption.
  • Poor reproducibility & significant biological variability (circadian rhythm).
  • Best measured fasting & on more than one occasion.
Recommendations for HFE Testing
  • Warranted when:
  • - Serum ferritin >200 mg/L (women) or >300 mg/L (men)
  • - AND Transferrin saturation >45%
Ruling Out Hemochromatosis
  • Combination of normal serum ferritin & transferrin saturation <45%.
  • Provides a Negative Predictive Value (NPV) of 97%.
Initial Workup in C282Y Homozygote
  • Full blood count, LFTs (AST, ALT), bilirubin, creatinine.
  • Fasting blood glucose, HbA1c.
  • Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), abdominal ultrasound.
  • Consider fibrosis biomarkers (FIB-4, APRI) or elastography.
Genetic Testing for HFE C282Y
  • Confirms the diagnosis.
  • Test is reproducible, inexpensive (as low as $5), and can be done on saliva.
Screening Considerations
  • Economic analyses show cost-effectiveness.
  • Logistical challenges exist; mail-back programs have low uptake.
  • Whole genome sequencing may increase detection in the future.
ACMG Recommendation
  • Hemochromatosis is a Tier 1 genetic result.
  • Notification of the result to the patient is recommended.
Approach to a New Diagnosis
  • Involves family screening & risk stratification based on iron burden.
Family Screening
  • Siblings & parents should be tested for HFE (C282Y).
  • Adult children may be tested, or their other parent can be tested first.
  • Screening of non-adult children is not recommended.
Role of Serum Ferritin
  • Best noninvasive marker of iron burden at diagnosis.
  • Predicts likelihood of complications & number of phlebotomies needed.
  • Key indicator of liver disease risk.
Ferritin >1000 mg/L
  • Significantly increases likelihood of severe liver fibrosis.
  • One study: 1 in 5 patients with ferritin >1000 mg/L (and/or ↑ transaminases) had advanced (F3/F4) fibrosis on biopsy.
Liver Fibrosis Assessment
  • All patients should be screened.
  • Liver disease affects ≈20% of adult male C282Y homozygotes.
Imaging
  • Baseline abdominal ultrasound: assess for steatosis, cirrhosis, portal hypertension, HCC.
  • MRI: Not required for diagnosis in C282Y homozygotes, but accurately quantifies iron burden in non-HFE cases.
Non-Invasive Markers
  • Blood-based scores (FIB-4, APRI) can be used.
  • - APRI >0.44 or FIB-4 >1.1: Good ability to detect advanced fibrosis.
  • - APRI <0.37 or FIB-4 <0.73: High NPV (88.6-92%) for advanced fibrosis.
Transient Elastography
  • Liver stiffness <6.4 kPa has a high NPV for ruling out advanced fibrosis.
Extra-hepatic Manifestations
  • Iron-related damage can occur outside the liver.
Bones & Joints
  • Osteoporosis: Odds Ratio of 2.30 in male C282Y homozygotes.
  • Arthritis: 27.9% of males require joint replacement vs. 17.1% of controls.
  • Recommendation: Consider baseline bone density scan in adults >40 years.
Cardiac Disease
  • Cardiomyopathy occurs in <3% of C282Y homozygotes.
  • Primarily in those with serum ferritin >1000 mg/L.
  • Recommendation: Cardiac testing (echo/MRI) warranted for ferritin >2000 mg/L or severe iron overload.
Neurological Manifestations
  • UK Biobank data shows ↑ risk of delirium, dementia, & Parkinson’s disease in males.
  • Iron deposition in the brain has been reported on MRI.
Treatment
  • Management focuses on removing excess iron, primarily via phlebotomy.
  • A correct diagnosis is essential to avoid unnecessary treatment.
Benefits of Treatment
  • Normalization of iron stores leads to:
  • - Improvement in fatigue.
  • - Reversal of liver fibrosis.
  • - Reduced risk of diabetes, cirrhosis, liver cancer, & death.
Indications & Urgency
  • Initiate treatment without delay.
  • Urgent in symptomatic patients or those with ferritin >1000 mg/L.
  • Beneficial even for moderate elevations (300-500 mg/L) → ↓ cardiovascular disease & extra-hepatic cancers.
Phlebotomy for C282Y Homozygotes
  • Standard of care for C282Y homozygotes with elevated serum ferritin.
Protocol
  • Induction: Weekly 500 mL phlebotomy.
  • Target: Serum ferritin in the low normal range (≈50 mg/L).
  • Side effects are minimal at experienced centers (local pain, syncope).
Maintenance Phase
  • Need for maintenance varies by age, sex, and initial iron burden.
  • Voluntary blood donation is the ideal solution.
  • Many transfusion services now accept blood from hemochromatosis patients.
Non-expressing Homozygotes
  • C282Y homozygotes with normal ferritin at diagnosis.
  • Unlikely to develop iron overload later in life.
  • Management: Observation with periodic ferritin checks every few years.
Management of Complications
  • Focus on surveillance for hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
Cirrhosis & HCC Risk
  • Established cirrhosis → mandatory 6-monthly ultrasound & AFP for HCC screening.
  • Hazard Ratio for hepatic malignancies in C282Y homozygous males: 10.5 (95% CI, 6.6–16.7).
  • HCC in hemochromatosis is often poorly differentiated with worse outcomes.
Management of Other HFE Genotypes
  • Includes C282Y/H63D compound heterozygotes & H63D homozygotes.
Clinical Significance & Approach
  • Large studies show no ↑ mortality or morbidity; patients can be reassured.
  • Mild ferritin elevation is almost always associated with cofactors (metabolic syndrome, alcohol).
  • Treatment: Promote voluntary blood donation & manage metabolic risk factors.
Management of Elevated Ferritin w/ Normal HFE
  • A very common clinical scenario; these patients rarely have true iron overload.
Diagnostic Pathway
  • If significant biochemical iron overload is suspected, MRI liver is preferred over a trial of phlebotomy.
  • If MRI confirms iron overload, refer to a specialist for advanced genetic testing (ferroportin, hemojuvelin, etc.).
Alternative & Adjunctive Therapies
  • Options beyond standard phlebotomy.
Erythrocytaphoresis
  • Automated red cell exchange.
  • Removes 2.3 times more iron per procedure than standard 500 mL phlebotomy.
  • Requires fewer treatments but has higher supply costs.
Dietary & Other Therapies
  • Iron-reduced diet: Minor effect.
  • English tea with meals: Can ↓ iron absorption.
  • Proton pump inhibitors: Can ↓ iron absorption but not enough to replace phlebotomy.
Chelation Therapy
  • Deferasirox (oral) has been studied but is not approved for hemochromatosis.
  • Less efficient than phlebotomy with more adverse effects.
  • Rarely used, typically in combination with phlebotomy for life-threatening cases.
Future Developments & Controversies
  • Evolving areas in diagnosis, screening, and treatment.
Screening
  • Population screening with C282Y genetic test remains controversial.
  • Whole genome testing could lead to a large number of new incidental diagnoses.
Diagnostics
  • Noninvasive markers for liver fibrosis need further study to optimize cutoffs specific to hemochromatosis.
Novel Therapies
  • Mini-hepcidin: Parenteral therapy that reduces intestinal iron absorption.
  • - Does not mobilize stored iron.
  • - Proposed for use after initial de-ironing to reduce need for maintenance phlebotomy.
  • Gene editing (CRISPR): Potential future cure, but likely to be developed for more severe diseases first.
