Management of Intraductal Papillary Mucinous Neoplasm (IPMN)
A Comparative Mind Map of Kyoto 2024 (IAP) vs. ACG 2018 Guidelines
  • - This mind map synthesizes and compares two major guidelines for the management of pancreatic cysts, with a focus on IPMN.
  • - Kyoto 2024: International evidence-based guidelines for the management of intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm of the pancreas (Ohtsuka et al., Pancreatology).
  • - ACG 2018: ACG Clinical Guideline: Diagnosis and Management of Pancreatic Cysts (Elta et al., The American Journal of Gastroenterology).
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the recommended surveillance interval for a BD-IPMN measuring 25mm according to the Kyoto 2024 guidelines?
  • - According to ACG 2018, what is the accuracy of MRI or MRCP in determining if a cyst is benign vs. malignant?
  • - The Kyoto 2024 guidelines state that Low-Grade Dysplasia (LGD) at a surgical margin justifies completion pancreatectomy. True or False?
  • - What is the 5-year disease-specific survival for Solid-Pseudopapillary Neoplasms (SPNs) according to ACG 2018?
  • - The Kyoto 2024 guidelines suggest that a family history of pancreatic cancer primarily increases the risk of what specific type of pancreatic cancer development in IPMN patients?

Guideline Comparison: Scope & Key Differences
  • - A high-level overview of the differences between the two guidelines.
Scope of Guideline
  • - Kyoto 2024: Focuses exclusively on IPMN, providing in-depth, updated recommendations.
  • - ACG 2018: Covers all pancreatic cysts (IPMN, MCN, SCA, pseudocysts), serving as a broader guide for gastroenterologists.

Risk Stratification Framework
  • - Kyoto 2024: Uses a two-tier system of High-Risk Stigmata (HRS) and Worrisome Features (WF). Explicitly incorporates EUS findings into this framework.
  • - ACG 2018: Uses a single list of High-Risk Characteristics that warrant referral to a multidisciplinary group or EUS.

Surveillance Strategy
  • - Kyoto 2024: Proposes a simplified, size-based surveillance protocol and, crucially, introduces the option to stop surveillance for small, stable BD-IPMNs after 5 years.
  • - ACG 2018: Recommends ongoing surveillance if the patient remains a surgical candidate, with potential to lengthen intervals for stable cysts. Does not recommend stopping.

Key Criteria Thresholds
  • - The guidelines differ on specific numerical cutoffs for key features.
  • - MPD Diameter: Kyoto 2024 uses ≥10mm for HRS, while ACG 2018 uses >5mm as a high-risk feature.
  • - Cyst Growth Rate: Kyoto 2024 defines WF as ≥2.5mm/year, while ACG 2018 uses >3mm/year as a high-risk feature.


Diagnosis & Initial Work-up
  • - Consensus and differences in the initial diagnostic approach to pancreatic cysts.
Initial Imaging
  • - Consensus: MRI/MRCP is the preferred initial imaging modality.
  • - Rationale: Non-invasive, no radiation, and superior accuracy for assessing communication with the main pancreatic duct (MPD), a key feature of BD-IPMN.
Imaging Accuracy
  • - ACG 2018: Accuracy of MRI/MRCP for diagnosing cyst type is 40-50% and for determining benign vs. malignant is 55-76%.
  • - Kyoto 2024: Overall accuracy for predicting HGD/IC is 73-97%, but for diagnosing specific cyst types is 47-78%.


Role of Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS)
  • - Consensus: EUS is a secondary tool for further investigation, not a primary screening modality for all cysts.
  • - Kyoto 2024: Explicitly recommends EUS for cases with suspicion of HGD/IC and incorporates its findings (mural nodule size, cytology) into the HRS/WF algorithm.
  • - ACG 2018: Recommends EUS for indeterminate cysts or when high-risk features are present.

EUS-Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) & Cyst Fluid Analysis
  • - Indication: Both guidelines agree EUS-FNA should only be performed when the results are likely to alter management.
  • - Kyoto 2024: Notes risk of peritoneal seeding is low (2.3% in surgical cases with HGD/IC) and not statistically different from non-FNA group.
Carcinoembryonic Antigen (CEA)
  • - Consensus: Useful to differentiate mucinous (IPMN/MCN) from non-mucinous cysts.
  • - ACG 2018: A cutoff of 192 ng/ml is commonly used. A very low level (<5 ng/ml) has >95% specificity for non-mucinous cysts.
  • - Kyoto 2024: Notes CEA has no correlation with the grade of dysplasia.

Cytology
  • - Consensus: Low sensitivity but high specificity for HGD/IC.
  • - Kyoto 2024: A 'suspicious' or 'positive' result is considered an HRS, with an absolute risk of HGD/IC of 91-100%.
  • - ACG 2018: 'High-grade dysplasia or pancreatic cancer' on cytology is a high-risk characteristic warranting referral.

Molecular Markers (Kyoto 2024 Focus)
  • - More emphasized in the recent Kyoto 2024 guidelines.
To Differentiate Cyst Type
  • - Mucinous Cysts (IPMN/MCN): Presence of KRAS and/or GNAS mutations.
  • - Serous Cystic Neoplasm (SCN): Presence of VHL mutation.
  • - GNAS mutation is highly specific for IPMN.

To Identify HGD/IC
  • - Mutations in TP53, SMAD4, CDKN2A, PIK3CA are useful for identifying HGD/IC.
  • - These have low sensitivity (9-39%) but high specificity (92-98%).




Risk Stratification & Indications for Surgery
  • - A direct comparison of the criteria used by each guideline to determine the need for surgery or intensive evaluation.
Kyoto 2024: HRS & WF Framework
  • - A structured, two-tiered approach to risk assessment.
High-Risk Stigmata (HRS)
  • - Presence of any HRS suggests a high likelihood of HGD/IC and is an indication for surgical resection.
Obstructive jaundice
  • - In a patient with a cystic lesion in the pancreatic head.

Enhancing mural nodule ≥ 5mm or solid component
  • - A strong predictor of HGD/IC.

Main Pancreatic Duct (MPD) ≥ 10mm
  • - Indicates significant obstruction or involvement.

Suspicious or positive cytology
  • - Confers a very high risk (91-100%) of HGD/IC.


Worrisome Features (WF)
  • - Presence of any WF is an indication for further investigation, typically with EUS.
List of Worrisome Features
  • - Cyst size ≥30mm
  • - Enhancing mural nodule <5mm
  • - Thickened/enhancing cyst walls
  • - MPD diameter 5mm to <10mm
  • - Abrupt change in MPD caliber with distal atrophy
  • - Lymphadenopathy
  • - Cyst growth rate ≥2.5mm/year
  • - Elevated serum CA19-9
  • - Acute pancreatitis
  • - New onset or exacerbation of diabetes mellitus



ACG 2018: High-Risk Characteristics Framework
  • - A single list of features that trigger referral to a multidisciplinary group and/or EUS±FNA.
List of High-Risk Characteristics
  • - Symptoms: Jaundice, acute pancreatitis secondary to the cyst, elevated serum CA 19-9.
  • - Imaging Findings: Mural nodule or solid component, MPD diameter >5mm, abrupt change in MPD caliber, cyst size ≥3cm.
  • - Cytology: Presence of high-grade dysplasia or pancreatic cancer.
  • - During Surveillance: Rapid increase in cyst size (>3mm/year).


Direct Comparison of Key Criteria
  • - Highlighting the different thresholds between the guidelines.
MPD Diameter
  • - Kyoto 2024: Stratified risk. WF at 5-9mm, HRS at ≥10mm.
  • - ACG 2018: Single threshold. High-risk feature at >5mm.

Cyst Growth Rate
  • - Kyoto 2024: WF at ≥2.5mm/year.
  • - ACG 2018: High-risk feature at >3mm/year.

Mural Nodule
  • - Kyoto 2024: Stratified by size. WF for enhancing nodule <5mm, HRS for ≥5mm.
  • - ACG 2018: Any mural nodule is a high-risk feature, without a size cutoff.



Surveillance of Non-Resected IPMN
  • - Protocols for monitoring IPMNs that do not meet criteria for immediate resection.
Kyoto 2024 Surveillance Protocol
  • - A simplified protocol based on cyst size.
Cyst <20 mm
  • - Initial follow-up at 6 months.
  • - If stable, surveillance every 18 months.

Cyst 20 mm to <30 mm
  • - Follow-up at 6 months, then again at 12 months.
  • - If stable, surveillance every 12 months.

Cyst ≥30 mm
  • - Surveillance every 6 months.


ACG 2018 Surveillance Protocol
  • - A more granular protocol for smaller cysts, with more frequent initial imaging.
Cyst <1 cm
  • - MRI every 2 years.

Cyst 1-2 cm
  • - MRI every 1 year.

Cyst 2-3 cm
  • - MRI or EUS every 6-12 months.

Cyst >3 cm
  • - Refer to multidisciplinary group.
  • - Consider alternating MRI and EUS every 6 months.


Stopping Surveillance: A Key Difference
  • - A major point of divergence between the two guidelines.
Kyoto 2024 Recommendation
  • - For small BD-IPMN (<20mm) with no morphological changes and no WF after 5 years of surveillance, two options are presented:
Option 1: Stop Surveillance
  • - Based on the low risk of progression of the IPMN itself.

Option 2: Continue Surveillance
  • - Rationale: To monitor for the possible development of concomitant PDAC, which remains a risk.


ACG 2018 Recommendation
  • - Surveillance should be discontinued only if a patient is no longer a surgical candidate.
  • - Does not recommend stopping after 5 years for stable cysts in fit patients.
  • - Suggests assessing the utility of ongoing surveillance in patients >75 years old.



Surgical Management & Post-Resection Surveillance
  • - Guidance for operative strategy and follow-up after surgery.
Surgical Principles
  • - Consensus: Organ-preserving partial pancreatectomy for presumed non-invasive IPMN; radical pancreatectomy with lymphadenectomy for suspected IC.
  • - Kyoto 2024: Prophylactic total pancreatectomy is not recommended due to metabolic consequences.
Intraoperative Frozen Section (Kyoto 2024)
  • - Critical for guiding the extent of resection.
Margin Status & Action
  • - IC or HGD at margin ---> Additional resection recommended.
  • - LGD at margin ---> Additional resection is unnecessary.
  • - Denuded duct at margin ---> Not a negative margin; consider additional resection.



Post-Resection Surveillance
  • - Consensus: All resected IPMNs require postoperative surveillance due to the risk of new or progressive disease in the remnant pancreas.
Kyoto 2024 Post-Resection Protocol
  • - Median 5-year risk of clinically significant remnant lesions is 10%.
  • - Surveillance should be continuous as long as the patient is fit for intervention.
Risk Factors for Recurrence
  • - HGD in the initial resected specimen.
  • - Family history of pancreatic cancer.

Surveillance Interval
  • - With risk factors ---> Imaging every 6 months.
  • - Without risk factors ---> Imaging every 12 months.


ACG 2018 Post-Resection Protocol
  • - Stratifies surveillance by the grade of dysplasia found in the resected IPMN.
Surveillance Interval
  • - Resected IPMN with HGD ---> Surveillance every 6 months.
  • - Resected IPMN with LGD ---> Longer intervals, e.g., every 24 months.


Surveillance for other resected cysts (ACG 2018)
  • - MCNs without invasive cancer ---> No postoperative surveillance required.
  • - SCAs, pseudocysts ---> No follow-up required.



Pathology & Special Topics
  • - Key pathological concepts and other important clinical considerations.
Pathological Aspects (Kyoto 2024)
  • - Grades of Dysplasia: Low-Grade (LGD), High-Grade (HGD), Invasive Carcinoma (IC).
  • - HGD is equivalent to 'carcinoma in situ'.
  • - Morphological Subtypes: Gastric (most common, best prognosis), Intestinal (associated with colloid carcinoma), Pancreatobiliary (worst prognosis).

Concomitant PDAC
  • - Refers to the development of a separate pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) in apancreas that also harbors an IPMN.
  • - This is part of the 'dual carcinogenesis' theory for IPMN patients.
Risk & Incidence
  • - Kyoto 2024: The yearly incidence of developing concomitant PDAC is 0.4-1.0%.
  • - The overall risk is about 3 to 5-fold higher than in the age-matched general population.
  • - It typically arises in patients with small, stable BD-IPMNs, making surveillance challenging.

Regional Discrepancy
  • - Kyoto 2024: Highlights that most studies reporting a high risk of concomitant PDAC originate from Japan.
  • - In Western countries, the risk appears to be much lower, closer to that of the general population.
  • - This discrepancy may be due to different pathological assessment methods (e.g., entire pancreas sectioning in Japan) and higher awareness.


Family History of Pancreatic Cancer
  • - Consensus: A family history of pancreatic cancer (one or more first-degree relatives) is a significant factor in IPMN management.
Impact on IPMN Progression
  • - Kyoto 2024: A family history does not appear to increase the risk of an existing IPMN progressing from LGD to HGD/IC.

Impact on Concomitant PDAC
  • - Kyoto 2024: A family history is a strong predictor for the metachronous development of concomitant PDAC.
  • - This justifies stricter and more prolonged surveillance, especially after resection.


Management of Multifocal IPMNs
  • - Incidence of multifocal BD-IPMNs is reported to be 20-40%.
  • - Consensus: Multifocality itself does not increase the risk of HGD/IC.
Management Strategy
  • - Management decisions should be based on the single lesion with the highest-risk features.
  • - If surgery is indicated, the goal is to resect only the high-risk lesion, leaving other low-risk cysts to avoid a prophylactic total pancreatectomy.


Other Therapies (Kyoto 2024 Focus)
  • - Non-surgical options for IPMN are generally not standard of care.
Chemotherapy & Radiotherapy
  • - The role for unresectable IPMN with IC or in an adjuvant setting is not established.
  • - In practice, regimens used for standard PDAC are often applied.

Ablation Therapy (e.g., Ethanol)
  • - Efficacy is highly variable, with cyst resolution reported in 9% to 72% of cases.
  • - Adverse event rate is significant at 9-23%, with pancreatitis being the most common.
  • - Long-term oncological benefit is unproven.
  • - Recommendation: Should only be performed within a clinical trial.


Characteristics of Specific Cyst Types (ACG 2018 Focus)
  • - The ACG 2018 guideline provides details on various cyst types beyond IPMN.
Mucinous Cystic Neoplasm (MCN)
  • - Occur almost exclusively in women (>95%), typically in the 4th-5th decade.
  • - Located in the pancreatic body or tail in 95% of cases.
  • - Characterized by ovarian-type stroma and no communication with the MPD.

Serous Cystadenoma (SCA)
  • - More common in women (~70%), typically in the 6th-7th decade.
  • - Have an extremely low risk of malignancy (0.1%).
  • - Classic imaging: microcystic or honeycomb appearance, with a central scar in <30% of cases.
  • - Asymptomatic SCAs do not require surveillance.

Solid-Pseudopapillary Neoplasm (SPN)
  • - Rare tumors, predominantly in young women (10:1 ratio), often in their 20s.
  • - Aggressive behavior (e.g., vascular invasion, metastasis) is found in ~10% pathologically.
  • - Prognosis after resection is excellent, with a 5-year disease-specific survival of >98%.
