Real‑world effectiveness of advanced therapies in microscopic colitis
  • Source: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology (2026) – DOI 10.1016/j.cgh.2025.12.019
  • Topic: Advanced IBD biologics & small molecules for budesonide‑refractory/dependent MC
1. Study Overview & Rationale
  • Background: MC → chronic watery diarrhoea, ↑ prevalence in elderly; budesonide first‑line but >50% relapse or become refractory/dependent.
  • Therapeutic Gap: Off‑label advanced IBD therapies increasingly used; real‑world efficacy data scarce.
  • Aim: Multinational retrospective assessment of clinical response, remission, drug durability, and predictors after budesonide failure/intolerance.
2. Design & Population
  • Network: ECCO CONFER (European Crohn’s & Colitis Organisation Collaborative Network for Exceptionally Rare cases).
  • Study Type: Observational, multicentre, retrospective cohort; STROBE‑compliant.
  • Inclusion Criteria: Histologically confirmed MC (collagenous or lymphocytic) treated with any biologic or small‑molecule after budesonide failure/intolerance.
  • Treatment Cycle Definition: Each initiation of a new advanced therapy counted as a distinct cycle, regardless of prior exposure.
  • Cohort Size: 142 patients → 229 treatment cycles across 15 countries & 38 centres.
Baseline Demographics (Table 1)
  • Sex: Female 85.9% (n=122).
  • Age at MC diagnosis: Median 52.6 y [IQR 38.3‑61.7].
  • Age at first advanced therapy: Median 56.6 y [44.3‑65.3]; median 2.7 y after diagnosis.
  • Histology: Collagenous colitis 52.8% (n=75); Lymphocytic colitis 47.2% (n=67).
  • Budesonide discontinuation reasons: PNR 11.3%, dependency 45.4%, secondary LOR 35.5%, adverse events 7.8%.
3. Therapeutic Landscape & Exposure
3.1 Drug Class Utilization (Figure 1)
  • Anti‑TNF agents: 55.9% of cycles (n=128).
  • Vedolizumab: 28.8% (n=66).
  • JAK inhibitors: 9.2% (n=21) – tofacitinib 3.9%, filgotinib 2.6%, upadacitinib 2.6%.
  • Ustekinumab: 4.8% (n=11).
  • Anti‑IL23p19 & S1PR modulators: <1% total.
3.2 Line of Therapy Distribution
  • First‑line: Predominantly anti‑TNF, followed by vedolizumab; JAK inhibitors mainly later lines.
  • Median exposure (years): infliximab 0.9, adalimumab 0.9, vedolizumab 0.7, ustekinumab 0.6, JAK inhibitors 1.6.
4. Short‑Term Efficacy (Induction Phase)
4.1 Overall Outcomes
  • Clinical response: 65.1% of cycles.
  • Clinical remission: 48.9% of cycles (Hjörtswang criteria: ≤3 stools/d or ≤1 watery stool/d).
4.2 Agent‑Specific Induction Results
Agent Baseline SF (stools/24 h) → End‑Induction SF Response % Remission %
Infliximab 7.0 → 3.0 (p<0.001) 68.1% 48.6%
Adalimumab 6.0 → 4.0 (p<0.001) 60.8% 51.0%
Vedolizumab ↓ significant (p<0.001) 59.1% 40.9%
Ustekinumab ↓ (p=0.01) 54.5% 36.4%
JAK inhibitors* 7.0 → 2.0 (p<0.001) 95.2% 81.0%

\*Predominantly later‑line use; small sample size but markedly superior outcomes.

4.3 Comparative Statistics
  • GEE analysis: JAK inhibitors vs anti‑TNF (reference) → OR 8.71 for response (95% CI 1.70‑44.74, p=0.009); OR 4.39 for remission (95% CI 1.42‑13.60, p=0.01).
  • Vedolizumab & ustekinumab: No significant difference vs anti‑TNF (p>0.3).
  • Binary JAK vs non‑JAK: OR 10.0 for response (95% CI 1.85‑50.0, p=0.008); OR 5.0 for remission (95% CI 1.64‑14.3, p=0.005).
  • Multivariable model: No clinical covariates (sex, age, smoking, histology) independently predicted short‑term response; drug class was sole predictor.
5. Long‑Term Drug Persistence & Durability
  • Overall discontinuation: 56 % of cycles stopped after induction; main reasons PNR 45.9%, secondary LOR 28.7%, AE/intolerance 18.0%.
  • Discontinuation by histology: CC 56.1% vs LC 53.8% (p=0.79) – no significant difference.
5.1 Agent‑Specific Discontinuation Patterns
5.1.1 Anti‑TNF agents
  • Infliximab: 62.5% discontinued (median 0.8 y); reasons: intolerance 33.3%, secondary LOR 31.1%, PNR 28.9%.
  • Monotherapy immunogenicity: 43.1% received infliximab alone → higher antibody formation risk.
  • Adalimumab: 54.9% discontinued (median 0.6 y); reasons: PNR 57.1%, secondary LOR 35.7%; intolerance rare (3.6%).
5.1.2 Vedolizumab
  • Discontinuation 56.1% (median exposure 0.5 y).
  • Predominant causes: PNR 62.2%, secondary LOR 27.0%.
  • No AE‑related stops reported.
5.1.3 Ustekinumab
  • Discontinued in 57.1% of cycles (median 0.3 y).
  • Reasons: PNR 57.1%, secondary LOR 14.3%, intolerance 14.3%.
5.1.4 JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, filgotinib, upadacitinib)
  • Durability advantage: OR 4.76 for persistence vs non‑JAK (95% CI 1.64‑14.3, p=0.004).
  • Discontinuation cause: intolerance only (median 0.4 y).
  • Cox proportional hazards: HR 0.41 (95% CI 0.21‑0.82, p=0.01) vs pooled biologics; marginal model HR 0.39 (p=0.008).
5.2 Multivariate Predictors of Persistence
  • GEE analysis (clustered on patient ID): drug class sole independent predictor (OR 5.07 for JAK vs others, 95% CI 1.52‑16.9, p=0.008).
  • Other covariates: age, sex, smoking status, histologic subtype → non‑significant.
5.2.1 Individual Drug Comparison (reference infliximab)
  • JAK inhibitors: OR 6.35 for continuation (95% CI 1.79‑22.5, p=0.004).
  • Anti‑TNF agents, vedolizumab, ustekinumab → no significant difference vs infliximab.
6. Safety Profile & Surgical Outcomes
  • New safety signals: none observed across entire cohort.
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE) concern: MC patients have baseline ↑ VTE risk; JAK inhibitors carry EMA warnings for elderly, but no excess VTE events recorded in this real‑world series.

6.2 Surgical Interventions
  • Overall: 4.2% (n=6) required colorectal surgery despite advanced therapy.
  • Breakdown: 5 proctocolectomies, 1 diverting ileostomy; 83.3% of surgical cases had collagenous colitis.
7. Discussion, Limitations & Future Directions
  • Key finding: JAK inhibitors provide superior short‑term response/remission and markedly better long‑term persistence compared with anti‑TNF agents, vedolizumab, and ustekinumab in budesonide‑refractory/dependent MC.
  • Contextualisation: Results align with prior meta‑analyses showing modest efficacy of anti‑TNFs/vedolizumab; extend evidence by providing standardized outcome definitions and larger sample (229 cycles).
7.1 Strengths
  • Largest multinational real‑world dataset for advanced therapies in MC.
  • Uniform data capture via CONFER reporting form; outcomes assessed at both induction and maintenance phases.
  • Robust statistical handling of intra‑patient clustering (GEE, mixed models, frailty Cox).
7.2 Limitations
  • Retrospective design: inherent selection and reporting bias; centres are expert IBD sites → may overestimate effectiveness.
  • Histologic reassessment absent: unable to correlate mucosal healing with clinical outcomes.
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) missing: especially for anti‑TNFs, limiting insight into immunogenicity mechanisms.
  • Microscopic Colitis Score (MCS) not collected: precludes standardized disease activity quantification beyond stool frequency.
7.3 Research Imperatives

1. Prospective registries integrating clinical, endoscopic, histologic, and biomarker endpoints (e.g., JAK/STAT activation signatures).

2. Randomized controlled trials of JAK inhibitors vs standard care to confirm efficacy, safety, and optimal dosing in MC.

3. Development of a standardized outcome framework endorsed by regulatory bodies to harmonize future trial reporting.

7.4 Clinical Implications
  • Consider JAK inhibitor therapy early for patients with budesonide‑refractory or -dependent MC, especially when rapid induction of remission is desired.
  • Monitor closely for intolerance; assess VTE risk in elderly/comorbid populations per EMA guidance.
  • Continue to use anti‑TNF agents and vedolizumab as viable options, recognizing higher discontinuation rates and modest durability.
