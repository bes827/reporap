üö® Rationale for GC Screening in the U.S.
  • - Gastric Cancer (GC) is a leading cause of preventable cancer death in certain US populations.
  • - The focus is on intestinal-type noncardia gastric adenocarcinoma, primarily related to chronic H. pylori infection.
U.S. Statistics & Disparities
  • - Estimated 26,500 new cases and 11,130 deaths in 2023.
  • - Dismal 5-year survival rate of 32%, as <25% are diagnosed at an early, curable stage.
  • - Noncardia GC disproportionately affects racial/ethnic minorities, immigrants, and underserved populations.
Comparison with Screening Programs (Japan/South Korea)
  • - In countries with screening, >60-70% of GCs are diagnosed early.
  • - Survival rates are >70% overall and 95-99% for early-stage GC.
  • - A meta-analysis showed early-stage GCs were more common in screened (73%) vs. non-screened (43%) groups.


Demographic Trends
  • - By 2065, immigrants and their descendants will account for nearly 90% of US population growth.
  • - Immigrants from high-incidence regions (Asia, Latin America) retain their elevated GC risk.


üéØ Identifying High-Risk Individuals for Screening
  • - Best Practice Advice 1: Identifiable high-risk groups in the US should be considered for GC screening.
  • - A personalized risk assessment is crucial; universal age-based screening is not cost-effective.
Key Risk Factors (See Table 1)
  • - First-generation immigrants from high-incidence regions (GC incidence ‚â•10‚Äì12 per 100,000).
  • - Regions include: Eastern Europe, Andean Latin America, East Asia.
  • - Family history of GC in a first-degree relative.
  • - Certain hereditary syndromes (e.g., FAP, Lynch syndrome).
Racial & Ethnic Groups
  • - Non-White groups have a 2- to >10-fold higher noncardia GC incidence.
  • - Korean Americans ‚â•50 years old have up to 14.5-fold higher risk.

Socioeconomic & Behavioral Factors
  • - Living in areas of persistent poverty (GC mortality is 43% higher).
  • - History of chronic H. pylori infection plus:
  • - - Smoking history (>20 pack-years).
  • - - Diet high in salt, red/processed meats.


Recommended Screening Age
  • - Optimal age to initiate screening is unclear, but age 45 is suggested.
  • - - Aligns with CRC screening age for combined procedures.
  • - For family history, start 10 years earlier than the youngest affected relative.


ü©∫ Primary Screening Modality: Endoscopy
  • - Best Practice Advice 2: Endoscopy is the best test for screening and surveillance.
  • - It allows direct visualization, mucosal staging, and biopsies for risk stratification.
Elements of High-Quality Endoscopy
  • - Best Practice Advice 3: A high-quality exam is critical for detection.
  • - The endoscopic miss rate for early GC is consistently around 10%.
Technical Requirements
  • - Use a high-definition white-light system.
  • - Employ image enhancement (e.g., NBI, LCI).
  • - Ensure adequate gastric mucosal cleansing and insufflation.
  • - Allow for adequate visual inspection time and photodocumentation.


Effectiveness & Justification
  • - A meta-analysis (10 Asian studies) showed endoscopic screening was associated with a 42% reduction in GC mortality.
  • - Cost-effectiveness analyses support adding EGD to screening colonoscopy in high-risk US groups (Asian, Hispanic, Black Americans).


ü¶† H. pylori Eradication: Adjunctive Prevention
  • - Best Practice Advice 4: H. pylori eradication is an essential adjunct to endoscopic screening.
  • - H. pylori is responsible for 90% of the global GC burden.
Evidence for Eradication
  • - Meta-analysis of non-US RCTs showed eradication leads to:
  • - - 46% reduction in GC incidence (NNT = 72).
  • - - 39% reduction in GC mortality (NNT = 135).
  • - Benefit is greatest before development of precancerous changes (atrophy/metaplasia).
Impact on Pre-existing Lesions
  • - A non-US RCT with 27-year follow-up found:
  • - - Eradication in patients without baseline precancerous changes ---> 63% GC risk reduction.
  • - - Eradication in patients overall ---> 43% GC risk reduction.


Screening & Treatment Strategy
  • - Consider opportunistic screening (non-serologic tests) in high-risk individuals.
  • - Consider familial-based testing (screening adult household members).
  • - - Family-based approach has a 3-fold higher eradication success rate and 70% lower recurrence rate.
  • - Confirm eradication in all patients at least 4 weeks after treatment.
Optimal Age for H. pylori Screening
  • - Not established for the US population.
  • - Taipei Global Consensus suggests screening between ages 20 and 40 for primary prevention.


Limitations
  • - Eradication is not a stand-alone strategy.
  • - Individuals with existing advanced histologic changes (GIM, atrophy) remain at risk and require endoscopic surveillance.


üî¨ Endoscopic & Histologic Management
  • - A systematic approach is needed to detect, biopsy, and report premalignant conditions.
Biopsy Protocol for Staging
  • - Best Practice Advice 5: Use a systematic protocol (e.g., updated Sydney System) for suspected atrophy/metaplasia.
Procedure
  • - Obtain a minimum of 5 total biopsies.
  • - Place samples from antrum/incisura and corpus in separately labeled jars.
  • - Biopsy any suspicious areas separately.


Improving Detection
  • - Best Practice Advice 6: GIM and dysplasia are endoscopically detectable but often missed in the US due to lack of familiarity.
  • - Image-enhancing technologies (NBI, LCI) can detect GIM with sensitivity/specificity >85-90% without magnification.

Pathology Reporting Consensus
  • - Best Practice Advice 7: Endoscopists and pathologists should agree on consistent reporting for atrophic gastritis/metaplasia.
Minimum Required Documentation
  • - Presence/absence of H. pylori.
  • - Severity of atrophy and/or metaplasia.
  • - Histologic subtyping of GIM (e.g., incomplete vs. complete), if applicable.



üó∫Ô∏è Post-Screening Management Pathway (See Figure 1)
  • - Management is determined by the findings of the index screening endoscopy.
Normal Mucosa (No Atrophy, GIM, or Neoplasia)
  • - Best Practice Advice 8: The decision to continue screening depends on individual risk factors and patient preference.
  • - Consider ongoing screening for those with a strong family history or multiple risk factors.
  • - Optimal interval is not well-defined, but consider every 3-5 years.

Atrophic Gastritis (AG) / Gastric Intestinal Metaplasia (GIM)
  • - Best Practice Advice 9: All individuals with confirmed AG/GIM require risk stratification.
High-Risk Features Warranting Surveillance
  • - Severe atrophic gastritis.
  • - Multifocal or incomplete-type GIM.
  • - Corpus-extended GIM.
  • - Other risk factors (e.g., family history).

Surveillance Interval
  • - Consider endoscopic surveillance every 3 years.
  • - Shorter intervals may be needed for multiple risk factors or severe, extensive GIM.


Dysplasia & Early Gastric Cancer (EGC)
  • - Best Practice Advice 10 & 11: Management requires expertise and often referral.
Indefinite (IND) & Low-Grade Dysplasia (LGD)
  • - All dysplasia diagnoses should be confirmed by an experienced GI pathologist.
  • - - Approx. 40% of IND cases are reclassified on expert review.
  • - Eradicate H. pylori to reduce inflammation and improve diagnostic accuracy.
  • - Refer patient to a center with expertise in gastric neoplasia.
Follow-up for Non-Visible Dysplasia
  • - After H. pylori eradication, perform a repeat high-quality EGD with IEE by an experienced endoscopist.
  • - For non-visible IND/LGD, repeat EGD within 6 months.
  • - If dysplasia persists as non-visible, repeat EGD in 6-12 months (LGD) or 12 months (IND).


High-Grade Dysplasia (HGD) & EGC
  • - Refer to an expert for Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD) with the goal of en bloc, R0 resection.
  • - H. pylori eradication is essential but should not delay intervention.
Follow-up for Non-Visible HGD
  • - A 'second-look' endoscopy should be done as soon as possible due to high risk of synchronous cancer.
  • - If still non-visible, repeat EGD with biopsies in 3 months.

Risk of Upstaging
  • - For visible dysplasia, endoscopic resection is both diagnostic and therapeutic.
  • - A meta-analysis showed that after resection, nearly 25% of lesions with LGD on biopsy were up-staged:
  • - - 17% to HGD.
  • - - 7% to carcinoma.



Surveillance After Neoplasia Resection
  • - Best Practice Advice 12: All patients with resected gastric neoplasia require ongoing endoscopic surveillance.
  • - Surveillance should be continued indefinitely for resected HGD or GC.
  • - Intervals depend on lesion histology and resection completeness (R0 status).


üè® Special Conditions & Considerations
  • - Management of specific findings and patient populations.
Type I Gastric Carcinoids (gNETs)
  • - Best Practice Advice 13: These are typically indolent tumors found in the setting of atrophic gastritis.
  • - Account for 70-80% of all gNETs.
Management by Size
  • - <1 cm: May consider resection.
  • - 1‚Äì2 cm: Should be endoscopically resected.
  • - >2 cm: High risk of metastasis. Refer for cross-sectional imaging and surgical resection.


De-escalation of Screening & Surveillance
  • - Best Practice Advice 14: Screening and surveillance should only be performed on individuals fit for endoscopic or surgical treatment.
  • - Stop when a person is no longer a candidate for intervention due to comorbidities or preference.

Implementation & Health Equity
  • - Best Practice Advice 15: A personalized approach is needed to address disparities.
  • - Address modifiable risk factors (H. pylori, poverty, diet, tobacco) that disproportionately affect high-risk groups.
Pillars for a Successful Program (See Figure 2)
  • - Pre-procedural: Identify high-risk individuals, ensure access and insurance coverage.
  • - Intra-procedural: Ensure high-quality endoscopy and ESD proficiency.
  • - Post-procedural: Equitable access to surveillance and oncologic care.
