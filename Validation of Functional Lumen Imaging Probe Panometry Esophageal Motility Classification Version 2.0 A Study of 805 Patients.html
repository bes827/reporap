Validation of Functional Lumen Imaging Probe Panometry Esophageal Motility Classification Version 2.0: A Study of 805 Patients
  • Journal: Neurogastroenterology & Motility.
  • Publication Date: 22 October 2025.
  • Funding: R01 DK137775 (J.E.P. and D.A.C.) from Public Health service.
  • Study validates FLIP panometry v2.0 classification for esophageal motility.
Knowledge Test

  • 2. What percentage of patients with non-spastic obstruction on FLIP panometry had a conclusive disorder of EGJ outflow on HRM?
  • 3. What pressure threshold at 60 mL EGJ-DI differentiates disordered from diminished contractile response in FLIP v2.0?
  • 4. What percentage of type III achalasia patients were classified as spastic obstruction on FLIP panometry v2.0?
  • 5. What is the maximum hiatal hernia size allowed for inclusion in this study?
Introduction to FLIP Panometry
  • FLIP panometry assesses esophageal motility.
  • Displays esophageal diameter topography + associated distensive pressure.
  • Evaluates EGJ opening mechanics + contractile response (CR) to distension.
Advantages over HRM
  • Performed during sedated endoscopy → ↑ patient tolerance.
  • Real-time motility interpretation.
  • Procedure + interpretation ≈ several minutes.
Evolution of FLIP Classification
  • Initial FLIP v1.0 effective, comparable to HRM/CCv4.0.
  • Dallas Consensus introduced FLIP v2.0 → standardized protocol + refined classification.
  • Refinement mirrors iterative changes in Chicago Classification for HRM.
Key Changes: v1.0 → v2.0
  • EGJ opening criteria unchanged; 'borderline' (v1.0) → 'inconclusive' (v2.0).
  • Impaired/disordered CR (v1.0) subdivided into diminished vs. disordered CR.
  • Obstruction w/ weak CR reclassified into non-spastic + spastic subtypes.
Contractile Response (CR) Pattern Changes
  • v1.0: Impaired/Disordered CR.
  • v2.0: Diminished CR (FLIP pressure ≤ 40 mmHg @ 60mL EGJ-DI); Disordered CR (FLIP pressure > 40 mmHg @ 60mL EGJ-DI).
Motility Classification Changes
  • v1.0: Obstruction w/ weak CR.
  • v2.0: Non-spastic obstruction; Spastic obstruction.
  • Aimed to improve achalasia phenotyping + align w/ HRM.
Methods: Study Design & Protocols
  • Multicenter study: 805 adult patients (18–91 yrs).
  • Completed both FLIP + HRM @ two tertiary esophageal centers.
  • Primary analysis: 704 patients w/ conclusive CCv4.0 diagnoses.
Subject Inclusion/Exclusion
  • Inclusion: Adult patients presenting for esophageal symptom evaluation, underwent FLIP during sedated EGD w/ corresponding HRM.
  • Exclusion: Technically limited FLIP/HRM; prior foregut surgery/pneumatic dilation; esophageal mechanical obstruction (stricture, EoE, severe reflux esophagitis [LA grade C/D], or hiatal hernia > 3 cm).
FLIP Study Protocol & Analysis
  • Performed during sedated EGD (moderate sedation/propofol).
  • 16-cm FLIP catheter (EndoFLIP EF-322N) per Dallas consensus.
  • Catheter positioned w/ distal 1–3 impedance sensors beyond EGJ.
Distension Protocol
  • Bag filled stepwise 10-mL increments from 40 mL to 70 mL.
  • Each distension volume maintained for 30–60 s.
Data Analysis
  • Post-endoscopy using customized program (wklytics.com/nmgi).
  • Raters blinded to clinical details (HRM, TBE).
  • Interpretation used FLIP Panometry motility classification v1.0 + v2.0 criteria.
Assessment Parameters
  • Contractile response (CR) pattern: evaluated during 50–70 mL fill volumes.
  • EGJ opening classification: based on EGJ-distensibility index (EGJ-DI) @ 60 mL + maximum EGJ diameter @ 60–70 mL.
FLIP Panometry v2.0 Classification Criteria
  • Defines CR patterns, EGJ opening, + motility classifications.
Contractile Response (CR) Patterns
  • Normal CR: >1 distinct antegrade contractions (≥6-cm axial length) w/ FLIP pressure rise >10 mmHg.
  • Diminished CR: Contractility present, not normal/spastic; FLIP pressure (w/ 60mL EGJ-DI) ≤40 mmHg.
  • Disordered CR: Contractility present, not normal/spastic; FLIP pressure (w/ 60mL EGJ-DI) >40 mmHg.
  • Absent CR: No contractile activity in esophageal body.
  • Spastic CR: Presence of sustained occluding contractions OR sustained lower esophageal sphincter contractions.
EGJ Opening Classifications
  • Reduced EGJ opening: EGJ-DI <2.0 mm²/mmHg AND Maximum EGJ diameter <12 mm.
  • Inconclusive EGJ opening: EGJ-DI <2.0 mm²/mmHg OR Maximum EGJ diameter <16 mm (not meeting reduced criteria).
  • Normal EGJ opening: EGJ-DI ≥2.0 mm²/mmHg AND Maximum EGJ diameter ≥16 mm.
Motility Classifications (v2.0)
  • Normal motility: Normal EGJ opening + Normal CR.
  • Hypocontractility: Normal EGJ opening + Absent/Diminished CR.
  • Possible spasm: Normal EGJ opening + Spastic/Disordered CR.
  • Possible obstruction: Inconclusive EGJ opening + Any CR (recommended to specify CR pattern).
  • Spastic obstruction: Reduced EGJ opening + Spastic/Disordered CR.
  • Non-spastic obstruction: Reduced EGJ opening + Absent/Diminished CR.
  • Obstruction w/ normal contractility: Reduced EGJ opening + Normal CR (none observed in study).
HRM Protocol & Analysis
  • Performed after ≥6-h fast.
  • 4.2-mm solid-state assembly w/ 36 circumferential pressure sensors.
  • Transnasal placement, positioned hypopharynx to stomach.
HRM Protocol
  • 2-min baseline recording.
  • CCv4.0 protocol: 10x 5-mL liquid swallows (supine), then 5x 5-mL liquid swallows (upright).
Data Analysis
  • Analyzed per CCv4.0 criteria; investigators blinded.
  • Abnormal IRP: >15 mmHg (supine swallows) OR >12 mmHg (upright swallows).
Role of Timed Barium Esophagram (TBE)
  • Applied for inconclusive HRM (EGJOO/absent contractility w/ IRP @ upper limit of normal).
  • Upright position: 200 mL low-density barium sulfate; anteroposterior images @ 1, 2, 5 min.
  • 12.5 mm barium tablet administered if no significant retention after 5 min.
TBE Interpretation
  • Abnormal TBE: Barium column height >5 cm @ 5 min OR >5 cm @ 1 min + tablet impaction → conclusive disorders of EGJ outflow.
  • Normal TBE: Normal EGJ outflow.
  • Missing/Inconclusive TBE: Excluded from primary analysis due to clinical uncertainty.
Statistical Analysis
  • Results: mean (SD) or median (IQR).
  • Group comparisons: χ² test (categorical); ANOVA/t-tests or Kruskal–Wallis/Mann–Whitney U test (continuous).
  • Statistical significance: two-tailed p-value <0.05.
  • Software: R version 4.4.2 (tidyverse, ggplot2 packages).
Results: Cohort Characteristics
  • Total cohort: 805 patients.
  • Mean age: 55 (17) years; 62% female.
  • Dysphagia: most common indication (75%).
Study Sites
  • Site 1: 607 subjects.
  • Site 2: 198 subjects.
Study Interval
  • 66% underwent FLIP + HRM on same day.
  • Remainder: median interval 1.5 (0.6–3.2) months between studies.
HRM Classifications (Total Cohort)
  • Normal esophageal motility: 264 (33%).
  • Achalasia (subtypes I, II, or III): 241 (30%).
Inconclusive HRM/CCv4.0 Diagnoses
  • 101 patients (mean age 61 (14) yrs; 66% female).
  • Most common reason: inconclusive HRM (typically EGJOO) + absence of TBE (88 participants).
Adverse Events
  • No adverse events during FLIP studies.
Cohort Characteristics by FLIP v2.0 Classification
  • Dysphagia: most common indication across all FLIP v2.0 classifications.
  • Reflux: more common in normal, hypocontractility, + possible spasm classifications.
  • Mild esophagitis (LA Grade A/B): more common in normal + hypocontractility classifications.
  • Hiatal hernia (on HRM; ≤3 cm): least frequent in non-spastic obstruction.
FLIP v2.0 Classification Distribution (Total Cohort)
  • Normal motility: 19%.
  • Hypocontractility: 11%.
  • Non-spastic obstruction: 23%.
  • Spastic obstruction: 11%.
  • Possible spasm: 7.2%.
  • Possible obstruction: 28%.
  • Obstruction w/ normal contractility: 0% (no patients classified).
Results: FLIP Panometry Evaluation (Primary Analysis, n=704)
  • Primary analysis focused on 704 participants w/ conclusive HRM/CCv4.0 diagnosis.
EGJ Opening Classifications
  • Normal EGJ opening: 266 (38%) patients.
  • Inconclusive EGJ opening: 199 (28%) patients.
  • Reduced EGJ opening: 239 (34%) patients.
Correlation w/ HRM/CCv4.0
  • Normal EGJ opening: 98.5% had normal EGJ outflow per HRM/CCv4.0.
  • Reduced EGJ opening: 81.6% had a disorder of EGJ outflow.
Contractile Response (CR) Patterns
  • Normal CR: 158 (22.4%) participants.
  • Diminished CR: 85 (12.1%) participants.
  • Absent CR: 252 (35.8%) participants.
  • Disordered CR: 113 (16.1%) participants.
  • Spastic CR: 96 (13.6%) participants.
Correlation w/ Achalasia/EGJOO
  • Only 1 patient w/ normal CR had achalasia (type III).
  • 3 patients w/ normal CR had conclusive EGJOO.
Results: FLIP Panometry v2.0 Motility Classifications (Primary Analysis, n=704)
  • FLIP v2.0 classifications frequently paralleled HRM/CCv4.0 diagnoses.
Normal Motility (FLIP v2.0)
  • 137 patients classified as normal by FLIP panometry.
  • 81% had normal motility per CCv4.0.
  • 12% had IEM per CCv4.0.
Predictive Value
  • 99% negative predictive value for disorders of EGJ outflow.
  • 0 patients w/ achalasia on HRM.
Hypocontractility (FLIP v2.0)
  • 78 patients classified as hypocontractility.
  • 72% had absent contractility or IEM.
  • 27% had normal motility.
  • 1 patient had type II achalasia (symptoms minimal, TBE normal clearance).
Non-Spastic Obstruction (FLIP v2.0)
  • 163 patients classified as non-spastic obstruction.
  • 77% had type I (31%) or II (47%) achalasia.
  • 8.0% had type III achalasia.
  • 5.5% had conclusive EGJOO.
Predictive Value
  • 91% had a disorder of EGJ outflow by HRM/CCv4.0.
Spastic Obstruction (FLIP v2.0)
  • 76 patients classified as spastic obstruction.
  • 32% had type I (2.6%) or II (29%) achalasia.
  • 21% had type III achalasia.
Type III Achalasia Correlation
  • Among 37 patients w/ type III achalasia:
  • 43% classified as spastic obstruction by FLIP v2.0.
  • 35% classified as non-spastic obstruction.
  • 22% classified as possible obstruction.
Possible Obstruction (FLIP v2.0)
  • 199 patients classified as possible obstruction.
  • 38% had normal motility.
  • 26% had type I/II achalasia.
  • 16% had IEM.
Results: Comparing FLIP Panometry v1.0 and v2.0
  • v2.0 refinements aimed to improve alignment w/ HRM findings.
Reclassification of 'Obstruction w/ Weak CR' (v1.0)
  • v1.0 'Obstruction w/ weak CR' patients (26% type I, 43% type II, 10% type III achalasia) were reclassified in v2.0.
  • Reclassified as:
  • Non-spastic obstruction (v2.0): 31% type I, 47% type II, 8.0% type III achalasia.
  • Spastic obstruction (v2.0): 3% type I, 29% type II, 21% type III achalasia.
Reclassification of 'Weak' (v1.0)
  • v1.0 'Weak' classification (32% absent contractility, 27% IEM, 2.7% hypercontractile esophagus) reclassified in v2.0.
  • Reclassified as:
  • Hypocontractility (v2.0): 44% absent contractility, 28% IEM, 0% hypercontractile/spasm.
  • Possible spasm (v2.0): 2.0% absent contractility, 24% IEM, 7.8% hypercontractile esophagus, 0% spasm.
Reclassification of 'Spastic Reactive' (v1.0)
  • v1.0 'Spastic reactive' classification (13% type III achalasia) reclassified in v2.0.
  • Reclassified as:
  • Spastic obstruction (v2.0): 21% of type III achalasia.
  • Possible obstruction (v2.0): 4.0% of type III achalasia.
  • Possible spasm (v2.0): 0% type III achalasia.
Discussion: Key Findings & Clinical Implications
  • FLIP panometry v2.0 effectively categorizes esophageal motility.
  • Frequently aligns w/ HRM + CCv4.0.
  • Supports FLIP panometry as effective for diagnosing esophageal motility disorders.
Normal Motility (FLIP v2.0)
  • Associated w/ normal motility or IEM on HRM in 93% (127/137) patients.
  • High negative predictive value (99%) for disorders of EGJ outflow (0 achalasia cases).
  • Suggests FLIP can rule out achalasia; may guide management away from HRM if assessed during index endoscopy.
Alignment w/ Healthy Volunteers
  • Proportion of DES (1%–14%) + IEM (7%–17%) in healthy volunteers on HRM aligns w/ findings in normal FLIP v2.0.
Non-Spastic Obstruction (FLIP v2.0)
  • Strongly associated w/ achalasia or conclusive EGJOO.
  • Positive predictive value: 91%.
  • 77% were non-spastic (type I or II) achalasia.
Differentiation of Impaired/Disordered CR (IDCR)
  • Major v2.0 update: IDCR (v1.0) → diminished CR or disordered CR.
  • Distinction based on 60 mL FLIP pressure measurements.
  • Pressure threshold: 40 mmHg @ 60-mL EGJ-DI.
Improved Achalasia Subtyping
  • Non-spastic obstruction (v2.0, includes diminished + absent CR): 77% non-spastic (type I/II) achalasia (vs. 69% w/ v1.0 'obstruction w/ weak CR').
  • Spastic obstruction (v2.0, includes disordered + spastic CR): encompassed nearly half (16/37) of type III achalasia patients (21% of spastic obstruction cases).
  • Also included 22 patients (29%) w/ type II achalasia → suggests identifiable spastic variant of type II achalasia.
Enhanced Identification of Hypocontractile Disorders
  • 72% of hypocontractility (FLIP v2.0) had IEM or absent contractility on HRM/CCv4.0.
  • ↑ from 59% using FLIP v1.0 'weak motility' classification.
'Possible Spasm' Classification (FLIP v2.0)
  • Introduced to distinguish primary esophageal spasm from hypocontractile disorders.
  • Only 8% of 'possible spasm' patients had hypercontractile esophagus or DES per HRM/CCv4.0.
  • 65% had normal HRM → suggests impaired secondary peristalsis, not spastic variant w/ normal HRM.
  • Area requiring further refinement + validation.
Study Strengths
  • Large patient cohort w/ comprehensive esophageal evaluations.
  • Application of HRM + CCv4.0.
  • Cohort distinct from initial FLIP v1.0 study.
  • Multicenter design → enhances generalizability.
  • Higher prevalence of achalasia (referral centers) is a strength, as achalasia identification is clinically significant.
Study Limitations
  • Absence of a definitive gold standard for diagnosis (key challenge in esophageal motility research).
  • Inconclusive/discordant diagnoses still occur despite state-of-the-art methods.
  • FLIP goal ≠ replicate HRM; methods assess distinct physiological processes (HRM: primary peristalsis; FLIP: secondary peristalsis).
Conclusion
  • FLIP panometry v2.0 accurately identified esophageal motility disorders.
  • Should be interpreted w/ endoscopic + clinical data, similar to HRM.
  • Potential to expedite diagnosis + inform immediate endoscopic interventions (reinspection, dilation) or guide further testing.
  • Complements HRM; may serve as viable alternative in select cases.
  • Future studies: broader populations, longitudinal outcomes, unique subgroups.
