  • - In a deposition, if a lawyer asks if you recognize a textbook as 'authoritative,' what is the legally safer response instead of 'Yes'?
  • - What is the term for the legal strategy, used in states like Oregon, that prohibits pretrial disclosure of expert witness identities?

The Interim: Between an Adverse Event and a Lawsuit
  • - Recommendations for physicians to manage the period following an adverse event but before litigation officially begins.
Initial Actions & Assessments
  • - Proactive steps to understand your position and prepare for potential legal action.
Inquire About the Statute of Limitations
  • - The risk manager or claims representative can provide this information.

Review Insurance Coverage
  • - Helps allay anxiety and identify immediate issues.
  • - Claims personnel are a key resource during this waiting period.

Reexamine Financial Health
  • - Review protections in place for financial security and personal assets.


Personal Well-being & Stress Management
  • - Strategies to manage personal health and stress, which is complicated by the adverse event.
Acknowledge & Observe Stress
  • - Recognize that an adverse event complicates ordinary work stress.
  • - Develop the habit of self-observation to monitor changes in feelings and regain emotional control.

Identify Controllable Stressors
  • - Balance sufficient control with healthy flexibility in medical practice.

Work for a Balanced Life
  • - Use the interim period as an opportunity to initiate constructive changes in behavior and goals.

Attend to Health
  • - Pay attention to any significant changes in physical or emotional health.
  • - If you don't have a personal physician, this is an ideal time to choose one.
  • - Promptly attending to health changes diminishes the risk of self-medicating or other destructive behaviors.

Address Personal Vulnerabilities
  • - Recognize and admit vulnerabilities exacerbated by stress to lessen their impact.


Handling Information Requests
  • - Be attentive and wary when receiving requests for information about the event; seek consultation if in doubt.
Recredentialing, Licensing, or Insurance Inquiries
  • - Honesty is the best policy, but it never demands saying more than the minimum required.
  • - If you have no knowledge of a pending claim (a demand for money or services), do not provide unnecessary information.

Serious or Catastrophic Events
  • - If an event is likely to lead to legal action, you should bring this to the attention of the appropriate forum.

Managed Care Inquiries
  • - Unless your agreement specifically requires it, you are under no obligation to reveal incidents that have not matured into legal cases.



Outline of the Legal Process
  • - A sequential overview of the key stages in a medical malpractice lawsuit, based on Table 7-1.
Summons & Complaint
  • - Summons: A formal legal document notifying that a suit has been filed.
  • - Complaint: Accompanies the summons and details the nature of the complaint in legal terms.

Pleading Stage
  • - The attorney communicates with the court by filing motions (requests for the court to do something).

Discovery Stage
  • - Process to discover information relevant to the case.
  • - Includes depositions (oral questions under oath) and interrogatories (written questions).

Expert Witnesses
  • - A malpractice case proceeds only if each side presents expert opinions on the standard of care.
  • - Exceptions: Cases of res ipsa loquitor or informed consent.

Summary Judgment
  • - An application for judgment before trial where no material questions of fact exist.
  • - If granted, the case is resolved without a jury trial.

Settlement
  • - An agreement between parties that resolves the legal dispute.

The Trial
  • - Occurs if pretrial maneuvers to resolve the case fail.

The Verdict
  • - The decision reached by the deciding body (judge or jury).

Post-Verdict Motions
  • - Legal maneuvers that can occur after a verdict is reached.
Directed Verdict
  • - A request made at the close of an opponent's case.
  • - If granted, the court decides as a matter of law that a party failed to prove their case, issuing a verdict without jury deliberation.

Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict (JNOV)
  • - A court may issue a judgment despite the verdict reached by a jury.
  • - A party requesting JNOV must have previously moved for a directed verdict.


Post-trial Activities
  • - Procedures to appeal the outcome, such as a post-trial motion or a formal appeal.


Physician's Role in Defense Preparation
  • - Key actions and mindset for physicians to adopt when a lawsuit begins.
Adopt a Proactive Stance
  • - A passive approach is a common regret among defendant physicians.
Decide to be a Good Defendant
  • - This requires active work and learning.

Resolve to be Active
  • - Passivity can lead to feelings of being vulnerable and defeated.
  • - Active participation motivates your lawyers and claims representatives to work more diligently.
  • - Your case is personal; it involves your integrity, reputation, and honor.

Balance Activity with Deference
  • - Be an active team member, but sensibly follow the advice of your attorneys.
  • - The attorney must lead the team and will decide how to best use your medical input.


First Meeting with Defense Counsel
  • - This is considered the most important meeting with your lawyers.
Tell the Truth
  • - Be completely honest; communication is protected by lawyer-client privilege.
  • - Avoid 'spinning' facts, as they will emerge later and cause embarrassment.

Come Prepared
  • - Before the meeting, memorize the facts of the case.
  • - Review your own notes and the entire hospital chart.
  • - Pay special attention to nurses' notes, as they often play a significant role in supporting or undercutting the case.

Disclose All Concerns
  • - Inform lawyers of any questions you had at the time of the incident.
  • - Mention any intuitions you had that the patient was a 'problem' even before the adverse event.

Avoid Blaming Others
  • - Your primary responsibility is to tell your side of the story honestly and fully.
  • - Do not speculate on the culpability or testimony of others.


Ongoing Collaboration with Legal Team
  • - Maintaining a strong, effective working relationship with your attorneys is crucial.
Clarify Misunderstandings
  • - Ask your attorney to summarize their understanding of what you've said.
  • - Immediately correct any misperceptions to prevent them from lingering.

Commit the Necessary Time
  • - Accept that the legal process has its own, often frustrating, timeline (delays, cancellations).
  • - Resisting or delaying requests from your attorney can harm your case.

Evolving Roles Throughout the Lawsuit
  • - Your responsibilities will change as the case progresses.
Initial Phase: Information Gathering
  • - Carefully review every document before forwarding it to lawyers.
  • - Check for missed signatures, unreviewed notes, or overlooked lab/radiology reports.

Expert Witness Selection
  • - You may be asked to help choose experts.
  • - You will be warned to refrain from contacting them yourselves.

Deposition Preparation
  • - Help lawyers prepare for depositions of both defense and plaintiff experts.
  • - Research experts' reputations, credentials, and publications.

Trial Preparation
  • - May involve formal coaching or 'witness school'.
  • - May be asked to help develop visual aids for the jury.




Demeanor and Behavior in Litigation
  • - Guidance on professional appearance and conduct during depositions and trial.
Professional Dress
  • - Dress as an approachable professional, similar to your office attire.
  • - For men: suit and tie. For women: business suit or attire.
  • - This conveys seriousness and respect for the process and participants.

Courtroom Conduct
  • - Your testimony is only one part of your defense; you are always being observed.
  • - Control emotional reactions (disappointment, elation) to court events.
  • - Jurors expect professionalism and respect, not overt anger or hostility.

Projecting Authenticity
  • - Be your 'best self': competent, caring, and knowledgeable.
  • - Show genuine interest by listening attentively and taking notes when appropriate.


The Deposition: A Deep Dive
  • - A detailed guide to the pretrial discovery process, with a focus on the physician's deposition.
Pretrial Discovery Process
  • - Goal: For both sides to learn everything possible about each other's case before trial.
Forms of Discovery
  • - Interrogatories: Written questions under oath.
  • - Requests for Admission of Documents: To verify authenticity of records.
  • - Requests for Production: To obtain an opponent's documentary evidence.

Disclosure Rules
  • - Most states and federal courts allow broad discovery and pretrial disclosure.
  • - This encourages settlement by revealing the strengths and weaknesses of each case.
Exception: Limited Discovery
  • - A few states, like Oregon, prohibit pretrial disclosure of expert witness identities or their testimony.
  • - This approach is known as 'trial by ambush'.



The Physician's Deposition
  • - You will almost certainly be deposed.
  • - Your testimony is under oath and is considered evidence that can decisively influence the outcome.
General Directions for Testifying
  • - Key principles for delivering effective and safe testimony.
Tell the Truth
  • - A single untruthful answer can destroy your defense.
  • - Ensure you fully hear and understand the question before answering.

Manage Feelings
  • - Plaintiff attorneys may try to provoke anger to disrupt your composure and accuracy.
  • - Respond to hostility with enlarged calm and increased self-control.

Answer ONLY the Question Asked
  • - Aim answers precisely at the 'bull's eye' of the question.
  • - Example: Q: 'When did you finish your residency?' ---> A: '1987' (NOT 'I finished at Stanford in 1987').

Take Time to Answer
  • - Beware of rapid-fire 'yes/no' questions designed to trap you.
  • - Never feel rushed; pause to think.

Avoid the Impulse to Teach
  • - Do not educate the opposing lawyer, even if they feign ignorance or mix up facts.
  • - Withhold correction of inaccurate science or loosely constructed hypotheses.

Hindsight is Not Admissible
  • - The focus is on events as they occurred at the time of the incident.
  • - Avoid the 'retrospectoscope'; frame your decisions as appropriate in the circumstances at the time.



Attorney Goals in Your Deposition
  • - Understanding the objectives of both the plaintiff and defense attorneys.
Plaintiff Attorney's Goals
  • - To establish what you know, get admissions, gauge your strength, and identify other evidence.
Get You to Make Admissions
  • - Example: Asking ifyou recognize a text as 'authoritative,' which can legally turn the book into an expert witness against you.
The 'Authoritative Text' Trap
  • - Q: '...you recognize this text as authoritative, do you not?'
  • - Inappropriate Response: 'Yes.'
  • - Appropriate Response: 'It is one of many useful texts.'


Ask Trick Questions
  • - Designed to elicit an emotional or expansive response that opens new lines of inquiry.
  • - Example: Q: 'Are you aware that... Dr. Brown just rolled his eyes and shook his head?'
  • - Inappropriate Response: 'Well, Dr. Brown has always been critical of my work...'
  • - Appropriate Response: 'First, I was not aware of that. And second, I know of no reason why Dr. Brown would act that way...'

Gauge Your Strengths & Weaknesses
  • - They assess if you are easily rattled, confused, or baited.
  • - A strong, credible performance can lead them to re-think whether they should continue with the case.

Identify Other Witnesses or Evidence
  • - They search your testimony for mention of other people or documents.
  • - They may ask directly who you have discussed the case with (other than your lawyer).

Make You a 'Hostile Witness'
  • - Using your own answers to undermine your testimony, often via 'dilemma questions'.
  • - Example: Q: '...if the physician uses reasonable care, this result does not ordinarily occur?'
  • - Inappropriate Response: 'That's correct.'
  • - Appropriate Response: 'Although the result may not ordinarily occur, when it does occur, it is most often due to the inherent risk of the procedure.'

Preserve Testimony for Trial
  • - If a key witness may be unavailable for trial (e.g., due to illness), their deposition (especially videotaped) can be used instead.


Defense Attorney's Goals & Tips
  • - Your attorney's role during the deposition and how they prepare you.
Filter Inappropriate Questions via Objections
  • - Objections are placed on the record for a judge to rule on later.
  • - In some instances, your attorney will instruct you not to answer (e.g., questions about privileged peer review findings).

Provide Clues Through Objections
  • - Listen to the objection; your lawyer is giving you important information.
Objection: 'Witness has no personal knowledge.'
  • - Message: You don't know the answer firsthand.
  • - Appropriate Response: 'I do not know.'

Objection: 'Compound question.'
  • - Message: The question has multiple parts.
  • - Your Action: Ask the examiner to rephrase or break it down.

Objection: 'Vague and ambiguous.'
  • - Message: The question is unclear.
  • - Your Action: Ask for clarification.


Prepare You for the Environment
  • - Preparation should be thorough, including practice questions and testing reactions to plaintiff lawyer tactics.
Presence of the Plaintiff
  • - All parties have the right to be present.
  • - The plaintiff may sit across from you as an intimidation tactic.
  • - Your job is to ignore them and focus on the attorneys and questions.

Videotaping
  • - Be prepared for the possibility of a videotaped deposition.
  • - This allows a jury to evaluate your demeanor as well as your words.

Attend Plaintiff's Depositions
  • - Unless advised otherwise, be present during the patient's deposition and those of key plaintiff experts.
  • - Your presence can make it more difficult for them to exaggerate.


Prepare You for Questioning Styles
  • - Understanding different types of questions and tactics.
Close-ended vs. Open-ended Questions
  • - Close-ended: Seek a concession (e.g., 'Isn't it true that...?').
  • - Open-ended: Seek information (e.g., 'What did you tell your patient...?'). Your goal is to provide minimal, precise information.

Questions with Assumed Facts
  • - A question may be embedded with a fact the attorney wants you to confirm.
  • - Example: 'Who was in the OR when you made the decision...?' (assumes you made the decision alone).
  • - Appropriate Response: 'I cannot answer the question as you have phrased it. Please break it up...'

Varying Cadence & Subject
  • - Lawyers may vary their pace or jump between subjects to throw you off.
  • - Key: Focus only on the one question being asked; do not anticipate the next one.

Getting You 'Off Script'
  • - Beware of broad questions like 'Can you explain why...?' or 'Give me all the reasons...'.
  • - These are designed to make you improvise and drift into dangerous areas. Respond truthfully but in a limited way.

Repeated Questions
  • - An attorney may ask the same question repeatedly to trick or frustrate you.
  • - Do not get angry or show irritation.




The Defendant Physician's Goals
  • - Your primary objectives for the deposition.
Finish Without a Major Mistake
  • - A deposition free of material error provides a firm basis for your defense expert witnesses.
  • - Accomplished by answering truthfully, behaving professionally, and mastering the case details.

Appear as a Formidable Opponent
  • - Convey that you are a competent, caring physician who practiced within the standard of care.
  • - A strong performance can lead the plaintiff's attorney to drop the case.
  • - Doing well gives you a sense of strength and resolve for a potential trial.
