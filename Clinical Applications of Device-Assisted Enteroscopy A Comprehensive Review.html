Introduction to DAE
  • Device-Assisted Enteroscopy (DAE) allows exploration of the small bowel, overcoming challenges of length and mobility.
  • - DAE types: Balloon-assisted (DBE, SBE, ODE) and Rotatable overtube-assisted (SE, MSE).
  • - Enables access via antegrade (mouth) or retrograde (anus) methods.

Procedure Techniques
  • DAE Techniques:
  • - DBE: Uses dual balloons for pleating the bowel, allowing deep access.
  • - SBE: A simpler, cost-effective method with a single balloon.
  • - ODE: On-demand balloon catheter system, no overtube required.
Double-Balloon Enteroscopy (DBE)
  • - Introduced in 2001, uses high-resolution enteroscope with dual balloons.
  • - Operated by pressure-controlled inflation pump at 5.6 kPa.
  • - Technique involves alternating scope and overtube advancement.

Single-Balloon Enteroscopy (SBE)
  • - Developed in 2007, simpler design without a distal balloon.
  • - Operated by pressure-controlled pump at -6 to +5.4 kPa.
  • - Requires effective overtube balloon control for successful advancement.

On-Demand Enteroscopy (ODE)
  • - Utilizes a through-the-scope balloon catheter.
  • - Allows for flexible, on-demand access without overtube preparation.
  • - Maintains pressure at 6 kPa for balloon inflation.

Spiral Enteroscopy (SE)
  • - Employs a screw-like mechanism for bowel advancement.
  • - Requires two operators for effective maneuvering.
  • - High risk of complications if not performed under controlled conditions.

Motor Spiral Enteroscopy (MSE)
  • - Introduced in 2016, allows motorized rotation.
  • - Withdrawn from clinical use due to adverse events.
  • - Initial studies showed promise but safety concerns arose.


Bowel Preparation
  • - Essential for clear visualization of the small bowel mucosa.
  • - Fasting: 8-12 hours for solids, 4-6 hours for liquids.
  • - Anti-foaming agents like simethicone can enhance visualization.

Diagnostic and Therapeutic Applications
  • Key Applications of DAE:
  • - Gastrointestinal Bleeding: 5-10% of GI bleeding from small bowel; DAE has a 70-90% success rate for hemostasis.
  • - Small-Bowel Tumors: DAE sensitivity of 89% for diagnosing tumors.
  • - Crohn's Disease: DAE alters management in 75% of patients.
Gastrointestinal Bleeding
  • - Common causes: angioectasia, tumors, NSAIDs.
  • - VCE identifies bleeding source in 60-80% of cases.
  • - DAE allows for therapeutic interventions with high success rates.

Small-Bowel Tumors
  • - 3-6% of GI neoplasms arise in the small intestine.
  • - DAE has a pooled sensitivity of 89% for diagnosing tumors.
  • - Allows for therapeutic interventions like endoscopic resection.

Crohn's Disease
  • - DAE identifies lesions often missed by other methods.
  • - Endoscopic Balloon Dilatation (EBD) has a success rate >90% for strictures.
  • - Complications occur in 0.12% without intervention.


Management of Obscure Gastrointestinal Bleeding
  • Obscure GIB: Defined as bleeding without identifiable source after standard evaluations.
  • - Video Capsule Endoscopy (VCE): Identifies bleeding in 63-67% of cases.
  • - Enteroscopy: Can be performed as first-line alternative to VCE.
Endoscopic Evaluation
  • - Second-look endoscopy: Success rates up to 25%.
  • - Push enteroscopy: Cost-effective for upper GIB evaluation.
  • - VCE: Best within 48 hours of bleeding onset.

Radiographic Evaluation
  • - CT Enterography (CTE): Recommended over MRE for GIB.
  • - Scintigraphy: Sensitive for identifying sources, but localization is challenging.
  • - Angiography: Newer modalities can detect bleeding rates as low as 0.1 mL/min.


Management Strategies
  • Endoscopic Hemostasis: Techniques include bipolar cautery, Argon Plasma Coagulation, and hemostatic clips.
  • - Somatostatin analogs: Octreotide reduces transfusion needs in angiodysplasia.
  • - Antiangiogenesis medications: Thalidomide shows promise in recurrent GIB.

Conclusion
  • DAE has revolutionized the approach to small bowel diseases, with ongoing technological advancements expected to enhance its capabilities.
  • - Essential for managing conditions like obscure GIB, small-bowel tumors, and Crohn's disease.
  • - Future innovations may improve diagnostic accuracy and therapeutic efficacy.
