Clinical Applications of Device-Assisted Enteroscopy: A Comprehensive Review
  • Overview of Device-Assisted Enteroscopy (DAE) - DAE enables exploration of the small bowel, overcoming challenges of traditional endoscopy. - It includes techniques like Double-Balloon Enteroscopy (DBE), Single-Balloon Enteroscopy (SBE), and Spiral Enteroscopy (SE). - DAE is crucial for diagnosing and treating small bowel diseases.

Introduction to DAE
  • Challenges in Small Bowel Evaluation - Small bowel's length and mobility complicate endoscopic access. - DAE allows for comprehensive exploration of the small bowel. - DAE techniques include balloon-assisted and overtube-assisted methods.

Types of DAE
  • Balloon-Assisted Enteroscopy - Double-Balloon Enteroscopy (DBE): Uses two balloons for pleating the bowel. - Single-Balloon Enteroscopy (SBE): More cost-effective, uses one balloon. - On-Demand Enteroscopy (ODE): Balloon catheter-guided, no overtube needed.
Double-Balloon Enteroscopy (DBE)
  • - Introduced in 2001, uses high-resolution enteroscope. - Operates with pressure-controlled balloons at 5.6 kPa. - Effective for both diagnostic and therapeutic interventions.

Single-Balloon Enteroscopy (SBE)
  • - Developed as a simpler alternative to DBE. - Lacks a distal balloon, making it user-friendly. - Effective for diagnostic and therapeutic procedures.

Spiral Enteroscopy (SE)
  • - Advances through the bowel using a screw-like mechanism. - Requires two operators for effective maneuvering. - Useful for both antegrade and retrograde approaches.


Bowel Preparation
  • Importance of Preparation - Essential for clear visualization of mucosa. - Fasting from solids (8-12 hours) and liquids (4-6 hours) is recommended. - Anti-foaming agents like simethicone can enhance visibility.

Diagnostic and Therapeutic Applications
  • Gastrointestinal Bleeding - 5-10% of GI bleeding originates from the small bowel. - VCE has a diagnostic yield of 60-80% for identifying bleeding sources. - DAE allows for therapeutic interventions with a success rate of 70-90%.
Small-Bowel Tumors
  • - 3-6% of GI neoplasms arise in the small intestine. - DAE has a pooled sensitivity of 89% for diagnosing small bowel tumors. - Allows for therapeutic interventions like endoscopic resection.

Crohn's Disease
  • - DAE aids in diagnosing and managing Crohn’s disease complications. - Endoscopic Balloon Dilatation (EBD) has success rates >90%. - Repeat dilatation may be needed in 30% of patients within a year.

Celiac Disease
  • - Up to 30% of patients may not respond to standard therapy. - DAE can help exclude conditions mimicking celiac disease. - Sequential VCE-DAE evaluation is recommended for non-responsive patients.

Foreign Body Retrieval
  • - DAE is effective for retrieving ingested foreign bodies. - Allows evaluation of underlying conditions causing retention. - Retention rates of video capsules range from 0.75% to 5%.

Feeding Tube Placement
  • - BAE is effective for direct percutaneous endoscopic jejunostomy (DPEJ) placement. - Technical success rates range from 81-89%. - Lower success in patients with obesity.

Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)
  • - DAE is crucial for ERCP in altered anatomy. - Technical success rates for DAE-ERCP range from 70-90%. - Complications include pancreatitis and bleeding (4-10%).


Procedure Safety
  • Adverse Events - Overall incidence of adverse events ranges from 1.2-1.6%. - Higher rates in patients with altered anatomy. - Serious complications are rare with on-demand enteroscopy.

Training and Skill Development
  • Training Recommendations - Trainees should have experience with EGD and colonoscopy. - Minimum of 10-15 supervised procedures for antegrade DAE recommended. - Specialized training in established centers is essential.

Future Directions and Innovations
  • Technological Advancements - Ongoing improvements in enteroscope design and imaging. - Integration of machine learning may enhance diagnostic yield. - Larger accessory channels will broaden therapeutic options.
