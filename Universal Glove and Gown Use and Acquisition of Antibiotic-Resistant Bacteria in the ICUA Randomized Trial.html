Universal Glove and Gown Use and Acquisition of Antibiotic-Resistant Bacteria in the ICU
A Randomized Trial
  • - Journal: JAMA
  • - Publication Date: October 4, 2013
  • - DOI: 10.1001/jama.2013.277815
Knowledge Test
  • - Did universal glove and gown use significantly reduce the primary composite outcome of MRSA or VRE acquisition?
  • - Which specific antibiotic-resistant bacteria showed a significant reduction in acquisition with the intervention?
  • - What was the compliance rate with wearing gowns in the intervention ICUs?
  • - How did the intervention affect the frequency of healthcare worker room entries?
  • - What was the effect on hand hygiene compliance upon room exit in the intervention group?

Study Objective & Hypothesis
  • - Objective: To assess if wearing gloves and gowns for all patient contact in the ICU decreases acquisition of Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) or Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococcus (VRE) compared to usual care.
  • - Hypothesis: The intervention would decrease MRSA or VRE acquisition.

Study Design
  • - Type: Matched pair cluster-randomized trial.
  • - Unit of Randomization: The ICU.
Setting & Participants
  • - Setting: 20 medical and surgical ICUs in 20 US hospitals.
  • - Participants: 26,180 patient admissions.
  • - Data Collection: 92,241 swabs collected for the primary outcome.

Intervention vs. Control Groups
  • - ICUs were pair-matched based on baseline MRSA/VRE acquisition rates, then randomized within each pair.
Intervention Group (10 ICUs)
  • - Protocol: All healthcare workers (HCWs) were required to wear gloves and gowns for all patient contact and upon entering any patient room.

Control Group (10 ICUs)
  • - Protocol: Followed usual care based on CDC guidelines.
  • - Details: Gloves and gowns were worn only for patients with known infection or colonization with antibiotic-resistant bacteria.



Outcomes Measured
  • - Surveillance cultures (nasal for MRSA, perianal for VRE) were collected on ICU admission and discharge.
Primary Outcome
  • - Acquisition of either MRSA or VRE (composite outcome).
  • - Definition: A negative initial ICU surveillance culture followed by a positive discharge culture for the same pathogen.

Key Secondary Outcomes
  • - MRSA acquisition (separate outcome).
  • - VRE acquisition (separate outcome).
  • - Frequency of HCW room entry.
  • - Hand hygiene compliance.
  • - Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs): CLABSI, CAUTI, VAP.
  • - Adverse events (measured by IHI Global Trigger Tool).


Key Results
  • - The study found no significant difference in the primary outcome but did find significant changes in some secondary outcomes.
Primary Outcome: MRSA or VRE Acquisition
  • - Finding: The intervention did NOT result in a statistically significant difference in the acquisition of MRSA or VRE.
  • - Both groups showed a decrease from baseline, but the difference in the amount of change was not significant (P = .57).
Intervention ICUs (Rate per 1000 patient-days)
  • - Baseline: 21.35
  • - Study Period: 16.91
  • - Change: -4.47

Control ICUs (Rate per 1000 patient-days)
  • - Baseline: 19.02
  • - Study Period: 16.29
  • - Change: -2.74

Statistical Comparison
  • - Difference in changes: -1.71 acquisitions per 1000 person-days.
  • - 95% CI: -6.15 to 2.73
  • - P-value: .57


Secondary Outcomes Analysis
  • - The intervention showed a significant effect on MRSA acquisition and HCW behaviors, but not on VRE, HAIs, or adverse events.
MRSA Acquisition
  • - Finding: A statistically significant reduction in MRSA acquisition was observed in the intervention group compared to the control group (P = .046).
Rate Changes (per 1000 patient-days)
  • - Intervention: Decreased from 10.03 to 6.00 (a 40.2% relative reduction).
  • - Control: Decreased from 6.98 to 5.94 (a 15.0% reduction).
  • - Difference in changes: -2.98 (95% CI, -5.58 to -0.38).


VRE Acquisition
  • - Finding: There was NO statistically significant difference in VRE acquisition between the groups (P = .70).

Healthcare Worker (HCW) Behavior
  • - The intervention significantly altered HCW room entry and exit behaviors.
Room Entry Frequency
  • - Result: HCW room entry was significantly lower in the intervention group.
  • - Intervention: 4.28 entries/hour
  • - Control: 5.24 entries/hour
  • - Difference: -0.96 visits/hour (P = .02).

Hand Hygiene Compliance
  • - Room Entry: No significant difference (P = .42).
  • - Room Exit: Compliance was significantly higher in the intervention group.
  • - Intervention: 78.3%
  • - Control: 62.9%
  • - Difference: +15.4% (P = .02).


Adverse Events & HAIs
  • - Finding: No statistically significant effect on rates of adverse events or HAIs.
Adverse Events
  • - Rates were lower in the intervention group, but this was not statistically significant.
  • - Intervention: 58.7 events per 1000 patient-days.
  • - Control: 74.4 events per 1000 patient-days.
  • - P-value: .24

HAIs (CLABSI, VAP, CAUTI)
  • - Changes in rates did not differ significantly between groups (all P > .10).



Intervention Compliance
  • - Compliance with the universal glove and gown protocol was high.
Intervention ICUs
  • - Glove Compliance: 86.18%
  • - Gown Compliance: 85.14%

Control ICUs (for patients on contact precautions)
  • - % Patients on Precautions: 10.52%
  • - Glove Compliance: 84.11%
  • - Gown Compliance: 81.21%



Conclusions & Discussion
  • - The study highlights the complexity of infection control interventions and their differential effects.
Primary Conclusion
  • - Universal glove and gown use in medical/surgical ICUs did not result in a difference in the primary outcome of acquiring MRSA or VRE.

Implications of Secondary Findings
  • - The lower risk of MRSA acquisition is a key finding but requires replication before definitive conclusions can be drawn.
  • - The lack of an increase in adverse events is reassuring, suggesting that decreased HCW contact from this intervention may not be harmful.
  • - The lack of effect on VRE may be due to other factors like antibiotic selective pressure.

Study Strengths
  • - Design: Cluster randomized design provides stronger evidence.
  • - Outcome: Objective measurement of acquisition via surveillance cultures.
  • - Fidelity: High study completion and intervention compliance.
  • - Generalizability: Conducted in a broad set of diverse US hospitals.

Study Limitations
  • - Blinding: Inability to blind ICUs to the intervention.
  • - Power: Not adequately powered to detect differences in rarer outcomes like specific HAIs or adverse events.
  • - Mechanism: The exact mechanism of the MRSA effect (fewer visits vs. better hand hygiene) is not completely clear.
