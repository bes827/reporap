AGA Clinical Practice Update on Incorporating Functional Lumen Imaging Probe Into Esophageal Clinical Practice: Expert Review
  • Authors: Anh D. Nguyen, Dustin A. Carlson, Amit Patel, and C. Prakash Gyawali
  • Journal: Gastroenterology
  • Publication Date: 2025
  • Purpose: Summarize evidence & offer expert advice on incorporating functional lumen imaging probe (FLIP) into clinical practice.
  • Focus: Utility in evaluating esophageal symptoms, motor dysfunction, gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), & eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE).
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the negative predictive value of normal esophagogastric junction opening (NEO) on FLIP for disorders of esophagogastric junction outflow obstruction (EGJOO) on high-resolution manometry (HRM)?
  • 2. What are the key metrics evaluated during a FLIP panometry study?
  • 3. What endoscopic findings, when combined with reduced EGJ opening (REO) on FLIP, have a high positive predictive value for nonspastic achalasia?
  • 4. What is the CARS score, and what does it quantify?
  • 5. Should FLIP findings be used to diagnose GERD or determine the necessity for antireflux intervention?
Best Practice Advice Statements
  • Statements developed based on literature review & expert opinion.
  • Formal systematic reviews were not performed.
  • Statements lack formal ratings of evidence quality or strength of considerations.
Advice 1: High-Quality Endoscopy First
  • Clinicians should perform a high-quality upper endoscopy evaluating for esophageal pathology immediately prior to considering FLIP.
Advice 2: Consider FLIP for Esophageal Obstruction Symptoms
  • Consider FLIP if alternate investigations are inconclusive in patients w/ symptoms of esophageal obstruction (dysphagia, regurgitation, chest pain).
  • May consider FLIP as part of index endoscopy for symptomatic patients when expertise is readily available.
Advice 3: Sedation Options
  • Common sedation options (propofol, fentanyl, midazolam) can be used.
  • These medications have not been demonstrated to have a clinically significant impact on FLIP findings.
Advice 4: NEO & EGJOO
  • Normal esophagogastric junction opening (NEO) on FLIP has a high negative predictive value for disorders of esophagogastric junction outflow obstruction (EGJOO) on high-resolution manometry (HRM).
Advice 5: Achalasia Diagnosis w/ Endoscopy
  • If upper endoscopy demonstrates findings strongly supportive of achalasia, clinicians may use FLIP findings to diagnose achalasia.
Advice 6: Further Testing for Abnormal EGJ Opening
  • If upper endoscopy does not demonstrate findings supportive of achalasia, abnormal esophagogastric junction opening on FLIP should prompt further diagnostic testing.
Advice 7: FLIP for Dysphagia in Treated EoE
  • Clinicians may consider performing FLIP if the mechanism of persisting dysphagia in treated eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE) is not apparent on high-quality upper endoscopy or histopathology.
Advice 8: Avoid FLIP for GERD Diagnosis
  • Clinicians should not use FLIP findings to diagnose gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) or to determine the necessity for antireflux intervention.
Advice 9: Intraprocedural FLIP During Myotomy
  • When available, clinicians should consider performing FLIP intraprocedurally during myotomy (per-oral endoscopic myotomy (POEM) or laparoscopic Heller myotomy (LHM)) to guide adequacy of LES disruption.
Advice 10: FLIP for Persistent Symptoms Post-Achalasia Treatment
  • Clinicians may consider performing FLIP in the evaluation of persisting symptoms of esophageal obstruction (dysphagia, regurgitation, chest pain) after treatment of achalasia spectrum disorders.
Advice 11: FLIP for Symptoms Post-Foregut Intervention
  • Clinicians may consider performing FLIP in the evaluation of symptoms of esophageal obstruction after invasive foregut intervention (antireflux or bariatric intervention).
FLIP Protocol and Interpretation
  • FLIP uses impedance planimetry to evaluate cross-sectional area (diameter), distensibility of EGJ &/or esophageal body, & esophageal body contractile response to volumetric distention.
  • Equipment: FLIP catheter w/ impedance planimetry electrodes & pressure transducer inside polyurethane bag, & digital data acquisition & display system.
  • Catheter types: 8-cm (EGJ metrics) & 16-cm (EGJ metrics & esophageal body contractile response).
Procedure
  • Performed in sedated patient after high-quality upper endoscopy in left lateral position.
  • Catheter calibrated, referenced to atmospheric pressure, & positioned transorally.
  • Positioning: 1–3 impedance sensor pairs distal to EGJ (16-cm catheter) or EGJ @ balloon center (8-cm catheter). Confirmed by identifying EGJ “waist” on real-time display @ 30–40 mL or 20-mL fill volumes, respectively.
Positioning Considerations
  • If resistance encountered during positioning, catheter withdrawn & repositioned to avoid damaging distal pressure transducer.
  • Endoscopic visualization may be helpful in patients w/ abnormal anatomy (esophageal diverticulum, hiatus hernia) or prior foregut surgery (Roux-en-Y gastric bypass).
FLIP Panometry Study
  • Sequential saline infusion into FLIP bag to 50 mL, 60 mL, & 70 mL.
  • Each fill volume held for ≥30 seconds while maintaining appropriate FLIP catheter position.
  • Key metrics: EGJ distensibility index (DI; ratio of cross-sectional area to bag pressure) & associated pressure measured @ 60-mL fill volume, maximum EGJ diameter @ 70-mL fill volume, & esophageal body contractile pattern based on aggregate of 50-mL, 60-mL, & 70-mL fill volumes.
8-cm Catheter
  • Similar stepwise FLIP filling to 30 mL, 40 mL, & 50 mL.
  • Evaluating EGJ diameter, pressure, & EGJ-DI @ each fill volume.
  • Recording metrics from 40-mL fill volume.
EGJ Opening Description
  • Described using 2 parameters: EGJ-DI & maximum EGJ diameter.
  • Categories: Normal EGJ opening (NEO), reduced EGJ opening (REO), or inconclusive EGJ opening (IEO).
  • Esophageal body contractile response patterns: normal (multiple distinct anterograde contractions &/or repetitive anterograde contractions) or abnormal (absent, diminished, disordered, or spastic contractile response patterns).
Pressure Measurements
  • FLIP pressure measurements discriminate diminished (≤40 mm Hg) from disordered (>40 mm Hg) contractility when distinct antegrade contractions or spastic contractile response are not seen.
FLIP in Evaluation of Esophageal Symptoms
  • FLIP findings should be interpreted as part of the global clinical impression, in the context of presenting symptoms, endoscopy findings, & relevant complementary test results.
High-Quality Upper Endoscopy
  • Carefully evaluates for structural & mucosal abnormalities.
  • Higher yield for erosive reflux changes or EoE if PPIs withheld for 2–4 weeks.
  • FLIP contraindicated in patients w/ actively bleeding varices.
Endoscopic Findings
  • Esophageal strictures, rings, mass lesions, diverticula, mucosal changes of EoE, erosive esophagitis, Barrett’s esophagus, & spastic changes should be noted.
  • Biopsies obtained when appropriate.
  • Careful retroflexion after gastric air insufflation evaluates for hiatus hernia (Hill or American Foregut Society grade), abnormalities in gastric cardia, & postoperative foregut anatomy (integrity & location of ARS).
FLIP for Inconclusive Investigations
  • Neither HRM, TBE, nor FLIP represents a “gold standard” for diagnosis of esophageal motility disorders in isolation.
  • Inconclusive findings of uncertain clinical relevance may be encountered (e.g., EGJOO on HRM is considered inconclusive).
Discordance Among Tests
  • Discordance between esophageal test findings is not unusual.
  • Concordant findings on >1 testing modality increase confidence in diagnosis of actionable obstructive motility disorders prior to pursuing irreversible LES disruption.
  • Conservative treatments should be used if a confident achalasia spectrum diagnosis cannot be made.
Cost-Effectiveness
  • FLIP can be cost-effective during index endoscopy when completely normal or facilitates early achalasia diagnosis.
  • Adds an average of only 5 additional minutes to the procedure time.
Alternative to HRM
  • FLIP remains an option to assess esophageal motility when HRM catheter fails to traverse the LES, discomfort/gagging precludes adequate HRM study, or alternate testing demonstrates dilated/tortuous esophagus.
Sedation Impact on FLIP
  • Chronic opiate use associated w/ opiate-induced esophageal dysmotility on HRM.
  • FLIP & HRM diagnoses not significantly different in study comparing FLIP w/ conscious sedation (midazolam & fentanyl) & monitored anesthesia care (propofol, w/ or w/o fentanyl).
Inhalation Anesthetics
  • Sevoﬂurane can result in higher EGJ diameter & EGJ-DI readings compared w/ propofol, & can impair secondary peristalsis.
  • Clinicians should avoid inhalation anesthetics when assessing esophageal motility w/ FLIP panometry until more data are available.
FLIP in Evaluation of Esophageal Motility Disorders
  • NEO on FLIP has a high negative predictive value for disorders of EGJOO on HRM.
NEO & EGJOO Studies
  • Study of 687 symptomatic patients w/o mechanical obstruction on endoscopy found NEO in <1% of 243 patients w/ HRM diagnoses of achalasia or conclusive EGJOO confirmed w/ TBE.
  • Study of 184 patients showed none of 23 patients w/ HRM-defined achalasia had NEO on FLIP.
Negative Predictive Value
  • NEO on FLIP offers high negative predictive value (NPV) for actionable EGJ obstruction as a mechanism for symptoms.
  • Further tests will have limited value.
EGJ-DI Thresholds
  • FLIP diagnostic performance to rule out achalasia or conclusive EGJOO varied according to the EGJ-DI threshold applied.
  • NPVs: 90% for EGJ-DI >2.0 mm²/mm Hg, 96% for EGJ-DI >2.8 mm²/mm Hg, & 97% for EGJ-DI >3.0 mm²/mm Hg.
NEO & Contractile Response
  • Combining NEO w/ normal esophageal body contractile response pattern provides additional evidence against achalasia.
  • Study of 706 symptomatic patients: none of 108 patients w/ normal contractile response (repetitive antegrade contractions) had achalasia on HRM.
Achalasia Diagnosis w/ Endoscopy & FLIP
  • FLIP findings of REO & abnormal contractile response patterns are strongly associated w/ achalasia.
  • Prior work: 92% of patients (n = 185 of 202) w/ REO & absent or impaired/disordered esophageal body contractile response had achalasia or conclusive (TBE-confirmed) EGJOO on HRM.
Advantage of FLIP
  • Key advantage of FLIP over HRM is accompanying high-quality endoscopy, which provides valuable pretest probability to complement FLIP findings, including distinction of idiopathic achalasia from pseudoachalasia.
Endoscopic Features of Achalasia
  • Endoscopic features of achalasia: retained food/fluid (esophageal contents), dilated lumen (anatomy), resistance traversing the EGJ, & stasis changes of esophageal mucosa, quantified as CARS score.
CARS Score
  • CARS score ≥4 had specificity of 99% & sensitivity of 68%–72% to identify achalasia.
  • CARS score of 0–1 combined w/ NEO on FLIP had NPV of 100% for achalasia spectrum disorders.
  • CARS score ≥4 w/ REO on FLIP had positive predictive value of 97% for nonspastic achalasia.
Further Testing for Abnormal EGJ Opening
  • REO can be seen in 5%–11% of symptomatic patients w/o HRM disorders of EGJOO.
  • Clinical significance of REO on FLIP when HRM EGJ outflow pressures are normal is incompletely understood.
  • Borderline/inconclusive EGJ opening reported in 25%–28% of patients w/ normal EGJ outflow pressure on HRM & could indicate obstruction.
FLIP-HRM Discordance
  • Given potential for FLIP-HRM discordance, if abnormal EGJ opening on FLIP is not supported by endoscopic evidence of achalasia, additional testing should be pursued before achalasia is diagnosed.
  • Careful re-inspection during the same endoscopic encounter can identify strictures or rings contributing to FLIP findings.
FLIP in Evaluation of Eosinophilic Esophagitis
  • FLIP can assess biomechanical properties of the esophageal wall in EoE.
Distensibility Plateau
  • Static esophageal diameter despite increasing distention volume & pressure (distensibility plateau) was lower in patients w/ prior food impaction compared w/ those w/ dysphagia alone.
  • Low DI, particularly low distensibility plateau, correlates w/ greater symptom duration & diagnostic delay in EoE.
Esophageal Body Contractile Response
  • Abnormal esophageal body contractile response is associated w/ more severe fibrostenosis, indicating that EoE disease severity can impact esophageal motor function.
Composite Assessment
  • Composite assessment of esophageal body compliance, contractility, EGJ-DI, & maximal diameter correlates w/ higher esophageal eosinophil counts & endoscopic reference scores when abnormal in a cohort of 215 patients w/ EoE.
Achalasia-like Pattern
  • Rarely, an achalasia-like pattern can be seen in EoE that may be related to fibrostenotic change manifesting as pseudoachalasia.
  • Irreversible LES-disruptive treatments should be applied cautiously & only if supported by additional complementary tests.
PPI Nonresponse
  • Abnormal composite FLIP assessment scores were associated w/ histologic PPI nonresponse in a cohort of 46 patients studied before treatment.
FLIP in Evaluation of Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease
  • FLIP studies evaluating EGJ barrier function in GERD have not demonstrated a discriminative threshold for EGJ-DI in segregating symptomatic GERD from controls.
EGJ-DI & AET
  • No correlation between EGJ-DI values & acid exposure times (AETs) in 2 retrospective evaluations of 164 patients & 69 patients who underwent both FLIP & ambulatory reflux monitoring.
PPI Nonresponders
  • Among 18 PPI nonresponders w/ normal AET on all 4 days of a 96-hour wireless pH monitoring study, the likelihood of DI <2.8 mm²/mm Hg was higher (83.3%) compared w/ patients w/ abnormal reflux burden (23.5%).
Esophageal Body Contractile Response
  • Impaired esophageal body contractile response has been associated w/ abnormal AET in a study w/ 25 patients, supporting abnormal esophageal clearance contributing to abnormal AET.
Hiatus Hernia
  • FLIP can identify a hiatus hernia, w/ 84% correlation w/ HRM-identified hiatus hernia in a study of 100 consecutive patients.
  • FLIP can elicit or enlarge a hiatus hernia in up to 25%; patients w/ these features trended toward having barium retention on TBE.
Utility of FLIP in Foregut Interventions
  • FLIP has demonstrable value when performed during intervention for achalasia spectrum disorders.
Intraprocedural FLIP
  • By evaluating the LES while POEM or LHM are in progress, intraprocedural FLIP can direct myotomy length & confirm the adequacy of LES disruption in real time.
  • EGJ-DI reliably improves after myotomy.
Intraoperative EGJ-DI
  • Intraoperative EGJ-DI <2–3 mm²/mm Hg may predict persisting symptoms, & may prompt additional myotomy.
  • High intraoperative EGJ-DI may associate w/ postoperative reflux &/or erosive esophagitis after LHM or POEM.
FLIP in Symptom Evaluation After Foregut Interventions
  • FLIP can be used to evaluate patients w/ persisting esophageal obstructive symptoms after achalasia management.
Post-LES Disruption
  • After LES disruption, an EGJ-DI <2–3 mm²/mm Hg in symptomatic patients is associated w/ higher Eckardt scores & suboptimal esophageal emptying, suggesting ineffective or incomplete LES disruption.
  • NEO on FLIP should prompt consideration of alternate explanations for symptoms, including abnormal esophageal body anatomy & GERD.
Post-ARS
  • Intraoperative FLIP during ARS & endoscopic reflux procedures is feasible, & EGJ-DI has been used to tailor fundoplication in attempts to prevent postoperative dysphagia.
EGJ-DI After ARS
  • EGJ-DI <2 mm²/mm Hg after ARS was associated w/ dysphagia symptoms.
  • Tightness of hiatal closure appears to impact DI more than the actual fundoplication.
Magnetic Sphincter Augmentation
  • Postoperative diameter & EGJ-DI were lower after magnetic sphincter augmentation compared w/ ARS, but symptomatic outcomes were similar.
Post-Bariatric Surgery
  • FLIP performed after Roux-en-Y gastric bypass or sleeve gastrectomy has demonstrated higher secondary dysmotility (most commonly absent contractility) compared w/ obese patients w/ native anatomy.
Abnormal Secondary Peristalsis
  • Among patients undergoing FLIP for symptomatic esophageal evaluation after ARS, abnormal secondary peristalsis was encountered in 84%, potentially worsening w/ longer duration post-ARS &/or EGJ outflow resistance.
Conclusions
  • FLIP is safe, adds minimal time to the standard endoscopic procedure, does not appear to be impacted by standard sedation used during endoscopy, & may provide immediate management options after real-time interpretation.
  • Operator education is paramount.
  • The recently published FLIP Panometry classification scheme standardizes protocols, terminology, & utilization of metrics in the diagnosis of motor & structural esophageal disorders.
Future Directions
  • Inconclusive & discordant findings are also encountered w/ FLIP, & future research is anticipated to further refine next steps in such scenarios.
  • The esophageal smooth muscle response to distention warrants further clarification.
  • Artificial intelligence is expected to further enhance FLIP interpretation.
  • The role of FLIP in adjudicating the diagnosis of functional dysphagia warrants additional research.
