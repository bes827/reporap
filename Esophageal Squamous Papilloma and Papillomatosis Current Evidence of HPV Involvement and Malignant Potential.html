Esophageal Squamous Papilloma and Papillomatosis: Current Evidence of HPV Involvement and Malignant Potential
  • Journal: Cancers (Basel). 2025 Jul 20;17(14):2404. doi: 10.3390/cancers17142404
  • PMCID: PMC12293905; PMID: 40723286
  • Keywords: Esophageal squamous papilloma, esophageal papillomatosis, human papillomavirus (HPV), endoscopy, malignant potential, surveillance
  • Summary: Rare esophageal lesions (ESP/papillomatosis) traditionally benign, but emerging evidence suggests possible association w/ high-risk HPV (hr-HPV) infection & potential malignant progression risk.
  • Review covers pathogenesis, clinical features, diagnostic approaches, management strategies, focusing on HPV role & advanced endoscopic imaging.
  • Further research urgently needed to clarify oncogenic potential & optimize surveillance/treatment protocols.

Introduction to HPV-Related Esophageal Lesions
  • HPV: established oncogenic agent in cervical, anogenital, oropharyngeal cancers.
  • Increasing attention on HPV's role in esophageal squamous pathology.
  • Esophageal squamous papilloma (ESP) & papillomatosis: rare, but growing evidence links them to hr-HPV & risk of dysplasia/malignant transformation.
Human Papillomavirus (HPV) Infection
  • HPV: small, non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA virus; infects basal keratinocytes.
  • Most prevalent sexually transmitted infection: lifetime risk ≈ 50% for men & women.
Biology & Oncogenesis
  • Risk factors: high # lifetime sexual partners, early sexual activity, co-infections (e.g., HIV).
  • Children transmission: hand-to-mouth contact, perinatal (vaginal delivery), less commonly in utero.
  • Esophageal transmission mechanisms: unclear; hypothesized neonatal contamination, sexual contact, direct mucosal exposure.
Sexual Behavior & Oral HPV Risk
  • Certain sexual behaviors → ↑ risk of HPV transmission to upper aerodigestive tract.
  • Early sexual activity, multiple partners, unprotected oro-genital contact, male-to-male intercourse.
  • Mistry et al. [16]: oral sex → significant association w/ oral/oropharyngeal HPV (p = 0.0038) in men who have sex with men.
  • Dalla Torre et al. [17]: high # vaginal (p = 0.0001) & oral (p < 0.0001) sexual partners → significant association w/ oral HPV positivity in young adults (18-30 yrs).
HPV Infection Outcomes
  • Most HPV infections: asymptomatic; ≈90% clear spontaneously.
  • Persistent infection (≈10% cases) → significantly ↑ risk malignant transformation.
  • Immunocompetent adults: transient mucosal infections resolve within 8–14 months; persistence > 24 months rare (except immunosuppressed/HIV+).
HPV Genotypes & Carcinogenesis
  • >100 HPV genotypes identified: classified as high-risk (hr-HPV) or low-risk.
  • IARC carcinogenic: 12 hr-HPV types (16, 18, 31, 33, 35, 39, 45, 51, 52, 56, 58, 59).
  • 'Probably'/'possibly' carcinogenic: 8 additional types (26, 53, 66, 67, 67, 70, 73, 82).
  • Most frequent w/ high-grade dysplasia/cancer: HPV types 16 & 18.
Malignant Progression Mechanism
  • Persistent hr-HPV infection → viral entry via microabrasions → basal layer epithelium.
  • Key event: viral DNA integration into host genome → overexpression of viral oncoproteins E6 & E7.
  • E6: promotes p53 degradation.
  • E7: inactivates retinoblastoma protein (pRb) → ↑ p16INK4a (p16).
  • p16: surrogate marker for oncogenic HPV activity (in cervical/oropharyngeal cancer).
HPV Detection Methods
  • Common methods: Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR), In Situ Hybridization (ISH), Immunohistochemistry (IHC) for p16.
  • PCR: high sensitivity & specificity for HPV DNA.
  • ISH: less sensitive, but provides spatial context by localizing viral DNA within tissue.
  • IHC for p16: widely accepted surrogate marker for transcriptionally active HPV in cervical/oropharyngeal cancer.
Limitations in Esophageal Pathology
  • In Esophageal Squamous Cell Carcinoma (ESCC): p16 expression not consistently correlated w/ HPV DNA.
  • ∴ p16 not reliable surrogate marker for HPV-driven oncogenesis in ESCC.
  • Most frequent detection methods for HPV in esophageal tissue: PCR & ISH.
  • Reported HPV detection rates: 10% to 80% (varies by technique, geography, patient selection); PCR generally higher sensitivity than ISH.
Histopathological Features
  • Koilocytic changes (nuclear enlargement, irregular membranes, perinuclear clearing) occasionally observed in HPV-related esophageal papillomas.
  • Not pathognomonic nor consistently present; diagnostic value ↑ w/ molecular confirmation (PCR/ISH).
  • Need for standardized diagnostic criteria & detection protocols in future studies.
HPV-Related Esophageal Lesions
  • Esophageal squamous papilloma (ESP) & papillomatosis are primary lesions potentially associated w/ HPV infection.
  • Relatively rare, but increasing attention due to suspected viral etiology & potential for malignant transformation.
Esophageal Squamous Papilloma (ESP)
  • Rare, benign epithelial tumor; first described 1959.
  • Prevalence: 0.01% to 0.45% during EGD; pediatric prevalence ≈ 0.08%.
  • Prevalence appears ↑: Pantham et al. reported rise from 0.13% (2000) to 0.57% (2013).
Demographics & Symptoms
  • Median age: 49-52 years; some reports indicate slight female predominance.
  • More common among White patients [41]. Recent study: younger (median 52 yrs, p=0.021), more African American (p<0.001), balanced M:F ratio [3].
  • Most ESPs: asymptomatic, incidentally discovered during EGD.
  • Symptoms (when present): non-specific (dyspepsia, heartburn, epigastric discomfort, dysphagia); often related to underlying esophageal conditions (reflux, inflammation).
Location & Endoscopic Appearance
  • Most common location: middle & distal thirds of esophagus; can occur throughout tract.
  • Endoscopic appearance: small (2–6 mm), solitary, whitish-pink, wart-like exophytic lesion; mostly pedunculated/semi-pedunculated; well-demarcated margins.
  • Virtual chromoendoscopy (NBI, BLI) improve diagnostic accuracy.
  • Triad: exophytic growth, wart-like projections, crossing surface vessels → PPV 88% [37].
  • NBI often reveals brownish vascular lines (fibrovascular core).
Histology & Classification
  • Histologically: finger-like projections of squamous epithelium overlying a fibrovascular core.
  • Larger lesions (>10 mm), clustered lesions, or diffuse involvement: classified as esophageal squamous papillomatosis.
Pathogenesis
  • Exact pathogenesis uncertain; two major hypotheses: chronic mucosal irritation & HPV infection.
  • Irritation-related factors: GERD, mechanical trauma (sclerotherapy, metal stent), alcohol & tobacco use.
  • GERD supported by frequent distal esophageal localization; however, no studies report reflux-associated histologic changes in non-papillomatous mucosa.
HPV Association in ESP
  • First suggested by Syrjanen et al. in 1982.
  • HPV detection rates in ESPs: 10% to 80% [32,35,39,47,51,52]; some studies failed to detect HPV [3,36,53,54,55].
  • Low-risk HPV types 6 & 11 most commonly detected.
  • Takeshita et al.: HPV DNA (type 6) in 10.5% of samples [35].
  • High-risk HPV also identified: Odze et al.: HPV in 50% of ESPs, type 16 most frequent [47].
  • Tiftikçi et al.: 19% of ESPs HPV-positive; 3 cases type 6, 4 cases types 16, 18, 31, 81 (oncogenic) [52].
  • Bohn et al.: ≈80% of ESPs HPV-positive, mainly low-risk types; ACISH sensitivity comparable to PCR [32].
  • Conclusion: Evidence increasingly supports HPV-ESP association; true prevalence may be underestimated due to limited genotype screening.
Esophageal Squamous Papillomatosis
  • Even more exceptional condition than solitary ESPs; literature scarce, primarily case reports.
  • First described case: 1977, 3.5-year-old boy w/ pyriform sinus papilloma.
  • Exhibits distinct clinical & pathological features from solitary ESPs.
Epidemiology & Symptoms
  • Recent review (Li et al. [2], 53 cases): mean age ≈ 46.8 years; no significant sex-based or geographic differences.
  • Frequently presents with symptoms (unlike solitary ESPs).
  • Dysphagia: most common complaint (32/53 cases), followed by heartburn, weight loss, epigastric discomfort, dyspepsia.
  • Symptoms due to extent & confluence of lesions, reflecting greater mucosal & inflammatory burden.
Diagnosis & Endoscopic Appearance
  • Diagnosis via EGD: multiple small, whitish or pinkish projections; smooth or slightly rough surface; sessile or pedunculated; tightly clustered pattern [57].
  • Lesion size: 1 to 15 mm [58]; number may be too high to count.
  • Involvement: limited to part of esophagus (usually middle/distal thirds) or entire tract, sometimes causing strictures [2].
  • Advanced imaging techniques: NBI w/ magnification [57,59,60], Lugol’s iodine staining [61,62], endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) [63,64] are valuable tools.
  • Histologic confirmation essential: multiple finger-like projections of hyperplastic squamous epithelium over fibrovascular cores [57].
Pathogenesis & HPV Involvement
  • Pathogenesis unclear, likely mirrors ESPs.
  • Chronic mucosal irritation: major factor (especially GERD, chemical irritants like tobacco/alcohol, mechanical injuries).
  • Animal studies: gastroesophageal & duodenal reflux can induce papillomatosis [65,66,67].
  • Evidence for HPV involvement: limited. Li et al. review [2]: 29/53 cases tested, 11 (37.9%) positive.
  • 12 HPV genotypes identified; low-risk types 6 & 11 in 6 cases.
  • Notably, 4 patients positive for HPV 16 (high-risk), half of whom developed esophageal cancer [68,69].
  • True prevalence of HPV may be underestimated due to limited genotype screening & lack of testing in many reports.
Advanced Endoscopic Techniques for Lesion Characterization
  • Endoscopy: central role in detection, characterization, surveillance of HPV-related esophageal squamous lesions.
  • Critical to distinguish benign from dysplastic or superficially invasive ESCC for appropriate management.
  • Standard high-definition white light endoscopy (WLE): first-line tool for location, morphology, size, extent.
Advanced Imaging Modalities
  • Virtual chromoendoscopy (NBI, BLI) & dye-based chromoendoscopy (Lugol’s iodine) → superior diagnostic performance for early neoplastic changes, especially superficial ESCC.
  • Enhance ability to differentiate benign HPV-related lesions from those w/ dysplastic or malignant features [70].
Lugol’s Chromoendoscopy
  • Well-established technique for early detection of squamous neoplasia.
  • Iodine solution selectively stains glycogen-rich normal squamousepithelium dark brown.
  • Dysplastic or neoplastic tissue: glycogen depletion → unstained or hypocolored ('Lugol-voiding lesions').
  • Useful in ESP/papillomatosis: hypopigmented/unstained regions may indicate dysplasia/carcinoma.
  • Case reports: successful application in delineating complex lesions & guiding targeted biopsies [57,61,62,70,72].
  • Caution: glycogen-depleted metaplastic epithelium (e.g., Barrett’s) may also appear Lugol-negative, mimicking dysplasia.
  • Correlation w/ virtual chromoendoscopy & targeted biopsy essential to avoid misinterpretation.
Virtual Chromoendoscopy (NBI & BLI)
  • ESGE guidelines: validated alternative to Lugol’s for superficial ESCC detection; outperform WLE alone [73,74].
  • Enables detailed visualization of mucosal microvascular patterns, especially intrapapillary capillary loops (IPCLs).
  • IPCLs: appear as brownish loops on magnified NBI; show characteristic morphological alterations in dysplastic/malignant tissue.
  • Changes in IPCL shape, caliber, distribution → correlate w/ degree of epithelial irregularity & neoplastic invasion.
IPCL Classification System
  • Morphological grading of vascular changes.
  • Types III to V: progressive dilation, tortuosity, irregularity → associated w/ high-grade dysplasia or carcinoma [75].
JES (Japan Esophageal Society) Classification
  • Stratifies lesions based on IPCL features to estimate invasion depth.
  • Type A microvessels (w/o severe irregularity): non-neoplastic or low-grade dysplastic lesions.
  • Type B microvessels (w/ severe irregularity): cancerous changes.
  • B1: High-grade intraepithelial neoplasia or carcinoma limited to mucosa (m1/m2).
  • B2: Invasion into muscularis mucosae (m3) or superficial submucosa (sm1).
  • B3: Invasion at least into deeper submucosa (≥sm2) [76,77,78].
  • Overall accuracy of type B vessels for predicting invasion depth: >90% [79].
Utility in Papillomas/Papillomatosis
  • No specific studies on virtual chromoendoscopy differentiating papillomas/papillomatosis from dysplasia/ESCC.
  • Case reports suggest utility: benign papillomas → typically regular vascular patterns or subtle brownish vascular lines [37].
  • Dysplastic/malignant transformation → irregular, dilated IPCLs.
  • Supports NBI value in improving diagnostic precision [57,59,60,72].
Comprehensive Endoscopic Strategy
  • Integration of high-definition WLE, virtual chromoendoscopy (NBI/BLI), & Lugol’s staining → comprehensive approach.
  • Improves lesion characterization, enhances risk stratification, guides management decisions.
  • Especially valuable in HPV-related esophageal lesions w/ potential dysplastic/early neoplastic foci, particularly extensive papillomatosis.
Histologic Examination: Gold Standard
  • Despite enhanced sensitivity of imaging techniques, histologic examination remains diagnostic gold standard.
  • Biopsy essential, especially for extensive or suspicious lesions.
  • Esophageal papillomatosis: multiple biopsies (typically 2-15) recommended due to potential hidden focal dysplasia.
  • Targeted sampling crucial for suspicious features (irregular vascular patterns on NBI/BLI, hypopigmented areas on Lugol’s) → more likely to harbor neoplasia [2].
Recurrence and Malignant Potential
  • ESP & papillomatosis generally benign, but concerns about recurrence & malignant transformation, especially w/ hr-HPV.
  • Conclusive evidence lacking due to rarity & limited long-term data.
  • Oncogenic HPV genotypes (16, 18) identified in benign lesions & ESCC specimens.
  • Meta-analyses: statistically significant association between HPV infection & ESCC (OR 2.7-3.8) [30,80,81,82].
  • Suggests potential HPV-related continuum in esophageal pathology.
  • Clearer understanding of recurrence/progression risk essential for surveillance/therapy.
Esophageal Squamous Papilloma (ESP)
  • Generally benign, managed w/ complete endoscopic resection.
  • Evidence on recurrence & malignant progression scarce due to rarity & limited follow-up [43].
Recurrence After Resection
  • Most cases: no recurrence after removal of benign, non-dysplastic lesions [3,34,36,83].
  • French study: 3.4% recurrence rate after 1st ESP resection, 0% after 2nd [34].
  • Possible residual disease vs. true recurrence: some lesions not fully excised initially.
  • Ahmad et al. [3]: 17/18 patients w/ follow-up EGD showed no recurrence after ESP resection.
  • Follow-up adherence suboptimal (<50% in all studies) → hinders definitive conclusions.
  • Current evidence: recurrence rare or possibly absent in benign, non-dysplastic ESPs.
Malignant Potential
  • Controversial; most lesions non-neoplastic, but isolated reports of progression to ESCC.
  • D’Huart et al. [34]: 1 case (1.3%) of malignant transformation among 78 patients; SCC developed at site of resected low-grade dysplastic ESP 2 yrs prior (75-year-old male, smoking/alcoholic liver disease).
  • Another case: 1.5 cm ESP at EGJ progressed to in situ carcinoma over 2 yrs (44-year-old female, no risk factors) [84].
  • Ahmad et al. [3]: 1/66 patients developed ESCC during follow-up (no known risk factors).
  • Cases support potential for malignant transformation; lack of prospective studies & limited follow-up preclude defining specific timeframe.
  • HPV implicated as potential contributor; however, HPV not detected in reported ESP-associated ESCC cases (likely due to testing limitations/restricted genotypes) [3,34,84].
  • Detection of high-risk genotypes in ESPs supports hypothesis of potential progression pathway [39,52].
Esophageal Squamous Papillomatosis
  • Higher risk of malignant transformation than solitary ESPs.
  • Macroscopic features (large size, multiplicity, confluence, circumferential distribution) → proposed indicators of malignancy risk [34].
  • Extensive disease: poses oncologic & quality of life concerns.
Malignant Transformation Risk
  • Li et al. review [2] (53 cases): 12 patients (22.6%) developed ESCC [61,63,64,68,69,85,86,87,88,89,90,91].
  • 3 cases: varying degrees of dysplasia within lesions [62,92,93].
  • 2 additional cases: low-grade dysplasia in patients w/ dysphagia [70,72].
  • Findings support papillomatosis as premalignant condition.
  • Malignant transformation: seems to occur within months to few years after diagnosis, especially w/ extensive mucosal involvement & hr-HPV infection.
  • Precise estimates limited by scarcity of long-term follow-up & reliance on case reports.
HPV Involvement in Malignant Transformation
  • HPV infection implicated; among 12 cases progressing to ESCC, only 2 confirmed HPV infection [68,69].
  • Among 5 dysplastic cases, only 1 tested positive for HPV [93].
  • All 3 HPV-positive cases: genotype HPV-16.
  • Data suggest possible oncogenic role, but association not consistent; additional cofactors (e.g., chronic GERD) may be involved.
  • Inconsistent HPV testing & limited genotype panels hinder interpretation.
  • Detection of high-risk HPV in cases progressing to dysplasia/carcinoma suggests possible contributory role.
Recurrence
  • Limited data. Li et al. review [2]: only 9/30 patients (30%) treated w/ medical, endoscopic, or surgical approaches remained recurrence-free (follow-up: 3 months to 2 years).
  • Illustrative case: diffuse HPV 16-positive papillomatosis w/ HCV co-infection; underwent Ivor Lewis esophagectomy, but recurrence @ gastroesophageal anastomosis 1 yr later [93].
  • Underscores importance of long-term surveillance & individualized therapeutic planning.
Conclusion on Papillomatosis Risk
  • Rare, but carries measurable risk of malignant transformation & recurrence.
  • Involvement of high-risk HPV genotypes suggests possible progression pathway (benign epithelial proliferation → malignancy), similar to other HPV-associated conditions.
  • Close clinical follow-up, personalized management, & regular endoscopic monitoring essential for early detection of progression.
Treatment and Surveillance
  • Potential for dysplasia & malignant progression → complete removal of ESPs & papillomatosis when feasible [2,3].
  • Specific clinical guidelines lacking due to rarity.
  • Available data support proactive approach to treatment & surveillance.
Esophageal Squamous Papilloma (ESP)
  • Low risk of malignant transformation; complete endoscopic removal recommended.
  • Management tailored to lesion size, morphology, histology, patient risk factors.
Treatment Modalities
  • Small (≤5 mm), pedunculated/sessile lesions w/o dysplasia: typically managed w/ biopsy forceps [94,95,96,97].
  • Larger lesions (>5 mm): may require snare polypectomy or endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR) [40,84].
  • Endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD): reserved for lesions suspicious for superficial ESCC (JES types A, B1, B2), especially for en bloc resection & accurate histologic staging [78,84].
  • Radiofrequency ablation (RFA): reported as potential treatment in selected cases [98]; minimally invasive alternative for small lesions, but lacks histologic samples.
Post-Treatment Evaluation
  • Histopathological evaluation: essential for diagnosis confirmation & exclusion of dysplasia/carcinoma.
  • Molecular testing for HPV: consider in lesions w/ atypical features or dysplasia.
Surveillance
  • Individualized.
  • Close endoscopic monitoring: for larger lesions, dysplasia, superficial carcinoma, or high-risk HPV positivity.
  • Routine surveillance not required: for small, non-dysplastic, HPV-negative lesions completely removed.
Esophageal Squamous Papillomatosis
  • Higher malignant potential than solitary papillomas, especially in extensive/multifocal disease.
  • Treatment aims: eradicate lesions, relieve symptoms (e.g., dysphagia), prevent malignant transformation.
Treatment Modalities
  • Endoscopic modalities: argon plasma coagulation (APC), radiofrequency ablation (RFA) [99,100], cryotherapy [101,102], photodynamic therapy (PDT) [86].
  • All 4 techniques: demonstrated efficacy in lesion treatment & potential prevention of malignant transformation.
  • EMR [45] or ESD [70]: for localized or suspicious lesions; ESD offers en bloc resection & improved histologic assessment (dysplasia/high-risk features) [78].
  • Esophagectomy: indicated in select cases w/ malignant transformation or failure of endoscopic therapy [63,68,69,85,87,88].
Post-Treatment Evaluation
  • Histologic & virologic assessment post-resection: crucial.
Surveillance
  • Risk-adapted: considers lesion extent, HPV status, histological findings, patient factors.
  • Chromoendoscopy during follow-up: aids detection of recurrent or progressing lesions.
  • Conservative management w/ periodic monitoring: reasonable in absence of dysplasia or high-risk HPV; no standardized protocols exist.
  • Multidisciplinary evaluation & individualized follow-up: key to optimal long-term outcomes.
  • Otolaryngologic assessment: consider to rule out synchronous laryngeal/tracheal involvement (especially pediatric patients, extensive/recurrent disease).
Conclusions
  • ESP & esophageal squamous papillomatosis: rare epithelial lesions; etiology, natural history, optimal management poorly defined.
  • Possible etiological role of HPV infection: low-risk genotypes w/ benign lesions, high-risk genotypes w/ dysplastic/malignant transformation.
  • Heterogeneity in study design, limited HPV genotyping, non-standardized detection methods → prevent definitive conclusions on HPV's true oncogenic role.
  • Malignant transformation infrequent, but multifocality, lesion size, dysplasia, high-risk HPV positivity → may warrant closer surveillance.
  • Complete endoscopic resection: treatment of choice; histopathological & virological assessment guide further management.
  • Prospective, standardized studies needed to clarify natural history & optimize diagnostic/therapeutic algorithms.
