Designing Case-Control Studies in Epidemiology
  • This chapter provides an in-depth guide to designing case-control studies in epidemiology.
  • It contrasts them with cohort studies, emphasizing their retrospective nature: investigators start with an outcome (cases) and look backward for prior exposures.
  • A key strength is efficiency for studying rare diseases and outbreaks, requiring smaller sample sizes and generally lower costs than cohort studies.
  • The text addresses major weaknesses: selection bias (how cases/controls are chosen) and measurement bias (recall biasdue to retrospective data collection), offering mitigation strategies like matching, multiple control groups, and blinding.
  • It also details specialized variations: nested case-control, incidence-density nested case-control, and case-crossover studies, which leverage existing cohorts or use cases as their own controls to increase validity and efficiency.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the primary advantage of a case-control study over a cohort study when investigating a rare disease like ocular melanoma?
  • 2. In the 2018 Australian Hepatitis A outbreak study, what was the odds ratio (OR) for consumption of frozen pomegranate arils and Hepatitis A infection?
  • 3. What is 'recall bias' in the context of case-control studies, and how might it affect study results?
  • 4. What is the key difference between a nested case-control study and a nested case-cohort study in terms of control selection?
  • 5. How does a case-crossover study address confounding by stable individual characteristics?
Case-Control Study Basics
  • Case-control studies are retrospective: investigators identify cases (with outcome) and controls (w/o outcome), then look backward to measure past exposures.
  • This design is relatively inexpensive and well-suited for rare diseases and disease outbreaks.
  • Cannot directly estimate disease incidence/prevalence; proportion of cases/controls is investigator-determined.
Core Methodology
  • Define selection criteria; recruit cases from population w/ outcome, controls from population w/o outcome.
  • Measure current values of relevant variables, often supplemented by historical information.
  • Compare previous exposures between cases and controls to find differences.
Example: Hepatitis A Outbreak (2018 Australia)
  • Cases: Hepatitis A genotype IB (April 13 - June 8, 2018).
  • Controls: Other reportable infectious diseases (salmonellosis, campylobacteriosis, cryptosporidiosis) from same/neighboring local government area.
  • Matching: Frequency matched by age group (0-14, 15-39, ≥40 years) in a 2:1 ratio to cases.
Key Finding
  • Exposure most strongly associated w/ case status: consumption of frozen pomegranate arils.
  • 9 of 13 cases vs. 1 of 21 controls → Odds Ratio (OR) = 45.0 (95% CI: 3.8 to 2065).
  • Subsequent investigation: Hepatitis A virus found in frozen pomegranate arils from Egyptian manufacturer.
Key Outputs
  • Provide descriptive info on case characteristics.
  • Estimate strength of association between predictor variable and outcome via odds ratios (ORs).
  • ORs approximate risk ratios (RRs) if outcome risk is low (≤10%) in both exposed/unexposed groups.
Application Beyond Risk Factors
  • Can study other uncommon outcomes (e.g., death in non-fatal disease).
  • Exposure of interest may be protective (e.g., vaccine/treatment effectiveness) → OR <1.
Strengths of Case-Control Studies
  • Efficiency for rare outcomes: rapid info yield w/ relatively few participants.
  • Useful for generating hypotheses: retrospective approach + ability to examine many predictor variables.
Efficiency for Rare Outcomes: Details
  • Example: Penile carcinoma (lifetime incidence ≈ 0.16% in uncircumcised men).
  • Cohort study to detect 50-fold ↑ risk w/ 80% power: >6000 men followed for decades.
  • Case-control study for same question/power: only 16 cases + 16 controls needed.
  • Often the only feasible option for rare diseases or those w/ long latent periods.
Hypothesis Generation: Details
  • Useful for new disease outbreaks.
  • Example: Acute renal failure deaths in Haitian children → OR = 53 for acetaminophen syrup ingestion.
  • Further investigation revealed diethylene glycol contamination.
Weaknesses of Case-Control Studies
  • Limited to one outcome variable (the disease defining cases/controls).
  • Cannot directly estimate disease incidence/prevalence w/o additional population data.
  • High susceptibility to bias, primarily from selection and retrospective measurement.
Selection Bias: How to Avoid It
  • Occurs when case sample is unrepresentative w/ respect to risk factor.
  • Goal: sample controls from population who would have become a case if they developed the disease.
Finding & Sampling Cases
  • Ideally, all cases in a defined population or a random selection.
  • Problem: cases sampled from already diagnosed/available patients → may not be representative.
  • Undiagnosed, misdiagnosed, unavailable, or dead patients unlikely to be included.
Impact of Unrepresentative Cases
  • Selection bias matters if sample is unrepresentative for the risk factor.
  • Diseases requiring hospitalization + straightforward diagnosis (e.g., hip fracture) can be safely sampled.
  • Conditions not always coming to medical attention (e.g., prostate cancer) are harder to study.
  • Example: Vasectomy and prostate cancer risk → diagnosis may depend on screening, over-representing men w/ medical care (e.g., vasectomy) → screening more common among vasectomized men.
Sampling Controls: Strategies
  • 1. Clinic-, hospital-, or registry-based controls.
  • 2. Population-based samples of cases and controls.
  • 3. Using two or more control groups.
Strategy 1: Clinic/Hospital/Registry Controls
  • Convenient: same facility/registry as cases.
  • May partially compensate for case selection bias if controls would also seek care there.
  • Problem: if risk factor causes medical problem for which controls seek care, prevalence of risk factor in controls will be falsely high.
  • Example: Hepatitis A study controls (salmonellosis, campylobacteriosis, cryptosporidiosis) would be problematic if studying general food/water safety exposures → poor food hygiene overrepresented.
Strategy 2: Population-Based Samples
  • Feasible w/ disease registries, digital clinical/administrative data.
  • Cases from registries are generally representative of patient population.
  • Control group: representative sample of 'non-cases' from same population/database.
  • Most desirable design when registries/databases available.
  • Bias can still occur if healthcare access/consent varies across population segments (e.g., language barriers, trust issues).
Strategy 3: Two or More Control Groups
  • Advisable when case selection is tricky.
  • Example: Oral cholera vaccine effectiveness study in Haiti used 'test-negative' controls (w/ watery diarrhea but cholera-negative) and 'community' controls (no diarrhea, matched by age, time, neighborhood).
  • Effectiveness estimates for 2 doses were similar (73% vs. 74%).
  • Caution: conflicting results from different control groups reveal inherent fragility → seek additional info on potential biases.
Matching
  • Simple method to ensure cases/controls are comparable on major factors (e.g., age, sex, location).
  • Makes study results more convincing.
  • Disadvantages: can introduce bias if modifiable predictors (e.g., income, cholesterol) are matched (discussed in Chapter 10).
Differential Measurement Bias (Recall Bias): How to Avoid It
  • Increased risk due to retrospective exposure measurement.
  • Imperfect memory for past exposures.
  • Nondifferential misclassification: imperfect memory in cases/controls → bias toward 1 (harder to find associations).
  • Differential misclassification (recall bias): disease diagnosis leads cases to remember/report exposures differently from controls → unpredictable effects on associations.
Example: Melanoma & Sun Exposure
  • Widespread publicity about sun exposure/melanoma link.
  • Twin study discordant for melanoma: matched OR for childhood sunbathing = 2.2 (95% CI: 1.0-4.7) when melanoma twin asked.
  • OR = 0.8 (95% CI: 0.4-1.8) when non-melanoma co-twin asked.
  • Suggests recall bias for sunbathing, but not for tanning/burning ease.
  • Recall bias cannot occur in cohort studies (exposures collected pre-diagnosis).
  • Nested case-control study on melanoma found inaccuracies but little evidence of recall bias.
Strategies for Avoiding Measurement Bias
  • 1. Use data recorded before outcome occurred.
  • 2. Use blinding.
Strategy 1: Pre-Outcome Data
  • Example: Oral cholera vaccine study used actual vaccination records + self-reported status.
  • Limitation: availability and reliability of recorded info about risk factor.
Strategy 2: Blinding
  • Ideally, participants + observers blinded to case-control status and risk factor.
  • Four types of blinding possible (Table 9.1).
Blinding Study Participants
  • Possible if both cases/controls have diseases plausibly related to risk factor.
  • Include 'dummy' risk factors; be suspicious if they differ between groups.
  • May not work if risk factor for disease already publicized.
Blinding Observers
  • Possible if cases not externally distinguishable from controls, but subtle signs/statements can make it difficult.
  • Possible if interviewer is not investigator, but hard to maintain.
  • Blinding to specific risk factors usually easier than to case-control status.
  • Include 'dummy' questions about plausible, unassociated risk factors to keep hypotheses secret.
  • Example: Infant botulism/honey study → include detailed questions on yogurt/bananas.
  • If cases report more honey but no ↑ in other foods, differential bias less likely.
  • This strategy fails if association widely publicized or dummy factors are real.
  • Crucial for laboratory measurements (e.g., blood tests, x-rays) → always blind by coded labels.
  • Example: Unblinded bone mass measurements in hip fracture studies showed larger differences than blinded studies (18).
Specialized Case-Control Designs
  • Variations that leverage existing cohorts or use cases as their own controls to increase validity and efficiency.
  • Include nested case-control, incidence-density nested case-control, and case-cohort studies.
Nested Case-Control Study
  • Case-control study 'nested' within a defined cohort.
  • Cohort may be pre-defined (w/ banked specimens) or investigator-designed de novo.
  • Investigator identifies cohort at risk, large enough for sufficient cases, w/ measurable exposure data (banked specimens/medical records).
  • Defines outcome occurrence criteria (after cohort entry, before follow-up end).
Methodology
  • If outcome rare, follow-up complete, single baseline exposure measurement sufficient:
  • 1. Identify all cohort members who developed outcome (cases).
  • 2. Select random sample of cohort members who did not develop outcome (controls).
  • 3. Measure predictor variables for cases/controls; compare risk factor levels.
Incidence-Density Nested Case-Control Study
  • Used when follow-up is variable/incomplete, or exposure changes over time.
  • Controls sampled from 'risk sets' for each case as it occurs.
  • Risk set: cohort members followed same length of time as case, but not yet cases.
  • Matching on follow-up time must be accounted for in analysis.
Methodology
  • Prospective version:
  • 1. Define selection criteria; recruit cohort.
  • 2. Define entry date for each member to align follow-up.
  • 3. Store specimens/data for later analysis.
  • 4. Follow cohort to identify cases and diagnosis dates.
  • 5. Sample 1+ controls per case from 'risk sets' (cohort members followed same time, not yet cases/dead/lost to follow-up).
  • 6. Measure predictor variables in stored specimens/data + other current variables on cases/matched controls.
  • Retrospective version: first 4 steps already done.
  • Allows for controls to later become cases (w/ appropriate statistical analysis).
  • Considers 'chunks' of person-time at risk, using predictor values in that period to predict case occurrence.
Example: Opiate Overdose Deaths in Italy
  • Study nested in VEdeTTE cohort (heroin users from public treatment centers, 1998-1999).
  • Mortality ascertained on 4444 participants (Sept 1998 - Dec 2005).
  • Cases: 95 overdose deaths during follow-up.
  • Controls: 4 randomly selected controls per case, alive on case's death date (index date), matched on region, age (±5 years), sex.
  • Controls could be matched w/ multiple cases; cases eligible as controls for earlier deaths.
  • Predictors: drug treatment type 2 months before death/index date (retrieved from clinical records, blinded).
  • Finding: Opiate agonist treatment → >90% reduction in odds of mortality (OR = 0.09, 95% CI: 0.03-0.24).
  • Adjusted OR = 0.08 (95% CI: 0.03-0.23) after controlling for homelessness, HIV-positivity, alcohol use, legal problems, baseline overdose.
Nested Case-Cohort Study
  • Similar to nested case-control, but controls are a random sample of ALL cohort members, regardless of outcome.
  • A few controls may have developed the outcome (small # if outcome uncommon).
Advantages
  • Single random sample of cohort can serve as controls for several different outcomes.
  • Provides info on overall prevalence of risk factors in the cohort.
Example: Plasma ACE2 & Cardiometabolic Diseases
  • Cohort: Prospective Urban Rural Epidemiology (PURE) study (35-70 years old, 27 countries).
  • Biobanking initiative w/ baseline blood samples from subset (14 countries).
  • Cases: Incident death (N=1985), MI (N=882), stroke (N=663), heart failure (N=264), diabetes (N=1715).
  • Subcohort (controls): Random sample of 5084 PURE participants w/ analyzable samples (not excluded if they had an outcome).
  • Predictors: Plasma ACE2 levels measured on biobank specimens; traditional cardiovascular risk factors (sex, BMI, smoking, BP) from collected data.
  • Finding: ACE2 strong predictor of all outcomes (HRs 1.21-1.44 per SD ↑ ACE2) after adjustment for traditional risk factors.
  • ACE2 levels stronger predictors than clinical risk factors.
Strengths of Nested Designs
  • Cost-effective for expensive measurements on archived specimens/images (only measure on cases + sample of controls).
  • Preserve advantages of cohort studies (predictor variables collected pre-outcome) when measurements are blinded.
  • Avoid biases of conventional case-control studies (cannot measure fatal cases, different populations for cases/controls).
Weaknesses of Nested Designs
  • Susceptible to confounding by unmeasured/imprecisely measured variables.
  • Baseline measurements may be affected by silent preclinical disease ('effect-cause' vs. cause-effect).
Other Considerations for Nested Designs
  • Underutilized design.
  • Investigators planning large prospective studies should consider:
  • 1. Preserving biological samples (e.g., frozen sera banks).
  • 2. Storing images/records expensive to analyze for future nested analyses.
  • 3. Ensuring storage conditions preserve substances for many years.
  • 4. Collecting new samples/info during follow-up for efficient use in incidence-density studies.
Case-Crossover Studies
  • Variant of case-control design for studying short-term effects of intermittent exposures.
  • Each case serves as their own control.
  • Compares exposures of cases at/just before outcome w/ exposures of same cases at other time points.
Methodology
  • Example: Car crashes & mobile phone use.
  • Cases: individuals injured in car crashes who owned/used a mobile phone.
  • Comparison: mobile phone usage in 10 minutes before crash vs. usage at same time of day 24h, 72h, 7 days before crash (using phone company records).
  • Finding: Mobile phone usage ≈4x more likely in 10 mins before crash vs. comparison periods.
  • Analysis similar to matched case-control, but control exposures are from different time periods for the same case.
Applications
  • Used in large populations to study time-varying exposures:
  • 1. Particulate air pollution levels → hospital admissions (23), total mortality in older adults (24), out-of-hospital cardiac arrest (25), infant mortality (26).
Choosing Among Observational Designs
  • No single 'best' or 'worst' design; each has its place depending on research question and circumstances.
  • Table 9.2 summarizes advantages and disadvantages.
Design Comparison Summary (Table 9.2)
  • All observational designs are susceptible to confounding variables (compared w/ randomized trials).
Cross-sectional
  • Advantages: Relatively short duration, good first step for cohort/trial, yields prevalence of multiple predictors/outcomes.
  • Disadvantages: Does not establish sequence of events, not feasible for rare predictors/outcomes, does not yield incidence.
Cohort Designs (General)
  • Advantages: Establishes sequence of events, multiple predictors/outcomes, # outcome events grows over time, yields incidence/relative risk/excess risk.
  • Disadvantages: Often requires large sample sizes, less feasible for rare outcomes.
Prospective Cohort
  • Advantages: More control over participant selection/measurements, avoids bias in measuring predictors.
  • Disadvantages: Follow-up can be lengthy, often expensive.
Retrospective Cohort
  • Advantages: Follow-up is in the past, relatively inexpensive.
  • Disadvantages: Less control over participant selection/measurements.
Multiple Cohort
  • Advantages: Useful when distinct cohorts have different/rare exposures.
  • Disadvantages: Bias and confounding from sampling distinct populations.
Case-Control
  • Advantages: Useful for rare outcomes, short duration, small sample size, relatively inexpensive.
  • Disadvantages: Bias/confounding from sampling two populations, differential measurement bias, limited to one outcome, sequence of events may be unclear, does not yield prevalence/incidence/excess risk (unless nested).
Hybrid Designs (General)
  • Combine features for specific benefits.
Nested Case-Control
  • Advantages: Advantages of retrospective cohort, less costly if predictor measurement is expensive.
  • Disadvantages: Risk factor measurements subject to bias if not previously measured/banked; usually requires pre-existing defined cohort.
Incidence-Density Nested Case-Control
  • Advantages: Allows analysis of risk relationships considering changes over time in risk factor levels and loss to follow-up.
  • Disadvantages: Requires measurements of risk factor levels and case incidence over time; usually requires pre-existing defined cohort.
Nested Case-Cohort
  • Advantages: Same as nested case-control; can use single control group for multiple case-control studies w/ different outcomes.
  • Disadvantages: Same as nested case-control.
Case-Crossover
  • Advantages: Cases serve as own controls, reducing random error and confounding.
  • Disadvantages: Requires exposure to have only immediate, short-term effects.
Odds Ratio Calculation (Appendix 9A)
  • The odds ratio (OR) quantifies the association between an exposure and an outcome.
  • It is the odds of exposure in cases divided by the odds of exposure in controls.
  • Mathematically, OR = (a/c) / (b/d) = ad/bc, where 'a' is exposed cases, 'b' is exposed controls, 'c' is unexposed cases, 'd' is unexposed controls.
Example: Hepatitis A Outbreak
  • Exposure: Ate frozen pomegranate arils. Outcome: Hepatitis A diagnosis.
  • Cases (Hepatitis A): 9 exposed, 4 unexposed.
  • Controls (No Hepatitis A): 1 exposed, 20 unexposed.
  • OR = (9 * 20) / (1 * 4) = 180 / 4 = 45.0.
  • Interpretation: People who ate contaminated arils were ≈45 times more likely to get Hepatitis A.
Matched Case-Control Study OR
  • For pair-matched studies, OR is the ratio of discordant pairs.
  • Discordant pairs: case exposed, control unexposed (numerator) vs. case unexposed, control exposed (denominator).
  • Concordant pairs (both exposed or both unexposed) provide no info on association.
Example: Cell Phone Use & Crash Injuries (Hypothetical)
  • Cases: w/ crash injuries. Controls: w/o crash injuries, matched by age, sex, phone prefix.
  • Exposure: habitual cell phone use while driving.
  • Pairs:
  • - Case user, Control user: 110
  • - Case user, Control nonuser: 90
  • - Case nonuser, Control user: 40
  • - Case nonuser, Control nonuser: 60
  • OR = 90 / 40 = 2.25.
  • Interpretation: Cell phone users had >2x risk of crash (if real, as crashes are rare).
Case-Crossover Study OR
  • Each cell represents # of cases, but compares two time periods for that one case.
  • OR is ratio of discordant time periods.
Example: Recent Cell Phone Use & Crash Injuries
  • Crash time period vs. 7 days before crash.
  • - Driver using phone @ crash, using phone 7 days before: 5
  • - Driver using phone @ crash, not using phone 7 days before: 27
  • - Driver not using phone @ crash, using phone 7 days before: 6
  • - Driver not using phone @ crash, not using phone 7 days before: 288
  • OR = 27 / 6 = 4.5.
  • Interpretation: Driving during mobile phone use associated w/ 4.5-fold higher crash risk than driving w/o phone use.
Odds Ratio vs. Risk Ratio (Appendix 9B)
  • Risk of disease: # people developing disease / # people at risk.
  • Odds of disease: # people developing disease / # people not developing disease.
  • Two ways to calculate OR, but only one value:
  • 1. OR1 (cohort studies): odds of disease in exposed / odds of disease in unexposed = (a/b) ÷ (c/d) = ad/bc.
  • 2. OR2 (case-control studies): odds of exposure in cases / odds of exposure in controls = (a/c) ÷ (b/d) = ad/cb.
  • Both formulas yield the same value: ad/bc = ad/cb.
Approximation When Disease is Rare
  • When disease is rare (≤10%) in both exposed/unexposed:
  • Most people don't develop disease → # people at risk ≈ # people not developing disease.
  • Therefore, odds of a rare disease ≈ risk of a rare disease.
  • Consequently, OR1 ≈ Risk Ratio (RR).
  • Since OR2 = OR1, then OR2 ≈ RR.
  • This means OR from case-control studies approximates RR when the disease is rare.
