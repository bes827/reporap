0. Study: ESGE AI Curriculum Position Statement
1. Introduction & Rationale
  • AI to transform GI endoscopy → ↑ accuracy, efficiency
  • Uncertainties: outcomes, adverse events, cost‑effectiveness, human‑AI dynamics
  • No existing training or competency framework for AI‑assisted endoscopy
  • ESGE identified urgent need for structured training program
2. Development Methodology
  • Aligned with ESGE postgraduate curricula guidance (ref [6])
  • Adopted Delphi consensus (Feb 2025) → 5‑point Likert, ≥80% agreement threshold
  • Three curriculum phases: preadoption, training, autonomous implementation
  • Taskforce (25 members) + 3 AI engineering advisors + 65 voting members
3. Preadoption Phase – Core Requirements
3.1 Fundamental Endoscopic Skills
  • Basic competency in standard endoscopy deemed prerequisite (Statement 1, 93%)
  • Skill set includes scope handling, lesion visualization, polyp characterization
3.2 AI Literacy Foundations
  • Understanding of machine‑learning concepts required (Statement 2, 90%)
  • Topics: algorithm basics, data quality, performance metrics, bias & limitations
3.3 Educational Program Design
  • Develop curricula to boost AI literacy with hands‑on training using approved systems (Statement 3, 99%)
  • Combine theoretical modules + supervised practice on real cases
4. Training Phase – Human‑AI Interaction
4.1 Cognitive Bias Awareness
  • Endoscopists must recognize automation, anchoring, algorithm aversion, conservatism biases (Statement 4, 93%)
  • Bias mitigation strategies outlined in Table 2 of the source document
4.2 Decision‑Making Independence
  • Training emphasizes avoidance of exclusive reliance on AI outputs (Statement 5, 97%)
  • Promote verification of AI suggestions before clinical action

5. Autonomous Implementation & Proficiency Assessment
5.1 Performance Monitoring Requirements
  • Monitor KPIs (e.g., ADR) pre‑implementation → baseline data
  • Continuous KPI tracking during AI use → detect drift
  • Post‑implementation audit → confirm sustained benefit
5.2 De‑implementation Criteria
  • Trigger: adverse KPI trend ↓ beyond predefined threshold
  • Consider temporary washout period to assess AI impact
  • Re‑evaluate system suitability before re‑deployment
5.3 Proficiency Validation
  • Require documented competence in AI‑assisted procedures → case log ≥30 AI cases
  • Use objective assessment tools (e.g., simulation scores, blinded video review)
  • Recertification every 2 years or after major software update
6. Safety & Adverse Event Considerations
6.1 Potential AI‑Related Risks
  • Over‑reliance → missed pathology if AI fails
  • Automation bias → reduced situational awareness
  • Deskilling of operators → long‑term skill erosion
6.2 Mitigation Strategies
  • Limit on‑screen alarms → reduce false positives (Mitigation 1)
  • Structured verification training → confirm AI suggestions before action (Mitigation 3)
  • Enhance system explainability → transparent decision logic (Mitigation 4)
  • Adaptive, user‑friendly interface design → lower cognitive load (Mitigation 5)
6.3 Reporting & Feedback Loops
  • Mandatory AE reporting form for AI‑related incidents
  • Real‑time feedback to developers → iterative algorithm refinement
  • Institutional safety committee oversight of AI deployment
7. Conclusions & Future Directions
7.1 Core Take‑aways
  • Foundational endoscopy skills prerequisite for safe AI use
  • AI literacy essential; combine theory + hands‑on practice
  • Recognize and mitigate cognitive biases in human‑AI interaction
  • Maintain clinical independence; avoid exclusive AI reliance
7.2 Ongoing Research Priorities
  • Prospective trials comparing early vs. late AI integration in training curricula
  • Longitudinal studies on operator deskilling and skill retention
  • Cost‑effectiveness analyses of AI‑assisted programs across healthcare settings
  • Development of standardized AI performance dashboards for routine monitoring
7.3 Implementation Outlook
  • ESGE to update curriculum biennially based on emerging evidence
  • Encourage multi‑center collaborations for shared KPI databases
  • Promote patient engagement → assess acceptance and informed consent models

9. Bias Definitions & Mitigation Measures (Table 2 Summary)
9.1 Automation Bias
  • Definition: Over‑acceptance of AI outputs, ↓ situational awareness
  • Mitigations:
  • Limit on‑screen alarms / false positives
  • Reduce cognitive load on endoscopist
  • Structured platform‑specific training
  • Increase explainability & transparency
  • Design adaptive, user‑friendly interfaces
9.2 Anchoring Bias
  • Definition: Decisions influenced by irrelevant cues (e.g., visual artefacts) when AI suggests pathology
  • Mitigations:
  • Provide sufficient decision time to weigh evidence vs. intuition
9.3 Algorithm Aversion
  • Definition: Disbelief in AI after observing errors, leading to under‑use even when accurate
  • Mitigations:
  • Familiarize users with algorithm fundamentals
  • Set realistic expectations based on training/validation data
  • Ensure correct system selection for specific tasks
  • Ongoing real‑world performance monitoring
9.4 Conservatism Bias
  • Definition: Resistance to new information that challenges established beliefs; “black‑box” reluctance
  • Mitigations:
  • Enhance explainability & transparency of AI decisions
  • Reduce black‑box perception through user education
