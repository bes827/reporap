Contemporary management of gastrointestinal, pancreatic and hepatic toxicities of immune checkpoint inhibitors

Introduction to Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICIs) & irAEs
  • - ICIs: Monoclonal antibodies blocking immune checkpoint receptors (CTLA-4, PD-1, PD-L1).
  • - Immune-Related Adverse Events (irAEs): 'Off-target' effects from T-lymphocyte-mediated immune response.
  • - Commonly Affected Tissues: Barrier tissues (skin, GI tract, liver, respiratory) and endocrine organs.
  • - Onset: Can occur anytime during treatment and even months to years after discontinuation.
ICI Classes
  • - CTLA-4 inhibitors: Ipilimumab, tremelimumab.
  • - PD-1 inhibitors: Nivolumab, pembrolizumab, cemiplimab.
  • - PD-L1 inhibitors: Atezolizumab, avelumab, durvalumab.


Diarrhoea, Colitis, and Enteritis
  • - Represents a spectrum of colonic inflammation; the most frequent GI toxicity of ICIs.
  • - Enteritis can occur with colitis or in isolation.
Epidemiology & Risk Factors
  • - Incidence by ICI Class: Combination therapy > CTLA-4 inhibitors > PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors.
  • - Symptom Onset: Typically within a few weeks to a few months after ICI initiation.
Incidence of Severe (Grade ≥3) Diarrhoea
  • - Anti-PD-1/PD-L1: 1% of patients.
  • - Anti-CTLA-4: 6%–8% of patients.
  • - Combination Therapy: 10% of patients.

Risk Factors for ICI Colitis
  • - Drug-related: Higher ipilimumab dose.
  • - Patient-related: Baseline gut microbiota (Firmicutes-enriched), pre-existing IBD/microscopic colitis.
  • - Medications: Antibiotic exposure within 3 months prior, NSAID use, chronic proton pump inhibitor (PPI) use.
Protective Factors
  • - Vitamin D supplementation may be protective.



Clinical Presentation & Complications
  • - Symptoms: Loose stools, increased frequency, abdominal cramping/pain, urgency, hematochezia.
  • - Complications: Ileus, toxic megacolon, colonic perforation, death.
  • - Fatality Risk: Colitis represents 70% of fatal irAEs with anti-CTLA-4 and 37% with combination therapy.

Diagnosis
  • - A diagnosis of exclusion requiring a thorough workup for mimics.
Initial Workup (All Grades)
  • - Infectious Studies: C. difficile, bacterial enteric pathogens (NAAT), stool O&P and Giardia antigen if indicated.
  • - Lab Tests: CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, TSH.
  • - Stool Markers: Fecal calprotectin is a useful adjunct for mucosal inflammation.
Fecal Calprotectin Utility
  • - In patients with transient grade 1 symptoms, a normal fecal calprotectin (<50 μg/g) allows deferral of endoscopy.


Endoscopic Evaluation
  • - Indicated for: Grade 2-4 symptoms, persistent grade 1 symptoms, or elevated fecal calprotectin.
  • - Procedure Choice: Flexible sigmoidoscopy is often sufficient, as over 95% of cases have left colonic involvement.
  • - Upper Endoscopy (OGD): Recommended if lower endoscopy is unrevealing and symptoms persist.
Histopathology Findings
  • - Typical ICI Colitis: Active colitis with inflammatory infiltrates (neutrophilic, lymphocytic, or mixed).
  • - Other Patterns: Lymphocytic/collagenous colitis, graft-versus-host disease (GVHD)-like pattern with crypt apoptosis.


Imaging
  • - Role of CT: Limited diagnostic utility; reserved for suspicion of complications (e.g., perforation, megacolon).
  • - CT Performance: Low specificity (53%–85%) and sensitivity (75%–78%).


Management (by Severity)
  • - Management is guided by CTCAE grading (See Figure 1).
Grade 1 Colitis
  • - Initial: Supportive care (hydration, bland diet), bile acid-binding resins. Avoid loperamide.
  • - ICI Therapy: Can often be continued.
  • - Persistent Symptoms: Trial of oral budesonide 9 mg daily for 8 weeks.

Grade 2-4 Colitis
  • - Action: Withhold/discontinue ICI therapy and consult Gastroenterology.
  • - First-line Therapy: Corticosteroids.
Microscopic Colitis
  • - Stop potential non-ICI culprit medications (PPIs, NSAIDs, SSRIs).
  • - For mild-moderate cases, a trial of budesonide is reasonable before systemic steroids.

Grade 2-3 (Outpatient)
  • - Treatment: Oral prednisone 1 mg/kg/day.
  • - Taper: Once symptoms improve to grade 1, taper over 4–6 weeks.

Grade 4 / Systemic Symptoms (Inpatient)
  • - Action: Hospitalize for IV therapy.
  • - Treatment: IV methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day.


Steroid-Refractory Colitis
  • - Definition: No improvement after 3 days of IV steroids, or symptom recurrence during taper.
  • - Action: Add an IV biologic agent (after screening for viral hepatitis/TB).
Biologic Options
  • - Infliximab (TNF-α inhibitor):
  • - Dose: 5 mg/kg at 0, 2, 6 weeks.
  • - Onset: Faster (72h to 1 week).
  • - Indication for higher dose: Consider 10 mg/kg for severe disease or hypoalbuminemia (<2.5 g/dL).
  • - Vedolizumab (α4β7 inhibitor):
  • - Dose: 300 mg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks.
  • - Onset: Slower (1-2 weeks).
  • - Advantage: Gut-selective, less systemic immunosuppression.

Third-line Therapies
  • - For colitis refractory to both steroids and initial biologics.
  • - Options: Ustekinumab, tofacitinib, fecal microbiota transplantation.



Rechallenge and Monitoring
  • - Decision to rechallenge depends on prior toxicity severity, ICI class, and oncologic status.
Rechallenge Guidelines
  • - Grade 4: Permanent discontinuation of ICI is recommended.
  • - Grade 2-3: Rechallenge is possible after resolution.

Recurrence Risk
  • - Anti-CTLA-4 monotherapy: Recurrence rate of 29%.
  • - Anti-PD-1/PD-L1 monotherapy: Recurrence rate of 37%.
  • - Strategy: Consider switching from combination/CTLA-4 regimen to PD-1/PD-L1 monotherapy to reduce risk.

Monitoring
  • - Follow-up endoscopy may be offered before resuming immunotherapy.
  • - Fecal calprotectin has low sensitivity for confirming histologic remission.



Hepatobiliary Toxicity (ICI Hepatitis)
  • - Typically presents as asymptomatic liver enzyme elevations with a hepatocellular pattern.
Epidemiology
  • - Onset: Median time to onset is 5–8 weeks after ICI initiation.
  • - Risk Factors: Combination therapy, first-line immunotherapy, pre-existing liver disease.
Incidence by Therapy
  • - Monotherapy (Any Grade): 3%–7.1%
  • - Monotherapy (Grade ≥3): 1%–3%
  • - Combination Therapy (Any Grade): 15%–30%
  • - Combination Therapy (Grade ≥3): 7.5%–18.8%


Diagnosis
  • - Requires exclusion of other causes of liver injury (viral, metastatic, biliary obstruction, other DILI).
Monitoring & Workup
  • - Monitoring: Monitor LFTs at baseline and every 2 weeks during ICI therapy.
  • - Workup: Viral serologies (HAV, HBV, HCV, HEV, EBV, CMV, HSV), review of medications/supplements.
  • - Imaging: RUQ ultrasound initially; consider contrasted CT or MRCP.

Liver Biopsy
  • - Consider for: Grade ≥3 hepatitis or steroid-refractory cases, especially with atypical features.
  • - Utility: Can be useful but may delay treatment. One study found 11.2% of biopsies revealed an alternative diagnosis.
  • - Findings: Periportal/centrilobular lymphocytic infiltrates, granulomas. Histologic severity does not correlate with LFT abnormalities.


Management (by Severity)
  • - Management is guided by CTCAE grading of LFT elevation (See Figure 3).
Grade 1 Hepatitis
  • - Action: Continue ICI therapy.
  • - Monitoring: Monitor LFTs every 1–2 weeks.

Grade 2 Hepatitis
  • - Action: Temporarily hold ICI.
  • - Steroids: If LFTs do not improve in 3–5 days, start prednisone 0.5–1 mg/kg/day.
  • - Resumption: May resume ICI when hepatitis is ≤ grade 1 and prednisone is ≤10 mg/day.

Grade ≥3 Hepatitis
  • - Action: Discontinue ICI treatment.
  • - First-line Therapy: IV methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day.
  • - Taper: Taper over 4–6 weeks upon improvement.

Steroid-Refractory Hepatitis
  • - Definition: No response after 3 days of steroids.
  • - Action: Add a second-line immunomodulator.
Second-Line Agents
  • - Preferred: Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) at a dose of 500–1000 mg twice daily.
  • - Other Options: Azathioprine, tacrolimus.
  • - TNF-α inhibitors: Generally avoided due to theoretical risk of liver injury.



Rechallenge
  • - Guidelines: Recommend permanent discontinuation for grade 3 or 4 hepatitis.
  • - Recurrence Risk: If rechallenged, recurrence rate is 17%–29%.
  • - Decision: Must be made on a case-by-case basis.

ICI Cholangitis
  • - Presentation: Rare, marked by a cholestatic pattern of LFTs.
  • - Workup: Includes MRCP to evaluate for biliary obstruction.
  • - Management: Corticosteroids (0.5-2 mg/kg/day). High risk of being steroid-refractory, often requiring second-line agents like MMF.


Pancreatic Injury
  • - Presents variably, from asymptomatic enzyme elevation to acute pancreatitis.
Epidemiology
  • - Asymptomatic Hyperlipasemia: Occurs in 1%–4% of patients on ICIs.
  • - Symptomatic Acute Pancreatitis: Less common, occurring in <2% of patients.
  • - Risk: More common with combination checkpoint blockade.

Diagnosis
  • - Most cases are asymptomatic hyperlipasemia found on routine labs.
Evaluation
  • - Onset: Median time to first elevated lipase is 66 days from ICI initiation.
  • - Differential: A study showed 71% of hyperlipasemia cases were due to other causes (gallstones, biliary obstruction, colitis).
  • - Imaging: Abdominal CT (pancreatic protocol) is advised for lipase >3x ULN, even if asymptomatic.


Management
  • - Management depends on the presence of symptoms and imaging findings.
ICI-induced Acute Pancreatitis
  • - Action: Hospitalize, provide supportive care (IV fluids, pain control), and hold ICI.
  • - Corticosteroids:
  • - Grade 2: 0.5–1 mg/kg/day prednisone/methylprednisolone.
  • - Severe/Life-threatening: 1–2 mg/kg/day prednisone/methylprednisolone.
  • - Taper: Taper over 4–6 weeks after improvement.
  • - ICI Rechallenge: Permanently discontinue for severe pancreatitis.
Steroid-Refractory Pancreatitis
  • - Limited evidence from case reports suggests potential utility of:
  • - Infliximab
  • - Rituximab
  • - Azathioprine


Asymptomatic Hyperlipasemia
  • - After a thorough workup excludes pancreatitis or other causes:
  • - Action: Continue immunotherapy.
  • - Treatment: Observe without glucocorticoids.
  • - Monitoring: No strong data to support serial lipase monitoring.


Outcomes and Monitoring
  • - Patients should be monitored for long-term complications after pancreatic injury.
Long-Term Consequences
  • - Recurrent acute pancreatitis.
  • - Pancreatic volume loss/atrophy.
  • - Endocrine injury (Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus).
  • - Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI).
Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI)
  • - Incidence: Reported rates of 3%–4% following ICI pancreatic injury.
  • - Onset: Median onset was 390 days after the first ICI dose in one study.
  • - Management: Responsive to pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT).




Other Gastrointestinal Toxicities
  • - Includes oesophagitis, gastritis, motility disorders, and coeliac disease.
  • - Data is largely limited to case reports and small series.
Oesophageal irAEs
  • - Spectrum: Oesophagitis, oesophageal stenosis, oesophagitis dissecans superficialis.
  • - Incidence: Rare; found in only 3% of patients undergoing OGD in one cohort.
  • - Presentation: Often occurs with concurrent gastritis and/or duodenitis.
Management
  • - A diagnosis of exclusion.
  • - Initial Therapy: Most patients respond to proton pump inhibitor (PPI) therapy.
  • - Refractory Cases: Steroids are reserved for persistent or severe symptoms.


Gastritis
  • - Incidence: Constitutes approximately 5% of all luminal GI irAEs.
  • - Symptoms: Dyspepsia, bloating. Diarrhea/pain suggests concomitant enterocolitis.
  • - Association: Majority of patients with ICI gastritis have concomitant enteritis or colitis.
Diagnosis
  • - Endoscopy: Approximately 20%–30% of patients with biopsy-proven ICI gastritis have a normal endoscopic appearance.
  • - Biopsy: Essential for diagnosis, even with unremarkable endoscopy.
  • - Differential: Rule out H. pylori, CMV, Crohn's disease, etc.

Management
  • - PPIs: Only a minority of patients respond to PPI therapy alone.
  • - Glucocorticoids: Nearly two-thirds of patients require glucocorticoid therapy.


Gastroparesis
  • - A very rare irAE.
  • - Symptoms: Bloating, dyspepsia, postprandial nausea.
  • - Diagnosis: Requires exclusion of mechanical obstruction and other causes; gastric emptying study can confirm.
  • - Management: Data on steroids is lacking; case reports show improvement with promotility and antiemetic agents alone.

Coeliac Disease
  • - Treatment-emergent coeliac disease has been reported.
  • - Presentation: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, villous blunting on histology.
  • - Management: Responds to a gluten-free diet.
  • - Etiology: Unclear if it is de novo disease or unmasking of subclinical coeliac disease.


Future Directions
  • - Recognition and prompt treatment of GI and hepatic toxicities are essential as ICI use expands.
  • - Quality prospective, controlled studies are needed to refine management algorithms, risk stratification for rechallenge, and the optimal use of steroid-sparing therapies.
