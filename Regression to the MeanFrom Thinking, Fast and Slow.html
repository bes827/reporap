Regression to the Mean
From 'Thinking, Fast and Slow'

üéØ Core Concept: Regression to the Mean
  • - A statistical phenomenon where an extreme result is likely to be followed by a result that is closer to the average (the mean).
  • - It is a mathematically inevitable consequence when random luck is involved in an outcome.
  • - It occurs whenever the correlation between two measures is less than perfect.
‚ùå Common Misinterpretation
  • - The biggest error is attaching a causal interpretation to these inevitable, random fluctuations.
  • - We invent stories to explain what is simply a statistical regularity.


‚úàÔ∏è Illustrative Examples
  • - Regression effects are everywhere, but they often hide in plain sight and are given incorrect causal explanations.
Israeli Flight Instructors
  • - Observation: Praising a great maneuver was often followed by a worse one. Shouting after a bad maneuver was often followed by a better one.
  • - Instructor's Flawed Conclusion: Punishment works better than reward.
  • - Statistical Truth: Performance naturally fluctuates. An exceptionally good or bad performance is statistically likely to be followed by a more average one, regardless of feedback.
Perverse Feedback Loop
  • - This creates a situation where life seems to statistically punish us for being nice and reward us for being nasty.


Golf Tournament: Talent vs. Luck
  • - An excellent score on Day 1 (e.g., 66) is a mix of above-average talent + good luck.
  • - A poor score on Day 1 (e.g., 77) is a mix of below-average talent + bad luck.
Predicting Day 2 Performance
  • - When predicting Day 2, assume talent remains constant, but luck will be average.
  • - The golfer who did well on Day 1 is likely to do well again, but less well than on Day 1.
  • - The golfer who did poorly on Day 1 will likely do poorly again, but will improve from Day 1.
  • - The key is that the prediction should be more moderate than the initial evidence.


The 'Sports Illustrated' Jinx
  • - The Claim: Athletes on the magazine's cover perform poorly the next season.
  • - Wrong Causal Explanation: Pressure, overconfidence.
  • - Correct Statistical Explanation: An athlete gets on the cover after an exceptionally good season, likely helped by luck. The next season is simply more likely to be closer to their average performance.

Hypothetical Study: Depressed Children
  • - Headline: "Depressed children treated with an energy drink improve significantly over a three-month period."
  • - This conclusion is unjustified because depressed children are an extreme group and are likely to improve over time simply due to regression to the mean.
  • - To prove effectiveness, a treatment group must improve more than a control group, which is expected to improve by regression alone.


üìä Underlying Principles
  • - The concept of regression is deeply tied to the formulas of success and the statistical measure of correlation.
The Formula for Success
  • - `success = talent + luck`
  • - `great success = a little more talent + a lot of luck`

Correlation and Regression
  • - They are not two separate concepts, but different perspectives on the same concept.
  • - General Rule: Whenever the correlation between two scores is imperfect (less than 1), there will be regression to the mean.
Correlation Coefficient Examples
  • - A measure of the relative weight of shared factors between two variables, from 0 (none) to 1 (all).
  • - 1.0: Perfect correlation (e.g., size in inches vs. cm).
  • - ~0.60: SAT scores and college GPA.
  • - ~0.40: Income and education level in the US.
  • - 0.0: No correlation (e.g., income and last 4 digits of phone number).

Example: Intelligence in Spouses
  • - Interesting Statement: "Highly intelligent women tend to marry men who are less intelligent than they are."
  • - Trivial Statement: "The correlation between the intelligence scores of spouses is less than perfect."
  • - These two statements are algebraically equivalent. The first is just a mathematical consequence of the second, not something that needs a complex causal explanation.



üß† Why We Misunderstand Regression
  • - Regression is strange to the human mind because of our cognitive biases.
Bias Toward Causal Explanations
  • - Our mind (System 1) automatically and insistently seeks causes for events.
  • - We do not deal well with "mere statistics."
  • - Regression has an explanation (imperfect correlation), but it does not have a cause in the way our minds like to think.

System 1 vs. System 2
  • - System 1 (Intuitive): Automatically matches the extremeness of a prediction to the extremeness of the evidence. It generates overconfident judgments based on a coherent story.
  • - System 2 (Effortful): Finds the concept of regression difficult to learn and apply. It requires special training to overcome System 1's intuitive pull.


üõ†Ô∏è Taming Intuition: Making Better Predictions
  • - Intuitive predictions are often non-regressive and therefore systematically biased. They are as extreme as the evidence they are based on.
  • - This happens because we substitute an evaluation of current evidence for a prediction of a future outcome.
A 4-Step Correction Method
  • - To make an unbiased, regressive prediction:
  • - 1. Start with a baseline: Estimate the average outcome for the reference category (e.g., average GPA).
  • - 2. Make an intuitive prediction: Determine the outcome that matches your impression of the evidence.
  • - 3. Estimate the correlation: Guess the correlation (from 0 to 1) between your evidence and the outcome.
  • - 4. Adjust: Move from the baseline towards your intuition by the percentage of the correlation. (e.g., if correlation is 0.30, move 30% of the distance from the average to your intuitive guess).

Characteristics of Corrected Predictions
  • - They are unbiased (equally likely to overestimate or underestimate).
  • - They are more moderate.
  • - They only permit prediction of rare or extreme events when the information is very good (high correlation).
The Downside
  • - If your predictions are unbiased, you will never have the satisfying experience of correctly calling an extreme case (e.g., predicting a top student will become a Supreme Court justice).


When Are Extreme Predictions Defensible?
  • - A preference for unbiased predictions is not always best. It depends on the cost of different types of errors.
  • - Venture Capitalist: The cost of missing the next Google is far greater than investing in a company that fails. The goal is to correctly call extreme successes.
  • - Conservative Banker: The cost of a single large loan default is far greater than turning down several good clients. The goal is to avoid extreme failures.

Example: Hiring Kim vs. Jane
  • - Kim: Spectacular recommendations, brilliant interview, but no track record (small sample of information).
  • - Jane: Excellent research record over 3 years, but less sparkling interview (large sample of information).
  • - Intuitive Choice: Kim, because of WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is).
  • - Regressive Choice: Jane. Kim's performance is more likely an extreme outcome from a small sample and should be regressed more heavily toward the mean.
