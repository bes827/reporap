Thinking, Fast and Slow: An Introduction to Cognitive Biases
  • - Source: Introduction from 'Thinking, Fast and Slow' by Daniel Kahneman.
  • - Objective: To provide a richer language for understanding and discussing errors of judgment and choice, both in others and ourselves.
Knowledge Test
  • - 1. What is the core principle of intuitive heuristics when faced with a difficult question?
  • - 2. In the "Invisible Gorilla" experiment, what percentage of viewers typically fail to see the gorilla?
  • - 3. According to Herbert Simon, what is expert intuition, in its simplest form?
  • - 4. What is the key statistical fact often ignored in the "Steve the librarian vs. farmer" problem?
  • - 5. What is the primary task of System 2 in relation to the impulses of System 1?

Core Premise: Improving Judgment Through Language
  • - The goal is to improve the ability to identify and understand errors of judgment and choice.
  • - This is achieved by providing a richer, more precise language to discuss them, similar to how medicine uses diagnostic labels for diseases.
  • - Expectation of intelligent gossip can be a powerful motive for self-criticism.
Systematic Errors (Biases)
  • - Systematic errors are predictable, recurring errors in judgment known as biases.
  • - They arise from the design of the cognitive machinery, not necessarily from emotional corruption of thought.
  • - A diagnostic label (e.g., Halo Effect) makes a bias easier to anticipate, recognize, and understand.


The Two Systems Model
  • - A psychological model describing mental life via two metaphorical agents: System 1 and System 2.
  • - These are fictitious characters used to simplify the explanation of different modes of thinking.
System 1: The Automatic System
  • - Operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort.
  • - No sense of voluntary control.
  • - It is the 'hero' of the book, the secret author of many choices and judgments.
Characteristics & Capabilities
  • - Includes innate skills (perceiving distance, fearing spiders) and learned, automatic skills (reading, 2+2=4, understanding simple sentences).
  • - Generates suggestions for System 2: impressions, intuitions, feelings.
  • - Cannot be turned off voluntarily (e.g., you will read a word in a language you know).

Examples of System 1 Activities
  • - Detect hostility in a voice.
  • - Complete the phrase "bread and butter".
  • - Drive a car on an empty road.
  • - Recognize a stereotype from a description.


System 2: The Effortful System
  • - Allocates attention to effortful mental activities.
  • - Associated with the subjective experience of agency, choice, and concentration.
  • - We identify our conscious self with System 2.
Characteristics & Limitations
  • - Operations require attention and are disrupted when attention is drawn away.
  • - Has a limited budget of attention; trying to go beyond it will cause failure.
  • - Effortful activities interfere with each other (e.g., cannot solve 17x24 while making a difficult left turn).
Physiological Markers of Effort
  • - When engaged, muscles tense, blood pressure rises, heart rate increases, and pupils dilate.


Examples of System 2 Activities
  • - Focus on a specific voice in a noisy room.
  • - Park in a narrow space.
  • - Fill out a tax form.
  • - Check the validity of a complex logical argument.


Interaction & Division of Labor
  • - A highly efficient arrangement that minimizes effort and optimizes performance.
  • - System 1 runs automatically, generating suggestions (impressions, intuitions).
  • - System 2 is in a low-effort mode, endorsing System 1's suggestions if all is smooth.
System 2 Mobilization
  • - System 2 is mobilized when:
  • - System 1 encounters difficulty or has no answer (e.g., 17 × 24 = 408).
  • - An event violates System 1's model of the world (e.g., a talking cat).
  • - An error is about to be made, requiring self-control.

Conflict & Self-Control
  • - Conflict occurs when an automatic reaction from System 1 interferes with an intended task by System 2.
  • - Example: Naming the text color when the word itself is a different color (Stroop test, see Figure 2).
  • - A key task of System 2 is to overcome the impulses of System 1, which is the essence of self-control.



Heuristics and Biases
  • - Heuristics are simplifying mental shortcuts (rules of thumb) used in intuitive thinking.
  • - Reliance on heuristics can lead to predictable, systematic errors known as biases.
Origins of the Research
  • - Collaboration between Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky starting in 1969.
  • - Initial question: Are people good intuitive statisticians?
  • - Conclusion: A qualified NO. Even expert statisticians showed poor statistical intuition, especially regarding the reliability of results from small samples.

The Availability Heuristic
  • - Judging the size of a category or frequency of an event by the ease with which instances come to mind.
Examples
  • - Letter K: People think 'K' is more likely to be the first letter of a word than the third, because it's easier to recall words starting with K. The opposite is true for letters like K, L, N, R, V.
  • - Politician Adultery: Transgressions of politicians are more widely reported by media, making them more 'available' in memory and leading to the impression that it's more common in that group.

Media Influence
  • - The media's choice of coverage heavily influences what is 'available' to the public.
  • - This explains why some issues are highly salient while others (e.g., declining educational standards) are neglected.


The Resemblance Heuristic (Representativeness)
  • - Using resemblance to a stereotype to make a difficult judgment of probability, while ignoring relevant statistical facts (base rates).
Example: Steve the Librarian
  • - A shy, meek, and tidy soul (Steve) is described.
  • - People judge him more likely to be a librarian than a farmer based on resemblance to the stereotype.
  • - This ignores the statistical base rate: there are more than 20 male farmers for each male librarian in the US.


The Affect Heuristic
  • - Judgments and decisions are guided directly by feelings of liking and disliking, with little reasoning.
Example: Ford Stock Investment
  • - An investment officer invested millions in Ford stock.
  • - His reasoning was not based on financial analysis (Is the stock underpriced?).
  • - Instead, it was based on his positive feeling: "Boy, do they know how to make a car!"


Core Mechanism of Heuristics
  • - Substitution: When faced with a difficult question, we often answer an easier one instead, usually without noticing the substitution.


Cognitive Illusions
  • - Illusions of thought, analogous to visual illusions.
  • - Errors of intuitive thought are often difficult to prevent because System 1 operates automatically.
Visual Illusion Example: Müller-Lyer
  • - Two lines of identical length appear to be different lengths due to fins pointing in or out (See Figure 3).
  • - Key Insight: Even when your System 2 knows the lines are equal, your System 1 still sees one as longer.
  • - To resist, you must learn to mistrust your impression in this specific context.

Cognitive Illusion Example: Psychopathic Charm
  • - A patient with a history of multiple failed treatments lucidly describes how previous therapists failed him, but is convinced you are different and can help.
  • - This triggers strong feelings of sympathy and competence in the therapist (System 1).
  • - Clinical Wisdom: This is a danger sign. The patient is likely a psychopath. Do not take on this patient.

Overcoming Cognitive Illusions
  • - Difficult because System 1 cannot be turned off and System 2 may have no clue an error is occurring.
  • - The best approach is a compromise: learn to recognize situations where mistakes are likely and try harder to avoid them when stakes are high.


Limitations & Marvels of the Mind
  • - The mind has puzzling limitations, but also performs marvelous feats of intuition.
Limitation: Inattentional Blindness
  • - We can be blind to the obvious, and we are also blind to our blindness.
The Invisible Gorilla Experiment
  • - Viewers are asked to count basketball passes by one team.
  • - A person in a gorilla suit walks through the scene for 9 seconds.
  • - Result: About half of the viewers do not notice the gorilla.
  • - Implication: Seeing and orienting, while automatic (System 1), depend on the allocation of some attention.


Limitation: Overconfidence
  • - We have excessive confidence in what we believe we know.
  • - We are prone to overestimate how much we understand about the world.
  • - We tend to underestimate the role of chance in events.

Marvel: Expert Intuition
  • - Accurate intuitions of experts are not magic, but a result of prolonged practice.
Herbert Simon's Definition
  • - "The situation has provided a cue; this cue has given the expert access to information stored in memory, and the information provides the answer."
  • - In short: "Intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition."

Development & Examples
  • - Develops after thousands of hours of practice.
  • - Examples:
  • - Chess master instantly seeing a strong move.
  • - Firefighter commander sensing danger and evacuating just before a floor collapses.
