Colonic Intussusception & Volvulus – Overview
  • Source: Chapter 155, Colonic Intussusception and Volvulus (2026)
  • Topic: Adult colonic intussusception, colonic volvulus, diagnosis & management
1. Pathophysiology of Colonic Intussusception
  • Invagination of intussusceptum into distal intussuscipiens.
  • 86‑90 % have a pathologic lead point.
  • Malignancy accounts for ~56 % of colonic cases.
  • Benign causes: GI neoplasms, adhesions, Meckel diverticulum, sprue, HIV, idiopathic.
  • Proposed mechanism: luminal stimulus → proximal constriction + distal relaxation → invagination.
  • Two categories: enteric (jejunal/ileal) vs colonic (ileocolic, colocolic, colorectal).
2. Clinical Presentation of Adult Intussusception
  • Represents ~1 % of bowel obstructions.
  • Classic pediatric triad rare in adults.
  • Most common symptoms: crampy abdominal pain (71 %), nausea/vomiting (68 %).
  • Additional findings: distention (45 %), tenderness (60 %).
  • <20 % develop complete obstruction.
  • History often includes intermittent, sub‑acute obstruction over days–years.
3. Diagnostic Imaging & Findings
Plain Radiography
  • Useful for enteric intussusception without colonic involvement.
  • Upper GI series may show “stacked coin” / “coiled spring.”
Barium Enema
  • Identifies colonic obstruction; shows cup‑shaped filling defect at distal intussusceptum.
Ultrasound (pediatric focus)
  • Detects “target,” “donut,” and longitudinal “pseudo‑kidney” signs.
  • Operator‑dependent; limited by bowel gas & habitus in adults.
Computed Tomography (CT) – Modality of Choice
  • Shows axial “target sign”: concentric layers of intussusceptum + mesentery within lumen.
  • Pathognomonic “bowel‑within‑bowrel” with compressed mesenteric vessels.
  • Highest sensitivity for adult cases presenting with obstruction or palpable mass.
4. Treatment Principles for Adult Colonic Intussusception
  • Surgery is definitive; non‑operative reduction not recommended due to malignancy risk.
  • Formal oncologic resection includes segmental removal + lymphadenectomy.
  • Reduction discouraged to avoid bowel trauma and perforation.
  • Endoscopic approaches reserved only for subacute/chronic colonic obstruction when malignancy excluded (rare).

5. Volvulus – General Concepts
  • Colonic volvulus accounts for 1.9 % of large‑bowel obstructions in the US.
  • Higher prevalence (10‑50 %) in Africa, Middle East, South America.
  • Torsion of a colon segment around its mesentery → obstruction.
  • 180° twist = clinical obstruction; 360° = strangulation, ischemia, perforation.
6. Sigmoid Volvulus – Pathophysiology & Epidemiology
  • Redundant sigmoid + elongated mesocolon predispose to torsion.
  • “Dolichomesocolic” colon: longer than wide; more common in males >30 y.
  • Incidence peaks in mid‑70s; chronic fecal loading (high‑fiber diet) is a key factor.
  • Forms 50‑90 % of colonic volvulus cases worldwide.
7. Sigmoid Volvulus – Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis
  • Western patients: elderly, diabetic, institutionalized, often on psychoactive meds; chronic constipation → gradual distention.
  • Endemic regions (Africa, India, Latin America): younger males; acute obstruction with vomiting, obstipation.
  • Typical delay to presentation: 3‑4 days in West; may present as paradoxical diarrhea.
Imaging Signs – Plain Radiography
  • “Coffee bean” / “omega loop” sign; apex points to RUQ (≈60 % cases).
Barium Enema
  • “Bird’s beak” at torsion point; therapeutic reduction possible temporarily.
CT Findings
  • Mesenteric twist (“whirl sign”) highly specific.
  • Transition point & sigmoid “X‑marks‑the‑spot” sign suggest complete twist.
8. Sigmoid Volvulus – Management Strategy
  • Endoscopic decompression (rigid sigmoidoscopy or flexible colonoscopy) attempted first in stable patients without ischemia; success 60‑90 %.
  • Rectal tube placement (36‑F) post‑decompression; plain film confirms detorsion.
  • Recurrence high (≈71 %); mortality of recurrent episodes up to 36 %.
  • Surgical resection (segmental colectomy or subtotal/total colectomy) is definitive; primary anastomosis preferred when feasible.
  • Non‑resective options (detorsion + fixation, sigmoidopexy) have recurrence 9‑44 % and mortality ≈7.8 %.
  • Overall mortality 9.4 %; highest with diffuse peritonitis, gangrene → Hartmann’s procedure advisable.

9. Pathophysiology of Sigmoid Volvulus
  • Redundant sigmoid + elongated mesentery → torsion predisposition.
  • Congenital “dolichomesocolic” colon more frequent in males, age >30.
  • Chronic fecal loading (high‑fiber diet) stretches sigmoid, precipitating torsion.
10. Presentation of Sigmoid Volvulus by Region
  • Western subtype: elderly, diabetic, institutionalized; chronic constipation → gradual distention.
  • Endemic subtype: acute obstruction in young males (≈80 % male).
  • Pregnancy: sigmoid volvulus accounts for ~45 % of intestinal obstructions in pregnant patients.
11. Diagnostic Work‑up for Sigmoid Volvulus
  • Plain film first; “coffee bean” or “omega loop” sign present in ≈60 % cases.
  • Barium enema shows “bird’s beak” at torsion point; may be temporarily therapeutic.
  • CT accuracy approaches 100 %; mesenteric twist confirms diagnosis.
12. Treatment of Sigmoid Volvulus
Endoscopic Decompression (Non‑definitive)
  • Rigid sigmoidoscopy bedside, minimal insufflation; visual inspection for ischemia.
  • Gentle passage through torsion point decompresses proximal sigmoid; rectal tube (36 F) placed.
Recurrence & Mortality
  • Recurrence after endoscopic decompression ≈71 %; mortality of recurrent episodes up to 36 %.
Operative Management – Resection
  • Formal oncologic resection (segment + lymphadenectomy) performed in 89 % of cases.
  • Stomas required in ~50 % when bowel unsalvageable; primary anastomosis safe with judgment.
13. Cecal Volvulus – Pathophysiology & Presentation
  • Incomplete congenital fixation → mobile cecum predisposes to volvulus.
  • Occurs in 25‑40 % of US colonic volvulus cases; 1 %–1.5 % of all intestinal obstructions.
  • Acute symptoms mimic small‑bowel obstruction: pain, emesis, distention, possible peritonitis.
14. Diagnosis of Cecal Volvulus
  • Plain film: massively dilated cecum (“kidney‑bean” under left diaphragm).
  • Barium enema shows ascending colon beak; hydrostatic reduction contraindicated (risk perforation).
  • CT hallmark: “whirl sign” of twisted mesenteric vessels; beaking of cecal margins with upstream obstruction.
15. Treatment of Cecal Volvulus
  • Endoscopic therapy <30 % success; generally avoided due to thin‑walled cecum risk.
  • Operative resection (right hemicolectomy or extended) is standard; primary anastomosis when viable.
  • Non‑resective detorsion + cecopexy recurrence 12‑13 %; mortality higher than resection.
16. Rare Forms of Colonic Volvulus – Transverse Volvulus & Ileosigmoid Knotting
Transverse Colon Volvulus (≈3 % of colon volvulus)
  • Congenital absence of gastrocolic/lienocolic/phrenocolic ligaments or acquired distention permits torsion.
Ileosigmoid Knotting (Compound Midgut Volvulus)
  • Redundant sigmoid mesentery wrapped by mobile ileum; causes simultaneous small‑bowel and large‑bowel obstruction.
  • Predominantly endemic in Asia, Africa, Middle East; male predominance in fourth decade.
