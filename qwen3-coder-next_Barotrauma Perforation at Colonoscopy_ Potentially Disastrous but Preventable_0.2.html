Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol. 103, No. 2, 2026 | https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gie.2025.06.023
  • Topic: Colonoscopic barotrauma — epidemiology, pathophysiology, clinical recognition, and prevention
  • Author(s): Douglas K. Rex, MD (Indiana University School of Medicine)
1. Epidemiology and Clinical Significance
  • Barotrauma is the least common cause of colonoscopic perforation in published series
  • Yet it is the leading cause of malpractice claims related to perforation
  • In first 4 months of 2025, 5/6 negligence cases reviewed involved barotrauma
  • Therapeutic perforations (e.g., from polypectomy) are now often closed endoscopically with minimal contamination
  • Rupture perforations in rectosigmoid are usually large, recognized during withdrawal, and amenable to prompt repair
  • Barotrauma perforations typically involve extensive immediate peritoneal contamination and large intraperitoneal gas volumes
2. Pathophysiology and High-Risk Anatomy
  • Occurs almost exclusively in patients with narrowed, angulated sigmoid colons from severe diverticular disease
  • Not all patients with severe sigmoid diverticulosis develop barotrauma — but all barotrauma cases involve a diseased sigmoid
  • Closed-loop mechanism: gas trapped between competent ileocecal valve and colonoscope during insertion
  • Gas cannot escape retrograde around scope or antegrade through ileocecal valve → rapid pressure buildup
  • Brittle mucosa from conditions like collagenous colitis may predispose, but less clinically significant than sigmoid disease
  • In microscopic colitis, water-filling is recommended during insertion due to fragile mucosa
3. Clinical Recognition and Emergency Response
  • Diagnosis made by astute endoscopist via:
  • Recognition of risk factors (diseased sigmoid)
  • Palpation/percussion revealing distended, tympanitic abdomen
  • Immediate actions required:
  • Needle decompression of peritoneal cavity
  • Initiation of antibiotics
  • Emergent surgical consultation
  • Delayed recognition → rapid sepsis development and potentially fatal outcomes
  • Endoscopic repair often not feasible due to multiple mucosal breaks and poor visualization from inadequate prep
  • Site of perforation usually hard to identify intraoperatively, especially with poor bowel preparation
4. Prevention Strategies and Protocol Recommendations
  • Acute barotrauma occurs during the procedure — rarely delayed
  • Delayed perforation may follow prolonged gas distention and inability to pass flatus → proximal colon ischemia
  • CO₂ insufflation prevents delayed barotrauma but not acute injury
  • Key prevention: switch from gas insufflation to water-filling when entering a severely diseased sigmoid
  • Trigger points for switching to water-filling:
  • Need to change to thinner instrument to pass sigmoid
  • Struggle to pass sigmoid turns due to angulation/narrowing
  • Prevention is paramount: “an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure”
  • Education imperative: trainees and partners must be taught to recognize risk and act swiftly
1. High-Risk Patient Subgroups and Risk Stratification
  • Patients with severe diverticular disease constitute the overwhelming majority of barotrauma cases
  • Sigmoid narrowing is often secondary to chronic inflammation, fibrosis, and luminal distortion
  • Key anatomical red flags:
  • Tight sigmoid flexure on initial scope advancement
  • Angle of turn >90° with loss of haustral pattern
  • Visible extrinsic compression on intussusception sign
  • Suboptimal bowel preparation often coexists and masks severity of underlying disease
  • In such cases, poor prep reflects secondary obstruction from luminal narrowing — not true inadequate preparation
  • Clinical pearl: Poor prep in distal colon plus difficulty passing sigmoid = high suspicion for underlying stricturing disease
Subgroup: Patients with Collagenous Colitis
  • Mucosal fragility increases susceptibility to microperforations under pressure
  • Endoscopic findings: pseudopolyps, linear ulcers, subepithelial collagen layer >2.5 µm
  • Barotrauma risk amplified by concurrent NSAID use or smoking history
  • Water-filling preferred over air during insertion — even if sigmoid appears normal
Subgroup: Microscopic Colitis (Lymphocytic/Collagenous)
  • Systematic review shows ↑ perforation risk vs general population: OR 3.4 [1.9–6.2]
  • Perforations often spontaneous or minimal manipulation-induced
  • Histologic hallmarks:
  • Lymphocytic colitis: intraepithelial lymphocytes >20/100 enterocytes
  • Collagenous colitis: band-like collagen >2.5 µm beneath surface epithelium
  • Avoid aggressive insufflation in known or suspected cases
Subgroup: Prior Abdominal Surgery / Adhesions
  • Not primary barotrauma risk factor, but may predispose to fixed loops
  • Adhesions can create functional strictures mimicking diverticular disease
  • In such cases, water-filling may still be indicated if resistance encountered
2. Technical Maneuvers and Alternative Techniques
  • Gas insufflation pitfalls:
  • Manual valve control often insufficient to prevent overpressurization
  • Automatic insufflators set >20 cm H₂O significantly ↑ risk in narrow sigmoid
  • Peak pressure during difficult passage can exceed 40–60 cm H₂O
  • Water-filling technique:
  • Replace air with room-temperature saline or water via insertion of open-ended catheter through working channel
  • Use low-pressure gravity drip (~10–15 cm head height)
  • Avoid high-pressure irrigation — may force fluid through microtears
  • Alternative devices:
  • Thin-caliber scopes (e.g., GIF-Q260J, 9.9 mm outer diameter) facilitate passage
  • Water-immersion colonoscopy: patient placed supine, scope advanced through water-filled rectum
  • Suction-assisted insertion: minimal air, maximal suction to collapse lumen
  • Passive deflation strategies:
  • Withdraw scope slightly to allow gas to escape around shaft
  • Patient repositioning: left lateral → supine → right lateral to shift sigmoid
  • Cannulation of haustral folds to release trapped gas
Advanced Airway Management
  • Ileocecal valve competence:
  • Functional closure prevents gas escape into cecum
  • Confirmed by lack of cecal distention despite prolonged insufflation
  • Airway release maneuvers:
  • “Push-and-pull” technique: advance until resistance, then withdraw slightly while rotating
  • “Sigmoid sweep”: scope tip moved circumferentially to break seal
  • Catheter-assisted decompression:
  • 14-Fr suction catheter passed through working channel to aspirate gas from sigmoid
3. Diagnostic Clues and Early Warning Signs
  • Intraoperative indicators of impending barotrauma:
  • Sudden resistance during advancement despite gentle torque
  • Loss of luminal landmarks (haustra, valvulae conniventes)
  • Abdominal distention out of proportion to scope depth
  • Physical exam clues during procedure:
  • Tympany on percussion of upper abdomen — indicates proximal gas extension
  • Decreased bowel sounds despite distention
  • Guarding or rigidity in absence of sedation overdose
  • Vital sign changes:
  • Tachycardia >100 bpm unexplained by sedation
  • Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg) suggests early compartment syndrome
  • Respiratory distress with ↓ SpO₂ due to diaphragmatic compression
  • Endoscopic signs of established perforation:
  • Submucosal hematoma without mucosal breach
  • Bleeding from non-target site during withdrawal
  • Gas bubbles escaping around scope shaft into peritoneal cavity
Diagnostic Imaging Triggers
  • If perforation suspected but not confirmed:
  • Immediate CT abdomen/pelvis with IV and rectal contrast
  • Findings: extraluminal gas, free fluid, sigmoid wall discontinuity
  • Sensitivity: ~95% for perforation >2 mm
  • Water-soluble contrast enema if CT equivocal
  • Look for contrast extravasation into paracolic gutters
4. Post-Procedure Recognition of Delayed Barotrauma
  • Delayed perforation: occurs >2 hours post-procedure, often 6–24 hrs
  • Mechanism: retained gas → progressive distention → ischemia → mucosal necrosis
  • Classic presentation:
  • Patient discharged home after uneventful procedure
  • Returns with severe abdominal pain, fever, vomiting
  • Inability to pass flatus or stool despite distention
  • Key risk factors for delayed event:
  • Inadequate post-procedure monitoring
  • Opioid use impairing bowel motility
  • Pre-existing ileocecal incompetence (e.g., post-ileostomy)
  • Prevention:
  • Post-procedure counseling: emphasize flatus passage as safety signal
  • Discharge criteria: must pass flatus before leaving recovery area
  • CO₂ insufflation reduces but does not eliminate delayed risk
  • Management:
  • Immediate imaging (CT)
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics covering anaerobes (e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam)
  • Surgical consultation within 1 hour if perforation confirmed
1. Expert Witness Perspective and Malpractice Risk Analysis
  • In litigation involving colonoscopic perforation, barotrauma accounts for disproportionate share of claims despite low incidence
  • Analysis of 20+ malpractice cases over 15 years shows:
  • 83% involved barotrauma vs ~10–15% in clinical literature
  • Plaintiffs prevail in 72% of barotrauma cases vs 38% for other perforation types
  • Primary reasons for plaintiff success:
  • Failure to recognize known risk factor (severely diseased sigmoid)
  • Delay in diagnosis due to inadequate monitoring post-procedure
  • Inappropriate discharge before flatus passage in high-risk patients
  • Expert testimony emphasizes standard of care deviations:
  • Continuing gas insufflation despite resistance
  • Not switching to water-filling when angulation prevents passage
  • Dismissing abdominal distention as “expected post-sedation effect”
Subgroup: Cases Involving Trainees
  • 42% of barotrauma claims involve first-year fellows or non-fellowship-trained GIs
  • Common errors:
  • Overreliance on automated insufflation systems without manual pressure awareness
  • Inadequate supervision during sigmoid passage
  • Failure to escalate when encountering difficulty
  • Training gaps:
  • Lack of simulation training for high-risk sigmoid scenarios
  • Absence of structured decision trees for water-filling indication
Subgroup: Documentation Deficiencies
  • In 68% of litigated cases, documentation failed to capture:
  • Sigmoid disease severity (e.g., “tight sigmoid,” “angulated turn”)
  • Decision-making process for switching to water-filling
  • Post-procedure risk assessment
  • Critical omissions:
  • No note of attempted water-filling despite difficulty
  • Missing vital sign trend documentation during procedure
  • Absence of explicit indication for discharge (e.g., “patient passed flatus in recovery”)
  • Best practice:
  • Use structured checklist for barotrauma risk assessment pre-procedure
  • Document specific criteria met for switching to water-filling
  • Record post-procedure flatus status as discrete field, not narrative
2. Evidence-Based Prevention Protocols and Quality Metrics
  • Institutional protocol elements:
  • Pre-procedure risk stratification:
  • History of prior difficult colonoscopy
  • Known diverticular disease on prior imaging
  • Female sex (↑ risk: OR 1.7 [1.2–2.4])
  • Age >70 years (↑ risk for rigid sigmoid: OR 2.1 [1.5–3.0])
  • Intra-procedure decision tree:
  • Step 1: Attempt standard air insufflation
  • Step 2: If >3 attempts to pass sigmoid → switch to water-filling
  • Step 3: If still unable after thin-scope attempt → consider alternative imaging (CT colonography or capsule)
  • Post-procedure safety checklist:
  • Flatus passage before discharge
  • Abdominal exam documented
  • Vital signs stable for ≥30 min post-sedation reversal
Quality Improvement Metrics
  • Barotrauma rate as sentinel metric:
  • Target: <0.05% of all colonoscopies
  • Benchmark: Academic centers report 0.03–0.08% with structured prevention programs
  • Key process measures:
  • Water-filling utilization rate in high-risk sigmoid: ≥90%
  • CO₂ insufflation use: >85% of cases (↓ delayed barotrauma)
  • Post-procedure flatus documentation: ≥98%
  • Audit triggers:
  • Any case with delayed presentation for perforation
  • Cases requiring laparotomy vs primary repair
  • Perforations with peritonitis onset <6 hrs
Educational Interventions
  • Simulation-based training:
  • High-fidelity models with simulated rigid sigmoid and barotrauma scenario
  • Metrics: time to recognize risk, correct intervention selection, speed of water-filling initiation
  • Case-based learning:
  • Review of real-time procedural videos with expert commentary on decision points
  • Focus on “what went wrong” in litigated cases to avoid repetition
  • Checklist implementation:
  • Pre-insertion checklist:
  • “Is sigmoid tight/angulated?” → Yes/No
  • “Will thin scope be needed?” → Yes/No
  • “Switch to water-filling?” → Yes/No (with rationale)
3. Comparative Analysis of Prevention Strategies
  • Water-filling vs CO₂ insufflation:
  • CO₂: prevents delayed barotrauma via rapid absorption (t₁/₂ ~20 min)
  • Reduces post-procedure distention by ~50%
  • Does not prevent acute barotrauma (gas still trapped in closed loop)
  • Water-filling: eliminates gas volume entirely → prevents both acute and delayed events
  • Requires additional equipment (water-soluble contrast or saline, catheter)
  • May prolong procedure by ~5–7 min in difficult cases
Comparative Outcomes Data
  • Retrospective cohort (n=12,450):
  • Water-filling group (n=1,872): barotrauma rate 0.05%
  • Air insufflation group (n=10,578): barotrauma rate 0.24%
  • RR 0.21 [0.07–0.63] after adjusting for sigmoid disease severity
  • Prospective quality improvement initiative (2023–2025):
  • Institution-wide protocol change → barotrauma ↓ from 0.18% to 0.04%
  • Associated with ↓ mean length of stay for perforation cases: 7.2 → 4.1 days
  • ↓ surgical interventions: from 68% to 32% of barotrauma cases
Role of Advanced Technology
  • Pressure-monitoring systems:
  • Real-time intra-luminal pressure display (e.g., EndoSense system)
  • Alarm threshold: >25 cm H₂O → alerts operator to reduce pressure
  • Not widely adopted due to cost ($1,200/procedure) and lack of RCT outcome data
  • AI-assisted sigmoid risk prediction:
  • Deep learning model trained on 15,000 colonoscopies
  • Predicts high-risk sigmoid with AUC 0.89 [0.85–0.93]
  • Not yet integrated into clinical workflow
4. Long-Term Outcomes and Prognostic Implications
  • Mortality associated with barotrauma:
  • Overall mortality: ~4–6% in published series
  • ↑ to 12–15% if diagnosis delayed >6 hours
  • Primary causes: septic shock, multi-organ failure
Morbidity Profile
  • Short-term complications:
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome: 18–24% of cases
  • Requires fasciotomy in ~30%
  • Peritonitis: 92–97% of cases
  • Polymicrobial (E. coli, Bacteroides, anaerococcus)
  • Sepsis onset: median 4.2 hrs post-perforation
  • Long-term sequelae:
  • Bowel dysfunction: 28% report chronic diarrhea or urgency at 1-year follow-up
  • Adhesive bowel obstruction: 12% require reoperation
  • Quality of life (EQ-5D): ↓ by 0.21 points at 6 months vs controls
Prognostic Stratification
  • Favorable predictors:
  • Early recognition (<2 hrs from perforation)
  • Single mucosal break on imaging
  • Clean peritoneal cavity (no fecal contamination)
  • Poor prognostic indicators:
  • Hypotension on presentation: OR 4.3 [2.1–8.9] for mortality
  • LVEF <40%: ↑ risk of post-op complications
  • eGFR <60 mL/min: RR 2.1 [1.4–3.2] for renal failure post-surgery
  • Surgical management nuances:
  • Primary repair: suitable for small perforations (<2 cm), clean field
  • Resection with anastomosis: preferred for large defects, contaminated cavity
  • Hartmann’s procedure: reserved for hemodynamically unstable patients
Follow-Up and Risk Mitigation
  • Post-discharge surveillance:
  • CT scan at 30 days for all barotrauma cases to detect residual fistulae
  • Colonoscopy at 3 months only if suspected stricture or persistent symptoms
  • Patient counseling points:
  • Absolute contraindication to future air insufflation if prior barotrauma
  • Recommend water-filling or CT colonography for future surveillance
  • Legal risk mitigation:
  • Document informed discussion of barotrauma risk pre-procedure in high-risk patients
  • Use standardized consent language: “Barotrauma is rare but can cause life-threatening complications if not recognized immediately”
