Suddenly Steakless: A Gastroenterologist's Guide to Managing Alpha-Gal Allergy
  • Article Info:
  • Authors: McGill, Sarah K. MD, MSc, FACG; Richards, Robert D. Jr MD; Commins, Scott P. MD, PhD
  • Journal: The American Journal of Gastroenterology
  • Publication Date: June 2022
  • DOI: 10.14309/ajg.0000000000001765
  • Alpha-gal allergy is an emerging clinical syndrome that can cause GI distress, hives, angioedema, and anaphylaxis hours after ingesting mammalian meat/products.
  • Gastroenterologists need to know how to diagnose and manage alpha-gal allergy.

Case 1
  • 58-year-old woman presented w/ 2 months of abdominal bloating, diarrhea, mucous in stools, & urgency.
  • No rash, breathing difficulty, or dizziness.
  • Stool studies normal; Serum alpha-gal IgE was 1.27 kU/L (normal <0.35 kU/L).
Intervention & Outcome
  • Instructed to stop eating mammalian meat & dairy.
  • Symptoms improved, colonoscopy canceled.
  • At 2-year follow-up: daily formed bowel movements w/o GI distress.
  • Avoided mammalian meat, ate small amounts of dairy.
  • Inhaled bacon fumes → urgency to defecate & mucous in stools (lasted <1 day).
Alpha-Gal Allergy: The Basics
  • Reaction ← hypersensitivity to mammalian sugar galactose-alpha-1,3-galactose (alpha-gal).
  • Affected individuals typically have ↑ IgE antibodies to alpha-gal in serum.
  • Research evolving since 2009; info based on current knowledge.
Etiology & Risk Factors
  • In US, bites from lone star tick (Amblyomma americanum) implicated as cause of IgE sensitization to alpha-gal.
  • People in Southeastern & Midwestern US most at risk, esp. those who spend time outdoors.
  • Other tick species linked to alpha-gal allergy outside US.
Geographic Distribution
  • Eastern US most frequently reported, extends from eastern Canada to Texas to Michigan.
  • Identified in Australia, Europe, Asia, & South Africa.
  • Climate change may lead to further range expansion & longer active periods of ticks.
Common Symptoms
  • GI symptoms: diarrhea, abdominal pain, nausea, & vomiting.
  • Skin symptom: urticaria.
  • Delayed reaction: patients may awake w/ symptoms late at night & not relate symptoms to meat ingestion.
Diagnosis
  • Diagnosis: + test for alpha-gal IgE & improvement of symptoms on appropriate avoidance diet.
  • Alpha-gal IgE: inexpensive serum-based test.
  • GI symptoms (abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) are nonspecific & overlap w/ other conditions.
Testing Recommendations
  • Reasonable to test for alpha-gal in patients w/ unexplained GI distress & no red flag symptoms.
  • Especially in those who live in/have traveled to areas where lone star tick is prevalent.
  • Patients w/ history of allergic rhinitis, eczema, & other allergic conditions may be at ↑ risk.
Limitations of IgE Testing
  • IgE antibodies are not a perfect diagnostic test.
  • Patients w/ alpha-gal IgE elevations may not have clinical allergy symptoms.
  • Patients w/ confirmed alpha-gal allergy on food challenge have tested negative for IgE antibodies.
Management: Meals, Mites, and Meds
  • Cornerstones: meals (avoiding alpha-gal in diet), mites (avoiding tick bites), & meds (for some patients).
Meals
  • Avoid: pork, beef, lamb, venison, other mammalian meats, organ meats, & derived fats (lard, tallow).
  • Safe to eat: fish, seafood, chicken, & other fowl.
  • If symptoms persist: exclude dairy (cow's milk, cheese, butter, sour cream, ice cream).
Further Dietary Restrictions
  • If symptoms still continue: avoid gelatin, food additives, & medications that contain a gelatin coating or are sourced from mammals.
  • Recommended order of alpha-gal elimination from diet: (i) mammalian meat w/ or w/o dairy; (ii) dairy; (iii) gelatin, gelatin capsules, & mammalian food additives.
Mites
  • Counsel patients on avoiding tick bites.
  • Wear boots, light pants, & tick repellant in the woods.
  • Further bites can worsen the allergy, which may wane over time.
Prevention Tip
  • Ticks typically access humans @ ankles.
  • Wearing a tight mesh stocking as a legging covering from boot laces to above pants cuff is very effective.
Meds and Medical Devices
  • Dietary alpha-gal exposure: long-acting antihistamine (fexofenadine) may ameliorate symptoms.
  • Systemic symptoms (urticaria, hives, angioedema): prescribe epinephrine autoinjector.
  • Not routinely prescribed in patients w/ GI-isolated symptoms.
Anaphylaxis Risk
  • In cohorts w/ GI-isolated symptoms, anaphylaxis on follow-up was rare.
  • Only occurred in 1 patient w/ alpha-gal IgE level >100 kU/L.
Alpha-Gal in Meds/Devices
  • Some meds & medical devices contain alpha-gal: pancreatic enzymes (derived from pork), porcine patches, & cardiac valves.
  • Alpha-gal allergic patients w/ porcine bioprostheses may experience early failure of devices.
  • Pigs engineered to not express alpha-gal have been approved by FDA for human consumption & medical use.
Follow-up
  • GI-isolated alpha-gal: patients may eventually tolerate eating mammalian products again.
  • Follow alpha-gal IgE levels until they normalize (months to years).
  • Then, patients may challenge themselves w/ small servings of dairy products.
Challenge Recommendations
  • If dairy tolerated, a small serving of lean meat is a reasonable next step.
  • Systemic allergy symptoms: significant risk of anaphylaxis w/ mammalian product consumption.
  • Challenge should only be performed in a monitored clinical setting w/ allergist assistance.
GI-Isolated Alpha-Gal Allergy: Previous Studies
  • Food challenges: most rigorous way to prove food allergy.
  • Combined cohort (South Africa & US): 40.7% (37/91) had isolated GI symptoms.
  • Elevated IgE antibodies to alpha-gal among 16 GI clinic patients in North Carolina & 122 patients in Virginia.
Study Findings
  • Most common GI symptoms: abdominal pain, diarrhea, nausea, & vomiting.
  • Small minority exhibited rash or other systemic symptoms.
  • Majority of patients followed up after starting alpha-gal avoidant diet endorsed improvement in symptoms.
Pathophysiology
  • All mammals except Old World primates carry alpha-gal carbohydrate antigen.
  • Tick injects alpha-gal & other haptens in saliva during blood feeding → IgE antibody formation & sensitization.
  • Human GI tract is rich in mast cells.
Mechanism of Reaction
  • Sensitized human eats mammalian meat → alpha-gal absorbed & presented to alpha-gal-specific IgE on mast cells → mast cell degranulation.
  • Mast cells release histamine & other products → pain, vasodilation, inflammation, ↑ mucous secretion, & peristalsis.
Alpha-Gal: The Strangest Food Allergy
  • Diagnostic challenge: doesn't fit typical pattern of food allergy.
  • IgE-mediated reactions are immediate; alpha-gal reaction is typically delayed by a few hours.
  • Most allergies cause reactions at each exposure; alpha-gal: quantity & type of mammalian product consumed are important; alcohol & exercise seem to potentiate the reaction.
Key Differences
  • Most food allergies are due to proteins, develop as infants, & wane over time.
  • Alpha-gal is a carbohydrate & has been described mostly in adults, although children also develop the allergy.
The Alpha-Gal Allergy Discovery Story
  • Alpha-gal sugar known to be problematic b/c it is the major barrier to xenotransplantation.
  • All humans carry IgM, IgG, & IgA to alpha-gal.
  • Pork kidney transplanted into human → almost immediate rejection by immunologic attack mediated by non-IgE alpha-gal antibodies.
Cetuximab Anaphylaxis
  • 2008: oncologists noted 1 in 5 patients receiving cetuximab developed anaphylaxis (rare in clinical trials).
  • Patients who anaphylaxed had alpha-gal IgE; cetuximab is a chimeric antibody heavily glycosylated w/ murine alpha-gal.
  • Researchers in Virginia & Australia noted patients w/ delayed anaphylaxis to mammalian meat & history of tick bites. Both observations were due to alpha-gal allergy.
Conclusion
  • Alpha-gal allergy is an emerging clinical syndrome.
  • Gastroenterologists, particularly those practicing in the Southeast, Midwest, & mid-Atlantic regions, along w/ those in Europe, Asia, & Africa, may see patients who have GI symptoms from this allergy to mammalian products.
  • Need more research to better understand the epidemiology, pathophysiology, & outcomes of patients w/ GI-isolated alpha-gal allergy.
