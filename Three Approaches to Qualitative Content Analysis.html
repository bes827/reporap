Qualitative Content Analysis: Overview
  • Widely used qualitative research technique in health studies.
  • Flexible method for analyzing text data.
  • Focuses on language characteristics as communication w/ attention to content/contextual meaning.
Definition & Purpose
  • Research method for subjective interpretation of text data.
  • Achieved via systematic classification: coding + identifying themes/patterns.
  • Goal: Provide knowledge + understanding of phenomenon under study.
Data Sources
  • Text data: verbal, print, electronic forms.
  • Obtained from: narrative responses, open-ended survey questions, interviews, focus groups, observations, print media (articles, books, manuals).
Beyond Word Counts
  • Goes beyond merely counting words.
  • Examines language intensely to classify large text amounts into efficient categories.
  • Categories represent: explicit communication / inferred communication.
Historical Context
  • Long history in research; dates back to 18th century (Scandinavia).
  • Early 20th century (US): used as qualitative / quantitative method.
  • Later: primarily quantitative (text coded into explicit categories, described statistically).
  • More recently: recognized for qualitative analysis in health research → ↑ application + popularity.
Comparison to Other Qualitative Methods
  • One of numerous methods for text data analysis.
  • Other methods: ethnography, grounded theory (GTM), phenomenology, historical research.
  • Shares similar initial analytical approach w/ GTM/phenomenology.
  • Differs: GTM/phenomenology go beyond to develop theory / nuanced understanding of lived experience.
End-of-Life (EOL) Research Example
  • Hypothetical studies drawn from EOL research illustrate applications.
  • EOL care: area of ↑ emphasis.
  • Included as one of 5 research themes by NINR (2003).
Three Approaches to Qualitative Content Analysis
  • Current applications show 3 distinct approaches: conventional, directed, summative.
  • All interpret meaning from text data; adhere to naturalistic paradigm.
  • Major differences: coding schemes, origins of codes, threats to trustworthiness.
Conventional Content Analysis
  • Aim: Describe a phenomenon.
  • Appropriate when existing theory/research literature is limited.
  • Avoids preconceived categories; categories + names flow directly from data.
Study Design & Data Collection
  • Researcher X example: Emotional reactions of hospice patients.
  • Research question: "What are the emotional reactions of terminally ill patients who are receiving hospice care?"
  • Data collection: Individual interviews w/ open-ended questions (e.g., "What has it been like to be in hospice care?").
  • Probes: Open-ended / specific to participant's comments (e.g., "Can you tell me more about that?").
Data Analysis Process
  • 1. Read all data repeatedly: achieve immersion, obtain sense of whole.
  • 2. Read word by word: derive codes.
  • 3. Highlight exact words capturing key thoughts/concepts.
  • 4. Make notes of first impressions, thoughts, initial analysis.
  • 5. Labels for codes emerge (often directly from text) → initial coding scheme.
  • 6. Sort codes into categories: based on relationships/links.
  • 7. Emergent categories organize/group codes into meaningful clusters.
Category Refinement
  • Ideal # clusters: 10-15 (broad enough for many codes).
  • Combine/organize subcategories into fewer categories based on relationships.
  • Develop tree diagram: organize categories into hierarchical structure.
  • Develop definitions for each category, subcategory, code.
  • Identify exemplars for each code/category from data.
  • Further identify relationships between categories/subcategories: concurrence, antecedents, consequences.
Advantages
  • Gains direct information from participants.
  • Avoids imposing preconceived categories/theoretical perspectives.
  • Knowledge generated: based on participants' unique perspectives, grounded in actual data.
  • Sampling (e.g., stratified): maximizes diversity of reactions.
  • Analysis: structured to capture complexity.
Challenges & Limitations
  • Failing to develop complete understanding of context → failure to identify key categories.
  • Results in findings that do not accurately represent data.
  • Limited in theory development + description of lived experience.
  • Sampling/analysis procedures: make theoretical relationship between concepts difficult to infer.
  • Result: at most, concept development / model building.
Trustworthiness: Credibility
  • Credibility (naturalistic paradigm) ↔ internal validity (reliability/validity paradigm).
  • Established via: peer debriefing, prolonged engagement, persistent observation, triangulation, negative case analysis, referential adequacy, member checks.
Confusion with Other Methods
  • Easily confused w/ grounded theory (GTM) or phenomenology.
  • These methods share initial analytical approach but go beyond content analysis.
  • GTM/phenomenology aim: develop theory / nuanced understanding of lived experience.
Directed Content Analysis
  • Used when existing theory/prior research is incomplete or needs further description.
  • Goal: Validate or extend conceptually a theoretical framework/theory.
  • Existing theory/research: helps focus research question, provides predictions for variables/relationships.
Study Design & Data Collection
  • Researcher Y example: How well Kübler-Ross's (1969) theory describes emotional passages of terminally ill patients.
  • Research question: "How well does Kübler-Ross’s model describe the emotional passages or journeys of patients who have been diagnosed with a terminal illness?"
  • Initial framework: Kübler-Ross's five stages of grief (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance).
  • Data collection: Interviews w/ open-ended questions (e.g., "What has your emotional journey been since being diagnosed with this illness?").
  • Probes: Targeted questions about predetermined categories (e.g., "Have you felt angry since your diagnosis?").
Data Analysis Process
  • More structured process than conventional approach.
  • 1. Identify key concepts/variables as initial coding categories from existing theory/research.
  • 2. Determine operational definitions for each category using the theory.
  • 3. Coding strategies:
  • - Strategy A: Read transcript, highlight all text appearing to represent phenomenon (e.g., emotional reaction). Then, code highlighted passages using predetermined codes. New codes for uncategorized text.
  • - Strategy B: Begin coding immediately with predetermined codes. Identify/analyze uncoded data later for new categories/subcategories.
  • Choice of strategy depends on data + researcher goals.
Category Refinement & Reporting
  • Identify subcategories if needed (e.g., anger toward self, doctors, spiritual being).
  • Reexamine data not coded into initial categories to describe different emotional reactions.
  • Findings: offer supporting/nonsupporting evidence for theory.
  • Presentation: codes w/ exemplars, descriptive evidence.
  • Use rank order comparisons of frequency of codes (e.g., incidence of Kübler-Ross categories vs. newly identified reactions).
  • Report percent of supporting vs. nonsupporting codes per participant/total sample.
Advantages
  • Existing theory can be supported + extended.
  • Explicitly acknowledges researchers are not working from a naive perspective.
  • Efficiently extends or refines existing theory.
Challenges & Limitations
  • Researchers approach data w/ informed but strong bias → more likely to find supportive evidence.
  • Participants might give desired answers to please researchers (e.g., agree w/ suggested emotional stages).
  • Overemphasis on theory can blind researchers to contextual aspects (e.g., cross-sectional design overemphasizing current reactions).
Trustworthiness: Confirmability
  • Related to neutrality / confirmability (naturalistic paradigm) ↔ objectivity.
  • Achieved via: audit trail + audit process.
  • Example: Auditor review of operational definitions before study ↑ accuracy of predetermined categories.
Summative Content Analysis
  • Starts w/ identifying + quantifying certain words/content in text.
  • Purpose: Understand contextual use of words/content.
  • Quantification: explores usage, not to infer meaning initially.
Manifest vs. Latent Content Analysis
  • Manifest content analysis: analyzing for appearance of particular word/content (quantitative, focuses on frequency).
  • Summative approach goes beyond word counts to include latent content analysis.
  • Latent content analysis: interpretation of content, discovering underlying meanings of words/content.
Study Design & Data Collection
  • Researcher Z example: How terms "die, dying, death" vs. euphemisms are used in clinician-patient communication about hospice care.
  • Research question: "How are the terms die, dying, and death used in clinician-patient communication when discussing hospice care, and what alternative terms are used?"
  • Sampling: Maximize diversity around clinician/patient/family demographics (gender, age, diagnosis, ethnic background, discipline, specialization).
  • Communication events sampled: Discharge teaching for hospitalized patients transferring to EOL care; clinician-patient/family conferences for EOL care planning.
  • Sample size: 50 separate communication events (50 clinicians + patient/family pairs).
  • Data collection: Audiotape-recorded + verbatim transcribed conversations.
Data Analysis Process
  • 1. Computer-assisted searches for occurrences of identified words (e.g., "die," "death," "dying").
  • 2. Calculate word frequency counts for each term; identify source/speaker (e.g., physician, nurse, patient, family member).
  • 3. Identify alternative/euphemistic terms; count occurrences.
  • 4. Compare frequencies of euphemisms vs. direct terms by speaker type, clinician/patient demographics, communication event, total sample.
  • Counting: identifies patterns in data, contextualizes codes.
  • Explores word usage / discovers range of meanings in normal use.
Advantages
  • Unobtrusive + nonreactive way to study phenomenon.
  • Provides basic insights into how words are actually used.
Challenges & Limitations
  • Findings limited by inattention to broader meanings in data.
Trustworthiness: Credibility
  • Relies on credibility (internal consistency).
  • Mechanism: show textual evidence consistent w/ interpretation.
  • Example: Validation by content experts on replacement terms for "death" terms.
  • Alternatively: member check w/ participants for intended meaning.
Key Aspects & Conclusion
  • All qualitative content analysis approaches require 7 classic steps.
  • Success depends greatly on coding process.
  • Important for health researchers to delineate specific approach before data analysis.
Seven Classic Steps
  • 1. Formulating research questions.
  • 2. Selecting sample to be analyzed.
  • 3. Defining categories to be applied.
  • 4. Outlining coding process + coder training.
  • 5. Implementing coding process.
  • 6. Determining trustworthiness.
  • 7. Analyzing results of coding process.
The Coding Process
  • Organizes large quantities of text into fewer content categories.
  • Categories: patterns/themes directly expressed or derived through analysis.
  • Identifies relationships among categories.
Coding Scheme Development
  • Researchers create/develop coding scheme to guide coders.
  • Scheme includes: process + rules of data analysis (systematic, logical, scientific).
  • Good coding scheme: central to trustworthiness.
Major Coding Differences
  • Key differences center on how initial codes are developed.
  • Conventional: Study starts w/ observation; codes defined during data analysis; codes derived from data.
  • Directed: Study starts w/ theory; codes defined before + during data analysis; codes derived from theory/relevant research findings.
  • Summative: Study starts w/ keywords; keywords identified before + during data analysis; keywords derived from researcher interest/literature review.
Recommendations for Health Researchers
  • Match specific research purpose + state of science to appropriate analysis technique.
  • Delineate specific approach to content analysis before data analysis.
  • Creating + adhering to analytic procedure/coding scheme ↑ trustworthiness/validity.
  • Careful description of approach: provides universal language, strengthens method's scientific base.
  • Content analysis: flexible, pragmatic method for developing/extending knowledge of human experience of health/illness.
