Qualitative Research Questions: A Framework for Physicians
  • This document outlines the principles and types of qualitative research questions.
  • It aims to guide physicians in formulating questions that explore patient experiences and clinical practices.
  • Focuses on understanding participant perspectives and uncovering new insights.
  • Article info: Not provided in the source text.

Nature of Qualitative Research Questions
  • Focus: Discover interviewee's own framework of meanings.
  • Goal: Describe participant experiences in their own language; explore details → uncover new ideas.
  • Avoid: Imposing researcher’s structures/assumptions.
Distinction from Quantitative Research
  • Address different types of questions.
  • Qualitative questions are generally open-ended; quantitative are often hypothesis-driven.
Types of Qualitative Research Questions
  • Categorized by aim & specific phenomena investigated.
  • Draws on various research approaches.
1. Exploring Experiences, Perceptions, Views
  • Common in qualitative descriptive studies.
  • Aligns w/ gauging clinicians' perspectives.
  • Focus: How individuals interpret/react to intervention/practice.
Understanding Experiences & Perceptions
  • Aim: Explore how people experience a phenomenon & their views.
  • Example: What were hospital clinicians’ experiences & perceptions of the Serious Illness Care Program (SICP)?
Describing Phenomena w/ Limited Theory (Conventional Content Analysis)
  • Approach: Inductively describe phenomenon.
  • Categories emerge directly from text data.
  • Example: What are the emotional reactions of terminally ill patients receiving hospice care?
Examining Concerns/Views of Professional Group
  • Focus: Specific professional group's perspectives.
  • Example: Examining concerns of single-handed general practitioners.
  • Example: Investigating hospital consultants' views of their patients.
Describing General Practice & Decision-Making
  • Focus: Clinicians' accounts of practice & decisions.
  • Example: Exploring general practitioners' accounts of uncomfortable prescribing decisions.
2. Focused on Specific Components of Experience (Patton's Types)
  • Formulated around 6 specific areas.
  • Elicit detailed frameworks of meaning during interviews.
Behavior/Experience Questions
  • Inquire about actions, events, or situations encountered.
Opinion/Belief Questions
  • Explore what participants think or believe about a topic.
Feelings Questions
  • Probe emotional responses & states.
Knowledge Questions
  • Assess what participants know about a subject.
Sensory Experience Questions
  • Focus on what participants saw, heard, touched, tasted, or smelled.
Background/Demographic Details Questions
  • Gather contextual information about participants.
3. Validating/Extending Theory (Directed Approach)
  • Used when existing theory/research is incomplete.
  • Goal: Validate or extend a theoretical framework/theory conceptually.
  • Research question structured based on existing framework.
Testing Existing Theoretical Models
  • Aim: Test how well an existing model applies to a new context.
  • Example: How well does Kübler-Ross’s model describe emotional journeys of patients diagnosed w/ terminal illness?
4. Exploring Contextual Meaning & Language Use (Summative Approach)
  • Focus: Identify & quantify specific words/content.
  • Followed by: Interpretation of underlying context (latent content analysis).
Investigating Contextual Usage & Underlying Meaning
  • Aim: Understand how specific terms are used & their deeper meaning.
  • Example: How are 'die,' 'dying,' 'death' used in clinician-patient communication re: hospice care, & what alternative terms are used?
