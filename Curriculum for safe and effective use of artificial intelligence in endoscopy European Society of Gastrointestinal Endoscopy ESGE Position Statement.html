Introduction & Key Recommendations
  • - The European Society of Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ESGE) has identified a critical need for structured training for the safe and effective use of artificial intelligence (AI) in endoscopy.
Key Recommendations
  • - Ensure basic competency in standard endoscopy procedures.
  • - Acquire foundational knowledge of AI principles.
  • - Implement educational programs to enhance AI literacy.
  • - Recognize and mitigate cognitive biases in human–AI interaction.
  • - Avoid over-reliance on AI in clinical decision-making.
  • - Continuously monitor key performance indicators (KPIs) throughout AI system integration.


Preadoption Phase
  • - This phase outlines the prerequisites for successfully integrating AI systems into clinical workflows.
STATEMENT 1: Basic Endoscopic Competency
  • - Statement: Clinicians using AI in endoscopy need to have basic competency in standard endoscopic procedures.
  • - Agreement: 93%.
Rationale
  • - Fundamental skills are necessary to use AI predictions effectively and maximize the benefits of AI tools.
  • - This includes both technical (scope handling) and cognitive (image interpretation) competencies.

Supporting Evidence
  • - AI improves detection/differentiation in colorectal polyps, early gastric cancer, and Barrett’s esophagus, but relies on operator proficiency in scope handling and lesion visualization.

Impact on Training
  • - AI has been shown to improve adenoma detection rates (ADRs) among trainees, provided they already possess basic endoscopy skills.
  • - An AI-based gastroscopy training system significantly enhanced the diagnostic proficiency of gastroenterology graduate students.


STATEMENTS 2 & 3: AI Literacy & Education
  • - Statement 2: Endoscopists require a foundational understanding of AI concepts to critically evaluate and effectively implement AI tools. (Agreement: 90%)
  • - Statement 3: Educational programs should be developed to enhance AI literacy with practical training using approved systems. (Agreement: 99%)
Core Components of AI Literacy
  • - Fundamentals of machine-learning algorithms.
  • - Role of data quality in AI development.
  • - Interpretation of model performance metrics.
  • - Recognition of system limitations and biases.
  • - Understanding of clinical implications.

Educational Approach
  • - Programs should be developed by gastroenterology societies in partnership with industry, computer scientists, and clinical educators.
  • - Training should balance basic AI literacy with hands-on training using approved systems, focusing on real-world application.

Key Learning Points for Clinicians
  • - Understand performance variability across different cases and products.
  • - Be aware of technical limitations.
  • - Recognize clinical scenarios where system reliability may be compromised.

Critical Unresolved Question
  • - When should AI be introduced into an endoscopist’s education?
  • - Options: At the very start of training OR after basic skills are acquired.
  • - Currently no recommendation due to limited evidence.



Training Phase
  • - This phase focuses on the complexities of human-AI interaction and the cognitive skills needed to use AI safely.
STATEMENT 4: Awareness of Cognitive Biases
  • - Statement: Endoscopists should be aware of the risk of potential cognitive biases, such as automation bias, algorithm aversion, and conservatism bias, which are detrimental to human–AI interaction.
  • - Agreement: 93%.
Automation Bias (Over-reliance)
  • - Definition: The tendency to over-rely on an AI system, assuming it will detect all pathology regardless of the endoscopist's performance.
  • - Risk: Can lead to reduced human detection and potential deskilling.
Mitigation Measures (See Table 2)
  • - Decrease prominence of false alarms.
  • - Reduce the endoscopist’s cognitive load.
  • - Provide thorough training on the specific AI platform.
  • - Address explainability and transparency.
  • - Design adaptive, user-friendly interfaces.


Anchoring Bias
  • - Definition: The tendency to be influenced by irrelevant factors when given uncertain external advice.
  • - Example: Overtreatment of normal mucosa that AI suggests is a 'polyp' due to an artifact like irregular light reflection.
Mitigation Measures (See Table 2)
  • - Provide enough time to overthink a decision and weigh external factors against one's own beliefs.


Algorithm Aversion (Under-reliance)
  • - Definition: The tendency to unconsciously disregard an algorithm's input after observing it make a mistake.
  • - Influencing Factors: Endoscopist's expertise, personal attitude to AI, and initial expectations.
Mitigation Measures (See Table 2)
  • - Familiarize endoscopists with AI.
  • - Create realistic expectations for each AI system.
  • - Use the correct system for the chosen task.
  • - Monitor AI performance in the real world.


Conservatism Bias
  • - Definition: The tendency to hold on to established beliefs, resisting new information that challenges them.
  • - Cause: The 'black box' phenomenon, or inability to understand how AI makes decisions.
Mitigation Measures (See Table 2)
  • - Address explainability and transparency.
  • - Reduce the 'black box' phenomenon.



STATEMENT 5: Avoiding Exclusive Reliance on AI
  • - Statement: Endoscopists should be trained not to exclusively rely on AI systems for clinical decision-making.
  • - Agreement: 97%.
AI's Role as an Assistant
  • - The current view is that AI operates as an assistant to the endoscopist, reassuring or elevating the confidence level for diagnosis.
  • - The final decision should never solely rely on AI.

Holistic Clinical Judgment
  • - Clinical decision-making involves more than diagnosis, including patient interaction, personal history, and overall clinical judgment.

Patient Acceptance
  • - A prospective study showed that more than 90% of patients accepted optical biopsy procedures using computer-aided diagnosis in colonoscopy.



Autonomous Implementation & Proficiency Assessment
  • - This phase addresses the ongoing monitoring required after AI systems are deployed in clinical practice.
STATEMENT 6: Continuous Performance Monitoring
  • - Statement: Key performance and quality indicators related to the intended use of the AI systems (e.g., adenoma detection rate) should be monitored before, during, and after their implementation.
  • - Agreement: 92%.
Evidence Discrepancy: RCTs vs. Real World
  • - RCTs: Have shown AI enhances endoscopist performance in highly controlled settings for applications like colonic polyp and upper GI neoplasia detection.
  • - Real-World Studies: A recent meta-analysis of non-randomized studies showed that computer-aided detection (CADe) in colonoscopies does not enhance the detection of colorectal neoplasia.
Implication
  • - This raises doubts about the generalizability of the positive findings from RCTs to routine clinical practice.


Potential Negative Consequences of AI
  • - The integration of AI could potentially lead to deskilling of endoscopists.
  • - Possible mechanisms include diverting concentration and altering technical skills or visual gaze patterns.

Implementation & Monitoring Strategy
  • - Consider de-implementation of AI systems if unwanted consequences are observed.
  • - A washout period following AI implementation might be needed to neutrally observe the influence of AI on the endoscopist's capability.



Conclusion
  • - ESGE has developed a comprehensive curriculum emphasizing:
  • - Foundational endoscopic skills and AI literacy.
  • - Awareness of cognitive biases to ensure appropriate human–AI interaction.
  • - Maintaining independent clinical judgment and not relying solely on AI.
  • - Ongoing monitoring of performance and quality indicators in clinical practice.
Future Research Direction
  • - Future research should prioritize prospective studies that assess various training approaches for using AI in endoscopy to develop an evidence-based educational strategy.
