Relationships during Litigation: Impact on Personal Connections During Medical Malpractice Suits
  • Source: Physician Litigation Stress Organization
  • Topic: Effects of Medical Malpractice Litigation on Personal and Professional Relationships
  • Author(s): Dr. Joseph Daley, Dr. Henry Clark, Dr. Tim Miller, Dr. Paul Young, Dr. Cynthia Davis, Dr. White, Dr. West (contributing physicians/quotes)
1. Impact on Marriages and Intimate Relationships
Decision to Share Lawsuit with Spouse
  • First major decision: whether to share being sued with spouse or closest intimates → sets stage for emotional life during long litigation passage
  • Choice function of relationship strength and personality style
  • Most choose what suits them → dramatically influences and alters lives
Effects on Marriages
  • Lawsuits markedly affect most intimate relationships, especially marriages
  • Spouses know dedication physicians feel and how hard they work to maintain competence
  • Spouses reinforce daily efforts to reassure themselves they are good physicians
Case Study: Dr. Henry Clark
  • First malpractice suit managed well → no symptoms, settled for minimum damages within one year
  • Second complaint filed 7 years later by fellow physician on behalf of birth-injured child → devastated
  • Wife told him his work was too preoccupying, not worth the cost → should give up obstetrical practice
  • Wife dismissed his distress, complained about lack of sexual interest and withdrawal from family activities
  • Result: felt alienated → confided in office nurse → separated within 8 months → affair led to marriage with nurse
2. Spousal Understanding and Support
How Spouses Understand
  • Only spouses know dedication physicians feel or how hard they work to maintain competence
  • Empathic spouses absorb irritability and share worrisome nighttime vigils
  • Maintain daytime calm in family lives, shield from unreasonable demands, manage social lives
  • Supportive spouses understand rather than resent time preempted from leisure and family activities by legal obligations
Spouses Reveal Dedication Through Active Participation
  • Dermatologist's wife became almost expert in malignant melanoma (husband's lawsuit clinical focus)
  • Searched literature, developed summaries for defense team, attended depositions
  • Filled courtroom with supportive family and friends at trial → acquitted
  • Message to jury: how highly esteemed husband was by colleagues and community
When Spouses Cannot Provide Understanding
  • After sharing distress, may be disappointed in spouse's lack of understanding and inability to offer support
  • Need for mutual understanding: physicians need spouses to understand preoccupation and unhappiness → spouses need physicians to appreciate their bewilderment and effort to hold together their world
  • Must work hard and patiently together to enlarge mutual understanding → protects and strengthens marriage during protracted litigation siege
3. Handling Litigation Alone: Silence and Isolation
Physicians Who Keep Issues to Themselves
  • Some physicians characteristically keep work issues to themselves → don't want to bother spouses
  • When sued, may be too ashamed or traumatized to share deep feelings about work with spouses (if never done before)
  • Habitually muffle suffering in silence → overwhelms ability to tell their side of story
Symptoms Manifesting from Suppressed Suffering
  • Falsely shrouded suffering manifests in array of behaviors and symptoms → easily misinterpreted by those close to them
  • Spouses cannot understand when physician spouses (usually in control and in command of feelings like Gary Cooper cowboy hero) become:
  • Increasingly preoccupied and irritable
  • Replace interest in sexual/romantic life with growling ruminations on sleepless nights
  • Seem to become different persons
  • Supplant interest in families with absorption about secret: being sued for malpractice
Isolation Without Spouse/Partner
  • No spouse or significant other (due to death, divorce, choice, circumstances) → isolated and alone without hope of obtaining needed support
  • May possess personality characteristics making entrusting needs to others awkward or difficult → even without particular stress
Case Study: Dr. Tim Miller
  • Sued for malpractice shortly after terminating 10-year relationship with male medical school classmate
  • Feelings of loss and isolation compounded by anger, guilt, distress about perceived patient injury
  • Able to attend patients but dreaded end of day → intense loneliness and sleep-disturbed nights
  • Became increasingly preoccupied and depressed → sought older respected colleague who suggested professional help
  • Personal physicians, colleagues, claims representatives, defense attorneys, risk managers → helpful resources
  • Support programs offered by malpractice insurance companies help discover support in personal networks
  • Short series of professional sessions enabled him to socialize with peers and share concerns with new friend
4. Communication Breakdown: Misunderstanding and Estrangement
Signs Spouses Cannot Miss
  • Backing away into emotional distance
  • Spending ever more time at work
  • Coming home as persons cut off seeking solitary rest
Escalation to Conflict
  • Such scenarios flare up easily into misunderstandings, accusations, bitter arguments
  • Wartime atmosphere can endure for months and sometimes years
  • Spouses may feel so shut out of intimacy → suspect involvement with another person
  • High possibilities of unnecessary pain and separation unless physicians reject passivity and fully incorporate spouses into partnership in suffering trauma of being accused of negligence
Case Study: Dr. Cynthia Davis (Medical Community Rupture)
  • Had good friend who repeatedly expressed concern about Davis's eye problem
  • Friend insisted Davis consult another friend (ophthalmologist) → later sued that ophthalmologist
  • Could not tell friend what happened → protected her from feeling guilty about referral
  • During jury trial, friend called repeatedly asking "What's going on?" → Davis told her nothing, resolved not to tell
  • Friend was withholding (knew but angry) → Davis was withholding (protecting)
  • After trial, friend said: "I know all about it, tell me, I'm very angry. A friend wouldn't do this; I can't trust you; we're done."
  • Very stressful → they met, partially resolved but remained tentative and tense for next year
  • Not back as close but restored as friends
Gift of Self-Observation
  • Most important gift: serious effort to observe feelings, needs, situation → take steps to relieve distress
  • Need to recognize how hard and patiently must work together to enlarge mutual understanding
5. Family Dynamics During Litigation
Emotional Spillover at Home
  • At work: apply selves to keep emotional balance → offer patients full attention and skills
  • At home: feeling of lowering guard results in being moody, irritable, short-fused with those closest to us
  • Behaviors may include:
  • Opting out of long-planned family events
  • Criticizing spouses for minor inconveniences
  • Yelling at children
  • Slamming door
  • Kicking dog
Destructive Coping Behaviors
  • Attempt to soothe discomfort with excessive drinking or television viewing
  • Isolating behaviors further complicate relationships
Self-Awareness and Behavioral Change
  • Best approach requires working at becoming aware of own reactions and monitoring effect on others
  • Self-knowledge gradually enables reduction of abrasive behaviors → constructive changes in responses to others
  • At minimum: can begin to talk calmly and openly about what experiencing → spouses and families can understand reaction that otherwise puzzles and frustrates them
Family Strengthening Potential
  • Being sued affects and changes family dynamics
  • Maturity in managing this event and family relationships may ultimately strengthen initially tested family ties
6. Impact on Children
Confiding in Adult Children
Benefits of Sharing with Adult Children
  • Older children, who may be physicians themselves → offer significant support to sued parents
  • Shielded as children from parental problems → may be parents themselves and understand adult burdens
  • If recognized and appreciated their growth → can be fortified by sharing distress about malpractice suit
Adult Children's Capacity for Understanding
  • Older children demonstrate amazing capacity for understanding and solicitude
  • Serve as confidants to one or both parents → able to mediate and alert about sometimes separate concerns
Case Example: Mediation by Adult Children
  • Grown daughter may tell father about mother's feeling that he does not appreciate how hard she works to manage life so he won't bear burden
  • Father informed by daughter may respond with positive tactics:
  • Weekend get-away with wife
  • Special dinner with close friends → conversation broadens everyone's perspective on current life stresses
  • Son can talk to father about latter's excessive ruminating and drinking alone → suggest positive alternatives:
  • Golf game together
  • Helping snap bad habits before they bring further sadness and disruption to family
Outcome of Involving Adult Children
  • Active support of older children deepens family relationships
  • Endows them with richness that, lacking crisis, they might not otherwise achieve
Explaining Malpractice to Younger Children
Age-Appropriate Explanations
  • Must inform in ways matching and not excessively taxing based on age or capacity to understand
  • May offer young children simplified versions: "Daddy or Mommy is having problems at work that may make them seem upset but they do not mean to be irritable and unhappy with the children and it is certainly not their children's fault"
  • Success depends on how well monitor feelings and behavior
  • When noticing overreaction (e.g., to child's untidy room) → explain reaction and apologize immediately
Media Attention Complications
  • Special problems arise when lawsuit receives media attention
  • Children need available explanation they can:
  • Understand easily
  • Tell their friends
  • Use if others taunt them with news reports about parent
Basic Explanation Framework
  • Part of what children need to learn: when people are sick, sometimes bad things may happen
  • Injured people can get money from insurance company to take care of problems only if they sue physician
  • This does not mean:
  • We are bad physicians
  • We deliberately hurt people
  • We are criminals
  • No chance of going to prison because of this
  • Episodes in family life may last for a while → may have to go to court before everything settled
  • Some version of this explanation usually allays their anxiety
Children's Sensitivity to Parental Feelings
  • Children have amazing capacity to sense parental feelings
  • More open and at same time protective of their feelings → healthier for all
7. Social and Professional Relationships
Pre-Litigation to Post-Litigation Continuity
  • Prelitigation manner of relating to extended family, medical colleagues, friends, acquaintances mirrored in postlitigation relationships
  • More intimate with extended family (parents, siblings, aunts, uncles) before being sued → more easily confide after suit filed
  • May share situation with close friends and trustworthy staff members OR continue usual style of confiding only in those closest
  • Even with best intentions → difficult to change behavior
Case Study: Dr. Paul Young - Extended Family
  • Describes family as "typical" midwestern clan
  • Men: hunt and fish together, talk politics, enjoy exchanging barbs and jokes
  • Women: talk about children, recipes, vacations without mentioning individual troubles
  • Learned his anesthesiologist cousin had been sued → felt impelled (given own experience) to schedule fishing trip
  • Fished most of weekend, talked constantly but never broached subject of either his own or cousin's lawsuit
  • Neither changed usual style of relating → aware talking about concerns might have been helpful
  • Nonetheless expressed satisfaction about sharing weekend "away"
Informing Colleagues and Coworkers
Colleague Awareness
  • Colleagues may suspect lawsuit after experiencing widely known catastrophic outcome but hesitate to mention subject
  • Sometimes helpful: senior physicians or department chairmen approach colleagues (with knowledge/suspicions) and offer to talk
  • Can be especially helpful if they have been through process themselves
Partners and Colleagues Support
  • Partners and other colleagues more helpful if they know about lawsuit
  • Can switch coverage → understand need for extra time to attend to litigation
  • Uninformed colleagues won't know request for time away is for trial, not vacation
  • Far better to be accepted with understanding and support than invite hard feelings from frequent unexplained absences
Involvement of the Medical Community
Colleague Involvement in Lawsuits
  • Sometimes sense one or more colleagues (especially well-meaning family friends of potential plaintiff) are involved in case
  • May have:
  • Made remarks to patient instrumental in plaintiff's filing of lawsuit
  • Serving as expert witness
  • Social relationship with one of litigants
  • No easy cure for disruption this introduces into previously harmonious relationships
Support from Colleagues as Expert Witnesses
  • On the other hand, support of colleagues who willingly serve as expert witnesses on principle is heartwarming
  • None could successfully defend case without testimony of colleagues willing to study case and place reputations on line to stand by them
Office Staff Relationships
Managing Staff Relationships
  • Must manage relationships with office staff in own way
  • When tell staff about situation → their knowledge of litigation stages and demands makes them supportive, solicitous, protective
Case Study: Dr. White
  • Still wonders what staff thought about noisy summons delivery
  • Now that case successfully dismissed → thinks about asking them what they thought of him during hectic years of legal involvement
Support from Religious Community
Church and Faith Community Resources
  • Relationships within churches or faith communities can give significant support
  • Can pray and share predicament with:
  • Priests, rabbis, or ministers
  • Bible study groups
  • Other members and leaders of religious communities
Confiding in Patients
Informing Patients About Lawsuits
  • Some physicians (especially obstetrician-gynecologists) tell patients about lawsuits, especially when highly publicized
  • Offer option to choose other physicians
  • Such informed patients almost overwhelmingly:
  • Appreciate knowing about lawsuit
  • Express confidence in physician
  • Respond with support
Case Study: Dr. West
  • Reflects about recent loss at highly publicized trial
  • "If I lose some patients, I'm too busy anyway"
  • Tried to be example for younger physicians → show them life goes on
  • "I'm sued, I'm humiliated, and I'm on the nightly news, the radio and the newspaper"
  • "But, guess what, I'm still earning a living, I still have my family, my patients are sending me flowers and writing me sweet notes"
  • Some left → doesn't know who they are
  • Partner got same treatment
  • "It didn't in the long run make any difference other than I think we're both a little embittered, I maybe more than she is"
  • "We like to practice medicine, even though you say to yourself, 'I'm not going to care about these people anymore,' but you still do"

9. Conclusion: Physician Agency and Action
Recognizing Personal Responsibility
  • Can we possibly do anything about this situation?
  • In fact, we are the only ones who can do something about this
Call to Action
  • Physicians must take active role in managing impact of litigation on personal relationships
  • Need to:
  • Recognize warning signs in own behavior and emotional state
  • Proactively communicate with spouse/family about lawsuit stress
  • Seek support from appropriate resources (colleagues, professionals, support programs)
  • Work actively to maintain family relationships during prolonged legal process
Resources Available
  • Free excerpt from "Adverse Events, Stress, and Litigation: A Physician's Guide" available for download
  • Malpractice insurance company support programs
  • Professional counseling services
  • Peer support from colleagues who have experienced litigation
Final Consideration
  • While litigation brings significant stress to personal relationships, physicians have agency in how they respond
  • Proactive engagement, honest communication, and seeking appropriate support can help preserve and even strengthen relationships during and after the legal process
  • The journey through litigation is long and difficult, but physicians are not without resources or ability to positively influence outcomes for themselves and their families
