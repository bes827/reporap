ACG Clinical Guidelines: Diagnosis and Management of Achalasia
  • Source: American College of Gastroenterology (2020)
  • Topic: Achalasia – esophageal motility disorder
1. Introduction & Scope
  • Defines achalasia: impaired LES relaxation + aperistalsis.
  • Typical presentation: dysphagia solids & liquids, regurgitation, chest pain, weight loss.
  • Highlights HRM identification of three subtypes (pressurization patterns).
  • Emphasizes endoscopic & barium signs (retained saliva, puckered EGJ, bird‑beak).
1.1 Guideline Structure
  • Recommendations ↔ GRADE quality/strength.
  • Key concepts: expert opinion + extrapolated evidence (non‑GRADE).
  • Evidence summaries provide definitions, data supporting each recommendation.
2. Methods & Grading Process
  • Systematic literature review; GRADE tables applied to each statement.
  • Two trained GRADE methodologists performed the assessment.
  • Evidence quality tiers: High, Moderate, ↓Low, ↓↓Very low*.
  • Recommendation strength: Strong (benefits ≫ harms) vs Conditional (uncertainty).
2.1 Quality Definitions
  • High: further research unlikely to change effect estimate.
  • Moderate: some confidence; new data may affect estimate.
  • Low: additional studies likely to impact estimate.
  • Very low: little confidence; true effect may differ substantially.
2.2 Recommendation Types
  • Strong: clear benefit‑risk advantage.
  • Conditional: balance of benefits/harms uncertain, patient preference matters.
3. Epidemiology & Diagnostic Framework
  • Global incidence ≈ 0.03–1.63 per 100 000 yr; prevalence ≈ 1.8–12.6 per 100 000 yr.
  • US burden: ~20 000–40 000 affected individuals.
  • No sex, racial predilection; peak age 30‑60 y.
3.1 Clinical Suspicion
  • Classic triad: progressive dysphagia (solids & liquids), regurgitation, weight loss.
  • Heartburn present in 27%–42% → frequent misdiagnosis as GERD → PPI trial often ineffective.
3.1.1 Recommendation 1
  • Strong (very low evidence): evaluate for achalasia if GERD therapy fails.
3.2 Pathophysiology Overview
  • Selective loss of inhibitory myenteric neurons (VIP, NO) → excitatory‑dominant LES tone.
  • Unopposed acetylcholine release → impaired LES relaxation & absent peristalsis.
4. Diagnostic Testing Algorithm
  • Core modalities: Endoscopy, Barium esophagram, High‑resolution manometry (HRM).
4.1 Endoscopic Findings
  • Retained saliva/food in esophagus.
  • Puckered gastroesophageal junction requiring increased force to traverse.
4.2 Barium Esophagram Signs
  • Dilated esophagus with “bird‑beak” tapering at LES.
  • Early disease may show retained barium above EGJ, mimicking reflux stricture.
4.3 High‑Resolution Manometry (Gold Standard)
  • HRM provides pressure topography; superior inter‑rater agreement vs conventional manometry.
  • Identifies three Chicago Classification subtypes:
Subtype Prevalence Key Manometric Feature
Type I 20%–40% Aperistalsis, ↓pan‑esophageal pressurization (<30 mm Hg)
Type II 50%–70% Aperistalsis + pan‑esophageal pressurization >30 mm Hg
Type III ~5% Spastic distal contractions ± pressurization
4.3.1 Recommendation 2
  • Strong (high evidence): use esophageal pressure topography (HRM) over conventional line tracing for diagnosis.
4.3.2 Recommendation 3
  • Conditional (low evidence): classify achalasia subtypes via Chicago Classification to inform prognosis & therapy choice.
4.4 Adjunctive Tests
  • Timed Barium Esophagram (TBE): measures barium column height at 1, 2, 5 min → predicts emptying.
  • Functional Lumen Imaging Probe (FLIP): assesses EGJ distensibility; useful when HRM equivocal or patient cannot tolerate manometry.
4.4.1 Key Concept Statements
  • TBE + bird‑beak may be diagnostic in appropriate clinical context.
  • Endoscopic retained saliva & puckered EGJ should raise suspicion.
  • FLIP can complement pre‑/post‑therapy assessment; high concordance with HRM.

5. Initial Treatment Options
5.1 Pharmacologic Therapy (non‑definitive)
  • Agents: nifedipine 10–30 mg SL qAC, isosorbide dinitrate 5 mg SL qAC, terbutaline, theophylline, anticholinergics, sildenaﬁl 50 mg.
  • Mechanism: ↓intracellular Ca²⁺ (CCB) or ↑NO (nitrates) → transient LES pressure reduction.
  • Efficacy: LES pressure ↓ 13%–65%; symptom relief 0%–87%.
  • Duration of action 30–120 min → multiple daily doses, common side‑effects (headache, hypotension, edema).
  • Recommendation 4 – Conditional (moderate): reserve pharmacotherapy for patients unfit for definitive therapy or after botulinum toxin failure.
5.2 Endoscopic Botulinum Toxin Injection
  • Dose: 100 U injected in 0.5–1 mL aliquots above squamocolumnar junction.
  • Effect: SNAP‑25 cleavage → ↓acetylcholine release, ~50% LES pressure reduction.
  • Symptom relief at 30 days: 78.7% (9 studies, 315 pts). Declines to 70% @3 mo, 53.3% @6 mo, 40.6% @12 mo.
  • Need for repeat injections in ~46.6%; subsequent interventions required in ~30%.
  • Complications: rare reflux, mediastinitis; histology shows inflammatory changes & mild fibrosis.
  • Recommendation 5 – Strong (moderate): botulinum toxin does not significantly affect later myotomy outcomes; can be first‑line for non‑surgical candidates.
5.3 Pneumatic Dilation (PD)
  • Devices: graded polyethylene balloons (RigiFlex) 3.0, 3.5, 4.0 cm.
  • Technique: fluoroscopic or endoscopic positioning; inflate to 10–15 psi for 15–60 s.
  • Protocol: start with 3.0 cm; if symptomatic at 4–6 wk, progress to larger size; younger men (<45 y) may begin with 3.5 cm.
  • Success rates: symptom relief 50%–93% overall; cumulative success after graded series (3→3.5→4 cm): 74% (1‑cm), 86% (3.5 cm), 90% (4 cm) at mean 1.6 y follow‑up.
  • Predictors of favorable response: age >45 y, female sex, nondilated esophagus, post‑PD LES pressure ≤10 mm Hg.
  • Complications: perforation overall 1.9% (range 0–16%); GERD post‑PD 15%–35%.
  • Recommendation 6 – Strong (very low): PD superior to medical therapy for symptom & physiologic improvement.
5.4 Laparoscopic Heller Myotomy (LHM)
  • Technique: longitudinal LES muscle division via laparoscopic approach; optional fundoplication (Dor or Toupet).
  • Outcomes: symptom improvement 80%–95%; long‑term success declines modestly (6 mo 89%, 6 y 57%).
  • Complications: perforation rare; postoperative GERD without fundoplication ~29%; with Dor/Toupet ↓ to ~9%.
  • Recommendation 7 – Strong (moderate): LHM superior to botulinum toxin in fit surgical candidates.
5.5 Peroral Endoscopic Myotomy (POEM)
  • Procedure: submucosal tunnel creation, circular muscle cut ≥6 cm esophagus + 2 cm gastric cardia; length tailored to subtype.
  • Success rates: overall >90% symptom relief in treatment‑naïve cohorts; type III response up to 98%.
  • Adverse events: GERD incidence higher than LHM (≈39% vs 16.8%); perforation rare.
  • Recommendation 8 – Strong (moderate): POEM comparable to LHM for overall symptomatic improvement; tailored longer myotomy advantageous in type III.
5.6 Comparative Effectiveness Summaries
Comparison Evidence Quality Recommendation
PD vs Medical Therapy Very low Strong – PD superior
PD vs Botulinum Toxin (12‑mo) Moderate Strong – PD more effective long‑term
PD vs LHM (2‑yr) High Strong – comparable efficacy
POEM vs PD (2‑yr) Moderate Strong – POEM superior symptom control

6. Post‑Therapy Assessment
6.1 Symptom Scoring (Eckardt Score)
  • Components: dysphagia, regurgitation, chest pain, weight loss (0–3 each; total 0–12).
  • Threshold for suboptimal outcome: >3.
  • Limitations: chest‑pain & weight‑loss items reduce reliability; may not reflect bolus retention.
6.2 Objective Testing After Definitive Therapy
6.2.1 Timed Barium Esophagram (TBE) – First‑Line
  • Measures barium column height at 1, 2, 5 min → assesses esophageal emptying.
  • Normalization: complete clearance by 1 min.
  • Recommendation 9 – Strong (very low): use TBE rather than HRM/ES alone to evaluate recurrent symptoms.
6.2.2 High‑Resolution Manometry (HRM)
  • Useful for detecting incomplete myotomy, residual spasm, or EGJ outflow obstruction.
  • Not sufficient alone to define treatment failure.
6.2.3 Endoscopy
  • Indicated when reflux suspected, stricture formation, or to assess mucosal integrity after therapy.
6.3 Definition of Treatment Failure
  • Persistent/recurrent symptoms with ES > 3 and objective evidence of bolus retention on TBE or abnormal LES relaxation on HRM.
7. Management of Failed Therapy / Recurrent Disease
7.1 PD After Prior LHM or POEM
  • Safe and effective salvage; success ~89% in post‑LHM cohort, low complication rate.
  • Recommendation 10 – Strong (moderate): PD appropriate after failed myotomy or POEM.
7.2 Repeat LHM After Failed PD or POEM
  • Consider when anatomy favorable and incomplete myotomy suspected; may avoid esophagectomy.
  • Recommendation 11 – Strong (very low): Heller myotomy before esophagectomy in patients with prior PD/POEM failure, if feasible.
7.3 POEM After Prior PD or LHM
  • High clinical success (~95% at median 28 mo) even after previous interventions; low adverse event profile.
  • Recommendation 12 – Strong (low): POEM safe as salvage after PD or LHM.
7.4 Esophagectomy (End‑Stage Disease)
  • Indicated for megaesophagus (>8 cm), refractory symptoms, or severe malnutrition despite other therapies.
  • Mortality ~2% in high‑volume centers; respiratory complications ~10%.
  • Recommendation 13 – Strong (low): esophagectomy for surgically fit patients with failed prior interventions and dilated esophagus.
7.5 Self‑Expanding Metal Stents (SEMS)
  • Temporary SEMS show limited benefit; higher symptom recurrence vs botulinum toxin; not widely available.
  • Recommendation 14 – Strong (low): advise against routine stent placement for long‑term dysphagia management.
8. Cancer Surveillance & Long‑Term Monitoring
8.1 Esophageal Carcinoma Risk
  • Incidence ≈ 1 per 300 patient‑years; hazard ratio ≈ 28 for squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Cumulative risk: ~205 cases/100 000 yr (squamous), ~21 cases/100 k yr (adenocarcinoma).
8.2 Surveillance Recommendations
  • Routine endoscopic surveillance not recommended due to low absolute yield (~400 scopes per cancer detected) and poor survival after diagnosis.
  • Consider targeted surveillance in patients with >10–15 yr disease duration, persistent dysphagia, or significant weight loss; interval every 3 yr may be reasonable (expert opinion).
8.3 Key Concept Statement
  • Despite elevated malignancy risk, current evidence does not support systematic screening; focus on symptom‑driven evaluation and management of megaesophagus.

9. Diagnostic & Treatment Algorithm
9.1 Initial Clinical Evaluation
  • Suspect achalasia in progressive dysphagia + regurgitation ± weight loss.
  • Exclude GERD: trial PPI; if no response → proceed to work‑up.
9.1.1 First‑Line Tests
  • Upper endoscopy – rule out pseudoachalasia, obstructing mass, assess mucosa.
  • High‑resolution manometry (HRM) – confirm diagnosis, assign Chicago subtype.
  • Timed barium esophagram (TBE) – supportive if HRM equivocal or to visualize bird‑beak.
9.2 Decision Nodes Based on Subtype & Patient Factors
Node Criteria Preferred Initial Therapy
A Type I or II, fit for definitive therapy PD (graded) OR LHM ± fundoplication OR POEM (tailored).
B Type III, fit for definitive therapy Tailored POEM (extended myotomy) or tailored LHM (longer myotomy).
C Significant comorbidities, unfit for invasive procedures Botulinum toxin injection or pharmacologic agents (CCB, nitrates).
D Prior definitive therapy failed (PD/LHM/POEM) Consider alternative definitive modality (e.g., POEM after PD, PD after LHM), then reassess.
E End‑stage megaesophagus (>8 cm), refractory symptoms Esophagectomy in high‑volume center.
9.2.1 Balloon Dilation Protocol (Node A)
  • Start with 3.0 cm balloon; assess symptom relief at 4–6 wk.
  • If inadequate → upsize to 3.5 cm, then 4.0 cm as needed.
  • Younger men (<45 y) may begin with 3.5 cm for better efficacy.
9.2.2 Surgical Myotomy Protocol (Node A/B)
  • LHM via laparoscopic approach; add Dor or Toupet fundoplication to reduce postoperative GERD.
  • Choose Dor for shorter operative time, Toupet if lower dysphagia recurrence desired (meta‑analysis suggests posterior may improve QoL).
9.2.3 POEM Protocol (Node A/B)
  • Create submucosal tunnel; perform myotomy ≥6 cm esophageal + 2 cm gastric.
  • Extend length for type III spastic segment based on HRM/FLIP/EUS findings.
9.3 Post‑Therapy Follow‑Up
9.3.1 Early Assessment (≤3 mo)
  • Eckardt Score: aim ≤3.
  • TBE: confirm emptying at 1 min; if retained column >5 cm → consider early retreatment.
9.3.2 Long‑Term Surveillance (≥12 mo)
  • Annual symptom review; repeat TBE or HRM if ES rises >3 or new dysphagia.
  • Endoscopy with pH monitoring if GERD symptoms develop, especially after POEM without fundoplication.
9.4 Management of Recurrence / Failure
Scenario Recommended Salvage
Persistent dysphagia after PD (type I/II) LHM or POEM (choose based on institutional expertise).
Recurrent symptoms after LHM (no fundoplication) Add fundoplication endoscopically if feasible; otherwise consider POEM.
Failure of POEM (type III) Tailored LHM with extended myotomy; consider repeat POEM if anatomy permits.
Multiple failed definitive therapies + dilated esophagus Esophagectomy (high‑volume center).
9.4.1 Perforation Management
  • Small perforations: conservative (antibiotics, NPO, possible endoscopic clipping).
  • Large perforations or mediastinal contamination: surgical repair via thoracotomy.

11. Implementation & Clinical Practice Points
  • Multidisciplinary centers (gastroenterology, surgery, anesthesia) improve outcomes; refer complex cases to high‑volume institutions.
  • Patient preference should be incorporated, especially when multiple equally effective options exist (PD vs LHM vs POEM).
  • Documentation: record subtype, balloon sizes used, myotomy length, fundoplication type, and post‑procedure ES/TBE results for longitudinal tracking.
  • Education: counsel patients on potential GERD after POEM; discuss need for lifelong PPI in a subset.
