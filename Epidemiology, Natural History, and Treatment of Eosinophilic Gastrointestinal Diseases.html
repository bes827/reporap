Introduction & Definitions
  • - Eosinophilic gastrointestinal diseases (EGIDs) are chronic, immune-mediated disorders characterized by pathologic infiltration of eosinophils in the GI tract.
  • - Pathophysiology involves type 2 immunity and allergen-driven inflammatory reactions.
  • - EGIDs are divided into Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE), restricted to the esophagus, and non-EoE EGIDs, affecting other GI segments.
Clinical Presentation: EoE
  • - Adults: Typically present with dysphagia and episodes of food bolus impaction.
  • - Adaptive Behaviors (Adults): Drinking water, cutting food into small pieces, eating slowly, and avoiding sticky foods.
  • - Children: Commonly report nonspecific upper GI symptoms, food refusal, picky eating, and fear of food.

Clinical Presentation: Non-EoE EGIDs
  • - Symptoms are heterogenous and depend on the location of inflammation.
  • - Common symptoms include abdominal pain/cramping, diarrhea, nausea/vomiting, bloating, anorexia/weight loss, and blood in the stool.


Nomenclature & Diagnostic Criteria
  • - A 2022 consensus established "EGID" as the umbrella term.
  • - EGIDs are subdivided into EoE and non-EoE EGIDs.
  • - Non-EoE EGIDs are named by location: Eosinophilic Gastritis (EoG), Eosinophilic Enteritis (EoN), and Eosinophilic Colitis (EoC).
EoE: Diagnostic Criteria
  • - Diagnosis requires a combination of three key elements:
  • - 1. Symptoms of esophageal dysfunction.
  • - 2. Histology showing at least 15 eosinophils per high-power field (eos/hpf) on esophageal biopsy.
  • - 3. Exclusion of competing causes of esophageal eosinophilia.
Severity Assessment
  • - The Index of Severity for EoE (I-SEE) is used at diagnosis and for longitudinal follow-up.
  • - It assesses three domains: symptoms/complications, inflammatory features, and fibrostenotic features.


Non-EoE EGIDs: Diagnostic Criteria
  • - The diagnostic process is more challenging than for EoE.
  • - Approach involves attributing GI symptoms to disease location, finding pathologically elevated eosinophils on biopsy, and excluding other causes of GI eosinophilia.
Diagnostic Challenges
  • - Symptoms are often nonspecific.
  • - Inflammation can involve muscular and serosal layers, not just the mucosa.
  • - Eosinophils are normally present in the stomach, small intestine, and colon, unlike the esophagus.

Histologic Thresholds for Pathologic Eosinophilia
  • - Stomach: ≥30 to 40 eos/hpf
  • - Duodenum: ≥50 to 60 eos/hpf
  • - Terminal Ileum: ≥60 eos/hpf
  • - Cecum & Right Colon: ≥100 eos/hpf
  • - Left Colon: ≥60 eos/hpf

Common Pitfalls Leading to Diagnostic Delay
  • - Lack of recognition by clinicians and pathologists.
  • - Collection of too few biopsy samples.
  • - Lack of eosinophil count quantification on pathology reports.



Global Epidemiology
  • - The epidemiology of EGIDs, particularly EoE, is evolving rapidly with a continuous increase in incidence and prevalence over the past 30 years.
Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)
  • - Incidence and prevalence are markedly and continually increasing worldwide, with no plateau observed.
Incidence & Prevalence Rates
  • - 2017-2022 Global Estimate:
  • - Incidence: 6.81 per 100,000
  • - Prevalence: 74.42 per 100,000
  • - 2037 Projection:
  • - Incidence: 12.67 per 100,000
  • - Prevalence: 110.82 per 100,000
  • - 2022 US Claims Data: Prevalence estimated at 142.5 per 100,000.

Underdiagnosis & Diagnostic Delay
  • - EoE may be significantly underdiagnosed (the "tip of the iceberg").
  • - Patients often experience symptoms for 5 to 10 years before diagnosis.
  • - A study noted >700,000 emergency visits for dysphagia/impaction in males <40 without an EoE code, suggesting a large undiagnosed population.

Prevalence in Special Populations
  • - Endoscopy for any indication: 6% to 7%
  • - Endoscopy for dysphagia: up to 25%
  • - Food bolus impaction: ≥50%
  • - Refractory reflux symptoms: 1% to 20%

Associated Atopic & Other Conditions
  • - Having food allergies increases odds of EoE by 9-fold.
  • - Relative increase in EoE over general population:
  • - Asthma, eczema, seasonal allergies: ~5-fold
  • - Food allergies: >15-fold
  • - Family history: 2% to 3% risk between first-degree relatives.

Risk Factors
  • - The rapid rise implicates changing environmental factors.
Environmental & Geographic Factors
  • - Decreasing prevalence of Helicobacter pylori.
  • - Geographic factors: low population density, arid/cold climates.
  • - Poor environmental quality (especially water), heavy metals, proximity to large farms, airborne particulate matter.

Early Life Exposures
  • - Risk Factors: Cesarean delivery, neonatal ICU admission, antibiotic exposure (infant and maternal), acid suppressant use in infancy.
  • - Protective Factor: Having furred pets in the home.

Gene-Environment Interaction
  • - Breastfeeding was protective against EoE only in children with the risk allele in the calpain 14 (CAPN14) gene.

Barrier Defect Hypothesis
  • - EoE may be driven by a defective esophageal epithelial barrier.
  • - Environmental detergents (e.g., sodium dodecyl sulfate in toothpaste) may disrupt this barrier, as shown in animal models.



Non-EoE EGIDs
  • - Far less is known about the epidemiology of non-EoE EGIDs, which are considered rare diseases.
Prevalence & Incidence
  • - US Prevalence (2022):
  • - EoG/EGE: 6.6 per 100,000
  • - EoC: 3.0 per 100,000
  • - Unlike EoE, a male predominance is not observed.
  • - Overlap is common: more than one-third of patients have >1 GI segment involved.

Underdiagnosis & Risk Factors
  • - Diagnostic delay of 3 to 5 years or longer is reported.
  • - Risk factors are poorly understood but include high rates of concomitant atopic conditions, family predisposition, and an inverse association with H. pylori.



Natural History
  • - The natural history differs between EoE and non-EoE EGIDs, with EoE being characteristically chronic and progressive.
Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)
  • - EoE is a chronic disease and is progressive in many patients.
Progression & Fibrostenosis
  • - In untreated adults, 97% have persistent dysphagia at ~7 years follow-up.
  • - Risk of progression to fibrostenotic disease is time-dependent.
  • - There is a 5% risk of developing fibrosis for each year of undiagnosed active disease.
  • - Gaps in care of 2 years or more are associated with developing fibrostenosis.

Remission & Recurrence
  • - Spontaneous disease remission is uncommon (0.5% in a large pediatric study).
  • - Disease almost universally recurs when effective treatment is withdrawn.
  • - After stopping budesonide orodispersible tablet (BOT), 99% of patients had clinical and histologic recurrence by 48 weeks.
Recurrence after other treatments
  • - After stopping topical steroids, median time to symptom recurrence was 244 days.
  • - After budesonide oral suspension (BOS) withdrawal, 78% had histologic recurrence by week 36.



Non-EoE EGIDs
  • - Data are less robust, but three main patterns of disease course have been identified based on intestinal layer involvement (mucosal, muscle, serosal).
Identified Disease Courses
  • - A longitudinal study identified three different courses over a median of 13.1 years:
  • - Single flare: 42% of patients
  • - Recurring course: 37% of patients
  • - Continuous course: 21% of patients

Disease Course Patterns
  • - A more recent study identified courses as:
  • - Continuous: Unchanged histology/symptoms for ≥6 months.
  • - Relapsing: Remitting histology or relapsing symptoms for ≥6 months.
  • - Progressive: Increasing histologic or symptomatic burden for ≥6 months.



Treatment
  • - Treatment strategies and available evidence differ significantly between EoE and non-EoE EGIDs.
Treatment of Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)
  • - Treatment endpoints include improvement of symptoms, endoscopic findings, inflammation, and quality of life.
  • - Histologic Remission: <15 eos/hpf
  • - Endoscopic Remission: EREFS score of ≤2
Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)
  • - Common first-line, off-label treatment.
  • - Efficacy (Meta-analysis):
  • - Clinical (symptom) response: 65%
  • - Histologic remission (<15 eos/hpf): 45.4%
  • - Dosing: Twice-daily treatment is more effective (51.7% remission) than once-daily (28.3%).
  • - Maintenance: 68.2% of patients maintain remission on a half-dose.

Swallowed Topical Corticosteroids (STCs)
  • - A first-line therapy for induction and maintenance of remission.
  • - Approved formulations: budesonide orodispersible tablet (BOT) in Europe, budesonide oral suspension (BOS) in the US.
Efficacy & Safety
  • - Induction Remission Rates:
  • - BOT: 57.6%
  • - BOS: >50%
  • - Maintenance: Efficacy sustained in ~80% of patients for up to 48 weeks.
  • - Long-term: 20% to 40% may lose response over 3-4 years.
  • - Most Frequent Adverse Event: Esophageal candidiasis.


Monoclonal Antibodies
  • - Several biologics failed to meet endpoints, indicating eosinophil depletion alone is not sufficient for remission.
Dupilumab (Anti-IL4/IL13)
  • - The only biologic currently approved for EoE in the US and Europe.
  • - Approved for patients aged ≥1 year, weighing ≥15 kg.
  • - Efficacy (Adolescents/Adults): ~60% clinico-histologic remission at week 24.
  • - Efficacy (Children 1-11 years): 68% histologic remission by week 16.
  • - Recommendation: Second-line after PPI/STC failure, or first-line in patients with other type 2 comorbidities.


Food Elimination Diets (FEDs)
  • - EoE is a delayed-type hypersensitivity; allergy tests (skin prick, patch, serum IgE) are not recommended to identify food triggers.
  • - Empiric elimination of common triggers is the recommended strategy.
Efficacy of Empiric FEDs
  • - Histologic remission rates from meta-analysis:
  • - One-Food (OFED - milk): 46.7%
  • - Two-Food (TFED - milk, wheat): 44.3%
  • - Four-Food (FFED - milk, wheat, egg, soy): 54.7%
  • - Six-Food (SFED - FFED + nuts, seafood): 63.9%
  • - Recommendation: Start with the least restrictive diet (e.g., OFED/milk elimination) and step-up if no response.



Treatment of Non-EoE EGIDs
  • - Management is challenging due to a lack of established endpoints, validated outcomes, and approved treatments.
  • - Evidenceis currently based on low-quality evidence from small case series and observational studies.
Corticosteroids
  • - Corticosteroids are the mainstay of treatment for non-EoE EGIDs.
Systemic Corticosteroids
  • - Prednisone is used at doses up to 60 mg per day, then tapered over 4 to 6 weeks.
  • - Induces remission with response rates ranging from 50% to 100%.

Topical Corticosteroids
  • - Enteral release budesonide (doses 0.25 mg to 9 mg per day) offers an alternative with fewer systemic adverse events.
  • - Capsules can be modified to target specific GI segments (stomach, proximal small bowel, distal small bowel/colon).


Food Elimination Diets (FEDs)
  • - Evidence is heterogeneous but supports a role for dietary management.
Elemental Diet
  • - In children, elemental diets led to clinical remission in 76% and histologic remission in 83%.
  • - A recent prospective trial in adults with EoG/EoN showed a 6-week elemental diet achieved 100% histologic remission.

Empiric & SFED
  • - Empiric FEDs (milk, wheat, etc.) showed symptomatic improvement in 63% of patients.
  • - Six-Food Elimination Diet (SFED) or 7-FED showed symptomatic improvement in 85%.


Monoclonal Antibodies
  • - Several biologics have been studied, but none are currently approved for non-EoE EGIDs.
Failed/Discontinued Trials
  • - Lirelimab (anti-Siglec-8): Phase 3 failed to show symptom improvement despite histologic response (85% of patients).
  • - Benralizumab: Phase 3 failed to demonstrate symptom improvement in EoG.

Agents with Ongoing Investigation/Data
  • - Dupilumab: Phase 2 RCT in EoG showed histologic/endoscopic improvement. Observational studies report positive symptom outcomes. A new RCT is ongoing.
  • - Cendakimab (anti-IL13): A Phase 3 RCT in Japan demonstrated clinical efficacy in EGE.
  • - Vedolizumab: Has shown efficacy in small case series of refractory patients.


Other Nonsteroidal, Nonbiologic Treatments
  • - Includes PPIs, mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn, ketotifen), leukotriene inhibitors (montelukast), and immunomodulators (azathioprine).
  • - Results are variable and based on case reports/series.
  • - Guidelines state there is insufficient data to recommend for or against their use.


Emerging Treatments for EGIDs
  • - Several novel therapeutic strategies are under investigation for both EoE and non-EoE EGIDs.
Novel Topical Corticosteroid Delivery Systems
  • - ESO-101: Capsule with a mucoadhesive film of mometasone furoate.
  • - EP-104GI: Long-acting fluticasone injected into the esophagus.
  • - APT-1011: Fluticasone orodispersible tablet.

Monoclonal Antibodies
  • - Cendakimab (anti-IL13)
  • - Tezepelumab & Solrikitug (anti-TSLP)
  • - Barzolvolimab (anti-KIT)
  • - CALY-002 (anti-IL15)

Small Molecules & Other Agents
  • - Immune-resetting peptide 1104
  • - Etrasimod
  • - Potassium-competitive acid blockers



Knowledge Gaps & Future Directions
  • - Significant gaps remain in understanding and managing EGIDs, highlighting key areas for future research.
Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)
  • - Key Gaps:
  • - Diagnostic delay remains an issue.
  • - Mechanisms of environmental/early life risk factors are unknown.
  • - Lack of predictors for treatment response and disease progression.
  • - No head-to-head drug comparisons to establish a treatment hierarchy.
Future Research Directions for EoE
  • - Study endotypes and phenotypes to personalize therapy.
  • - Develop noninvasive or minimally invasive monitoring strategies (e.g., blood-based, esophageal string/sponge tests) to make management less cumbersome.


Non-EoE EGIDs
  • - Key Gaps:
  • - Diseases are rare and possibly underdiagnosed.
  • - Pathophysiology, risk factors, and pathogenesis are largely unknown.
  • - Natural history data needs corroboration.
  • - No approved treatments are available.
Future Research Directions for Non-EoE EGIDs
  • - Adherence to new nomenclature and guidelines is crucial for better disease characterization.
  • - Prospective studies are needed to validate natural history and guide therapy development.
