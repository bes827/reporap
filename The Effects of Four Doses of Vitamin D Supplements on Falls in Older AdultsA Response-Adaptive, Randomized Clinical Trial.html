The Effects of Four Doses of Vitamin D Supplements on Falls in Older Adults
A Response-Adaptive, Randomized Clinical Trial
  • - Journal: Ann Intern Med. 2021;174:145–156.
  • - Publication Date: 8 December 2020.
  • - DOI: 10.7326/M20-3812
Knowledge Test
  • - What was the serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25-(OH)D] level range for trial participants?
  • - Which dose of Vitamin D3 was ultimately selected as the 'best dose' for preventing falls during the dose-finding stage?
  • - In the final analysis, what was the hazard ratio for a serious fall in the group receiving 1000 IU/d or more compared to the 200 IU/d group?
  • - What was the hazard ratio for a fall with hospitalization in the group receiving 1000 IU/d or more compared to the 200 IU/d group?
  • - What was the final conclusion regarding vitamin D supplementation at doses of 1000 IU/d or higher for fall prevention in this population?

Objective & Background
  • - Objective: To compare the effects of 4 different doses of vitamin D3 supplements on falls in older adults.
  • - Background: Evidence for vitamin D in fall prevention is inconsistent, possibly due to differences in dosage, administration, and study design.
Primary Hypothesis
  • - The primary hypothesis was that high-dose vitamin D supplementation would reduce the risk for falls.


Study Design (STURDY Trial)
  • - Design: 2-stage Bayesian, response-adaptive, randomized trial.
  • - Setting: 2 community-based research units in Maryland.
Participants
  • - Total: 688 participants.
  • - Age: 70 years and older.
  • - Criteria: Elevated fall risk and low serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25-(OH)D] levels.
Inclusion Criteria
  • - Serum 25-(OH)D level: 25 to 72.5 nmol/L.
  • - Elevated fall risk defined by self-report of one or more:
  • - ≥2 falls or ≥1 injurious fall in the past year.
  • - Fear of falling due to balance or walking problems.
  • - Difficulty maintaining balance.
  • - Use of an assistive device when walking.

Exclusion Criteria
  • - Cognitive impairment.
  • - Hypercalcemia.
  • - Kidney, bladder, or ureteral stones.
  • - Use of supplemental vitamin D > 1000 IU/d or calcium > 1200 mg/d.


Intervention & Trial Stages
  • - The trial involved four daily doses of vitamin D3 and was conducted in two stages.
Dose Groups
  • - Control: 200 IU/d.
  • - Non-control: 1000 IU/d, 2000 IU/d, and 4000 IU/d.
Rationale for Doses
  • - 200 IU/d (Control): To ensure average total daily intake would be at least 800 IU (RDA).
  • - 1000 IU/d: Seemed to prevent falls in previous studies.
  • - 2000 IU/d: Dose used in the VITAL trial.
  • - 4000 IU/d: Maximum tolerable daily allowance.


Stage 1: Dose-Finding
  • - Goal: Identify which of the 3 non-control doses was most effective (the 'best dose') for preventing falls.
  • - Participants were randomly assigned to one of the 4 doses.
  • - The probability of assignment to the control dose (200 IU/d) was fixed at 0.50 throughout the trial.

Stage 2: Confirmatory
  • - After the 'best dose' was identified:
  • - Participants on other non-control doses were switched to the 'best dose'.
  • - New participants were randomly assigned 1:1 to the 'best dose' or the control dose.


Outcomes
  • - The trial measured primary, secondary, and safety outcomes.
Primary Outcome
  • - Time to first fall or death over 2 years, whichever occurred first.

Secondary Outcome
  • - Gait speed, measured at baseline and at 3, 12, and 24 months.

Safety Outcomes
  • - Rates of serious adverse events (SAEs).
  • - Serious falls (resulting in fracture, dislocation, or associated with hospitalization/SAE).
  • - Falls with hospitalization.
  • - Events potentially related to vitamin D supplementation (e.g., hypercalcemia, kidney stones).



Results
  • - The trial was terminated early due to futility after a recommendation from the Data and Safety Monitoring Board (DSMB).
Participant Characteristics
  • - Mean age: 77.2 years.
  • - Sex: 56.4% men.
  • - Race: 18.2% Black.
  • - Mean baseline total 25-(OH)D level: 55.3 nmol/L.

Dose-Finding Stage Results
  • - The 1000 IU/d dose was declared the 'best dose' based on having the lowest rate of the primary outcome.
Primary Outcome Rates (per 100 Person-Years)
  • - 1000 IU/d: 72.1 (lowest rate).
  • - 2000 IU/d: 108.0.
  • - 4000 IU/d: 99.4.
  • - 200 IU/d (Control): 90.5.

Hazard Ratios vs. 1000 IU/d
  • - 2000 IU/d: HR 1.54 (95% CI, 1.01–2.34).
  • - 4000 IU/d: HR 1.41 (95% CI, 0.92–2.16).

DSMB Intervention
  • - The DSMB recommended ending randomization to the 2000 IU/d and 4000 IU/d groups due to higher event rates and safety concerns, even though pre-specified statistical thresholds were not met.


Confirmatory Stage Results
  • - This stage compared the 'experience-with-best-dose' group (those receiving 1000 IU/d) against the control group (200 IU/d).
Primary Outcome: First Fall or Death
  • - No statistically significant difference was found.
  • - Hazard Ratio (HR): 0.94 (95% CI, 0.76 to 1.15; P = 0.54).
  • - Event Rate (per 100 PY): 76.9 (1000 IU/d) vs. 76.0 (200 IU/d).

Secondary Outcome: Gait Speed
  • - There was no significant difference in the change in gait speed between the pooled higher-doses group and the 200 IU/d group (P=0.153).


Achieved 25-(OH)D Levels
  • - Serum levels increased progressively with higher doses.
Mean Levels at 3 Months
  • - 200 IU/d: 67.1 nmol/L
  • - 1000 IU/d: 80.3 nmol/L
  • - 2000 IU/d: 87.3 nmol/L
  • - 4000 IU/d: 119.2 nmol/L


Adverse Events & Safety Concerns
  • - Several analyses raised safety concerns about vitamin D3 doses of 1000 IU/d and higher.
Serious Falls
  • - Significantly more likely in the experience-with-best-dose (≥1000 IU/d) group compared to the 200 IU/d group.
  • - HR: 1.87 (95% CI, 1.03 to 3.41).

Falls with Hospitalization
  • - Significantly more likely in the experience-with-best-dose (≥1000 IU/d) group compared to the 200 IU/d group.
  • - HR: 2.48 (95% CI, 1.13 to 5.46).



Conclusion & Clinical Implications
  • - In older persons with elevated fall risk and low serum 25-(OH)D levels, high-dose vitamin D supplementation (≥1000 IU/d) did not prevent falls compared with 200 IU/d.
Key Takeaways
  • - There was no beneficial effect on fall prevention from supplemental vitamin D at doses of 1000 IU/d or higher.
  • - Several analyses raised safety concerns about vitamin D3 doses of 1000 IU/d and higher, with increased risk of serious falls and falls with hospitalization.

Study Limitations
  • - The control group received 200 IU of vitamin D per day, not a placebo.
  • - The dose-finding stage ended before pre-specified thresholds for dose selection were reached.
  • - The trial was stopped early based on conditional power analyses that were not pre-specified.

Implications for Practice
  • - Findings support guidelines (e.g., 2018 U.S. Preventive Services Task Force) that do not recommend vitamin D supplementation for fall prevention in older adults without osteoporosis or vitamin D deficiency.
  • - Suggests a lack of benefit even in persons with baseline 25-(OH)D levels of 25 to 72.5 nmol/L.
  • - Policymakers should re-evaluate the upper dose limit considered safe, given the widespread use of high-dose vitamin D.
