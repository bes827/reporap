Thinking, Fast and Slow
Chapter: Attention and Effort
  • - Book by Daniel Kahneman.
knowledge test
  • - What is the correct answer to the bat-and-ball puzzle where they cost $1.10 in total and the bat is $1 more than the ball?
  • - In the parole judge study, what was the approximate parole approval rate immediately after a food break?
  • - The Add-3 task is so demanding that in the first 5 seconds, the pupil dilates by what percentage of its original area?
  • - What is the name of the phenomenon where exerting self-control on one task makes you less willing or able to exert it on a subsequent task?
  • - What simple substance was found to reverse the effects of ego depletion?

System 2: The 'Hero' of the Story
  • - System 2 is the conscious, reasoning self that has beliefs, makes choices, and decides what to think about and what to do.
  • - It operates through effortful mental activities.
  • - A main characteristic is laziness; a reluctance to invest more effort than necessary.
Mental Effort & Its Physical Indicators
  • - Mental effort is a form of work, and its exertion can be physically measured.
Cognitive Pupillometry: A Window to the Soul
  • - The pupil of the eye is a sensitive indicator of mental effort.
  • - Pupil dilation corresponds directly to the cognitive load of a task, distinct from emotional arousal.
The 'Add-N' Tasks
  • - Paced tasks like 'Add-1' or 'Add-3' demonstrate the limits of cognitive ability.
  • - In Add-1, a person reads 4 digits (e.g., 5294) and must report a new string with each digit incremented by 1 (e.g., 6305).
Physiological Response Pattern
  • - Pupil size response follows an inverted V-shape.
  • - Effort builds with each digit, peaks during transformation, and relaxes as short-term memory is unloaded.
  • - The most difficult task, Add-3, caused pupils to dilate by 50% of their original area and heart rate to increase by 7 beats per minute in the first 5 seconds.

Reaching Cognitive Limits
  • - When subjects were given more digits than they could remember, their pupils stopped dilating or shrank.
  • - This indicated they had given up on the task.


Effort as an Energy Meter
  • - Pupils act like an electricity meter, showing the current rate of mental energy use.
  • - We decide what to do, but have limited control over the effort required.



Limited Capacity & Cognitive Overload
  • - System 2 has a limited capacity for effort, similar to an electrical circuit.
Response to Overload
  • - Unlike a circuit breaker that shuts everything down, mental overload response is selective and precise.
  • - System 2 protects the most important activity, allocating it the necessary attention.
  • - 'Spare capacity' is allocated to secondary tasks.

Inattentional Blindness
  • - When engaged in a mental 'sprint', people can become effectively blind to other stimuli.
  • - In an experiment, when mental effort was at its peak, subjects missed seeing a target letter (K) almost half the time, despite their eyes being open and looking at it.


The Law of Least Effort
  • - This law applies to both cognitive and physical exertion.
  • - If there are multiple ways to achieve a goal, people will gravitate to the least demanding course of action.
  • - In the economy of action, effort is a cost. Laziness is built into our nature.
Skill Acquisition
  • - As you become skilled in a task, its demand for energy diminishes.
  • - Brain studies show fewer brain regions are involved as skill increases.
  • - Highly intelligent individuals need less effort to solve the same problems.


Tasks Requiring System 2
  • - System 2 is required for tasks that System 1 cannot perform.
Core Capabilities
  • - Maintaining several ideas in memory simultaneously that require separate actions.
  • - Following rules, comparing objects on multiple attributes, and making deliberate choices.
  • - Adopting 'task sets' (executive control) to obey instructions that override habitual responses (e.g., counting all 'f's on a page).

Drivers of Effort
  • - Task Switching: Switching from one task to another is effortful, especially under time pressure.
  • - Time Pressure: The rate at which material decays in memory forces a hurried pace to refresh and rehearse information.


The Lazy Controller & Intellectual Sloth
  • - A primary function of System 2 is to monitor and control thoughts and actions suggested by System 1.
  • - However, System 2 is often 'lazy' and fails to check intuitive answers from System 1.
The Bat-and-Ball Puzzle
  • - A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?
  • - Intuitive (wrong) answer: 10¢.
  • - Correct answer: 5¢ (Ball=5¢, Bat=$1.05).
  • - A failure to check this reflects adherence to the law of least effort.
Shocking Results
  • - More than 50% of students at Harvard, MIT, and Princeton gave the intuitive, incorrect answer.
  • - At less selective universities, the failure rate was in excess of 80%.


Flawed Syllogism Example
  • - Premise 1: All roses are flowers.
  • - Premise 2: Some flowers fade quickly.
  • - Conclusion: Therefore some roses fade quickly.
  • - This argument is flawed, but a large majority endorse it as valid because the conclusion seems plausible.

Michigan Murder Rate Puzzle
  • - When asked to estimate the murder rate in Michigan, many people fail to recall that Detroit, a high-crime city, is in Michigan.
  • - This failure of memory search leads to a significant underestimation.

Engaged vs. Lazy Minds
  • - People who avoid these intuitive errors are described as more 'engaged'.
  • - They are more alert, intellectually active, and more skeptical of their intuitions.


Self-Control & Ego Depletion
  • - Self-control and cognitive effort are forms of mental work that draw on a shared pool of mental energy.
Cognitive Load Weakens Self-Control
  • - People who are cognitively busy (e.g., remembering 7 digits) are more likely to:
  • - Yield to temptation (e.g., choose chocolate cake over fruit salad).
  • - Make selfish choices, use sexist language, and make superficial judgments.

Ego Depletion
  • - An effort of will or self-control is tiring. This phenomenon is called ego depletion.
  • - If you force yourself to do something, you are less willing or able to exert self-control on the next challenge.
  • - Unlike cognitive load, ego depletion is partly a loss of motivation.
Tasks that Cause Depletion
  • - Inhibiting emotional responses.
  • - Making a series of choices involving conflict.
  • - Trying to impress others.
  • - Responding kindly to a partner's bad behavior.

Signs of Depletion
  • - Deviating from one's diet.
  • - Overspending on impulsive purchases.
  • - Reacting aggressively to provocation.
  • - Performing poorly in cognitive tasks and logical decision making.


The Role of Glucose
  • - Effortful mental activity is expensive in the currency of glucose.
  • - When engaged in difficult cognitive reasoning or self-control, blood glucose levels drop.
Reversing Ego Depletion
  • - The effects of ego depletion can be undone by ingesting glucose.
  • - In an experiment, participants who drank lemonade with glucose did not show depletion effects, while those who drank it with Splenda did.

The Israeli Judges Study
  • - A study of parole judges showed a disturbing pattern related to food breaks.
  • - The proportion of approved parole requests spiked to about 65% after each meal.
  • - The approval rate then dropped steadily to about zero just before the next meal.
  • - This suggests tired and hungry judges fall back on the easier default position of denying requests.



Intelligence, Rationality & Control
  • - There is a strong link between cognitive control, self-control, and measures of intelligence.
The Marshmallow Test (Walter Mischel)
  • - Four-year-olds were given a choice: one Oreo now, or two Oreos if they could wait 15 minutes alone with the first one.
  • - Years later, the children who resisted temptation had:
  • - Higher measures of executive control.
  • - Substantially higher scores on tests of intelligence.
  • - Were less likely to take drugs as young adults.

Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT)
  • - A test (including the bat-and-ball problem) designed to measure the strength of System 2's supervisory function.
  • - Low scorers are prone to answer with the first idea that comes to mind and are unwilling to check their intuitions.
Characteristics of Low Scorers
  • - Impulsive and impatient: 63% of low scorers preferred $3,400 this month over $3,800 next month (vs. 37% of high scorers).
  • - Keen for immediate gratification: Willing to pay twice as much as high scorers for overnight delivery of a book.


Stanovich's Model: Intelligence vs. Rationality
  • - Proposes a distinction within System 2 into two 'minds'.
Algorithmic Mind
  • - Deals with slow thinking and demanding computation.
  • - Corresponds to traditional measures of intelligence (IQ).

Reflective Mind
  • - Controls whether we check our intuitions and avoid cognitive biases.
  • - Corresponds to rationality.
  • - Stanovich argues that lazy thinking is a failure of rationality, not necessarily of intelligence.



Flow: The State of Effortless Attention
  • - A concept studied by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi.
  • - Described as 'a state of effortless concentration so deep that they lose their sense of time, of themselves, of their problems.'
  • - Called an 'optimal experience'.
Separating Forms of Effort
  • - Flow neatly separates two forms of effort:
  • - 1. Concentration on the task (which can be very high).
  • - 2. Deliberate control of attention (which requires no exertion of self-control).
  • - This frees up mental resources to be directed entirely to the absorbing task at hand.
