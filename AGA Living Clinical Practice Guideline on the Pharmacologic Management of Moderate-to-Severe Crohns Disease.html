AGA Living Clinical Practice Guideline on the Pharmacologic Management of Moderate-to-Severe Crohn’s Disease
  • - Journal: Gastroenterology
  • - Publication Date: 2025;169:1397–1448
  • - Source: American Gastroenterological Association (AGA)
Knowledge Test
  • - What genetic risk factor is associated with an increased risk of immunogenicity to TNF antagonists, where adding an immunomodulator may be beneficial?
  • - In the SONIC trial, what was the rate of sustained remission at 26 weeks for infliximab + azathioprine combination therapy compared to infliximab monotherapy?
  • - What is the recommended weekly dose of subcutaneous methotrexate for induction of remission?
  • - In advanced therapy-exposed patients, which two medications are classified as 'lower efficacy'?
  • - In a French population-based study, by how much was the risk of lymphoma higher in patients treated with TNF-antagonist monotherapy compared to unexposed patients?

Guideline Scope & Objectives
  • - Provides evidence-based recommendations for pharmacologic management of adults with moderate-to-severely active Crohn's Disease (CD).
  • - Target Audience: Gastroenterologists and primary care providers.
  • - Intended for use in the ambulatory setting.
Definition of Moderate-to-Severe CD
  • - Characterized by moderate to severe daily abdominal pain and diarrhea.
  • - Must be coupled with confirmed active intestinal inflammation.

Applicable Patient Populations
  • - Patients with moderate-to-severe symptoms.
  • - Patients with mild symptoms but predictors of future complications (e.g., high inflammation burden, extensive disease).
  • - Patients who are corticosteroid-dependent.
  • - Patients where disease significantly impacts quality of life.

Exclusions
  • - This guideline does not cover management of:
  • - Postoperative CD
  • - Perianal CD
  • - Internally penetrating or stricturing CD


Key Overarching Considerations (Table 2)
  • - Confirm active inflammation before starting advanced therapies (symptoms, biomarkers, and/or endoscopy).
  • - Perform pretreatment workup: screen for Hepatitis B and Tuberculosis for all advanced therapies, and thiopurine methyl transferase (TPMT) testing before thiopurines.
  • - Assess for factors increasing treatment risk: comorbidities, frailty, cardiovascular risk factors.
  • - Ensure vaccinations (influenza, pneumococcus, herpes zoster) are up-to-date before starting immunosuppression.
  • - Monitor for symptomatic response and objective markers of inflammation after therapy initiation.

Use of Advanced Therapies vs. No Treatment (Recs 1 & 2)
  • - Based on evidence from 45 placebo-controlled RCTs.
Strong Recommendation (Moderate to High Certainty)
  • - AGA recommends using the following over no treatment:
  • - Infliximab
  • - Adalimumab
  • - Ustekinumab
  • - Risankizumab
  • - Mirikizumab
  • - Guselkumab
  • - Upadacitinib
Efficacy Data
  • - These agents met the predefined threshold of a clinically meaningful benefit (CMD) of >10%.
  • - Magnitude of benefit for inducing remission over placebo ranged from 119 to 353 per 1000 patients treated.


Conditional Recommendation (Moderate Certainty)
  • - AGA suggests using the following over no treatment:
  • - Certolizumab pegol
  • - Vedolizumab
Efficacy Data
  • - These agents did not meet the a priori CMD threshold of >10% for inducing remission.
  • - Benefit per 1000 patients treated:
  • - Certolizumab pegol: 48
  • - Vedolizumab: 90


Implementation Considerations
  • - Biosimilars of infliximab, adalimumab, and ustekinumab are considered equivalent to originator drugs.
  • - Subcutaneous formulations of infliximab and vedolizumab show comparable efficacy to IV maintenance doses.
  • - Dose escalation or extended induction may be beneficial for partial responders, especially with severe disease.
  • - Higher maintenance doses of risankizumab, guselkumab, and upadacitinib may be preferred for patients with high inflammation or prior TNF antagonist failure.

Harms & Safety Profile
  • - Advanced therapies are associated with risks of serious infection and malignancies.
  • - Ustekinumab was associated with a 51% lower risk of serious infections compared to TNF antagonists.
  • - TNF antagonists are associated with an increased risk of lymphoma and melanoma.
  • - A French study showed the risk of lymphoma was 2.4-times higher in patients on TNF-antagonist monotherapy vs. unexposed patients.


Positioning of Advanced Therapies
  • - Recommendations are informed by a Network Meta-Analysis (NMA) due to a lack of head-to-head trials.
  • - A clinically meaningful difference (CMD) between two active therapies was set at >5% absolute risk difference.
Advanced Therapy-Naïve Patients (Rec 3)
  • - AGA suggests using a HIGHER efficacy medication over a LOWER efficacy medication.
  • - This is a conditional recommendation (low to high certainty evidence).
Higher Efficacy Medications
  • - Infliximab
  • - Adalimumab
  • - Vedolizumab
  • - Ustekinumab
  • - Risankizumab
  • - Mirikizumab
  • - Guselkumab
Criteria for 'Higher Efficacy'
  • - Must meet all three criteria:
  • - 1. Absolute efficacy difference vs lower efficacy therapies: CMD ≥5%.
  • - 2. P score in NMA vs placebo: 0.49–1.0.
  • - 3. Absolute difference vs placebo in Phase 3 trials: ≥15%.


Lower Efficacy Medications
  • - Certolizumab pegol
  • - Upadacitinib
Note on Upadacitinib
  • - Lower efficacy in biologic-naïve patients was noteworthy.
  • - Efficacy may be lower in ileal CD vs. isolated colonic disease.
  • - FDA label recommends use after TNF-antagonist failure.


Implementation Considerations
  • - Use shared decision-making, considering patient factors (age, comorbidities, pregnancy plans, route of administration).
  • - JAK inhibitors (upadacitinib) should generally be avoided in women contemplating pregnancy due to limited safety data.


Advanced Therapy-Exposed Patients (Rec 4)
  • - Primarily applies to patients with prior TNF antagonist exposure.
  • - AGA suggests using a HIGHER or INTERMEDIATE efficacy medication over a LOWER efficacy medication.
  • - This is a conditional recommendation (low to moderate certainty evidence).
Higher Efficacy Medications
  • - Adalimumab
  • - Risankizumab
  • - Guselkumab
  • - Upadacitinib

Intermediate Efficacy Medications
  • - Ustekinumab
  • - Mirikizumab

Lower Efficacy Medications
  • - Vedolizumab
  • - Certolizumab pegol

Implementation Considerations
  • - Second-line TNF antagonists (e.g., adalimumab) may be effective if first TNF antagonist was stopped for immunogenicity or intolerance, but not for primary non-response.
  • - JAK inhibitors may have higher risk of MACE and cancer in older adults with cardiovascular risk factors.
  • - Avoid JAK inhibitors in women of childbearing age contemplating pregnancy.



Use of Immunomodulators (Recs 5-8)
  • - Covers thiopurines (azathioprine, mercaptopurine) and methotrexate as monotherapy.
Thiopurines
  • - AGA suggests AGAINST using thiopurine monotherapy for inducing remission (very low certainty).
  • - AGA SUGGESTS using thiopurine monotherapy for maintaining remission (low certainty), typically after corticosteroid induction.
Dosing
  • - Azathioprine: 2.0–2.5 mg/kg/d
  • - Mercaptopurine: 1.0–1.5 mg/kg/d


Methotrexate (MTX)
  • - AGA SUGGESTS using subcutaneous or intramuscular MTX monotherapy for induction and maintenance (moderate certainty).
  • - AGA suggests AGAINST using oral MTX monotherapy (very low certainty).
Dosing
  • - Induction: 25 mg weekly for 16–24 weeks.
  • - Maintenance: 15 mg weekly.
  • - Should be accompanied by daily folic acid supplementation.

Contraindication
  • - Use of methotrexate is contraindicated in women actively considering pregnancy or who are pregnant.


Monitoring & Harms
  • - Routine monitoring of complete blood counts and liver function tests is recommended for both thiopurines and methotrexate.
  • - Risks include bone marrow suppression, hepatotoxicity, infections, and nonmelanoma skin cancers.


Combination Therapy
  • - Focuses on combining biologics with immunomodulators.
TNF Antagonists + Immunomodulators (Recs 9-11)
  • - Evidence is mixed and depends on the specific agents and patient population.
Infliximab + Thiopurines
  • - In thiopurine-NAÏVE patients starting infliximab, AGA SUGGESTS using combination therapy over infliximab monotherapy (low certainty).
  • - Based on the SONIC trial, which showed higher remission rates with combination therapy.
SONIC Trial Data
  • - Sustained remission at 26 weeks: Combination therapy was associated with higher rates (RR, 1.28) compared to infliximab monotherapy.


Infliximab + Methotrexate
  • - NO RECOMMENDATION due to knowledge gap.
  • - The COMMIT trial showed no significant difference between combination therapy and monotherapy.

Adalimumab + Thiopurines/Methotrexate
  • - NO RECOMMENDATION due to knowledge gap.
  • - The DIAMOND trial (adalimumab + azathioprine) showed no difference in clinical remission, though endoscopic remission was higher at week 26.

Implementation Considerations
  • - Benefit is uncertain in patients who have previously failed immunomodulator monotherapy.
  • - Combination therapy may be beneficial in patients at high risk for immunogenicity (e.g., history of immunogenicity, re-exposure after a lapse, HLA-DQA1*05 carriers).


Non-TNF Biologics + Immunomodulators (Rec 12)
  • - Applies to vedolizumab, ustekinumab, risankizumab, mirikizumab, guselkumab.
  • - NO RECOMMENDATION in favor of or against combination therapy due to a knowledge gap.
  • - Post-hoc analyses and observational studies show no clear benefit.
Rationale
  • - Non-TNF biologics appear to be less immunogenic than TNF antagonists.
  • - Concomitant immunomodulators do not seem to significantly alter their pharmacokinetic properties (e.g., trough concentrations).



De-Escalation of Therapy (Recs 13 & 14)
  • - For patients in corticosteroid-free clinical remission for at least 6 months on combination therapy (TNF antagonist + immunomodulator).
Withdraw the IMMUNOMODULATOR (Conditional Rec)
  • - AGA SUGGESTS withdrawing the immunomodulator.
  • - Meta-analysis showed no significant difference in relapse risk (16.8% with withdrawal vs. 14.9% continued combo).
  • - Rationale: Reduce long-term risks of immunomodulator (infection, malignancy).
Consider Continuing Combo Therapy If:
  • - Patient places high value on avoiding even a small risk of relapse.
  • - Patient is at high risk of immunogenicity (e.g., prior failure, low trough levels, HLA-DQA1*05 carrier).


AGAINST Withdrawing the TNF ANTAGONIST (Conditional Rec)
  • - AGA suggests AGAINST withdrawing the TNF antagonist.
  • - Meta-analysis showed a significantly higher risk of clinical relapse.
  • - Relapse rate: 30.6% with TNF antagonist withdrawal vs. 11.2% with continued combo therapy.


Early Advanced Therapy (Rec 15)
  • - Compares upfront use of advanced therapy vs. a step-up approach (corticosteroids/immunomodulators first).
Recommendation
  • - AGA SUGGESTS upfront use of advanced therapy over step-up therapy.
  • - This is a conditional recommendation based on very low certainty evidence.

Evidence Summary
  • - Meta-analysis of 3 RCTs showed the benefit for clinical remission at 12 months was uncertain.
  • - However, there was a trend towards a lower risk of disease-related complications (hospitalization, surgery, fistula, stricture) with early combination therapy.
PROFILE Study Highlight
  • - In newly diagnosed patients, early combo therapy led to significantly higher rates of steroid- and surgery-free remission (79% vs 15%) and endoscopic healing (67% vs 44%).


Implementation Considerations
  • - All trials used combination therapy with a TNF antagonist and an immunomodulator.
  • - Corticosteroids may still be used for initial symptom management while initiating advanced therapy.


Treat-to-Target Strategies (Rec 16)
  • - Compares treating to a target of endoscopic remission vs. symptomatic remission.
Recommendation
  • - NO RECOMMENDATION in favor of or against treating to a target of endoscopic remission.
  • - This is a knowledge gap due to very low certainty of evidence from available RCTs (STARDUST, REACT-2).

Evidence Summary
  • - The two available RCTs showed no significant differences in achieving clinical or endoscopic remission with a treat-to-endoscopic-target strategy vs. usual care/symptom-based strategy.
  • - There was significant heterogeneity between the trials in design and patient populations.
Context & Rationale
  • - While observational data suggest achieving endoscopic remission leads to better long-term outcomes, RCTs have not yet proven that an algorithmic strategy of treating toward this target improves outcomes over a symptom-based approach.
