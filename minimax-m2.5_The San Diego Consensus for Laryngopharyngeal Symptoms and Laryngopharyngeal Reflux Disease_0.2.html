The San Diego Consensus for Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, 2026 (Vol. 121)
  • Topic: Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS) and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD)
  • Author(s): Yadlapati et al. (28-member international interdisciplinary working group)
1. Introduction and Background
1.1 Clinical Context and Problem Statement
  • Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR) is widely used in clinical practice as descriptor for proximal esophageal and aerodigestive symptoms, yet lacks finite diagnostic criteria
  • LPR accounts for up to 10% of otolaryngology and gastroenterology visits
  • Healthcare costs reported 5.4 times higher than for gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), primarily from prolonged acid suppression trials and diagnostic uncertainty
  • Significant negative impact on quality of life: higher rates of anxiety, depression, and impaired social functioning, regardless of diagnosis method
  • Life-threatening long-term sequelae (e.g., cancer) are infrequent
1.2 Evidence Gap
  • Many symptoms attributed to retrograde flow of gastric contents to upper aerodigestive tract, yet few LPS are directly linked to this mechanism
  • Evidence of GERD is not uniformly present despite LPR being categorized as extraesophageal GERD
  • As many as 60% of patients diagnosed with LPR have normal objective ambulatory reflux monitoring studies
  • This challenges the theory that reflux is mechanism underlying most presentations → objective evidence of LPR disease (LPRD) is low
2. Methods
2.1 Working Group Composition
  • 28-member international interdisciplinary working group:
  • 12 esophageal expert gastroenterologists/physiologists (GIs)
  • 9 laryngologists (ENTs)
  • 6 speech-language pathologists (SLPs)
  • 1 clinical psychologist
  • Timeline: 1-year initiative (October 2023 to October 2024)
  • Meetings: 5 virtual, 2 hybrid (Hong Kong May 2024, Austria October 2024)
2.2 Subgroups and Process
  • Six subgroups: (i) definition/terminology, (ii) initial diagnostic evaluation, (iii) reflux monitoring, (iv) therapeutic trial, (v) behavioral factors/therapy, (vi) risk stratification
  • Each subgroup led by 2 interdisciplinary co-chairs → literature review, statement development, evidence presentation
2.3 Voting Methodology
  • RAND Appropriateness Method: formally validated methodology for determining statement appropriateness
  • Independent electronic voting via UC San Diego Research Electronic Database Capture
  • Statements ranked as appropriate with ≥80% agreement included as final recommendations
3. Definition and Terminology
3.1 Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS)
  • Definition: Chronic and frequent throat and upper airway symptoms that have potential to be induced by retrograde flow of gastric content to the upper esophagus, pharynx, and larynx
  • Frequency criterion: Symptoms occurring at least twice weekly
  • Duration criterion: Present for 8 weeks or more
LPS Symptoms (with agreement levels):
  • Cough (89% agreement)
  • Regurgitation (89%)
  • Throat pain/sore throat (89%)
  • Throat clearing (86%)
  • Excess phlegm (82%)
  • Hoarseness/voice change (80%)
Important Notes:
  • Multiple mechanisms can lead to LPS; term does not implicate reflux as etiology
  • All aforementioned symptoms are poorly specific for true LPRD → presence of LPS does NOT equate to LPRD
  • Globus is important symptom warranting evaluation (often negative predictor of reflux physiology)
  • Symptoms NOT meeting agreement: tongue burning, nasal burning, postnasal drip, halitosis, belching, dysphagia
3.2 Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD)
  • Definition: Requires chronic troublesome LPS + objective evidence supporting relationship between symptoms and gastroesophageal reflux
  • Prevalence: While LPS is high, proportion with objective LPRD on diagnostic evaluation is low (~35%)
  • Objective evidence required: LPS AND either:
  • (i) Concurrent esophageal reflux-like symptoms with LPS that respond to trial of anti-reflux therapy, OR
  • (ii) Diagnostic evidence of gastroesophageal reflux on upper GI endoscopy or ambulatory reflux monitoring
3.3 Symptom Categorization
  • LPS can occur with esophageal reflux symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain) OR
  • LPS can occur independently (isolated LPS)
  • Diagnostic and management algorithms differ based on this distinction
4. Diagnostic Evaluation: Role of Laryngoscopy
4.1 Key Principles
  • Laryngoscopy is necessary in evaluating LPS primarily to rule out alternate laryngeal pathologies including neoplasia
  • LPRD cannot be diagnosed based on laryngoscopy findings alone
  • Primary role: evaluate for other non-reflux related otolaryngologic processes including malignancy
4.2 Laryngoscopic Findings Limitations
  • Findings on white light examination (infraglottic edema, interarytenoid mucosal hypertrophy, erythematous changes, postcricoid mucosal edema) are nonspecific for LPRD
  • May result from other causes of laryngeal inflammation or be found in healthy, asymptomatic individuals
  • LPRD often erroneously assumed to cause LPS despite changes in other vibratory parameters (symmetry, amplitude, mucosal wave, glottic closure) visualized by stroboscopy
4.3 Clinical Recommendations
  • Laryngoscopic finding instruments (Reflux Finding Score, Reflux Sign Assessment) should NOT be used to diagnose LPRD due to suboptimal specificity
  • When LPS includes hoarseness not explained by white light flexible laryngoscopy → videostroboscopic examination should be performed to evaluate for other etiologies
  • Benign vocal fold lesions, keratotic/premalignant dysplastic/malignant lesions can present with dysphonia, chronic throat clearing, globus sensation → often ascribed to LPRD
5. Symptom Characterization and Medical Trials
5.1 Initial Assessment Algorithm
  • Presence of concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms vs. absence (isolated LPS) should be determined early
  • Algorithms differ for: (i) LPS with concurrent esophageal symptoms vs. (ii) isolated LPS
5.2 Empiric Antireflux Trials
  • When appropriate: LPS with concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms
  • Trial components:
  • PPI (omeprazole 20 mg) twice daily for 3 months
  • May include alginate therapy (4 times daily: after meals and before bedtime)
  • Dietary and lifestyle modifications
  • Alginate therapy may augment acid suppression effectiveness
5.3 Isolated LPS Management
  • Acid-reducing medications NOT recommended as first-line therapy without demonstration of diagnostic evidence of reflux
  • Esophageal evaluation recommended instead of empiric medical trials
6. Upper Gastrointestinal Endoscopy
6.1 Conclusive Evidence of GERD
  • Los Angeles grades B, C, D esophagitis
  • Biopsy-proven Barrett's esophagus
  • Using Lyon Consensus paradigms, these findings establish GERD presence when encountered off acid suppressive therapy
6.2 Supportive Findings
  • Disrupted esophagogastric junction (e.g., hiatal hernia, Hill grade)
  • Recent multicenter study: up to 47% of patients presenting with LPS demonstrated erosive findings and/or hiatal hernia
  • Findings may be present in both patients with esophageal symptoms AND those with isolated LPS
6.3 Transnasal Esophagoscopy
  • Office-based transnasal esophagoscopy can screen for erosive esophagitis and Barrett's esophagus
  • Shown to be similarly diagnostic compared with standard upper endoscopy
  • Should be performed by providers with specialized training in esophageal endoscopy
6.4 Cervical Inlet Patch
  • Area of heterotopic gastric mucosa in proximal esophagus
  • May secrete acid, mucus, or pepsin; can be associated with LPS and globus
  • Ablation shown to reduce LPS in small case series
  • Panel could NOT reach agreement on value of identifying/treating inlet patch (limited evidence)
7. Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring for Evaluation of LPS
7.1 Role and Key Principles
  • Ambulatory reflux monitoring is the reference standard for diagnosing LPRD
  • Essential evidence before endoscopic or surgical antireflux interventions (invasive, irreversible, expensive)
  • In patients with isolated LPS → lower pretest likelihood of GERD → early ambulatory reflux monitoring helps confirm or exclude GERD and personalize management
  • In absence of endoscopic evidence of GERD → ambulatory reflux monitoring is reference standard for diagnosing LPRD
7.2 Clinical Indications
  • Next step in evaluating patients with LPS and concurrent esophageal symptoms who do not respond to empiric trials of antisecretory and/or alginate therapy, in absence of endoscopic evidence of GERD
  • Should be considered in patients requiring long-term medical antireflux management → primarily to confirm pathologic reflux burden before long-term acid suppressive therapy
  • Diagnostic evaluation of whether LPS are potentially related to reflux first requires determining whether pathologic gastroesophageal reflux is present
  • Objective evidence of GERD on reflux testing in patients with LPS does NOT necessarily implicate reflux as causative of patient's LPS
  • Absence of GERD makes it unlikely that GERD is mechanism underlying LPS or that antireflux therapies will improve LPS
8. Modality and Interpretation of Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
8.1 Testing Requirements
  • Performed off acid suppression therapy in patients with unproven GERD
  • Two primary modalities: 24-hour multichannel intraluminal pH-impedance monitoring AND prolonged wireless pH monitoring
  • Both provide measurement of distal esophageal acid exposure time (AET)
  • Each modality provides differing capabilities and value → not mutually exclusive
8.2 pH-Impedance Monitoring
  • Offers added ability to detect proximal reflux events with greater detail depending on probe placement and catheter configuration
  • Particularly important since hypopharyngeal events may be more likely to contribute to LPS
  • Superior to traditional esophageal pH monitoring in detecting proximal esophageal reflux events
  • Hypopharyngeal reflux events demonstrated using dual channel or hypopharyngeal-esophageal multichannel intraluminal impedance with dual pH (HEMII-pH) catheters
  • These events often occur as weakly-acidic or nonacidic reflux episodes that may predict treatment outcomes
  • HEMII-pH catheter configurations identified pharyngeal reflux events in 81% of 42 patients with suspected reflux-mediated pathology in LPS
  • Includes 72% who tested negative for esophageal-only criteria → highlights diagnostic gap addressed by standard dual-channel pH-impedance monitoring
  • Panel could NOT reach agreement for thresholds of >80 esophageal reflux events and >10 hypopharyngeal events to be considered abnormal → further research needed
8.3 Prolonged Wireless pH Monitoring
  • Uses single pH sensor positioned in distal esophagus during sedated endoscopy → wirelessly transmits data to recorder for up to 96 hours
  • Major advantage: capacity to monitor for prolonged periods and capture variability rather than relying solely on 24-hour snapshot
  • Significant day-to-day variability in esophageal acid exposure
  • When goal is highest diagnostic accuracy in demonstrating pathologic GERD → 96-hour prolonged wireless pH monitoring outperforms 24-hour or 48-hour studies
  • Double-blind, multicenter trial of 100 patients with troublesome esophageal reflux symptoms and inadequate PPI response:
  • Participants with 0 days of elevated AET had 10 times increased odds of stopping PPI compared to those with 4 days of elevated AET
  • When AET elevated on 2 or more days, odds ratio for predicting need to continue PPI treatment was 5.3 (95% CI 2.19–13.44)
  • Recent guidance positions 96-hour wireless pH testing off antisecretory therapy as preferred test for unproven GERD
  • Prolonged pH monitoring provides higher diagnostic yield of pathologic GERD in patients with LPS
  • Recent multicenter international study of 813 patients: diagnostic yield of GERD in patients referred for LPS was 50% on prolonged wireless pH monitoring vs 27% on 24-hour pH impedance monitoring (including HEMII-pH)
8.4 Interpretation Criteria
Definitive Abnormal (Proven GERD):
  • Distal esophageal AET >6.0% on ambulatory reflux monitoring → meets criteria for proven GERD
  • On 96-hour wireless pH: AET >6.0% on 2 or more days of monitoring
Physiologic (Exclusionary):
  • Distal esophageal AET <4.0% is considered physiologic
  • On 96-hour prolonged wireless pH: AET <4.0% on all 4 days predicts low likelihood of symptom response with antisecretory therapy
  • AET >4.0% on 1 or more day of monitoring → supportive but not conclusive
Positive Symptom-Reflux Association:
  • Strengthens diagnostic confidence for LPRD
  • Particularly valuable on 96-hour wireless pH monitoring
8.5 Oropharyngeal pH Monitoring Limitations
  • Introduced as minimally invasive pharyngeal pH probe to detect pharyngeal acid including aerosolized acid (Restech Dx-pH probe)
  • NOT specific → cannot detect weakly-acidic or nonacidic reflux
  • Has poor concordance with traditional pH-impedance monitoring
  • Does NOT correlate with clinical outcomes
  • Panel agreed: oropharyngeal pH monitoring alone has poor specificity for LPRD in patients with LPS → NOT supported as stand-alone tool

9. Laryngeal Hyperresponse and Hypervigilance
9.1 Conceptual Framework
  • The larynx is critical intersection of breathing, communication, and swallowing
  • These processes can become measurably disrupted with pathologic reflux, stress, vagal sensitivity, and sequelae from medical comorbidities
  • Term "laryngoresponders" first coined by Aronson in 1990 → describes predisposition (by physical makeup or personality) to manifest emotional distress through sensory changes in larynx or voice
  • Survey of 1,217 adults: 16%–46% of general population self-identifies as laryngoresponders
9.2 Laryngeal Hyperresponse
  • Manifests as repetitive laryngeal behaviors such as throat clearing or coughing, breath-holding, or biomechanical strain at vocal folds
  • Can alter voice quality or cause globus sensation
  • May contribute to or be primary etiology of LPS with or without true LPRD
9.3 Laryngeal Hypervigilance
  • Definition: increased threat-induced attention to the larynx
  • Often coexists with symptom-specific anxiety, fear/worry about LPS and perceived consequences
  • Psychological processes that can influence LPS
9.4 Symptom-Specific Anxiety
  • Fear/worry about LPS and perceived consequences
  • Can contribute to or be primary etiology of LPS with and without pathologic reflux
9.5 Psychological Contribution
  • People may be predisposed to manifest emotional distress through their larynx → may contribute to or be primary etiology of LPS
  • Both general psychological processes (e.g., depression) and symptom-specific cognitive/behavioral processes (e.g., laryngeal hypervigilance, symptom-specific anxiety) can contribute to LPS symptom perception
  • Patients with LPS and typical esophageal symptoms may report increased psychological symptoms such as depression, hypervigilance, and/or symptom-specific anxiety
9.6 The Laryngeal Cognitive-Affective Tool (LCAT)
  • Validated patient-reported outcome (PRO) instrument
  • Helps distinguish candidates with elevated levels of hypervigilance and anxiety related to LPS
  • Identifies individuals who may improve with behavioral intervention
9.7 Clinical Implications
  • Patients with LPS being evaluated for LPRD who have negative reflux monitoring studies and nonresponse to trial of acid reducing therapy should be considered for:
  • Disorder of brain-larynx interaction
  • Hypervigilance
  • Nongastrointestinal process
10. Laryngeal Behavioral Therapy
10.1 Role of Speech-Language Pathologists (SLPs)
  • SLPs specializing in voice and upper airway disorders offer interventions aimed at modifying dysregulated patterns
  • Use mechanical reorganization and healthful cognitive perspectives associated with LPS
  • Precedent for interventions working in individuals with:
  • Confirmed reflux
  • Isolated cough
  • Inducible laryngeal obstruction/paradoxical vocal fold movement
  • Muscle tension dysphonia
10.2 Laryngeal Recalibration Therapy (LRT)
  • Novel structured approach that infuses traditional SLP techniques with:
  • Heart rate variability biofeedback
  • Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)
  • Addresses perceived and confirmed irritation manifesting as cough, throat-clearing, dyspnea, and voice change
  • Individuals with LPS can undergo initial laryngeal recalibration training provided by voice-specialized speech-language pathologist
10.3 Clinical Application Guidelines
  • If LPS is accompanied by typical reflux symptoms → LRT can follow trial of dietary/lifestyle modification and acid suppression
  • Upon completion of laryngeal recalibration training,
10.3 Clinical Application Guidelines (continued)
  • Patients with ongoing hypervigilance, anxiety, depression, and LPS symptoms may benefit from psychological intervention
  • Behavioral therapies used to address psychological targets (e.g., cognitive behavioral therapy) can be used alone or in combination with other modalities (LRT, neuromodulators, dietary management)
10.4 Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
  • If psychological processes continue to affect LPS and/or patients experience incomplete relief after LRT or pharmacologic neuromodulation
  • Targets specific cognitive (e.g., "my symptoms will never get better"), emotional (e.g., fear, worry), physiological (increased autonomic arousal), and behavioral processes (avoidance of foods, laryngeal hypervigilance)
  • NOT designed to address comorbid general mental health disorders
  • Focused on psychological processes specifically affecting LPS
  • Delivered by mental health professional with specialized health psychology training
11. Pharmacologic Neuromodulation
11.1 Role and Indications
  • May attenuate laryngeal hypersensitivity
  • Consider in:
  • LPRD patients with suboptimal response to antisecretory therapy or SLP intervention
  • Isolated LPS with negative esophageal testing
11.2 Evidence Base
  • Current clinical evidence for neuromodulators, notably gabapentin and pregabalin, focuses primarily on treatment of chronic cough through mediation of vagal hypersensitivity
  • Neuromodulators may improve laryngeal hypersensitivity
11.3 Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
  • Commonly prescribed for treatment of functional GI disorders
  • May help LPS symptoms but anticholinergic side effects (e.g., xerostomia) should be observed
  • Xerostomia may aggravate LPS
11.4 Gabapentin and Pregabalin
  • May be effective adjunct to laryngeal recalibration therapy for LPS, especially cough
  • Delta ligands that have demonstrated value in cough
11.5 SSRIs and SNRIs
  • Have NOT been studied specifically in LPS
  • Individuals with LPS and clinical anxiety and/or depression may derive benefit
  • Can be considered in individuals who have concomitant anxiety and depression
11.6 Clinical Recommendations Summary
  • If individuals with isolated LPS and negative reflux testing experience persistent symptoms after laryngeal recalibration training → pharmacologic neuromodulation may be helpful
  • Neuromodulators and laryngeal recalibration training may be helpful in patients with LPRD and partial or poor response to acid suppression therapy
12. Patient-Reported Outcome Instruments and Risk Stratification Tools
12.1 GERD-Specific PRO Instruments
  • Common instruments (GERDQ, GERD-HRQL) are valuable for assessing severity and treatment response of esophageal symptoms
  • Have suboptimal diagnostic yield for LPS and LPRD
  • Useful for assessment of esophageal symptom severity and evaluating treatment response but NOT specific for LPS and LPRD
12.2 LPRD-Specific PRO Instruments
  • Reflux Symptom Index (RSI): symptoms assessed are nonspecific for LPRD but can be used to follow many laryngological complaints over time
  • RSI cutoff previously considered clinically significant (≥13) could NOT distinguish patients with increased proximal/pharyngeal reflux or significant symptom correlation on HEMII-pH testing
  • Reflux Symptom Score (RSS): developed in conjunction with HEMII-pH testing; demonstrates higher specificity and sensitivity in predicting increased proximal reflux burden and treatment outcomes
  • However, not considered definitively diagnostic of LPRD based on current evidence by the panel
  • LPR-HRQL: not specific for diagnosing LPRD
12.3 General Voice and Cough Instruments
  • Voice Handicap Index-10 (VHI)
  • Leicester Cough Questionnaire (LCQ)
  • Cough Severity Index (CSI)
  • Value unclear in diagnosing LPRD → should only be used as prescribed PROs, NOT diagnostic instruments
12.4 Risk Stratification Tools
  • HAS-BEER: developed to help direct initial diagnostic and management strategies in patients with LPS
  • COuGH ReFlX: validated clinical score to predict GERD in patients with chronic laryngeal symptoms
  • Hold promise in aiding clinicians by classifying patients into risk categories for LPRD based on clinical and demographic factors
  • More data needed before full adoption in consensus algorithm for LPS/LPRD
  • Further validation of these scores for treatment outcomes and in diverse populations represents important future research
13. The San Diego Consensus Paradigm: Clinical Application
13.1 Initial Evaluation Framework
  • Patients meeting definition for LPS → initial evaluation includes:
  • Considering non-GI causes of symptoms
  • Laryngoscopy to evaluate for nonreflux-related causes of LPS
  • Discerning presence of concomitant esophageal reflux symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation)
13.2 LPS with Concomitant Esophageal Reflux Symptoms (Algorithm Left Side)
  • Personalized lifestyle and empiric trials of acid suppression appropriate
  • Symptom assessment at 3 months
  • Those with esophageal symptom and LPS response → likely have LPRD → continue management
  • Those with isolated esophageal symptom response may have ongoing LPS with GERD overlap → can continue management, important to consider disordered laryngeal behavioral response
  • For partial or no response of esophageal symptoms or LPS → next step is objective esophageal testing and consideration of disordered laryngeal behavioral response
  • For any patient with LPS and concomitant esophageal reflux symptoms → objective esophageal testing to assess for GERD/LPRD required before invasive antireflux therapy (endoscopic or surgical intervention)
  • Also should be considered if long-term antireflux management is planned
13.3 Isolated LPS (Algorithm Right Side)
  • Critical to consider disordered laryngeal behavioral response at outset
  • Suggested to proceed with objective esophageal testing before any antireflux management to personalize management
  • Dashed curved arrow highlights that disordered laryngeal behaviors can coexist/overlap in setting of objective GERD/LPRD and be driver of symptoms
  • If disordered laryngeal behaviors suspected → speech-language pathology-guided behavioral therapy (e.g., LRT), neuromodulation, and/or cognitive behavioral therapy can be trialed
13.4 Paradigm of Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms and Disease
  • Presence of LPS does NOT equate to LPRD
  • More commonly, patients with LPS have no objective evidence of reflux → evaluation should focus on nongastrointestinal causes including brain-larynx interaction
  • For patients with objective evidence of LPRD → therapeutic constructs include antireflux management and therapy focused on laryngeal behavioral responses
  • Laryngeal involvement does NOT implicate mental health disorder; symbiotic relationship exists between various stressors and laryngeal hyperresponsiveness independent of diagnosed disorder of affect
13.5 Laryngeal Behavioral Response Model
  • Cyclical relationship between:
  • Laryngeal hypersensitivity
  • Laryngeal hyperresponse
  • Laryngeal hypervigilance
  • Can contribute to or drive LPS burden
14. Mechanisms of Laryngopharyngeal Reflux
14.1 Pathophysiological Mechanisms
  • Physiological mechanisms underlying LPR often unclear
  • Simplistically: reflux of gastric contents to upper aerodigestive tract implies incompetence of both lower and upper esophageal sphincters
  • Several potential mechanisms for LPR giving rise to symptoms:
  • Neurogenic
  • Inflammatory
  • Behavioral
  • Motility related
14.2 Direct vs Indirect Injury
  • Hypothesis: injury to larynx and pharynx occurs:
  • Directly: through exposure to gastric acid, pepsin, and bile salts
  • Indirectly: through repetitive mucosal trauma from vagally mediated or reactive cough and throat clearing
14.3 Esophageal Dysfunction Pathways
  • May affect tracheobronchial airway by:
  • Reflux pathway (microaspiration)
  • Reflex pathway (vagally mediated airway stimulation)
14.4 Cognitive-Behavioral Perpetuation
  • Heightened cognitive-behavioral responses may perpetuate laryngeal behaviors
14.5 Why Traditional Therapies Often Fail
  • Traditional antireflux therapies do NOT address varied mechanisms
  • Fewer than 50% of LPS patients respond to antisecretory therapy
  • Antireflux surgery frequently yields suboptimal outcomes
15. Management Recommendations Summary
15.1 Antisecretory Therapy
  • Type, dose, frequency, and duration vary widely in literature
  • Unit dose PPI (e.g., omeprazole 20 mg) up to twice daily for 3 months recommended for LPS response
  • Novel class: potassium competitive acid blockers → approved for reflux-driven conditions including nonerosive reflux disease and erosive esophagitis; could be used in place of PPI therapy
15.2 Alginate Therapies
  • Inexpensive, generally well-tolerated
  • May reduce patient perception of globus
  • May offer increased benefit compared with PPI monotherapy
15.3 Lifestyle Modifications for Managing GERD
  • Reduction of intragastric pressure: weight loss/management, avoiding tight fitting clothing around abdomen
  • Minimizing postprandial reflux: maintaining upright posture, avoiding recumbency 2–3 hours after meals, practicing diaphragmatic breathing
  • Modifying sleeping position for nocturnal reflux: head of bed elevation, sleeping in left lateral position
  • Avoiding foods that trigger symptoms
15.4 Personalizing Management
  • Recommendations regarding reflux management beyond scope of current initiative
  • Requires consideration of:
  • Available expertise
  • Response to previous treatment trials
15.5 When LPS Fails to Respond
  • In case of isolated LPS or when LPS fail to respond to antireflux therapies even in confirmed LPRD
  • Overlap with laryngeal hyperresponsiveness and hypervigilance should be considered
  • Behavioral therapy and/or neuromodulation recommended
16. Future Directions and Research Priorities
16.1 Validation Needs
  • Risk stratification tools (HAS-BEER, COuGH ReFlX) need further validation for:
  • Treatment outcomes
  • Diverse populations
16.2 Reflux Event Thresholds
  • Identifying clinically relevant reflux episode thresholds requires further research
  • Panel could not reach agreement for thresholds of >80 esophageal reflux events and >10 hypopharyngeal events to be considered abnormal
16.3 Inlet Patch
  • Further research including controlled studies of ablation impact needed to establish importance of identifying this endoscopic finding in patients with LPS
16.4 Potassium Competitive Acid Blockers
  • Outcomes research focused on LPS will clarify role in LPR treatment
