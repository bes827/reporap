Introduction & Background
  • - This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to determine if Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI) use is associated with improved outcomes after Endoscopic Band Ligation (EBL) in cirrhotic patients.
Burden of Liver Cirrhosis
  • - A critical global health concern with increasing prevalence.
  • - Affects an estimated 112 million individuals worldwide.
  • - Results in 1.48 million deaths annually (2019 data).

Gastroesophageal Varices (GEVs)
  • - A major complication of portal hypertension in advanced liver disease.
Prevalence
  • - Present in 30%-40% of patients with compensated cirrhosis.
  • - Present in 48%-85% of patients with decompensated cirrhosis.

Bleeding Risk & Mortality
  • - Annual bleeding risk: 10%-15%.
  • - 6-week mortality rate post-bleed: 15-20%.
  • - Rebleeding rate without endoscopic treatment: up to 60%, with mortality reaching 33%.


Management of GEVs
  • - Focus on primary and secondary prophylaxis to mitigate bleeding risk.
Pharmacological Therapy
  • - Non-selective beta-blockers (NSBBs) are central to management.
  • - Preferred agent per BAVENO VII consensus: Carvedilol.

Endoscopic Variceal Ligation (EVL/EBL)
  • - Primary Prophylaxis: Recommended for high-risk varices in patients intolerant to NSBBs.
  • - Secondary Prophylaxis: Combination of EVL + NSBBs is the standard of care, reducing rebleeding by 27%-38% compared to monotherapy.
  • - Acute Bleeding: EVL is the cornerstone of endoscopic therapy.


The PPI Controversy Post-EBL
  • - Ulceration at ligation sites is a known cause of rebleeding.
Rationale for Use
  • - PPIs are widely used to promote mucosal healing and prevent ulcer-related bleeding.
  • - Periprocedural administration reported in up to 80% of patients undergoing prophylactic EBL.

Concerns & Inconsistent Data
  • - Efficacy in variceal bleeding is controversial.
  • - Bleeding is driven by portal hypertension, not acid-related injury.
  • - Potential increased risks in cirrhosis: spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, C. difficile infection, pneumonia, and hepatic encephalopathy.

Guideline Divergence
  • - ASGE (older): Supported use.
  • - ESGE & Baveno VII (recent): Provide weak recommendations against routine PPI use after acute variceal bleeding management.



Meta-Analysis Overview
  • - 11 studies included (4 RCTs, 7 observational).
  • - Total of 7,459 cirrhotic patients undergoing EBL.
Patient Demographics
  • - Mean age: 55.5 years.
  • - Sex: 75% male.
  • - Mean MELD score: 16.8.
Child-Pugh Classification (n=5,865)
  • - Class A: 47%
  • - Class B: 34%
  • - Class C: 19%


Primary Outcomes
  • - Post-EBL Ulcer Bleeding: Bleeding with endoscopic proof of a post-banding ulcer as the source.
  • - Post-EBL Bleeding (Overall): Any clinical/endoscopic evidence of UGIB after EBL.
  • - All-Cause Mortality: Death from any cause.
Follow-up Period
  • - All primary outcomes were assessed within an 8-week follow-up period.


Secondary Outcomes
  • - Number and size of post-banding ulcers.
  • - Length of hospitalization.
  • - Adverse events (e.g., chest pain, dysphagia).


Key Findings & Results
  • - The most rigorous analyses (adjusted observational data and RCTs) did not show a statistically significant benefit for PPI use in reducing bleeding or mortality.
Post-EBL Bleeding (Overall)
  • - Analysis of all 11 studies (n=7,459).
Combined Unadjusted Analysis
  • - Showed a statistically significant reduction in bleeding with PPIs.
  • - OR: 0.76 (95% CI, 0.62 - 0.92).
  • - Finding Not Robust: Became non-significant in leave-one-out analysis when the Hu et al. study was removed.

Adjusted Observational Data
  • - Pooled analysis of 5 studies (n=5,907).
  • - No significant benefit for PPIs.
  • - OR: 0.64 (95% CI, 0.33 - 1.23).

RCTs Only (Highest Quality)
  • - Pooled analysis of 4 RCTs (n=445).
  • - No significant benefit for PPIs.
  • - RR: 0.71 (95% CI, 0.39 - 1.30).
Trial Sequential Analysis (TSA)
  • - Confirmed that the cumulative RCT evidence is underpowered.
  • - Required Information Size (RIS) for a definitive conclusion: 2,501 patients.
  • - Current cumulative sample size: 445 patients (17.8% of RIS).



Post-EBL Ulcer Bleeding (Mechanistic Outcome)
  • - This is the most specific outcome for evaluating PPI efficacy.
Combined Unadjusted Analysis
  • - Showed a significant reduction in ulcer bleeding with PPIs (OR 0.76).
  • - Finding Not Robust: Became non-significant when the Hu et al. study was removed.

RCTs Only
  • - Within 8 weeks: No statistically significant effect (RR 0.54, 95% CI 0.25 - 1.16).
  • - Within 2 weeks: No statistically significant effect (RR 0.59, 95% CI 0.27 - 1.27).
  • - The average time to bleed from a post-EBL ulcer is 11 days.


All-Cause Mortality
  • - Analysis of 6 studies (n=959).
Combined Unadjusted Analysis
  • - Showed a significant reduction in mortality with PPIs (OR 0.48).
  • - Finding Not Robust: Became non-significant in leave-one-out sensitivity analysis.

RCTs Only
  • - Pooled analysis of 3 RCTs (n=268).
  • - No significant benefit for PPIs.
  • - RR: 0.75 (95% CI, 0.23 - 2.53).


Secondary Outcomes Analysis
  • - Findings were either non-significant or inconsistent.
Length of Hospitalization
  • - No significant difference between PPI and control groups.

Post-Banding Ulcer Size & Number
  • - Data could not be meta-analyzed due to metric variability.
Narrative Summary of 3 RCTs
  • - Ulcer Size: Consistently and significantly smaller in the PPI group.
  • - Ulcer Number: No significant difference between groups.


Adverse Events
  • - Findings were inconsistent across studies.
Chest Pain
  • - Lashen et al: Incidence was significantly lower in the PPI group (39.6% vs. 73.6%).
  • - Shaheen et al: No significant difference.

Other Events
  • - No significant difference in total adverse events, odynophagia, or dysphagia.




Discussion & Interpretation
  • - The divergence in results suggests that apparent benefits in unadjusted analyses are likely driven by residual confounding rather than a true therapeutic effect.
Pathophysiological Disconnect
  • - GEV bleeding is driven by portal hypertension, not acid-related mucosal injury.
  • - EBL ulcers are mechanically induced and usually self-limiting.
  • - Reduced ulcer size with PPIs did not translate to a reduction in the clinically relevant outcome of rebleeding.

Limitations of the Evidence
  • - The current body of evidence has significant limitations.
Underpowered RCTs
  • - The primary limitation is the small cumulative sample size in RCTs, making the evidence inconclusive.

Heterogeneity
  • - Significant clinical heterogeneity across studies in:
  • - PPI type, dose, and duration.
  • - EBL indication (prophylaxis vs. emergency).
  • - Inclusion of non-specific bleeding events (e.g., recurrent variceal hemorrhage) dilutes any potential treatment effect.

Influence of a Single Study
  • - The observational study by Hu et al. had a substantial statistical influence, making the overall pooled estimates for bleeding unstable and not robust.



Conclusion & Clinical Recommendation
  • - The current evidence is insufficient to draw a definitive conclusion on the efficacy of PPIs after EBL.
Clinical Takeaway
  • - Given the lack of strong supporting evidence and the potential for harm (e.g., infections, HE), routine prophylactic PPI use after EBL cannot be recommended at this time.

Future Direction
  • - High-quality, adequately powered randomized trials are urgently needed to resolve this important clinical uncertainty.
