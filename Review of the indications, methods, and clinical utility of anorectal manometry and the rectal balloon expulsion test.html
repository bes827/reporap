Review of the indications, methods, and clinical utility of anorectal manometry and the rectal balloon expulsion test
  • Authors: Adil E. Bharucha, Guido Basilisco, Allison Malcolm, Tae Hee Lee, Matthew B. Hoy, S. Mark Scott, Satish S. C. Rao
  • Journal: Neurogastroenterology & Motility
  • Publication Date: 2022
  • DOI: 10.1111/nmo.14335
  • This review examines the indications, techniques, interpretation, strengths, and weaknesses of anorectal manometry (ARM) and the rectal balloon expulsion test.
  • It focuses on high-resolution ARM (HR-ARM), 3-dimensional high-resolution anorectal manometry (3D-HR-ARM), and portable ARM.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of anal resting tone is generated by the internal anal sphincter (IAS)?
  • 2. What is the recommended upper limit of normal for the rectal balloon expulsion test using a conventional balloon?
  • 3. What is the minimum number of participants recommended to estimate the 2.5 and 97.5 percentiles for normal values in ARM studies?
  • 4. In the London Classification, what other tests are incorporated with anal manometry and rectal sensation test?
  • 5. What is the primary criterion of interest during a Valsalva maneuver in anorectal manometry?
Introduction
  • ARM quantifies defecation and continence mechanisms.
  • It records IAS and EAS contractility/tone, rectal sensitivity/compliance, RAIR, EAS contraction to cough/Valsalva, and dynamic pressures during simulated evacuation.
  • Technological advances (HR-ARM, MRI proctography) enhance understanding of defecation/continence disorders.
Indications for ARM
  • Recommended initial test for diagnosing defecatory disorders (DD) in patients w/ chronic constipation, anal fissure, or chronic anorectal pain.
  • Useful for identifying anal weakness and rectal sensory disturbances in fecal incontinence (FI).
  • Assesses RAIR in Hirschsprung disease, megarectum, or megacolon.
Evolution of ARM
  • HR-ARM and 3D-HR-ARM are used more frequently than conventional catheters.
  • HR-ARM and 3D-HR-ARM measure circumferential pressures throughout the anal canal's longitudinal axis.
  • Portable ARM systems w/ single-use, air-charged catheters are available, increasing ARM utilization.
Standardization Efforts
  • International survey revealed variations in ARM methods among institutions.
  • The International Anorectal Physiology Working Group (IAPWG) developed recommendations to standardize methods and interpretation.
  • The London Classification uses manometry, rectal sensory testing, and balloon expulsion test for anorectal disorder diagnosis.
Methods
  • MEDLINE search for ARM articles (2008-2021) excluded reviews, animal/pediatric studies.
  • Search yielded 1133 results; 89 relevant citations included + 26 additional citations.
  • ARM systems vary in pressure-sensing tech, sensor distribution, pressure display, user-friendliness, pressure analysis, and catheter use (single vs. multiple).
Equipment
  • Systems differ in pressure-sensing technology, number and spatial distribution of pressure sensors, pressure display, user-friendliness, pressure analysis, and catheter use (single- vs. multiple-use).
Pressure Measurement
  • Pressures measured w/ water-perfused, solid-state, or air-charged catheters.
  • HR-ARM catheters measure circumferential pressures.
  • ManoScan (Medtronic) has 36 circumferential sensors per level. Unisensor has circumferential sensors in a silicone gel membrane.
  • HR-ARM systems offer advanced software and higher spatial resolution than non-HR-ARM.
Sensor Types
  • Older systems used nitrogen-pressurized water-perfused sensors. Newer systems use electronic pumps.
  • Solid-state catheters are less durable and have a shorter lifespan than water-perfused systems.
  • Pressure-sensing technology, number/orientation of sensors, and pressure displays vary among HR-ARM catheters.
3D-HR-ARM
  • HR-ARM systems provide multiple pressure values at locations along the anal canal.
  • ManoScan 3D-HR-ARM (Medtronic), HRAM-200 (Diversatek), and UniTip HRAM(3D) (MMS/Laborie) depict pressure topography in 3D.
  • Most catheters are 12F-16F, except Medtronic 3D-HR-ARM (32F) and Anopress (THD) (48F).
Thermal Drift
  • Manoscan HR-ARM susceptible to thermal drift (initial + baseline).
  • Thermal compensation software corrects for thermal effect but not baseline drift.
  • Residual drift affected clinical interpretation in ≈10% of patients.
Air-Charged Catheters
  • Air-charged catheters provide 1 (Anopress) or 4 (mcompass) anal pressure values.
  • T-DOC (Laborie) and mcompass measure rectal and anal pressure; Anopress measures only anal pressure.
  • Anopress cannot measure rectoanal gradient during evacuation.
  • Air-charged systems are portable, less expensive, and use 1 catheter. mcompass has biofeedback software.
  • HR-ARM and 3D-HR-ARM have higher spatial resolution than air-charged catheters.
Display and Analysis
  • HR-ARM systems display pressures as line plots or color contour plots. Color plots are easier to interpret.
  • Analysis often uses only the greatest value at any level in the canal.
  • eSleeve option (ManoScan) considers greatest pressure at any instant, averaged over 20s.
  • During evacuation, eSleeve identifies most positive (or least negative) rectoanal gradient over 20s.
  • HR-ARM anal pressures are correlated with, but greater than, non-HR-ARM pressures.
Test Procedure
  • Verbal consent is sufficient due to ARM's safety.
  • Inform patients about the desire to defecate during insertion/procedure and to report discomfort (suggests anal fissure). Constant reassurance is essential.
  • Mental stressors ↑ anal pressure in healthy/DD patients. Acute stress doesn't affect rectal sensation/compliance.
  • Fasting unnecessary; avoid caffeine 60 min before exam (↑ anal resting/squeeze pressure, rectal sensation).
Patient Preparation
  • ARM can be performed at any time of day.
  • Enemas are routinely administered at some centers if stool is detected. Probe placement 30 min post-enema is ideal.
  • Patients should be kept informed and reassured.
  • ARM is generally performed in the left lateral position with knees/hips bent at 90°.
  • Lubricated probe is gently inserted into the rectum. Catheter should be accurately oriented for 3D-HR-ARM.
  • Probe should remain in situ. Voluntary contraction may ↑ anal pressure upon insertion.
  • Pressures equilibrate in <90s; 150s is sufficient (less than IAPWG's 3 min recommendation).
Components
  • Evaluate RAIR, rectal sensation, and rectal balloon expulsion test (for DDs) during the same visit.
  • Details of each procedure (duration, squeeze maneuvers, pressure summarization) vary across sites.
  • IAPWG suggests a minimum standard ARM protocol, but users have limited ability to adjust specifications.
  • IAPWG protocol is primarily based on opinion, rather than evidence.
  • Recent study in women w/ FI found IAPWG protocol redundant; resting period can be abbreviated to 30s, and 2 squeeze maneuvers are sufficient.
Anal Resting Pressure
  • Anal resting pressure should be measured.
  • IAPWG suggests 60s duration. In women w/ FI, 30s is sufficient. In healthy persons, 20s is almost perfectly correlated vs. 60s and 300s.
  • Longer duration needed to identify ultra-slow waves (1-2 cycles/min), associated w/ chronic constipation/hemorrhoids (uncertain significance).

Analysis
  • Most software averages the highest anal pressure at every instant, then averages these values over 20 or 30s.
  • HRAM-200 (Diversatek) averages anal pressures across radial sensors, potentially yielding lower values than eSleeve.
Interpretation
  • Anal resting tone is generated by IAS (55%), EAS (35%), and hemorrhoidal plexus (15%).
  • In constipated women, 12% had low, and 4% had high anal resting pressures.
  • Anal resting pressure is increased in anal fissure or anal pain.
  • Anal fissure patients w/ ultra-slow waves have ↑ tone, impaired relaxation of IAS, and enhanced after-contraction, suggesting impaired nitrergic innervation.
Normal Range
  • Normal range of anal pressures is wide (e.g., 33-93 mm Hg in women ≥50 years).
  • Scleroderma, IAS injury (vaginal delivery, lateral sphincterotomy), or hemorrhoidectomy can reduce anal resting pressure.
  • Internal/external sphincter injury and patulous canal, but not puborectalis injury, predicted anal resting pressure in anorectal disorders.
  • Reduced anal resting pressure had 51% sensitivity and 70% specificity for identifying IAS injury or patulous canal.
  • Meta-analysis: maximum resting pressure had 0.60 sensitivity (95% CI, 0.38-0.79) and 0.93 specificity (95% CI, 0.80-0.97) for FI diagnosis.
High-Pressure Zone (HPZ)
  • Software computes HPZ length at rest.
  • For Manoscan HR-ARM, HPZ length is the length of the average pressure profile in the resting pressure frame.
  • HPZ is longer in asymptomatic men (3.9 cm) than women (3.4 cm); not correlated w/ age. Some DD patients have longer HPZ.
Anal Squeeze Pressure
  • Anal pressure is measured during voluntary anal sphincter contraction (≤20s, 2 occasions, 30s rest).
  • IAPWG recommends 1 long (30s) and 3 short (5s) squeeze maneuvers to assess endurance and maximum voluntary contraction.
  • Only anal pressure should ↑ during squeeze; ↑ rectal pressure reflects abdominal wall activation.
  • Monitor probe movement after maneuvers and adjust as needed. Frequent/resistive squeeze maneuvers evoke sphincter fatigue.

Analysis
  • Three parameters: absolute squeeze pressure, squeeze increment (squeeze - resting pressure), and squeeze duration. Definitions vary among programs.
  • Several programs identify peak pressure sustained for a few seconds.
  • Manoscan HR-ARM/3D-HR-ARM software erroneously measure squeeze increment relative to rectal resting pressure. Measured squeeze duration is often inaccurate.
Interpretation
  • Causes of ↓ squeeze pressure: noncompliance, failure to understand instructions, neuromuscular injury (obstetric).
  • Sexual abuse history associated w/ ↓ squeeze pressure, even w/o sphincter injury.
  • Weak squeeze pressures (and resting pressure) are relatively insensitive but more specific for identifying sphincter injury or patulous anal canal.
  • Anal imaging probably unnecessary when anal pressures at rest/squeeze are normal.
Muscle Fiber Types
  • EAS maintains resting tone via type 1 (fatigue-resistant, slow-twitch) fibers, predominant in human anal sphincter.
  • Squeeze duration <10s (non-HR-ARM) suggests ↓ endurance, associated w/ impaired continence for liquid, but not formed, stools.
  • Anal squeeze pressure was lower, but EAS was less fatigable in women w/ FI vs. asymptomatic women.
Anal Pressure During Cough or Valsalva Maneuver
  • Performed once or twice, useful for evaluating spinal reflex pathway to EAS in FI patients w/ low anal squeeze pressure.
  • Patient coughs or exhales into a balloon (20 mm Hg). Increased abdominal pressure triggers external sphincter contraction.
  • Avoid Valsalva in patients w/ retinopathy history.

Analysis
  • Pressure increment during Valsalva is the primary criterion of interest.
Interpretation
  • Low squeeze pressure + normal cough reflex may reflect impaired volitional control of EAS and/or damage to central motor pathways.
  • Low squeeze pressure + abnormal cough reflex suggests a defect in the sacral reflex arc.
  • Pressure change during cough was 10 mm Hg higher in HR-ARM than non-HR-ARM (not significant).
  • HR-ARM uncovered qualitative/quantitative differences in anorectal pressures during coughing between nulliparous/parous healthy volunteers and healthy volunteers/FI patients. Clinical significance unclear.
Rectoanal Pressures During Simulated Evacuation
  • Patients bear down as if to defecate (simulated evacuation preferred to defecation).
  • Maneuver repeated after distending a 50-ml rectal balloon at some centers (incremental utility unclear).
  • At least 30s should elapse before next attempted “defecation.” Coach patients to expel (not withhold) the probe.
  • Coaching changed diagnosis from “pathologic” to “normal” in some patients.

Analysis
  • Summarized by rectal pressure increment, anal relaxation (absolute pressure and % change), and rectoanal gradient.
  • Generally over the 2-s epoch during which the rectoanal pressure gradient is the most positive or least negative.
Interpretation
  • Defecation requires coordination between ↑ rectal pressure and relaxation of anal sphincters/pelvic floor.
  • Rectoanal pressure profile during simulated evacuation was useful for diagnosing DD in earlier studies.
  • Rectoanal pressure profiles suggest dyssynergia in ≈20% and 90% of healthy persons w/ non-HR-ARM and HR-ARM, respectively.
  • Differences may be due to inability to replicate defecation, artifact, absence of rectal filling, and/or left lateral position.
  • Findings undermine manometry's utility for diagnosing DD. Criteria for dyssynergia may not be ideally suited to discriminate between healthy people and DD.
  • HR-ARM criteria (e.g., rectoanal gradient during evacuation) can discriminate between patients w/ normal and abnormal BET.
  • Findings must be compared to age-/sex-matched normal values measured with the same technique.
RAIR and Rectal Sensation
  • Rectal balloon distension is very safe; perform in a graded manner to avoid rectal rupture.
  • Balloon distended w/ air in 10-ml increments until first sensation, then 20-ml increments to maximum tolerated volume (≤400 ml).
  • Each distension maintained for ≥30s. Patients report sensations (first sensation, desire to defecate, urgency, maximum tolerable sensation).
  • Rectal sensory thresholds are influenced by the stiffness of the rectal balloon. Latex-free balloons are less elastic.

Analysis
  • RAIR amplitude/duration depend on rate/volume of rectal distention.
  • HR-ARM software (e.g., Manoscan) considers RAIR present when anal relaxation is ≥25%.
  • Volume thresholds for rectal sensation are assessed.
  • Rectal balloon pressure (compliance) can be measured, but the balloon is relatively stiff.
  • Rectal compliance measured w/ manometry is less accurate than w/ a barostat.
Interpretation
  • If no RAIR, repeat after excluding fecal impaction, ↑ distending volume, and asking patient not to contract EAS.
  • Besides Hirschsprung disease, RAIR may be absent in dysganglionosis, postcircular myotomy, and lower anterior resection.
  • 3D-HR-ARM reveals spatial differences in anal relaxation during RAIR. No studies assess RAIR in Hirschsprung disease w/ HR-ARM.
  • Studies w/ non-HR-ARM: ↑ rectal sensation in urge incontinence, proctitis, IBS; ↓ rectal sensation in chronic constipation and diabetes w/ FI.
  • HR-ARM: ↓ rectal sensation in DD patients. Rectal sensory disturbances may be primary or secondary to abnormal rectal capacity/compliance.
  • Evaluate profound sensory disturbance w/ a barostat. Visceral hypersensitivity, abnormal transit, and psychological factors are associated w/ IBS symptoms.
  • Rectal barostat distention to 40 mm Hg was 96% sensitive and 72% specific for distinguishing between patients w/ vs. w/o IBS.
  • ≈25% of patients w/ chronic constipation have ↓ rectal sensation (hyposensitivity), often associated w/ attenuated/absent urge to defecate.
  • Biofeedback therapy to correct sensory disturbances is beneficial in FI. Use in DD not evaluated.
Interpretation of HR-ARM
  • ARM systems differ in design/methods; catheter-specific normal values are necessary.
  • No normal values for Diversatek HRAM-200, UniTip 3D, and T-DOC air-charged catheters.
  • ≥40 participants are required to estimate the 2.5 and 97.5 percentiles. Ideally, ≥120 reference values are required.
  • Association between rectoanal pressures and age, sex, parity, and BMI varies among studies.
  • Differences may be related to study populations vs. methods used for summarizing pressures.
Normal Values
  • Measurements w/ ManoScan HR-ARM/3D-HR-ARM and Diversatek catheter, but not UniTip, revealed ↓ anal resting/squeeze pressures in women vs. men, and ↓ anal resting pressure in older vs. younger people.
  • With UniTip, age was not significantly associated w/ anal pressures after adjusting for parity. Separate normal values for men, nulliparous women, and parous women are provided.
  • Rectal pressure during simulated evacuation is greater in older than in younger asymptomatic women/men.
  • Normal values should ideally be assessed in healthy persons w/ normal rectal balloon expulsion time, as up to 15% of asymptomatic healthy persons have prolonged rectal balloon expulsion time.
Refined Analysis
  • HR-ARM measures pressures in 2D (length of anal canal and time). 3D catheters also assess circumferential asymmetry.
  • Commercial software condenses data to summary variables (e.g., maximum squeeze pressure), representing pressures at one location over a few seconds.
  • eSleeve-based analysis (Medtronic) of rectoanal gradient uses the highest anal pressure at each instant to summarize anal pressure.
  • Gradient is generally not the greatest pressure difference between the rectum and the anal canal.
  • Specific sensor used to summarize anal pressure often moves over the 20-s evacuation period. Highest anal pressure measurement is prone to artifact.
Identifying Phenotypes of Functional DDs
  • Rectoanal pressure profile during simulated evacuation can be used for subtyping DD.
  • Using solid-state, non-HR-ARM, Rao et al. visually recognized that 30% of constipated patients had a normal rectoanal pressure profile.
  • Remainder had paradoxical anal contraction (type I), impaired propulsion (type II), or impaired anal relaxation w/ (type III) or w/o (type IV) adequate propulsion.
  • Experts agree on the characterization of rectoanal pressure profiles by HR-ARM as type I (κ = 0.71), IV dyssynergia (κ = 0.61), normal pattern (κ = 0.47), type II (κ = 0.40), and III dyssynergia (κ = 0.35).
Circumferential Pressure Topography
  • EAS has a triple-looped anatomy. Puborectalis surrounds the posterior/lateral aspects of the anal canal. Anal pressure profile is asymmetric, especially in the upper canal.
  • Symmetry (or lack thereof) in the anal pressure profile may provide insight into normal/disordered contraction/relaxation of EAS vs. puborectalis and weakness of these muscles.
  • Anal squeeze is normally generated by EAS and puborectalis, respectively, in and above the resting anal HPZ.
  • Alterations in pressure symmetry may suggest anal sphincter defects.
  • Compared to endoanal ultrasound, 3D-HR-ARM had false negative/positive rates of 14%/41% for IAS defects and 21%/30% for EAS defects.
  • Insufficient agreement between manometry and ultrasound could not support the reliable use of 3D-HR-ARM for diagnosis.
Posterior Distal Pressure Zone
  • Incomplete relaxation or paradoxical contraction of the puborectalis muscle may manifest as a persistent HPZ on the posterior aspect of the upper end of the anal sphincter.
Perineal Descent, Rectal Intussusception, and Rectal Prolapse
  • During simulated defecation, a band of high pressure extending from the rectal balloon to the anal canal strongly suggests rectal intussusception or prolapse.
  • Appearance is useful for distinguishing between early and more advanced rectal prolapse, which are generally associated w/ high and low anal pressures, respectively.
  • Perineal descent during evacuation can be estimated w/ 3D-HR-ARM; measurements are correlated w/ barium defecography.
Opportunities for Future Research
  • Expand the database of age- and sex-appropriate normal values with HR-ARM and interpret patient data relative to normal values for the technique.
  • Refine the assessment of anal pressure topography.
  • Use seated ARM.
  • Use an evidence-based process to standardize the performance of maneuvers during manometry.
  • Use adjunct maneuvers during manometry.
  • Investigate newer technologies for measuring anal sphincter functions.
Rectal Balloon Expulsion Test
  • Simple, outpatient test to assess rectal evacuation.
  • In suspected DD, perform w/ ARM or as a stand-alone test.
  • Time to evacuate a balloon filled w/ warm tap water (typically 50 ml) in the seated position is assessed.
  • Normal balloon expulsion time depends on the type of rectal balloon, is reproducible, and is inversely related to age.
Procedure and Normal Values
  • Test performed w/ latex or latex-free balloon. Upper limit of normal is 1 min. Shorter cutoff (e.g., 26 s) may be acceptable.
  • Latex-free balloons are set to a standard size. For a Foley catheter inflated to 50 ml, the upper limit of normal is 2 min.
  • Even w/ the 2-min cutoff, up to 25% of healthy persons would be misclassified as abnormal.
Technique Variations
  • Defecation is usually preceded by the desire to defecate. Some DD patients have reduced rectal sensation.
  • Patients can be asked to expel a balloon inflated until they experience the desire to defecate.
  • In patients w/ FC and DD, the BET identified those w/ DD, documented w/ defecography, w/ 88% sensitivity and 89% specificity.
  • Rectal balloon was inflated until patients experienced the desire to defecate, averaging 183 ml.
  • Further studies are necessary to compare fixed vs. variable balloon inflation.
Diagnostic Utility
  • In patients w/ anorectal symptoms, a prolonged BET was very specific (90%) for identifying reduced rectal evacuation measured w/ MRI.
  • Sensitivity of BET was only 27% compared to MRI. Compared to barium defecography, the BET had a sensitivity of 88%.
  • BET longer than 1 min had 71% sensitivity and 100% specificity for predicting paradoxical contraction based on ARM.
  • Normal BET may not always exclude a DD. Degree of agreement among major diagnostic assessments varies.
  • Gold standard remains unclear. Rectal balloon expulsion test cannot identify structural abnormalities.
  • Abnormal rectal balloon expulsion test predicts the response to biofeedback therapy.
  • Rectal balloon expulsion test is a useful, low-cost, radiation-free, outpatient assessment tool for impaired evacuation.
  • Optimum methodology, normal values, and clinical utility need further clarification.
Putting It Together
  • ARM is useful for diagnosing DD in constipated patients, identifying anal weakness/rectal sensory abnormalities in FI, and evaluating patients w/ megacolon/megarectum.
  • Usefulness endorsed by AGA, ACG, and ASCRS.
  • Test results should be interpreted in the context of clinical features and in conjunction w/ other tests.
  • The London Classification incorporates anal manometry, rectal sensation test, and tests to assess rectal evacuation into four diagnostic algorithms.
  • Algorithms have not been clinically tested or validated.
Current Limitations
  • ARM is more widely used due to HR-ARM, 3D-HR-ARM, and portable manometry.
  • Many practitioners are not adequately trained in conducting/interpreting HR-ARM.
  • HR-ARM/3D-HR-ARM provide a more refined assessment of anorectal pressure topography, which may uncover anorectal structural abnormalities.
  • Use of HR-ARM/3D-HR-ARM in diagnosing DD is limited by the recognition that even asymptomatic healthy persons may exhibit dyssynergia during simulated evacuation.
  • Patients w/ chronic constipation may be dissatisfied despite normal stool frequency/form.
  • Larger database of technique-specific normal values and newer paradigms of analyzing anorectal pressure profiles will increase the precision of HR-ARM.
  • Prospective long-term studies are necessary to define the relevance of ARM in clinical practice.
