  • - This section covers the psychology of procrastination and provides practical, low-willpower strategies to overcome it.
Introduction to Procrastination
  • - Procrastination is intimately related to memory because building solid long-term memory takes time and cannot be done at the last minute.
The Arsenic Analogy
  • - Just as arsenic eaters build up an immunity to a poison that is slowly damaging them, procrastinators get used to putting things off, which has unseen long-term negative effects.

The Neurological Basis
  • - We procrastinate on things that make us uncomfortable.
  • - Thinking about an unpleasant task activates the pain centers of the brain.
  • - To stop the pain, you shift your focus to something more enjoyable, providing temporary relief.

Consequences of Habitual Procrastination
  • - It can become a keystone bad habit, influencing many important areas of your life.
  • - It shares features with addiction, offering temporary excitement and relief from boring reality.
  • - It leads to cramming, which does not build solid neural structures for learning.

Willpower is a Limited Resource
  • - Willpower uses a lot of neural resources.
  • - It's best not to waste it on fending off procrastination except when absolutely necessary.
  • - The following strategies are designed to minimize the need for willpower.


The Habit Loop: Your Inner Zombies
  • - Habits are energy savers that allow our minds to free up for other activities by going into a 'zombie mode'.
  • - Understanding the components of a habit is key to changing it.
1. The Cue
  • - The trigger that launches you into 'zombie mode'.
  • - A cue itself is neither helpful nor harmful.

2. The Routine
  • - This is the 'zombie mode' itself â€“ the habitual response to the cue.
  • - This is the part of the habit you want to change.

3. The Reward
  • - Every habit develops because it rewards us with an immediate feeling of pleasure.
  • - Procrastination's reward is fast: shifting focus to something more pleasant.

4. The Belief
  • - Habits are powerful because of our belief in them (e.g., 'I'll never be able to change').
  • - To change a habit, you must change the underlying belief.


Strategies to Overcome Procrastination
  • - Focus on simple, practical techniques that don't require much willpower.
Focus on Process, Not Product
  • - Product: The outcome (e.g., a finished homework assignment). Focusing on the product can trigger the pain that causes procrastination.
  • - Process: The flow of time and the actions within it (e.g., 'I will spend 20 minutes working').
Benefits of Process-Focus
  • - It's easier to enlist your 'zombie' habit to help with a process.
  • - It allows you to relax into the flow of work without judging your progress towards the final product.

The Pomodoro Technique
  • - A key process-based tool.
  • - Involves a timed work session of 25 minutes of uninterrupted focus, followed by relaxation.


Harnessing Your Zombies: Modifying the Habit Loop
  • - The trick is to change your reaction to a cue.
  • - This is the only place you need to apply willpower.
1. The Cue: Isolate Your Triggers
  • - Recognize what launches your procrastination.
  • - Cues usually fall into four categories: Location, Time, How you feel, and Reactions.
  • - Strategy: Prevent cues by shutting off your phone or disconnecting from the internet for brief periods.

2. The Routine: Have a Plan
  • - You must actively focus on rewiring your old habit by planning a new routine.
  • - Examples: Go to a quiet spot in the library, leave your phone in the car, use the Pomodoro technique as your new routine.

3. The Reward: Give Yourself a Treat
  • - Habits are powerful because they create neurological cravings.
  • - You must add a new reward to overcome previous cravings.
  • - The brain will only start rewiring once it begins expecting the new reward.
  • - Examples: A latte, guilt-free web surfing, a sense of pride, setting a mini-deadline like stopping work at 5 p.m.

4. The Belief: Trust the New System
  • - The most important part is believing you can do it.
  • - Strategy: Develop a new community by hanging out with classmates who have a 'can-do' philosophy.


Practical Planning: Juggling Life and Learning
  • - Effective planning frees up mental resources for problem-solving.
Weekly & Daily Lists
  • - Weekly: Once a week, write a brief list of key tasks.
  • - Daily: Each evening, write a list of tasks for the next day.
Why the Evening Before?
  • - Research shows this helps your subconscious grapple with the tasks.
  • - It enlists your 'zombies' to help you accomplish the items the next day.


Plan Your Quitting Time
  • - Planning your quitting time is as important as planning your work time.
  • - Study expert Cal Newport used a 5 p.m. quitting time throughout his student career, eventually getting a PhD from MIT.

Eat Your Frogs First
  • - Work on your most important and most disliked task first thing in the morning.
  • - Even just one Pomodoro on this task can be incredibly effective.



Summary: Tackling Procrastination
  • - Keep a planner journal to track goals and what works.
  • - Commit to routines and tasks each day.
  • - Write task lists the night before to engage your subconscious.
  • - Arrange work into small challenges (e.g., Pomodoros).
  • - Reward yourself after completing tasks.
  • - Watch for procrastination cues and change your environment (e.g., go to a library).
  • - Trust your new system so you can relax guilt-free.
  • - Have backup plans for when you still procrastinate.
  • - Eat your frogs first every day.

  • - This section explores how memory works and introduces powerful techniques to improve retention and recall.
Tapping into Natural Memory Systems
  • - Humans have outstanding visual and spatial memory systems.
Evolutionary Basis
  • - Our ancestors needed to remember locations (how to get home, where berries were) more than names or numbers.
  • - This resulted in a superior 'where things are' and 'how they look' memory system.

Technique: Create Memorable Visual Images
  • - To remember a key item, create a memorable visual image for it.
  • - Images connect directly to the right brain's visual-spatial centers.
  • - Example: To remember Newton's second law (F=ma), imagine a gigantic Flying Mule (F, m) braying 'a'.
Enhance the Image
  • - The more evocative and funny the image, the better.
  • - Engage multiple senses: smell the mule, feel the wind, hear it braying.



From Working Memory to Long-Term Memory
  • - To move information from temporary working memory to long-term memory, two things must happen.
1. Make it Memorable
  • - The idea must be encoded in a way that is easy to recall, like the flying mule example.

2. Repeat It (Spaced Repetition)
  • - Repetition is crucial to strengthen the neural pattern of a memory before it fades.
  • - Repetition should be done sporadically over several days, not all at once.
  • - As you become more certain of mastery, you should gradually extend the time between repetitions.
Helpful Tools
  • - Index Cards (Flashcards): Writing by hand helps encode information. Saying the word and meaning aloud creates auditory hooks.
  • - Interleaving: Mix cards from different topics to strengthen learning.
  • - Flashcard Systems: Apps like Anki use built-in algorithms for spaced repetition.



The Neuroscience of Memory
  • - Insights from clinical cases and modern research.
Patient H.M. and the Hippocampus
  • - Patient H.M. had his hippocampus removed at age 27 to treat epilepsy.
  • - The operation was a success, but he became profoundly amnesic, unable to form new memories of facts and events.

Memory Consolidation & Reconsolidation
  • - Consolidation: The process of storing new memories in the cortex. This can take many years and occurs during sleep.
  • - Reconsolidation: The process where recalling a memory changes it. Memories are not fixed; they are living parts of the brain.

Astrocytes and Intelligence
  • - Astrocytes are a type of glial (supporting) cell in the brain.
  • - An experiment showed that when human astrocytes were put into mouse brains, the mice learned faster.
  • - The only difference found in Einstein's brain was that he had many more astrocytes than the average human.


Advanced Memory Techniques
  • - Powerful methods for grouping and remembering information.
Create Meaningful Groups
  • - Simplify material by grouping it.
  • - Example: To remember four plants (Garlic, Rose, Hawthorn, Mustard), the first letters abbreviate to GRHM, which can be remembered as a graham cracker.

Use Mnemonics
  • - Memorable sentences where the first letter of each word corresponds to an item on a list.
  • - Medical Example: To memorize carpal bones: 'Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can't Handle'.

The Memory Palace Technique
  • - A powerful way of grouping items by using a familiar place (like your house) as a visual notepad.
  • - You 'deposit' concept images you want to remember in different locations within your mental 'palace'.
  • - Example (Grocery List): Imagine a giant bottle of milk by your front door, a loaf of bread on the couch, and a cracked egg on the coffee table.
Benefits
  • - A study showed a person could remember more than 95% of a 40-50 item list after only one or two practice walks.
  • - Speeds up the acquisition of chunks and big-picture understanding.
  • - Expands working memory by providing easy access to long-term memory.



Summary: Memory
  • - Two Main Systems: Long-term memory (storage warehouse) and working memory (a poor blackboard that holds about four items).
  • - Chunking: Mastering a concept compacts it, freeing up space in working memory.
  • - Tap into Visual/Spatial Systems: Use memorable, evocative, and funny images to encode information.
  • - Repetition is Key: Use spaced repetition over several days to store items in long-term memory.
  • - Use Memory Techniques: Create meaningful groups, use mnemonics, and build a Memory Palace to simplify and chunk what you are trying to learn.
