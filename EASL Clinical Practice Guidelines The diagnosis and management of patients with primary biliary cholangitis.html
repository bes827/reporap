Introduction to Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)
  • - A chronic, progressive, autoimmune cholestatic liver disease predominantly affecting women.
  • - Untreated, it can lead to end-stage biliary cirrhosis.
  • - Goal of therapy is to prevent disease progression and manage symptoms.
Epidemiology
  • - Affects predominantly women.
  • - Estimated global prevalence: 1 in 1,000 women over the age of 40.
  • - European incidence: 1–2 per 100,000 population per year.

Pathogenesis
  • - A cycle of immune-mediated biliary epithelial injury, cholestasis, and progressive fibrosis.
  • - Involves loss of immunological tolerance to biliary epithelial cells (associated with AMA).
  • - Key immune pathways: IL-12 and JAK-STAT signalling.
Biliary 'Umbrella' Failure
  • - The Cl-/HCO3- exchanger (AE2) and biliary glycocalyx normally protect against hydrophobic bile acids.
  • - In PBC, downregulation of AE2 sensitizes cholangiocytes to apoptosis, weakening this protective 'umbrella'.


Prognosis without Treatment
  • - Historic cohorts showed an average survival of 9–10 years from presentation.
  • - 25% of untreated patients developed liver failure during this time.
  • - Median time to develop extensive fibrosis is 2 years.


Diagnostic Approach to Cholestasis
  • - Cholestasis is the impairment of bile formation/flow, considered chronic if it lasts >6 months.
  • - Early biochemical markers: Increased serum ALP and GGT.
Initial Evaluation
  • - Step 1: Detailed history, physical examination, and abdominal ultrasound.
  • - Ultrasound is the first-line imaging to exclude mechanical bile duct obstruction.
Associated Diseases
  • - Autoimmune Hashimoto’s thyroiditis
  • - Sjögren disease/sicca complex
  • - Celiac disease
  • - Systemic sclerosis


Serological Tests
  • - Step 2: In chronic intrahepatic cholestasis, test for serum AMA and PBC-specific ANA.
  • - AMA is highly sensitive and specific for PBC in the context of unexplained chronic cholestasis.

Extended Imaging
  • - Step 3: MRCP is a safe and accurate method for imaging the biliary tree.
  • - Essential for diagnosing primary or secondary sclerosing cholangitis.
  • - EUS is an alternative for evaluating the distal bile duct.

Liver Biopsy
  • - Step 4: Perform if the cause of chronic intrahepatic cholestasis remains unclear after other steps.
  • - An adequate biopsy should contain at least 11 portal fields.

Genetic Testing
  • - Consider for inherited cholestatic syndromes (e.g., PFIC1-3, BRIC) when other diagnoses are ruled out and clinical suspicion is high.


Initial Diagnosis of PBC
  • - Suspect PBC in patients with persistent cholestatic liver tests or symptoms like pruritus or fatigue.
Diagnostic Criteria
  • - A diagnosis can be made based on:
  • - 1. Elevated ALP.
  • - 2. Presence of AMA at a titre of ≥1:40.
  • - This applies to adult patients with cholestasis and no likelihood of systemic disease.
AMA-Negative PBC
  • - Can be diagnosed in patients with cholestasis and specific ANA patterns (nuclear dots or perinuclear rims) or ELISA results for sp100 or gp210.
  • - Occurs in 5–10% of PBC patients.

Isolated AMA Positivity
  • - AMA reactivity alone is NOT sufficient to diagnose PBC.
  • - Patients with normal liver tests who are AMA positive should have annual biochemical reassessment.
  • - Only one patient out of six with AMA positivity and normal ALP develops PBC within 5 years.


Biochemical & Immunological Markers
  • - ALP elevation is typical and associated with disease progression.
  • - Increased IgM is a common feature.
  • - AST/ALT ratio > 1 may be a marker of ongoing liver fibrosis.
  • - Hyperbilirubinemia is typical of advanced disease.

Role of Liver Biopsy
  • - Not necessary for diagnosis if PBC-specific antibodies are present.
  • - Essential when:
  • - PBC-specific antibodies are absent.
  • - Co-existent AIH or NASH is suspected.
  • - Other systemic co-morbidities are present.
Histological Features
  • - Hallmark: Chronic, non-suppurative inflammation destroying bile ducts, termed 'florid duct lesions'.
  • - Progressive damage leads to ductopenia and fibrosis.
  • - Staging systems (Ludwig, Scheuer, Nakanuma) classify disease from stage 1 to 4 (cirrhosis).



Risk Stratification
  • - All patients should be evaluated for their risk of progression to guide therapy.
  • - High-risk patients are those with inadequate biochemical response to therapy and/or cirrhosis.
Static Risk Markers (at any time)
  • - These markers can be assessed at presentation or during treatment.
Demographics
  • - Younger age at diagnosis (e.g., <45 years) is a strong risk factor for inadequate response.
  • - Male sex is associated with more advanced disease at presentation and poorer response to UDCA.

Serum Tests & Serology
  • - Elevated bilirubin and low albumin are markers of advanced disease.
  • - Presence of anti-gp210, anti-sp100, and anti-centromere antibodies may predict an unfavorable course.

Liver Stiffness Measurement (LSM)
  • - Assessed by Vibration-Controlled Transient Elastography (VCTE).
  • - LSM values ≥9.6 kPa are associated with a 5-fold increased risk of liver decompensation, transplant, or death.
  • - Worsening LSM over time is a strong predictor of poor outcomes.

Histology
  • - Advanced histological stage is associated with poor prognosis.
  • - The degree of lymphocytic interface hepatitis is an independent predictor of cirrhosis development.


Dynamic Risk Markers (Response to UDCA)
  • - Biochemical response to UDCA, typically assessed after 12 months, is the most validated tool to select patients for second-line therapies.
Qualitative Binary Definitions
  • - Simple criteria to define 'responders' vs. 'non-responders'.
  • - The Paris-I and Paris-II criteria are widely validated.
Paris-I Criteria
  • - Defines inadequate response at 12 months if any of the following are met:
  • - ALP ≥3 x ULN
  • - AST ≥2 x ULN
  • - Bilirubin >1 mg/dl

Paris-II Criteria (for early-stage disease)
  • - Defines inadequate response at 12 months if any of the following are met:
  • - ALP ≥1.5 x ULN
  • - AST ≥1.5 x ULN
  • - Bilirubin >1 mg/dl

Toronto Criteria
  • - Defines inadequate response at 24 months if:
  • - ALP >1.67 x ULN


Continuous Scoring Systems
  • - Incorporate both treatment response and disease severity parameters.
  • - GLOBE score and UK-PBC score have shown better performance than binary models.
  • - The GLOBE score improves patient classification by nearly 10% compared to Paris-I.

Prognosis for Responders
  • - Early-stage patients with ALP <1.5 x ULN and a normal bilirubin after one year of UDCA have a transplant-free survival not significantly different from a healthy population.



Treatment: Therapies to Slow Disease Progression
  • - Treatment has been dominated by bile acid-based approaches.
Licensed Therapies
Ursodeoxycholic Acid (UDCA)
  • - First-line pharmacotherapy for ALL patients with PBC, usually continued for life.
Dose & Mechanism
  • - Recommended dose: 13–15 mg/kg/day, can be given as a single daily dose.
  • - Mechanism: Acts as a secretagogue, stimulating bile secretion, and has cytoprotective effects.

Efficacy
  • - Reduces annual progression rate to extensive fibrosis/cirrhosis (from 34% with placebo to 7% with UDCA).
  • - Reduces 4-year probability of developing esophageal varices (from 58% to 16%).
  • - Combined analysis showed a one-third reduction in the risk of death or transplant for patients with moderate to severe PBC.

Safety
  • - Very safe with minimal side effects (e.g., weight gain, hair thinning).
  • - Considered safe to use during pregnancy and breastfeeding.


Obeticholic Acid (OCA)
  • - Conditionally approved for patients with an inadequate response to UDCA, or as monotherapy in those intolerant to UDCA.
Dose & Mechanism
  • - Initial dose: 5 mg daily.
  • - Dose titration to 10 mg daily at six months, according to tolerability.
  • - Mechanism: A potent, selective agonist of the farnesoid X receptor (FXR).

Efficacy (POISE Trial)
  • - For patients with ALP ≥1.67x ULN and/or elevated bilirubin <2x ULN.
  • - Primary endpoint (ALP <1.67x ULN with ≥15% reduction + normal bilirubin) met in:
  • - 47% of 10 mg OCA group
  • - 46% of 5-10 mg OCA group
  • - 10% of placebo group
  • - Mean decrease in serum ALP was 39% in the 10 mg group vs. 5% for placebo.

Side Effects
  • - Dose-dependent pruritus, leading to treatment discontinuation in 1–10% of patients.
  • - Reversible decrease in HDL cholesterol.



Unlicensed (Off-Label) Therapies
  • - Data for these agents are not yet sufficient for a formal recommendation.
Budesonide
  • - A synthetic corticosteroid with high first-pass metabolism.
  • - Studied as add-on to UDCA in non-cirrhotic patients with interface hepatitis.
  • - Caution: Can have deleterious outcomes in patients with cirrhosis and portal hypertension.

Fibric Acid Derivatives (Fibrates)
  • - E.g., Bezafibrate, Fenofibrate.
  • - Exert anti-cholestatic effects via activation of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPAR).
  • - Bezafibrate (400 mg/day) as adjunctive therapy showed normalization of ALP in 45% of UDCA non-responders.
  • - Caution: May cause elevations in ALT/AST and requires monitoring of renal function.



Special Settings & Variants
Pregnancy
  • - Typically well tolerated in non-cirrhotic patients.
  • - UDCA use is recommended to be continued (considered safe).
  • - Pruritus management may require specialist advice; Rifampicin has been used by experts during the third trimester.
  • - Patients with cirrhosis have higher risks and require pre-conception counselling and specialist monitoring.

PBC with Features of Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)
  • - Occurs in ~8-10% of cases, presenting simultaneously or sequentially.
  • - Suspect in patients with disproportionate elevations in ALT and/or IgG.
Diagnosis (Paris Criteria)
  • - Liver biopsy is mandatory for confirmation.
  • - Diagnosis requires meeting criteria for both PBC and AIH.
  • - AIH features (need ≥2 of 3):
  • - ALT ≥5 x ULN
  • - IgG ≥2 x ULN or positive smooth muscle antibody (SMA)
  • - Histology: Moderate or severe interface hepatitis

Treatment
  • - Patients may benefit from immunosuppressive treatment in addition to UDCA.
  • - Recommended for patients with severe interface hepatitis.
  • - Should be considered for patients with moderate interface hepatitis.



Management of Symptoms & Complications
  • - Symptom severity does not necessarily correlate with the stage of liver disease.
Pruritus (Itch)
  • - A characteristic cholestatic symptom that impairs quality of life.
  • - A step-wise therapeutic approach is recommended.
1st Line: Bile Acid Sequestrants
  • - Cholestyramine is recommended first-line despite limited evidence, due to its safety profile.
  • - Important: Must be given 2–4 hours before or after other medications (including UDCA/OCA) to avoid interfering with their absorption.

2nd Line: Rifampicin
  • - Dose: 150 mg–300 mg daily.
  • - Requires monitoring of serum liver tests due to potential hepatotoxicity.

3rd Line: Opiate Antagonists
  • - Naltrexone can reduce the sensation of itching.
  • - Start at a low dose to avoid opiate withdrawal-like reactions.

Other Therapies (Empirical)
  • - Sertraline (SSRI) and Gabapentin are used empirically for unresponsive pruritus.
  • - Evidence is limited to small trials or case reports.

Salvage & Experimental Therapies
  • - For extreme, unresponsive pruritus, restricted to specialist centres.
  • - Options include:
  • - Nasobiliary drainage
  • - Molecular Adsorbance Recirculating System (MARS)
  • - Ultraviolet (UV) light therapy

Liver Transplantation
  • - Highly effective, with rapid reduction in pruritus severity, often within 24 hours post-transplant.
  • - An indication for 'persistent and intractable' pruritus refractory to medical therapy.


Fatigue
  • - Frequently reported by >50% of patients; severe in 20%.
  • - Not related to liver disease severity (except in end-stage disease).
  • - Not responsive to UDCA or OCA therapy.
  • - Transplantation is not an indication for fatigue alone, as it often persists post-transplant.
Management Approach
  • - A structured approach is essential.
  • - Seek and treat associated/alternate causes: anaemia, hypothyroidism, sleep disturbance.
  • - Advise on coping strategies and avoidance of social isolation.

Contributing Factors
  • - Pruritus at night
  • - Autonomic dysfunction
  • - Dehydration
  • - Restless legs
  • - Concurrent medications (e.g., beta-blockers)


Sicca Complex (Dry Eyes/Mouth)
  • - Common in PBC; most patients have sicca symptoms rather than primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
Treatment
  • - Artificial tears and saliva are often helpful.
  • - Pilocarpine or cevimeline can be used for refractory symptoms.
  • - Advise on oral hygiene to prevent dental caries.


Miscellaneous Manifestations
  • - Raynaud’s phenomenon: Occurs in up to one-quarter of patients.
  • - Limited scleroderma (CREST syndrome): Occurs in ~8% of patients. Refer for rheumatology advice.


Management of Liver Disease Complications
Osteoporosis
  • - A common complication in PBC.
Risk Assessment & Monitoring
  • - Assess risk in all patients.
  • - Use DEXA scan at presentation to assess bone mineral density.
  • - Follow-up assessment between 1 and 5 years later depending on risk.

Treatment
  • - It seems reasonable to treat patients with a femur T-score lower than -1.5.
  • - Bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate) are effective.
  • - Caution: Oral bisphosphonates may cause esophagitis; use with care in patients with esophageal varices. Parenteral forms can be considered.

Supplementation
  • - Calcium and Vitamin D supplements can be considered, according to local practice.


Fat-Soluble Vitamin Deficiency
  • - Deficiencies in vitamins A, D, E, and K are uncommon in PBC, except in patients with prolonged jaundice.
  • - Consider measuring levels and supplementing on an individual basis, particularly if icteric.

Hyperlipidaemia
  • - Elevated serum lipids occur in up to 80% of patients.
  • - Characterized by elevated HDL and presence of anti-atherogenic lipoprotein X.
  • - No substantial evidence supports an elevated cardiovascular risk in PBC.
  • - Treatment is not routinely indicated unless concomitant classical cardiovascular risk factors are present.

Varices & Portal Hypertension
  • - Can develop in pre-cirrhotic stages, but this is rare.
  • - 95% of patients with varices have other markers of high-risk disease.
  • - Management should follow the Baveno-VI guidelines.
Screening Indication
  • - Consider screening endoscopy if:
  • - LSM ≥20 kPa
  • - Platelet count <150 x 10^9 cells/L


Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
  • - A serious complication of cirrhosis in PBC.
Incidence & Risk Factors
  • - Incidence rate: 3.4 cases per 1,000 patient-years.
  • - Key risk factors:
  • - Male sex
  • - Inadequate response to UDCA
  • - Advanced histological stage (cirrhosis)

Surveillance
  • - HCC surveillance according to EASL guidelines is indicated for patients with suspected or confirmed cirrhosis.
  • - Typically involves ultrasound +/- alpha fetoprotein every 6 months.



Liver Transplantation
  • - The prevalence of PBC as an indication for transplant has declined.
Indications for Assessment
  • - Complications of cirrhosis (variceal bleed, ascites, encephalopathy).
  • - Markers of disease severity:
  • - MELD score ≥15
  • - Progressively rising bilirubin >50–85 μmol/L (3–5 mg/dl)
  • - Severe, medically resistant pruritus.

Outcomes
  • - Generally favorable, with 5-year patient survival rates of 80–85%.
  • - Symptoms like fatigue frequently persist post-transplant.

Recurrence of PBC
  • - Reported in ~20% of patients on average, though histological recurrence may be higher.
  • - Infrequently leads to graft loss.
  • - Use of UDCA post-transplant is safe and can improve liver biochemistry, but evidence for preventing recurrence is insufficient for a strong recommendation.


Organisation of Clinical Care
  • - Optimal care requires a structured, life-long, and individualized approach.
Care Pathways
  • - All patients should have a structured life-long follow-up.
  • - A minimum of an annual assessment of treatment needs and symptom status is recommended.

Clinical Audit Standards
  • - Proposed standards to improve quality of care:
Baseline Assessment
  • - All patients with suspected PBC should have an abdominal ultrasound (standard 90%).

First-Line Therapy
  • - UDCA at 13–15 mg/kg/day should be used in all patients (standard 90%).

Risk Stratification
  • - Document biochemical response after one year of UDCA (standard 80%).

Symptom Evaluation
  • - Evaluate all patients for pruritus, sicca complex, and fatigue annually (standard 90%).

Transplant Referral
  • - Discuss patients with bilirubin >50 μmol/l or decompensated disease with a transplant-linked hepatologist (standard 90%).


Patient Support
  • - Patients should be informed of available support from patient groups.
  • - Psychological distress associated with fatigue may warrant referral to psychological services.
