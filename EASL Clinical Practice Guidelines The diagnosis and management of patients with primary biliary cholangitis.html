Introduction to Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)
  • - A chronic, progressive, autoimmune cholestatic liver disease.
  • - Predominantly affects women, with an estimated global prevalence of 1 in 1,000 women over 40.
  • - Untreated, it can culminate in end-stage biliary cirrhosis.
Key Characteristics
  • - Cholestasis: Impaired bile formation and/or flow.
  • - Serologic Reactivity: Presence of antimitochondrial antibodies (AMA) or specific antinuclear antibodies (ANA).
  • - Histology: Chronic, non-suppurative, granulomatous, lymphocytic inflammation of small bile ducts.

Epidemiology
  • - European incidence: 1–2 per 100,000 population per year.
  • - Prevalence: 1.9–40.2 per 100,000.
  • - Female predominance, though male prevalence may be increasing.

Pathogenesis
  • - A cycle of immune-mediated biliary injury, cholestasis, and fibrosis.
  • - Involves interaction of immune (T-cells, B-cells, AMA production) and biliary pathways (impaired bicarbonate umbrella, AE2 downregulation).
  • - Gut-liver axis involvement via the FXR/FGF-19 signalling pathway.

Clinical Impact & Prognosis
  • - Disease Progression: Leads to cirrhosis and need for liver transplantation.
  • - Symptoms: Significant impact on Quality of Life (QoL), including pruritus, sicca complex, and fatigue.
Prognosis without Treatment
  • - Historic cohorts showed average survival of 9–10 years from presentation.
  • - 25% developed liver failure during this time.
  • - Median time to develop extensive fibrosis is 2 years.

Prognosis with UDCA Treatment
  • - 10-year survival approaches 80%.
  • - Progression to cirrhosis after 6 years is significantly lower (1 in 10 on UDCA vs. 1 in 2 on placebo).



Diagnostic Approach to Cholestasis
  • - A structured, systematic approach is recommended for any patient with chronic cholestasis (lasting >6 months).
Initial Evaluation
  • - History & Physical Exam: Look for clues of associated diseases (e.g., Sjögren's with PBC, IBD with PSC), drug history (DILI), and signs of advanced liver disease (icterus, xanthelasma).
  • - Biochemical Markers: Initial signs are elevated serum ALP and GGT. Conjugated hyperbilirubinemia occurs at more advanced stages.

Imaging
  • - Abdominal Ultrasound: First-line imaging to exclude mechanical bile duct obstruction. A normal ultrasound suggests intrahepatic cholestasis.
  • - Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP): Recommended for detailed imaging of the biliary tree if cholestasis is unexplained. Essential for diagnosing sclerosing cholangitis.
  • - Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS): An alternative to MRCP, particularly for evaluating the distal bile duct.

Serological & Further Testing
  • - Serology: Screen for AMA and PBC-specific ANA in all patients with unexplained cholestasis.
  • - Liver Biopsy: Consider if serology and imaging do not yield a diagnosis. An adequate sample should contain at least 11 portal fields.
  • - Genetic Testing: Reserved for specialist centers when inherited syndromes (e.g., PFIC, BRIC) are suspected.


Specific Diagnosis of PBC
  • - Diagnosis is based on a combination of cholestatic liver tests and specific immunological markers.
Diagnostic Criteria
  • - A diagnosis of PBC can be made in an adult with elevated ALP and the presence of AMA at a titre of ≥1:40.
  • - Liver biopsy is not necessary for diagnosis in this context.
AMA-Negative PBC
  • - Accounts for 5-10% of cases.
  • - Diagnosis can be made in patients with cholestasis and specific ANA patterns (nuclear dots or perinuclear rims) or positive ELISA for sp100 or gp210.

Role of Liver Biopsy
  • - Biopsy is recommended when:
  • - PBC-specific antibodies are absent.
  • - Co-existent AIH or NASH is suspected.
  • - Other systemic co-morbidities are present.


Key Investigations (Table 4)
  • - Biochemistry: Elevated ALP is typical. AST/ALT may be elevated (AST/ALT ratio >1 may suggest fibrosis). Elevated IgM is common. Bilirubin rises in late-stage disease.
  • - Immunology: AMA is the diagnostic hallmark, positive in >90% of patients. Specific ANA (anti-gp210, anti-sp100) are highly specific.
  • - Histology: The characteristic feature is the 'florid duct lesion' (non-suppurative destructive cholangitis). Staging systems (Ludwig, Scheuer, Nakanuma) assess fibrosis and bile duct loss.
Isolated AMA Positivity
  • - AMA reactivity alone with normal liver tests is not sufficient to diagnose PBC.
  • - These individuals should be followed up with annual biochemical reassessment.
  • - Only one in six such patients develops PBC within 5 years.



Risk Stratification
  • - All patients should be evaluated for their risk of disease progression to guide management and need for second-line therapies.
High-Risk Patient Profile
  • - Demographics: Younger age at diagnosis (<45 years) and male sex are associated with poorer response and outcomes.
  • - Disease Stage: Advanced stage at presentation is a strong risk factor.
  • - Biochemical Response: Inadequate response to UDCA therapy is the most critical factor.

Prognostic Tools
  • - A combination of non-invasive tests should be used at baseline and during follow-up.
Biochemical Markers
  • - ALP and Bilirubin are the strongest surrogate markers for outcome.
  • - Albumin and platelet count are also key indicators of disease stage.

Liver Stiffness Measurement (LSM)
  • - Measured by Vibration-Controlled Transient Elastography (VCTE).
  • - An excellent surrogate marker for fibrosis and cirrhosis.
  • - LSM >9.6 kPa is associated with a 5-fold increased risk of liver decompensation, transplant, or death.

Assessing Response to UDCA
  • - Response is typically assessed after 1 year of therapy (though 6 months may be equivalent).
  • - Inadequate response is seen in 25% to 50% of patients.
Qualitative Criteria (Table 5)
  • - Paris-I: Widely validated. Failure is defined as ALP ≥3x ULN OR AST ≥2x ULN OR Bilirubin >1 mg/dl.
  • - Paris-II: Designed for early-stage disease. Failure is defined as ALP ≥1.5x ULN OR AST ≥1.5x ULN OR Bilirubin >1 mg/dl.
  • - Patients with ALP <1.5x ULN and normal bilirubin after 1 year of UDCA have a survival rate similar to the general population.

Continuous Risk Scores (Table 5)
  • - GLOBE Score and UK-PBC Score incorporate on-treatment biochemistry and baseline severity markers.
  • - They offer better performance than binary criteria for predicting death or transplant.




Treatment to Slow Disease Progression
  • - The goal of therapy is to prevent end-stage liver disease complications.
First-Line Therapy: Licensed
Ursodeoxycholic Acid (UDCA)
  • - Recommended as first-line pharmacotherapy for ALL patients with PBC, usually for life.
Dose & Safety
  • - Dose: 13–15 mg/kg/day, can be given as a single daily dose.
  • - Safety: Very safe, minimal side effects. Considered safe in pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Efficacy
  • - Reduces risk of death or transplant by one-third in moderate-to-severe disease.
  • - Slows histological progression: 4-year probability of remaining in early-stage disease is 76% (vs. 29% on placebo).
  • - Reduces risk of oesophageal varices: 4-year probability is 16% (vs. 58% in untreated).



Second-Line Therapy: Licensed
  • - For patients with an inadequate response to UDCA or who are intolerant.
Obeticholic Acid (OCA)
  • - A semi-synthetic bile acid analogue that is a potent and selective FXR agonist.
Indication & Dose
  • - Approved for use in combination with UDCA or as monotherapy.
  • - Starting dose: 5 mg daily.
  • - Titration: Increase to 10 mg daily after 6 months, according to tolerability.

Efficacy (POISE Trial)
  • - For patients with ALP ≥1.67x ULN and/or elevated bilirubin <2x ULN.
  • - Primary endpoint met in 47% (10mg group) and 46% (5-10mg group) vs. 10% in placebo group.
  • - Mean decrease in ALP was 39% in the 10mg group vs. 5% for placebo.

Side Effects
  • - Pruritus: Dose-dependent, leading to discontinuation in 1-10% of patients.
  • - Lipid Changes: Reversible decrease in HDL cholesterol.



Off-Label / Unlicensed Therapies
  • - Data are not yet sufficient for a formal recommendation.
Budesonide
  • - A corticosteroid with high first-pass metabolism.
  • - Considered for non-cirrhotic patients with features of AIH (interface hepatitis).
  • - Efficacy data is mixed; can improve histology but may worsen bone density. Avoid in patients with cirrhosis.

Fibric Acid Derivatives (Fibrates)
  • - e.g., Bezafibrate, Fenofibrate. Act as PPAR activators.
  • - Can significantly improve biochemistry (e.g., normalize ALP) in UDCA non-responders.
  • - Long-term benefit on clinical outcomes is not yet proven; concerns exist regarding potential renal and liver toxicity.



Management in Special Settings
Pregnancy
  • - Typically well-tolerated in non-cirrhotic patients.
  • - UDCA should be continued as it is considered safe.
  • - Pruritus can worsen; cholestyramine is safe, and rifampicin can be used in the third trimester.
  • - Patients with cirrhosis have higher risks and require pre-conception counselling and specialist monitoring, including variceal screening in the second trimester.

PBC with Features of Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)
  • - Occurs in ~8-10% of patients with PBC.
  • - A liver biopsy is mandatory for diagnosis.
Diagnosis (Paris Criteria)
  • - Requires meeting criteria for both diseases.
  • - PBC features (≥2 of 3): ALP ≥2x ULN or GGT ≥5x ULN; AMA ≥1:40; Florid duct lesion.
  • - AIH features (≥2 of 3): ALT ≥5x ULN; IgG ≥2x ULN or positive SMA; Moderate/severe interface hepatitis.

Treatment
  • - Patients may benefit from immunosuppressive treatment in addition to UDCA.
  • - Immunosuppression is suggested for severe interface hepatitis and should be considered for moderate interface hepatitis.



Management of Symptoms & Extrahepatic Manifestations
  • - Symptoms often do not correlate with disease severity and do not improve with UDCA or OCA.
  • - A structured approach to symptom evaluation and management is essential.
Pruritus (Itch)
  • - A characteristic cholestatic symptom. Severe pruritus may indicate an aggressive ductopenic variant with a poor prognosis.
Stepwise Treatment Approach
  • - 1st Line: Cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant). Must be taken 2-4 hours apart from other medications.
  • - 2nd Line: Rifampicin (150-300 mg daily). Requires monitoring of liver tests due to potential hepatotoxicity.
  • - 3rd Line: Naltrexone (oral opiate antagonist). Start at a low dose.
  • - Other: Sertraline (SSRI) used empirically.
  • - Refractory Cases: May require referral for experimental therapies or consideration for liver transplantation.


Fatigue
  • - Frequently reported (>50% of patients) and can be debilitatingly severe in 20%.
  • - Not related to liver disease severity (except in end-stage disease) and persists post-transplant.
  • - Management focuses on treating associated causes (anaemia, hypothyroidism, sleep disturbance) and developing coping strategies.

Sicca Complex (Dryness)
  • - Dry eyes and/or dry mouth are common.
  • - Management includes artificial tears/saliva. Refractory cases may use pilocarpine or cevimeline.
  • - Good oral hygiene is important to prevent dental caries.


Management of Liver Disease Complications
Osteoporosis
  • - A common complication. All patients should have arisk assessment for osteoporosis.
  • - DEXA scan is recommended at presentation to assess bone mineral density, with follow-up intervals of 1 to 5 years depending on risk.
  • - Treatment with bisphosphonates is effective but should be used with caution in patients with oesophageal varices.

Fat-Soluble Vitamin Deficiency
  • - Deficiencies in vitamins A, D, E, and K are uncommon in PBC.
  • - Risk is higher in patients with prolonged jaundice.
  • - Supplementation should be considered on an individual basis, with a lower threshold for icteric patients.

Hyperlipidaemia
  • - Elevated serum lipids occur in up to 80% of patients but is not associated with an increased cardiovascular risk.
  • - This is partly due to the presence of an anti-atherogenic particle, lipoprotein X.
  • - Treatment is generally not required unless the patient has concomitant classical cardiovascular risk factors.

Varices & Portal Hypertension
  • - Portal hypertension can develop in pre-cirrhotic stages, though this is rare.
  • - The Baveno-VI guidelines for screening and management of varices apply to patients with PBC.
  • - Screening is indicated for patients with LSM ≥20 kPa or a platelet count <150 x 10⁹ cells/L.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
  • - Patients with cirrhosis are at risk of developing HCC.
  • - Incidence is estimated at 3.4 cases per 1,000 patient-years.
  • - Key risk factors are male sex, inadequate response to UDCA, and cirrhosis.
  • - Surveillance with ultrasound every 6 months is recommended for patients with cirrhosis, per EASL guidelines.


Liver Transplantation
  • - The need for transplantation in PBC has declined with effective medical therapy.
Indications for Assessment
  • - Decompensated Cirrhosis: Variceal bleed, ascites, encephalopathy.
  • - Markers of Severity: MELD score ≥15 or persistent bilirubin >50 µmol/L (3 mg/dl).
  • - Symptoms: Severe, medically intractable pruritus.
  • - Fatigue is NOT an indication for transplantation.

Outcomes & Post-Transplant Management
  • - Outcomes are favorable, with 5-year patient survival rates of 80-85%.
  • - PBC Recurrence: Occurs in ~20% of patients but rarely leads to graft loss. UDCA may be used for recurrent PBC.
  • - Symptoms: Fatigue frequently persists after transplantation.


Organisation of Clinical Care
  • - A structured, life-long, and individualized approach to care is essential.
Care Pathways
  • - All patients should have at least an annual assessment of their disease status, treatment needs, and symptoms.
  • - Care pathways should be developed to ensure equitable and effective care, ranging from primary care to tertiary centers based on patient risk.

Clinical Audit Standards
  • - Proposed standards to improve quality of care:
Diagnosis & Initial Management
  • - Ultrasound at baseline: Standard 90%.
  • - UDCA at 13-15 mg/kg/day: Standard 90%.

Risk & Symptom Assessment
  • - Risk stratification after 1 year of UDCA: Standard 80%.
  • - Symptom evaluation (pruritus, sicca, fatigue) annually: Standard 90%.

Advanced Disease Management
  • - Transplant discussion for bilirubin >50 µmol/L or decompensation: Standard 90%.
  • - Osteoporosis risk assessment within last 5 years: Standard 80%.
  • - Liver biopsy for suspected AIH overlap: Standard 90%.


Patient Support
  • - Patients should be informed about patient support groups and educational materials.
  • - Fatigue has the biggest impact on QoL; coping strategies and addressing social isolation are important.
  • - Patients with profound psychological distress should be referred to appropriate services.


Conclusion
  • - PBC is a common cause of chronic cholestasis, primarily in women over 40.
  • - Diagnosis relies on biochemical and serologic findings, with UDCA as the cornerstone of therapy.
  • - Risk stratification is critical to identify patients needing second-line therapy (e.g., OCA).
  • - A holistic, life-long approach is needed, focusing on both slowing disease progression and managing debilitating symptoms.
