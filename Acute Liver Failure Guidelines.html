Acute Liver Failure Guidelines
  • Official practice recommendations from the American College of Gastroenterology (ACG).
  • Published in The American Journal of Gastroenterology 118(7):p 1128-1153, July 2023.
  • DOI: 10.14309/ajg.0000000000002340.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of patients with Acute Liver Failure (ALF) due to acetaminophen toxicity typically require liver transplantation?
  • 2. Which prognostic scoring system is generally more accurate for acetaminophen-associated ALF versus non-acetaminophen-associated ALF?
  • 3. What is the recommended first-line vasopressor for hypotension refractory to fluid resuscitation in patients with ALF?
  • 4. What is the approximate incidence of Acute Liver Failure in developed countries per million population?
  • 5. In patients with ALF and grade 2 or higher encephalopathy, what early intervention is suggested for hyperammonemia, even without conventional renal replacement therapy indications?
Introduction & Definition of Acute Liver Failure (ALF)
  • ALF is a rare, acute, potentially reversible condition.
  • Characterized by liver injury (abnormal liver tests), coagulopathy (INR >1.5), and hepatic encephalopathy (HE).
  • Occurs in patients w/o preexisting liver disease, leading to severe liver impairment & rapid clinical deterioration.
Key Characteristics
  • Illness duration <26 weeks.
  • No preexisting liver disease or cirrhosis (exceptions: AIH, Budd-Chiari, Wilson disease).
  • Any degree of mental status alteration (encephalopathy).
  • Coagulopathy: INR ≥1.5.
O'Grady Classification of ALF Presentation
  • Differentiates based on rapidity of HE onset.
  • Utility of acute/subacute categories can be less useful in clinical management due to overlap.
Hyperacute ALF
  • Predominantly seen in viral hepatitis A/E, acetaminophen (APAP) toxicity, ischemic injury.
  • High risk of cerebral edema (CE).
  • Best prognosis w/o transplantation.
Acute ALF
  • Can be seen in hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection.
  • Lower risk of CE than hyperacute.
  • Poorer outcome w/o transplantation.
Subacute ALF
  • More often seen w/ non-APAP drug-induced liver injury.
  • Lower risk of CE than hyperacute.
  • Poorer outcome w/o transplantation.
  • Care must be taken not to mistake for chronic liver failure.
Etiology of ALF
  • Varies geographically; essential for prognosis & treatment strategy (esp. LT necessity).
  • Independent predictor of waitlist mortality, not post-LTx outcomes.
  • Waitlist mortality & spontaneous survival for DILI, AIH, HBV were ↓ than for acetaminophen toxicity.
Common Causes
  • North America, Japan, Europe: Drug-induced liver injury (DILI), viral hepatitis, cryptogenic (indeterminate) liver failure.
  • Developing countries: Acute viral hepatitis (AVH) remains predominant etiology.
Indeterminate ALF
  • Percentage varies globally: 5%–70%.
  • Substantial proportion may be due to unidentified viral infection or underrecognized AIH.
ALF vs. Other Liver Conditions
  • Crucial to distinguish ALF from acutely decompensated cirrhosis or acute on chronic liver failure (ACLF).
  • Management differs significantly.
Acutely Decompensated Cirrhosis
  • Much more likely in hospitalized patients w/ liver failure.
  • Generally easily distinguishable from true ALF.
Acute on Chronic Liver Failure (ACLF)
  • Presents w/ acutely decompensated cirrhosis; very high short-term (<28 days) mortality.
  • Develops in cirrhosis w/ superimposed liver injury → profound systemic inflammation (e.g., viral infection, DILI, alcohol-associated hepatitis, bacterial infection).
  • Characterized by: intense systemic inflammation, close temporal relationship to precipitating events, single- or multiple-organ failure.
Distinguishing Features (ALF vs. ACLF)
  • ALF: No prior liver disease, acute onset, often younger patients, high potential for LT.
  • ACLF: Preexisting cirrhosis, acute decompensation, older patients, often multiple organ failures, LT more complex.
Epidemiology
  • ALF remains rare; incidence data not robust.
  • Studies limited by cohorts, patient population source, or single-cause inclusion.
  • Data often tainted by inaccurate coding of decompensated cirrhosis or ACLF as ALF.
Incidence Rates
  • Developed countries: ≈1–6 cases per million population.
  • United States: ≈2,000–3,000 cases per year.
  • UK & Scotland: Up to 0.8 per 100,000 person-years (PY) & ≈0.62 per 100,000 PY, respectively.
  • Germany (state-insured cohort): Up to 1.13 per 100,000 PY.
  • US-based health system (drug-induced ALF): 0.161 per 100,000 PY.
  • Higher in Taiwan (8.02 per 100,000 PY) & Thailand (6.29 per 100,000 PY).
Economic Burden & Research Gaps
  • Less data exist on economic burden.
  • Few controlled clinical trials on management.
  • Guidelines predominantly based on expert opinion, not evidence-based medicine, due to lack of solid data.
General Management
  • Focus on distinguishing acute from chronic/acute-on-chronic liver failure.
  • Early recognition & timely referral to a liver transplant center are critical for potentially lifesaving treatment & transplant considerations.
Initial Assessment & Diagnostic Evaluation
  • Comprehensive history: symptom timeline, chronic liver disease, alcohol, viral risk factors, medication review (Rx, OTC, CAM).
  • Contact next of kin if patient history unobtainable.
  • Thorough physical exam: vital signs, jaundice, signs of chronic liver disease, mental status assessment.
Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE) Grading
  • Type A HE graded by West-Haven Criteria.
  • Grade 2 encephalopathy → transfer to ICU.
  • Grades 3 & 4 HE → consider intubation for airway protection.
Coagulopathy Assessment
  • Assessed in every patient.
Laboratory & Diagnostic Workup
  • Initial labs: CBC, electrolytes, LFTs, INR, PTT, ammonia, glucose, lactate, ABG, viral hepatitis serologies (A, B, C, E), HIV, autoimmune markers (ANA, ASMA, IgG), ceruloplasmin, alpha-1 antitrypsin, toxicology screen (urine/serum), APAP level, pregnancy test.
  • Imaging: Abdominal ultrasound w/ Doppler, CT/MRI abdomen.
  • Special tests: Urine ethyl glucuronide or serum phosphatidyl ethanol (PETH) for alcohol suspicion.
Imaging Studies
  • Help identify underlying chronic liver disease/ACLF (shrunken liver, regenerative nodules, irregular surface).
  • Liver may appear shrunken in ALF due to massive collapse.
Alcohol-Induced Liver Disease
  • Chronic alcohol consumption adversely affects outcomes.
  • Biomarkers (ethyl glucuronide or PETH) should be used + psychosocial assessment.
Key Concepts: Initial Assessment
  • Comprehensive testing to elucidate diagnosis & exclude underlying chronic liver disease is essential.
  • Biomarkers (ethyl glucuronide or PETH) should be used in addition to psychosocial assessment when alcohol-induced liver disease is suspected.
  • Grade 2 encephalopathy → prompt transfer to ICU; intubation for airway protection considered for grades 3 & 4 HE.
  • Referral for hepatology/gastroenterology consultation ASAP after ALF identification.
  • Early communication w/ transplant center is crucial.
Liver Biopsy
  • Can help diagnose ALF etiology & predict outcomes in selected patients.
  • Helps rule out infiltrative disease/malignancy & identify contraindications to LT.
  • Can aid in AIH diagnosis, which may respond to immunosuppressive therapy, potentially sparing LT.
Safety & Efficacy
  • Concerns exist regarding risks in coagulopathic patients (bleeding, organ perforation, sepsis, death).
  • Small observational studies suggest transjugular liver biopsy (TJLB) is safe & effective.
  • Mini laparoscopy w/ liver biopsy also safe but not widely available.
TJLB vs. Other Methods
  • TJLB is frequently used.
  • Despite smaller sample, TJLB valuable in determining hepatocellular necrosis, which predicts higher death rate in non-APAP ALF.
Key Concepts: Liver Biopsy
  • Liver biopsy may help exclude infiltrative disease/malignancy & identify LT contraindications.
  • Liver biopsy may help diagnose AIH, which may respond to immunosuppressive therapy.
  • Insufficient evidence to recommend routine use in other settings.
  • Suggest using TJLB over other methods when considering liver biopsy.
Referral for Liver Transplantation (LT)
  • LT is a lifesaving procedure for some patients w/ ALF.
  • Relies on graft availability, significant resources, morbidity/mortality, lifelong immunosuppression.
  • Approximately half of ALF patients undergo LT; 1-year survival rates: 79% (Europe), 84% (US).
Prognostic Scoring Systems
  • King's College Criteria (KCC) & Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) are most studied.
  • KCC more accurately predicts hospital mortality in APAP-associated ALF.
  • MELD scores more accurately predict mortality in non-APAP-associated ALF.
System-Specific Management
  • ALF affects virtually every organ system, requiring targeted management strategies.
Central Nervous System (CNS)
  • Neurological manifestations range from hyperammonemia to HE & ultimately cerebral edema (CE) w/ increased intracranial pressure (ICP).
  • Progression to grade 2 encephalopathy necessitates ICU monitoring.
  • Advanced grade 3–4 HE → endotracheal intubation for airway protection.
Hyperammonemia Management
  • Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) effectively ↓ ammonia levels.
  • ↓ serum ammonia w/ CRRT → ↓ mortality & ↑ transplant-free survival (TFS) @ 21 days.
  • ↓ intracranial hypertension (ICH) & CE associated w/ ↑ CRRT use in APAP-induced ALF.
  • Ornithine phenylacetate: failed to show improvement in RCT for cirrhosis, thus not endorsed for ALF.
Recommendation: CRRT for Hyperammonemia
  • In patients w/ ALF & grade 2 or higher encephalopathy, suggest early CRRT for hyperammonemia management, even w/o conventional RRT indications.
  • GRADE: conditional, very low quality of evidence.
Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE) Treatment
  • Lactulose: first-line for HE in chronic liver disease; adopted orally for ALF patients alert enough to maintain airway (grade 1–2).
  • Discontinue lactulose in advanced grade 3–4 HE.
  • Rifaximin: effective w/ lactulose in chronic liver disease; adopted by many transplant centers for ALF, but data lacking.
Cerebral Edema (CE) & Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)
  • Overall incidence of ↑ ICP ↓ in ALF, but mortality remains high when present.
  • First-line treatment: hyperosmolar therapy (mannitol, hypertonic saline), hyperventilation, CRRT.
  • Avoid hyponatremia; target serum sodium 145–150 mmol/L (no supporting literature for this specific range).
  • Correction rate: not exceeding 6–8 mmol/L in 24 hours.
  • Hypertonic saline (3%): bolus 250–500 mL or continuous infusion to maintain serum sodium <160 mmol/L.
ICP Monitoring
  • Utility questioned due to ↑ CRRT use & ↓ ICH rates.
  • No survival advantage for ICP monitoring in recent reviews; favor general ICP-lowering strategies.
  • May be considered in centers w/ expertise & highly selected patients.
Therapeutic Hypothermia
  • Studies heterogeneous; intervention did not improve overall patient survival despite efficiency & low bleeding risk.
Key Concepts: CNS Management
  • Patients w/ ALF w/ grade 2 or higher encephalopathy should be monitored in an ICU setting.
  • Patients w/ ALF w/ grade 3 & 4 encephalopathy should be intubated for airway protection.
  • No conclusive evidence to recommend for/against lactulose or rifaximin for HE in ALF.
  • No conclusive evidence to recommend routine ICP monitor placement in ALF.
  • No conclusive evidence to recommend routine use of hypothermia to control ICP in ALF.
Coagulopathy
  • Complex mechanism, incompletely understood; multiple factors contribute to hemostasis changes.
  • INR is not an accurate predictor of bleeding risk in ALF despite frequent extreme elevation.
  • Evidence suggests a rebalanced state of hemostasis.
  • Clinically significant bleeding uncommon (≈5% of deaths).
Misinterpretation of INR
  • Elevated INR often misinterpreted as ↑ hemorrhagic tendency → improper prophylactic transfusion.
  • Routine use of FFP, cryoprecipitate, platelets, or other correction may ↑ risk of death or need for LT due to transfusion reaction, thrombosis, transfusion-related acute lung injury.
Viscoelastic Testing (VET)
  • May allow more global assessment of procoagulant/anticoagulant pathways, hyperfibrinolysis, platelet function, clot formation.
  • 62% of ALI/ALF patients had normal mean rotational thromboelastography parameters despite mean INR of 3.4 ± 1.7.
  • Associated w/ disease severity, but less clear association w/ bleeding events.
  • Limitation: lacks ability to assess protein C & von Willebrand factor activity.
  • Society of Critical Care Medicine recommends VET over INR for bleeding/thrombosis risk in critically ill ALF patients.
Correction for Invasive Procedures
  • May be necessary before high-risk procedures (e.g., ICP monitoring) due to intracranial hemorrhage risk.
  • FFP & platelet transfusions have volume overload risk; Factor VII used in these settings.
  • ICP monitors can be placed safely when clinical protocol followed, including aggressive coagulopathy correction.
Key Concepts: Coagulopathy
  • INR does not accurately reflect bleeding risk in ALF patients.
  • Viscoelastic tests may provide a more accurate assessment of coagulopathy in ALF patients.
Recommendation: Routine Coagulopathy Correction
  • In patients w/ ALF, in absence of active bleeding or impending high-risk procedure, recommend against routine correction of coagulopathy.
  • GRADE: conditional, very low quality of evidence.
Infection
  • High incidence of bacterial infections in ALF, associated w/ high mortality.
  • Fungal infections account for up to 32% of infections.
  • Prophylactic antimicrobials: not supported by data; did not ↓ bloodstream infection rate or 21-day mortality.
Early Detection & Biomarkers
  • Usual indicators (leukocytosis, fever) absent in up to 30% of infected ALF cases.
  • Procalcitonin: elevated in both infected & uninfected ALF/chronic liver disease patients; poor indicator of infection, seems to indicate inflammation.
  • Regular surveillance of blood, urine, sputum cultures recommended.
Empiric Therapy
  • Empiric antibiotic & antifungal therapies may be considered w/ clinical deterioration.
  • If antimicrobial prophylaxis undertaken, consider local microbial resistance patterns.
Key Concepts: Infection
  • Early assessment for infection is prudent because clinical signs are frequently absent.
  • Insufficient evidence to recommend procalcitonin as infection biomarker.
  • Empiric antibiotic & antifungal therapies may be considered w/ clinical deterioration.
  • Suggest regular surveillance cultures; optimal frequency unknown.
Recommendation: Prophylactic Antimicrobials
  • In patients w/ ALF, recommend against routine use of prophylactic antimicrobial agents, given no improvement in bloodstream infection rate or 21-day mortality.
  • GRADE: conditional, low quality of evidence.
Hemodynamics and Renal Failure
  • Hemodynamic profile resembles septic shock: hyperdynamic circulation, high cardiac output, low systemic vascular resistance, ↓ effective circulating volume.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) common (up to 70% of US ALFSG patients; 30% required RRT).
Hemodynamic Management
  • IV fluid resuscitation: primary intervention to maintain adequate tissue perfusion.
  • Pulse pressure variation measurements: proposed to assess fluid responsiveness & guide vasopressor need (requires local expertise, further validation needed).
  • Vasopressor use: if IV fluids ineffective, to maintain satisfactory mean arterial pressure (MAP).
  • Target MAP: maintain cerebral perfusion pressure of 60–80 mm Hg.
  • Norepinephrine: preferred vasopressor → survival benefit & ↓ adverse outcomes.
  • Vasopressin: may be added to potentiate norepinephrine effects.
Recommendations: Hemodynamics
  • In patients w/ ALF, recommend norepinephrine as first-line vasopressor for hypotension refractory to fluid resuscitation.
  • GRADE: strong, moderate quality of evidence.
  • In patients w/ ALF w/ hypotension not responsive to norepinephrine, suggest adding vasopressin as a secondary agent.
  • GRADE: conditional, low quality of evidence.
Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)
  • Pathogenesis multifactorial: direct nephrotoxicity, sepsis, hemodynamic instability.
  • Increased severity of renal dysfunction associated w/ ↑ mortality.
  • Most patients w/ AKI in US ALFSG cohort did not require ongoing renal support after ALF resolution.
Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)
  • Indications: acid-base disturbances, oliguria, volume overload.
  • CRRT: preferred modality → ↓ risk of cardiovascular instability & CE vs. intermittent hemodialysis.
  • CRRT effectively ↓ ammonia level; hyperammonemia is an increasing indication for RRT independent of AKI.
  • Early CRRT for hyperammonemia improves survival by preventing severe complications.
Key Concepts: Hemodynamics & Renal Failure
  • In patients w/ ALF & hypotension, IV fluid resuscitation should be initiated.
  • RRT should be considered early in patients w/ AKI, electrolyte/metabolic abnormalities, and/or volume overload.
  • In patients w/ ALF requiring RRT, recommend CRRT over intermittent hemodialysis.
Nutritional and Metabolic Support
  • Severe loss of hepatocellular function → abnormal carbohydrate, protein, lipid metabolism.
  • Energy expenditure ↑ by 18%–30% vs. healthy controls.
  • Nutritional support recommended if oral intake not expected within 5–7 days.
  • Oral nutrition for mild mental status alterations; otherwise, enteral support preferred.
Protein Intake
  • Increased protein intake not shown to worsen encephalopathy in cirrhosis; 1.0–1.5 gm/kg daily recommended.
  • Concern w/ severe hyperammonemia (>150 μMol/L) & HE in ALF.
  • Consider delayed supplementation for first 24–48 hours, then restart at lower range (1.0 gm/kg) w/ close ammonia monitoring.
Glucose, Fluid, and Electrolyte Support
  • More urgent concern due to short ALF duration.
  • Hypoglycemia: frequent manifestation → ↓ hepatic glycogen, impaired gluconeogenesis, insulin resistance.
  • Hypoglycemia contributes to encephalopathy & associated w/ ↑ mortality.
  • Monitor mental status every 1–2 hours.
  • Dextrose 10% solution: constant infusion to maintain blood sugar 150–180 mg/dL.
  • Avoid hypotonic solutions → hyponatremia risk & CE worsening.
  • Magnesium & phosphorus: monitor every 8–12 hours, replenish as needed.
Key Concepts: Nutritional & Metabolic Support
  • In patients w/ ALF, monitoring & correction of glucose, fluid, & electrolyte imbalances should be performed.
  • In patients w/ ALF, enteral nutritional support should be started if patient unable to resume oral intake within 5–7 days.
Other Management Considerations
  • Exploration of advanced therapies for liver support.
High-Volume Plasma Exchange (HVPE)
  • Plasmapheresis of 8–12 L or 15% of ideal body weight w/ FFP.
  • Associated w/ improved transplant-free survival.
  • Retrospective review (32 ALF patients): overall survival 94% (treated) vs 69% (untreated).
  • HVPE improved coagulopathy, bilirubin, ammonia levels.
  • Concern regarding applicability across varying ALF etiologies.
Artificial Liver Support Systems
  • Great interest as bridge to transplant or liver recovery.
  • Two types: artificial & bioartificial.
  • None received US FDA marketing approval; available for investigational/compassionate use.
  • Examples: Molecular Adsorbent Recirculating System, Single-Pass Albumin Dialysis, Fractionated Plasma Separation and Adsorption system (FPSA).
  • Efficacy data: lack of evidence to support any particular system.
  • Only RCT (102 patients): improved 6-month survival only in APAP-induced ALF group.
Key Concept: HVPE & Artificial Liver Support
  • Insufficient evidence to recommend for/against routine use of HVPE or artificial/bioartificial liver support devices in ALF patients.
Etiology-Specific Management
  • Time-sensitive, etiology-specific interventions are crucial in addition to general management.
Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI)
  • Leading cause of ALF in US & many Western countries.
Acetaminophen Hepatotoxicity (APAP)
  • Leading cause of DILI & ALF in US.
  • Occurs dose-dependently: single intentional overdose (>10–15g) or unintentional (>10g over days).
  • Fasting/alcohol ingestion may contribute to toxicity even at recommended dosages.
  • Mechanism: high doses overwhelm sulfation/glucuronidation → shunted to cytochrome P450 pathways → toxic metabolite N-acetyl-p-benzoquinoneimine (NAPQI) formation → oxidative hepatocyte injury & necrosis.
  • APAP levels may be undetectable @ presentation; NAPQI-protein adducts may aid identification.
  • Presentation: asymptomatic or nonspecific constitutional symptoms initially; rapid progression to liver failure within 72–96 hours.
  • Labs: predominantly hepatocellular pattern, marked transaminase elevations (>3,000 U/L), coagulopathy, relatively mild hyperbilirubinemia.
  • Prognosis: most recover w/ aggressive medical management, esp. w/ early identification/treatment.
  • Mortality rate ≈28%; up to 1/3 require LT.
Management of APAP Hepatotoxicity
  • Immediate intervention for suspected toxicity.
  • Gastric decontamination: 1–2 g/kg single-dose activated charcoal effective if administered within 4 hours post-ingestion; data support administration after 4 hours, esp. w/ NAC.
  • N-acetylcysteine (NAC): only effective antidote; IV preferred route (ease of use, tolerability).
  • Continue NAC beyond initial protocol for persistent coagulopathy (INR >1.5) & encephalopathy.
  • Begin NAC as soon as toxicity suspected, even if ingestion time unknown (proven benefit of late administration).
  • Rumack-Matthew nomogram: guides administration for single time point ingestions.
  • End point: INR <1.5, ALT <50% of peak (or 3 consecutive values <1,000 IU/L), and/or undetectable APAP level.
  • Novel therapies: 4-Methylpyrazole (NAPQI inhibition), Calmangafodipir (early safety/tolerability w/ NAC, but mechanism/benefits lacking).
Key Concepts: APAP Hepatotoxicity
  • Duration of NAC treatment should be individualized based on clinical condition & lab values.
  • For APAP overdose, recommend single-dose activated charcoal if ingestion known within 4 hours.
Recommendation: Early NAC for APAP Toxicity
  • In patients w/ suspected APAP toxicity, recommend early administration of NAC.
  • GRADE: strong, low quality of evidence.
Nonacetaminophen Drug Hepatotoxicity (I-DILI)
  • Second leading cause of ALF after APAP in US & Western countries.
  • Not dose-dependent; variable latency period.
  • Most commonly implicated: antimicrobials (antituberculosis, sulfa, nitrofurantoin, terbinafine, azole antifungals).
  • Second most common: Complementary & Alternative Medications (CAM) → 8-fold ↑ in CAM-associated ALF over 25 years (2.9% to 24.1% of I-DILI ALF cases).
  • Demographics: most I-DILI patients are women (66%–71%).
  • Presentation: historically advanced coma grade ≥2 (68%); newer data show most (66.4%) w/ lower-grade encephalopathy.
  • Labs: deep jaundice (bilirubin >15 mg/dL), predominantly hepatocellular injury (aminotransferases generally <1,000 IU/L).
  • Prognosis: favorable generally, but ALF progression has dismal TFS (23.5%–38.7% @ 3 weeks); overall survival 66%.
  • CAM-induced ALF: more severe than prescription DILI → significantly higher LT rates (61% vs 36%, P < 0.005) & lower 21-day TFS (17% vs 34%, P = 0.044).
  • Resources: NIH-funded database www.LiverTox.nih.gov for DILI evaluation.
Management of Non-APAP Drug Hepatotoxicity
  • Discontinue suspect drug immediately.
  • Supportive care is primary.
  • IV NAC: improved TFS in non-APAP ALF w/ early coma grade (I-II) → 52% (treatment) vs 30% (placebo).
  • Meta-analysis: overall survival, posttransplant survival, TFS better in NAC-treated group (5% of patients had DILI).
  • Corticosteroid therapy: may be effective for hypersensitivity or autoimmune features (e.g., minocycline, nitrofurantoin).
  • Less certain efficacy w/o immune-related features; studies small/heterogeneous.
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitor hepatitis: cessation crucial; corticosteroid therapy for persistent grade 2 or any grade 3/4 aminotransferase/bilirubin elevation.
  • ALF rarely described; LT may not be viable due to underlying malignancy/hyperacute rejection risk.
Recommendation: IV NAC for Non-APAP ALF
  • In patients w/ non-APAP ALF, suggest initiation of IV NAC.
  • GRADE: strong, moderate quality of evidence.
Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms (DRESS)
  • Rare, severe drug-induced systemic illness.
  • Characterized by extensive skin rash, fever, eosinophilia, atypical lymphocytosis, multiorgan dysfunction.
  • Presentation: typically 2–8 weeks after exposure.
  • Hepatotoxicity: most commonly cholestasis (37%) or cholestatic hepatitis (27%).
  • May progress to ALF: 7 of 16 patients developed ALF, 2 died, 5 underwent LT.
Viral Hepatitis
  • Worldwide, AVH is leading cause of ALF.
  • In US ALFSG, AVH accounts for 12% of ALF cases (Hepatitis A: 3%, B: 7%, E: 2%).
  • Japan: 40% of ALF due to HBV; India/Bangladesh: nearly half due to acute HEV.
  • Acute HCV generally not associated w/ ALF.
  • Rare causes: HSV, zoster, CMV, EBV, adenovirus (often in immunocompromised).
  • AVH-induced ALF suspected to be underrecognized; substantial proportion of indeterminate cases may be viral.
  • Presentation: acute-to-subacute w/ viral prodrome (fevers, chills, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, malaise, body aches, flu-like illness) preceding ALF signs/symptoms.
  • Labs: predominantly hepatocellular pattern, markedly elevated transaminase levels.
  • Prognosis: poor spontaneous survival (25%) for AVH; manage in transplant center.
Hepatitis A (HAV)
  • Favorable prognosis generally; <1% of adults progress to ALF.
  • HAV ALF: transplant-free survival only 70%.
  • Prognostic model (day 1): ALT <2,600 IU/L, creatinine >2.0 mg/dL, intubation, pressor use → ≥2 factors predict worse outcomes (LT/death) w/ 92% sensitivity, 88% specificity.
  • 25% of patients showed negative PCR, correlating w/ significantly worse outcomes (lower spontaneous survival).
  • Vaccination available for postexposure prophylaxis.
Management of HAV
  • Largely supportive; no specific antiviral agent proven effective.
  • Early LT associated w/ good outcomes when needed.
Hepatitis B (HBV)
  • Causes ALF in ≈1% of infected patients; incidence may be underestimated (HBV DNA & HBsAg undetectable @ presentation).
  • US: de novo infection & reactivation of latent infection (immunocompromised) are significant causes.
  • Unfavorable prognosis: up to 75% require LT or die.
  • Major risk factor: coinfection/superinfection w/ hepatitis D virus (HDV) → up to 20% of HBV-HDV acute coinfection & 5% of HDV superinfection result in fulminant ALF (up to 80% mortality w/o transplant).
  • Vaccination safe, effective, widely available.
Management of HBV and HDV
  • Distinguishing primary vs. exacerbation of chronic infection can be difficult.
  • Antiviral therapy in acute HBV-ALF: controversial.
  • Less relevant in primary infections (VL already low, immune-mediated injury).
  • Earlier studies: clear benefit of lamivudine in fulminant HBV, esp. if initiated early.
  • Later study: no difference in outcomes between nucleoside(tide) analog-treated vs. untreated.
  • Antiviral therapy justifiable in severe hepatitis due to HBV reactivation (high VL).
  • Early lamivudine/entecavir for severe reactivation HBV: significantly ↓ VL; tenofovir showed improved survival.
  • No data for tenofovir alafenamide.
  • HDV superinfection: no effective therapy beyond HBV antiviral therapy (interferon contraindicated in ALF).
  • Myrcludex B (bulevirtide) shows early promise but not evaluated in ALF.
  • Lonarfarnib (prenylation inhibitor) in phase 3 trials for non-ALF patients.
Recommendation: Antiviral for HBV Reactivation
  • In patients w/ ALF due to reactivation of HBV, recommend starting antiviral therapy w/ entecavir-based or tenofovir-based regimen.
  • GRADE: strong, low quality of evidence.
Hepatitis E (HEV)
  • Accounts for up to 40% of ALF in developing countries; grossly underrecognized in Western countries.
  • US/Germany studies: up to 1/5 to 1/2 of DILI-attributed ALF cases were HEV.
  • Most cases: pregnancy or immunocompromised patients.
  • Pregnant women: highest mortality risk (up to 25%).
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation: distinctive feature of HEV-ALF during pregnancy.
  • Severity of HEV infection & VL in mother predicts vertically transmitted infection in fetus.
  • HEV recombinant vaccine approved in China (2012); no US FDA-approved vaccine.
Management of HEV
  • Most cases self-limited; usually supportive care only.
  • Some evidence for chronic HEV treatment w/ pegylated interferon or ribavirin, but no proven benefit in ALF.
  • LT remains only treatment option in HEV-ALF.
Rare Viral Infections
  • Case reports/series of ALF attributed to HSV, zoster, CMV, EBV, adenovirus.
  • Most often implicated in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Not always clear if reactivation of latent viruses (EBV, CMV) is primary insult or systemic disease consequence.
  • Herpes viruses: skin manifestations not always apparent.
Hepatitis C (HCV)
  • Can cause severe hepatitis, but no definitive evidence for ALF.
  • Current antiviral therapies → >95% cure rate.
Management of Rare Viral Infections
  • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): Early IV acyclovir indicated if suspected (best chance for good outcome); do not wait for serology.
  • HSV-induced ALF carries poor prognosis despite rapid antiviral treatment.
  • Viral resistance to acyclovir low, but up to 10% in immunocompromised → IV foscarnet alternative.
  • Risk factors for death/LTx: male, older, immunocompromised, ALT >5,000 IU/L, platelets <75 × 103/L, coagulopathy, encephalopathy.
  • LT is rescue therapy; lifelong antiviral suppressive therapy indicated due to recurrence risk.
  • Varicella Zoster Virus: Rare cause of ALF; suspect w/ characteristic rash.
  • Prompt treatment w/ IV acyclovir.
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV): Rarely implicated in immunosuppression.
  • IV ganciclovir recommended for CMV hepatitis.
  • Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV): Primary infection seen in <1% of ALF cases; high case fatality rate.
  • Treatment: acyclovir or ganciclovir.
Key Concept: HSV/Zoster Empiric Treatment
  • In patients w/ ALF, grade 2 encephalopathy, & features suggestive of HSV or zoster infection, suggest empiric treatment w/ IV acyclovir until confirmatory viral PCRobtained.
Mushroom Poisoning
  • Hepatotoxicity after ingestion of amatoxin-containing mushrooms (Amanita sp., Galerina sp., Lepiota sp.).
  • ≈50 lethal exposures annually in US, mostly Amanita species.
  • Amanitin: heat stable, water insoluble; 1-2 medium caps lethal dose.
  • Toxins concentrated in hepatocytes → apoptosis.
  • Patients progress through 3 distinct clinical phases after ingestion.
Clinical Phases
  • 6–12 hours: Gastroenteritis (vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dehydration).
  • 24–36 hours: Quiescent period (clinical symptom improvement, but evolving hepatotoxicity labs).
  • 4–7 days: Onset of progressive liver & multiorgan failure (coagulopathy, acidosis, encephalopathy, seizures, renal failure).
Diagnosis & Prognosis
  • Confirmatory tests not available; exclude mushroom ingestion in all ALF patients.
  • Previously high death risk w/o LT; recent data: most (23/27) survive w/o transplantation.
  • Favorable prognosis predictors: peak AST <4,000 IU/mL, peak INR <2, serum factor V >30%.
  • Mushroom-specific prognostic criteria (Escudie criteria) can predict 28-day mortality w/ 100% accuracy, identifying fatal cases earlier than KCC.
  • LT evaluation can be initiated before HE development based on Escudie criteria.
Management of Mushroom Toxicity
  • Contact poison control center for guidance.
  • Gastric lavage: recommended within 1 hour of toxin ingestion to prevent absorption (may not be possible due to lag time).
  • Contraindications to lavage: recent surgery, GI hemorrhage, altered mental status.
  • Activated charcoal: recommended soon after ingestion to disrupt enterohepatic circulation of amatoxin.
  • Doses: 50g every 4 hours or 25g every 2 hours; reduced to 12.5g every hour for tolerability.
  • IV hydration: maintain urinary output 100–200 mL/hr for 4–5 days to eliminate toxins (60%–80% amatoxins filtered by kidneys in first few hours).
  • IV silibinin dihemisuccinate: sufficient evidence to support use in acute Amanita phalloides poisoning.
  • Dosing: 20–50 mg/kg/d for 48–96 hours, or 5 mg/kg over 1 hour then 20 mg/kg/d for 6 days or until transaminases normalize.
  • Mechanism: α-amanitin membrane transport inhibitor & free radical scavenger.
  • US ALFSG data: only 23% received silibinin (not US FDA approved).
  • IV penicillin G: believed to block hepatic uptake of α-amanitin; second-line therapy (inferior to silibinin).
  • Dosing: continuous infusion 1,000,000 IU/kg day 1, then 1,500,000 IU/kg days 2 & 3.
  • IV NAC: beneficial in Amanita phalloides poisoning.
  • Dosing: 150 mg/kg over 15 min, then 50 mg/kg over 4 hours, then 100 mg/kg over 16 hours (or similar to APAP toxicity).
  • Hemodialysis, hemoperfusion, plasmapheresis: little benefit due to rapid absorption/excretion of amatoxin.
  • Molecular Adsorbent Recirculating System: some evidence of benefit, but more data needed.
  • LT: effective intervention for ALF due to Amanita phalloides poisoning w/ excellent outcomes.
Key Concepts: Mushroom Poisoning
  • Escudie criteria can predict need for LT even before HE development.
  • Gastric lavage & activated charcoal should be administered within first few hours after ingestion, provided no contraindications.
Recommendation: IV Silibinin for Mushroom Poisoning
  • In patients w/ ALF due to mushroom poisoning, recommend initiation of IV silibinin ASAP.
  • IV penicillin G may be used if IV silibinin unavailable.
  • GRADE: conditional, very low quality of evidence.
Wilson Disease (WD)
  • Can manifest from asymptomatic liver enzyme abnormalities to acute decompensated disease.
  • Most patients 5–35 years old; women more likely to present w/ ALF (2:1 female:male).
  • ALF can occur in known WD patients discontinuing or poorly adhering to copper-chelating therapy.
  • Labs: Coombs-negative hemolytic anemia (acute intravascular hemolysis), rapid progression to renal failure, modest transaminase elevation, normal/very low alkaline phosphatase (<40 IU/L).
  • Serum copper: may be markedly elevated due to sudden release from injured liver tissue.
  • Low ceruloplasmin: common in ALF from other etiologies too.
  • Liver biopsy: rarely needed; if performed, often shows underlying advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis.
  • Diagnosis: quantitative copper measurement of liver tissue or genetic testing (should not delay LT consideration).
  • Prognosis: guarded w/o LT; medical therapy alone rarely successful.
Management of Acute Decompensated WD
  • Copper depletion: plasmapheresis, albumin dialysis, plasma exchange, continuous hemofiltration (temporizing measures to avoid renal injury).
  • Copper chelators (D-penicillamine, trientine): ineffective in acute decompensated WD.
  • Prognostic criteria: WD prognostic index, revised King's College score of WD prognostic index.
  • LT: excellent outcomes; 1-year & 5-year patient survival 90.1% & 89% (children), 88.3% & 86% (adults).
Key Concept: LT Evaluation for WD
  • In patients w/ ALF due to suspected/confirmed WD, LT evaluation should be initiated during diagnosis due to lack of effective medical therapy.
Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)
  • Acute severe AIH (AS-AIH): jaundice, no cirrhosis, INR ≥1.5, symptom onset <26 weeks.
  • Progresses to ALF in up to 3%–6% of patients.
  • Black patients w/ AIH: higher risk of ALF requiring LT vs. White patients.
  • HLA types DR3 & DR7: more common w/ type 2 AIH, associated w/ more severe disease.
  • Diagnosis: difficult due to overlap w/ exacerbation of chronic AIH or immune-mediated DILI.
  • Presentation: typically subacute; hepatocellular injury pattern.
  • Serology: elevated ANA, ASMA, IgG.
  • Liver biopsy: may show nonspecific findings; distinguishing features: centrilobular zone injury, prominent lymphoplasmacytic lobular inflammation, centrilobular confluent necrosis w/o significant portal inflammation.
  • US ALFSG: 2 specific patterns of massive hepatic necrosis (centrilobular hemorrhagic necrosis or confluent necrosis superimposed on chronic hepatitis) more specific for autoimmune etiology.
  • Plasma cell infiltrate: characteristic of AIH.
  • AS-AIH likely underrecognized → delayed treatment.
  • Prognosis: significantly improved w/ corticosteroid therapy & LT.
  • Pretransplant era survival <20%; recent series mortality 16%–19%; prognosis associated w/ initial disease severity.
Management of AIH
  • Corticosteroid therapy: well-established for chronic AIH.
  • AS-AIH w/o ALF: glucocorticoid therapy (prednisone/prednisolone 0.5–1 mg/kg or 60 mg daily) beneficial w/o ↑ infection risk.
  • Therapy should not delay LT evaluation.
  • Up to 48% of treated patients likely to require LT.
  • SURFASA score (INR + bilirubin): highly predictive (88% specificity, 84% sensitivity) of LT or death in AS-AIH w/o ALF.
  • Identifies nonresponders within 3 days of corticosteroids for LT referral.
  • Corticosteroid therapy in chronic AIH exacerbation or ALF: mixed data (improved outcome/TFS vs. no benefit).
  • More recent data: in select patients, corticosteroids may improve outcome & TFS in AIH-ALF.
  • Vigilant surveillance for infection.
  • Budesonide & other immunosuppressants (tacrolimus): not well supported for severe acute AIH; patients should be immediately evaluated for LT.
Key Concepts: AIH Management
  • In patients w/ AS-AIH, recommend use of IV corticosteroids.
  • In patients w/ AS-AIH that has progressed to ALF, recommend early evaluation for LT.
Pregnancy-Related Acute Liver Failure
  • Rare (2.2% of >3,100 patients in US ALFSG).
  • Pregnancy-specific causes: HELLP syndrome & acute fatty liver of pregnancy (AFLP).
  • Present during third trimester or immediate postpartum period.
  • Difficult to distinguish HELLP & AFLP due to overlapping features.
  • HELLP & AFLP each accounted for 25% of cases; remaining half due to nonpregnancy-specific causes (APAP, HSV, AIH, DILI, cancers, Kikuchi-Fujimoto, thyrotoxicosis).
  • Median gestational age @ presentation: higher in HELLP/AFLP (36 weeks) vs. nonpregnancy-specific ALF (30 weeks).
  • 2/3 of HELLP/AFLP patients experienced preeclampsia/eclampsia; ≈90% required emergency cesarean delivery.
  • Outcomes: 69% spontaneous recovery, 14% survived after LT, 11% died @ 21 days.
  • Predictors of worse survival in HELLP/AFLP ALF: HE, elevated lactate, higher MELD score.
Management of Pregnancy-Related ALF
  • Obstetric emergency; multidisciplinary care.
  • Cornerstone: prompt delivery of fetus ASAP.
  • Supportive care for metabolic, renal, respiratory complications.
  • Clinical/laboratory abnormalities may persist for weeks post-delivery; careful decision for LT.
  • Swansea criteria: developed for AFLP diagnosis.
  • Increased Swansea score, HE, platelet-to-white blood cell ratio (PWR) may be helpful prognostic indicators.
  • LT for AFLP: good survival outcomes comparable to other etiologies.
  • Hepatic bleeding, hematoma, rupture: may occur (esp. HELLP); may require urgent surgical intervention.
Key Concepts: Pregnancy-Related ALF
  • Supportive care & multidisciplinary management essential; prompt delivery of fetus crucial.
  • In patients w/ pregnancy-associated ALF who fail to improve after delivery, suggest prompt evaluation for LT.
Budd-Chiari Syndrome (BCS)
  • Rarest cause of ALF; most often White women in 4th–5th decades.
  • Characterized by obstruction of hepatic venous outflow tract (venous thrombosis).
  • Underlying hypercoagulable states: pregnancy, oral contraceptive use, polycythemia vera, membranous webs.
  • Complete occlusion of hepatic veins → ALF via severe ischemia & massive hepatocyte necrosis.
  • Presentation: acute-to-subacute illness w/ abdominal pain & ascites.
  • Labs: predominantly hepatocellular liver injury, marked aminotransferase elevation (AST > ALT in 1000s).
  • Diagnosis: evaluate for underlying hypercoagulable disorder.
  • Prognosis: historically dismal TFS (37%–40%); ↑ to 80% in recent decades.
Management of BCS
  • Mainstay: initiate anticoagulation to halt thrombosis & restore patency.
  • IV heparin: start promptly if no contraindications.
  • ALFSG review: 71% anticoagulated w/ heparin; overall survival 42%; anticoagulated patients more likely to survive, esp. w/ early initiation.
  • Other interventions: thrombolysis + angioplasty, TIPS, surgical shunting, LT.
  • Thrombolytic therapy: risk of bleeding, stroke, pulmonary embolism; reserved for select patients w/ recent clots.
  • Angioplasty: effective for membranous web obstruction.
  • Shunt creation: necessary for those failing medical therapy, angioplasty, or stenting.
  • TIPS or direct intrahepatic portocaval shunt preferred over transabdominal surgical shunts.
  • TIPS data increasing despite lack of RCTs; polytetrafluoroethylene stent preferred (↓ occlusion rate).
  • LT: timely listing for BCS-ALF patients not responding to medical/therapeutic intervention.
  • UNOS data: BCS status 1A patients had comparable posttransplant survival to non-status 1A (1-year: 82% vs 86%).
  • Large European study: 1-year, 3-year, 5-year posttransplant survival rates of 76%, 71%, 68%.
Key Concepts: BCS Management
  • In patients w/ BCS leading to ALF, TIPS is preferred intervention in those who fail anticoagulation.
  • In patients w/ BCS-induced ALF, recommend heparin as initial therapy, in absence of contraindications to anticoagulation.
  • In patients w/ BCS-induced ALF who do not respond to medical & therapeutic interventions, recommend LT.
Secondary Causes of Acute Liver Failure
  • Less common but important etiologies.
Ischemic Liver Injury
  • Also called 'hypoxic hepatitis' or 'shock liver'.
  • Results from transiently or persistently ↓ hepatic perfusion.
  • Often occurs w/ congestive heart failure, sepsis, traumatic injury, major surgery.
  • Documented hypotension not always identified.
  • Presentation: acute-to-subacute; severe hepatocellular injury (marked AST >10,000 IU/L).
  • Labs: bilirubin typically normal initially, often worsens w/ rising INR despite transaminase improvement.
  • Prognosis: rapid clinical improvement w/ restored hemodynamic status; largely dictated by underlying condition.
  • LT not usually indicated or necessary.
Management of Ischemic Liver Injury
  • Largely supportive; aimed at correcting underlying cause & restoring hemodynamic stability.
  • Vasopressor use: may be necessary to maintain satisfactory MAP.
  • NAC: 2 case reports suggest helpful in ischemic hepatitis (vascular obstruction or heat stroke).
  • ALFSG report (55 ALF patients due to ischemic hepatitis): 8 of 9 spontaneous survivors treated w/ NAC (needs larger numbers).
Malignant Infiltration
  • Accounts for minority of ALF cases (1.4% in US ALFSG registry).
  • Etiologies: lymphoma/leukemia (33%), breast cancer (30%), colon cancer (7%).
  • Demographics: median age 47 years, mostly female (67%) & White (67%).
  • Presentation: abdominal pain, jaundice, HE, hepatomegaly.
  • Labs: mixed hepatocellular & cholestatic injury, markedly elevated transaminases (≈40× ULN), prolonged INR, thrombocytopenia.
  • Diagnosis: <half had liver masses on imaging; typically relied on liver or bone marrow biopsy.
  • Prognosis: dismal; 85% die within 3 weeks.
Management of Malignant Infiltration
  • High suspicion required, esp. if malignancy undiagnosed.
  • Malignancy-directed treatment may be indicated.
  • LT generally not performed due to tumor infiltration & poor patient outcome (only 2 reported cases in US ALFSG, 1 survived >5 years).
Coronavirus 2019–Related Disease (COVID-19)
  • Patients w/ COVID-19 frequently have elevated liver enzymes (hepatic injury).
  • Pattern: typically hepatocellular vs. cholestatic.
  • Liver injury multifactorial: direct viral cytotoxicity, immune-mediated damage, ischemic liver injury, thrombotic complications, endotheliitis, DILI.
  • Management: supportive.
  • Severity: generally mild, but severe hepatic dysfunction can develop w/ multiorgan failure.
  • LT generally not possible due to multiple comorbidities.
  • Optimal timing of LT in COVID-19 positive ALF patients unknown.
  • American Society of Transplantation: candidate should have complete symptom resolution (ideally negative COVID-19 PCR) before transplant.
  • Asymptomatic ALF patients w/ COVID-19: multidisciplinary approach to consider risks/benefits of transplant.
Indeterminate Etiology
  • Clear etiology cannot be determined in some ALF instances.
  • US ALFSG: 5.5% eventually adjudicated as indeterminate.
  • Review: nearly half (46.9%) of previously indeterminate cases found to have an etiology (APAP 45, AIH 24, DILI, viruses like HEV, miscellaneous).
  • Liver biopsy may be warranted.
  • Steroid therapy: not found to improve survival in indeterminate cases.
  • NAC: consideration given due to evidence of ↑ TFS in non-APAP ALF w/ low-grade encephalopathy (stage 1–2).
  • Otherwise, patients should be considered for LT due to poor spontaneous survival.
Liver Transplantation (LT)
  • Pre-LT era: ALF mortality approached 80%.
  • Advances in LT & critical care: patient & graft survival rates dramatically improved over 20 years.
  • Survival rates remain lower than for patients w/ cirrhosis.
  • 1-year & 5-year post-LT patient survival ≈80% & 75% respectively.
  • Most deaths occur within months post-transplantation; long-term survival excellent otherwise.
Prognostic Models
  • Identifying patients w/ low chance of spontaneous recovery is paramount, esp. for initial assessment outside transplant center.
  • Encephalopathy development w/ acute liver injury → trigger transfer to transplant center.
  • APAP-induced ALF: particularly at risk of rapid clinical progression vs. non-APAP cases.
King's College Criteria (KCC)
  • Most used prognostic model for predicting transplant-free survival.
  • Reported sensitivity/specificity: 68%/82% (non-APAP ALF), 65%/93% (APAP-induced ALF).
  • Addition of lactate improved KCC performance to 91% sensitivity.
  • Meta-analyses: good specificity but limited sensitivity → concerns about poor prediction of death w/o transplantation.
Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD)
  • Evaluated in ALF in multiple studies.
  • Meta-analysis (23 studies, 2,153 patients) comparing MELD vs KCC:
  • MELD thresholds not standardized (25–37).
  • Pooled data: KCC had lower sensitivity for mortality than MELD (59% vs 74%), but slightly higher specificity (79% vs 67%).
Clichy Criteria
  • Widely used in France, not popular in US.
  • Based on age, Factor V levels, presence of grade 3–4 HE.
  • Low specificity (56% for APAP-induced ALF, 50% for non-APAP-induced ALF) → not widely used.
ALF Study Group Model
  • Incorporates HE grade, ALF etiology, vasopressor use, bilirubin, INR.
  • Showed c-statistical value of 0.84.
  • Prospective validation needed.
Key Concept: Prognostic Models
  • Identifying ALF patients at risk of poor outcomes is important & should trigger transfer to a transplant center early in presentation.
Recommendation: KCC or MELD for Prognostication
  • In patients w/ ALF, recommend using either KCC or MELD score for prognostication.
  • Patients meeting KCC criteria or presenting w/ MELD >25 are at high risk of poor outcomes.
  • GRADE: conditional, low quality of evidence.
Transplant Evaluation
  • Transplant team (hepatology, surgery, psychiatry/social work) engaged promptly to guide evaluation & management.
  • Team considers patient's candidacy based on medical & psychosocial factors.
  • Brain death: only absolute contraindication for LT.
  • Multiorgan dysfunction, sepsis, ARDS, pancreatitis, cancer: relative contraindications.
  • Decisions made in multidisciplinary discussion.
Alcohol Use Disorder Recurrence
  • No studies specifically address recurrence in ALF patients.
  • Great heterogeneity in center-specific protocols for psychosocial assessment & alcohol abstinence requirements.
  • Efforts to gather collateral information from family/friends to inform decision regarding post-transplant alcohol-related liver disease risk.
Key Concept: Multidisciplinary Discussion
  • Multidisciplinary discussion involving the transplant team to determine individual transplant candidacy should be undertaken at the transplant center.
Graft Considerations (Living Donor and ABO-Incompatible Grafts)
  • Most status 1A patients receive timely organ offer, but 18.2% died or became too sick for transplantation (UNOS database).
  • Led to consideration of living donor grafts & ABO-incompatible deceased organ grafts.
Living Donor Liver Transplantation (LDLT)
  • Concerns: limited time for donor evaluation, informed consent (minimize coercion), donor safety.
  • Systematic review (3 studies, 2,533 adult ALF patients): 155 underwent LDLT.
  • Comparison of LDLT w/ DDLT: no significant differences in survival at 1, 3, & 5 years.
  • UNOS database (since 2011): only 3 patients, confirming not widely practiced.
Key Concept: LDLT in ALF
  • In patients w/ ALF, listed as status 1A priority, LDLT may be considered in centers w/ LDLT experience when DDLT is not readily available.
ABO-Incompatible (ABO-I) Grafts
  • Described w/ living & deceased donors (mostly observational retrospective studies).
  • Two observational studies (China n=22, Norway n=33): showed inferior graft, patient survival, & ↑ risk of antibody-mediated rejection.
  • Earlier study (China n=33): noninferior 3-year patient & graft survival w/ only 2 rejections.
  • Significant heterogeneity in immunosuppressive regimens across studies.
  • Data from 2000s; medical supportive care & ABO-I protocols (rituximab) have since improved.
Key Concept: ABO-I Grafts in ALF
  • In patients w/ ALF, listed as status 1A priority, suggest consideration of ABO-I grafts in a rapidly declining patient.
Experimental Procedures
  • Procedures not routinely recommended due to paucity of evidence.
Two-Staged Liver Transplantation
  • Involves hepatectomy, portocaval shunt formation, prolonged anhepatic state while awaiting organ.
  • Not routinely recommended in ALF patients.
Auxiliary Orthotopic Liver Graft (APPOLT)
  • Surgical practice of adding auxiliary partial/whole liver graft alongside recipient's native liver to support regeneration.
  • Predominantly used in children; several reports in adult ALF w/ living & deceased donor grafts.
  • Reported outcomes mixed.
Conclusion
  • ALF is a medical emergency, potentially reversible if recognized & treated early.
  • Must be differentiated from ACLF & decompensated cirrhosis (management vastly different).
  • Affects multiple organs & carries high short-term mortality.
  • Timely transfer to transplant center is a priority early in patient management.
  • Patients at high risk of death have excellent prognosis after lifesaving LT.
