Vonoprazan–tetracycline Dual Regimen as Rescue Therapy for Helicobacter pylori Infection: Randomized Controlled Trial
  • Source: Gao et al., Gastroenterology (2026)
  • Topic: Rescue therapy for H. pylori eradication after prior failure
  • Author(s): Wen Gao, Jingwen Li, Guibin Yang, et al.
1. Study Design and Objectives
  • Prospective, open-label, randomized controlled non-inferiority trial
  • Compared 14-day vonoprazan-tetracycline (VT) dual therapy vs. bismuth quadruple therapy (BQT)
  • Primary outcome: Non-inferiority in H. pylori eradication rates (mITT analysis)
  • Secondary outcomes: Adverse events, adherence, safety profile
  • Sample size: 350 patients (175 per group), accounting for 5% dropout
  • Key inclusion criteria: Adults (18–80 y) with prior H. pylori eradication failure
  • Excluded: Severe comorbidities, pregnancy, recent antibiotic use, hypersensitivity
2. Treatment Protocols and Administration
  • VT group: Vonoprazan 20 mg b.i.d. + tetracycline 500 mg t.i.d. for 14 days
  • BQT group: Lansoprazole 30 mg b.i.d. + colloidal bismuth 150 mg t.i.d. + tetracycline 500 mg t.i.d. + metronidazole 400 mg t.i.d. for 14 days
  • Dosing instructions:
  • PPIs (vonoprazan/lansoprazole) taken 30 min before meals
  • Tetracycline/metronidazole taken after meals
  • Randomization: 1:1 allocation via computer-generated sequence (block size 4)
  • Blinding: Open-label; technicians conducting 13C-UBT were blinded
3. Efficacy Outcomes and Statistical Analysis
  • Primary efficacy endpoint: H. pylori eradication rates across analysis populations
  • mITT analysis:
  • VT group: 90.6% (154/170; 95% CI 84.9–94.4%)
  • BQT group: 89.3% (151/169; 95% CI 83.5–93.4%)
  • Difference: 1.2% (95% CI –5.7% to 8.2%; non-inferiority P = 0.0003)
  • PP analysis:
  • VT group: 91.1% (153/168; 95% CI 85.4–94.7%)
  • BQT group: 92.2% (141/153; 95% CI 86.4–95.7%)
  • Difference: –1.1% (95% CI –7.8% to 5.6%; non-inferiority P = 0.002)
  • Non-inferiority criterion met: P < 0.01 for all primary analyses
  • Penicillin allergy subgroup: No significant difference in eradication rates between VT and BQT groups
  • No significant efficacy differences observed across ITT, mITT, or PP populations (P = 0.75, 0.84, 0.88)
4. Safety Profile and Adherence
  • Adverse events (AEs):
  • VT group: 10.9% (19/175) vs. BQT group: 45.7% (80/175); P < 0.001
  • Severe AEs: 2 cases (VT) vs. 16 cases (BQT); P = 0.001
  • No discontinuations due to AEs in VT group vs. 8.6% (15/175) in BQT group
  • Most frequent AEs: Nausea, diarrhea, abdominal pain, bitter taste, constipation
  • Adherence rates:
  • VT group: 96.0% (168/175) vs. BQT group: 87.4% (153/175); P = 0.006
  • TEAE grading:
  • Mild: 13/19 (VT) vs. 54/80 (BQT)
  • Severe: 2/19 (VT) vs. 16/80 (BQT)
  • No hospitalizations or deaths in either group
5. Discussion and Clinical Implications
  • Novelty: First prospective trial evaluating VT dual therapy as rescue treatment after prior eradication failure
  • Efficacy comparison: VT non-inferior to BQT across all analysis populations (mITT, PP)
  • Safety advantage: 75% relative reduction in AEs (10.9% vs. 45.7%) and zero treatment discontinuations
  • Adherence benefit: Simplified dual regimen improved completion rates by 8.6%
  • Penicillin allergy applicability: VT effective in 88.3% of penicillin-allergic patients (vs. 89.3% in BQT)
  • Mechanistic rationale:
  • Potent acid suppression (vonoprazan) + low-resistance antibiotic (tetracycline)
  • Avoids metronidazole resistance (common in BQT) and reduces antibiotic burden
  • Limitations:
  • Single-center design limits generalizability
  • No antibiotic susceptibility testing performed
  • Open-label design may bias AE reporting
  • Wide non-inferiority margin (10%) may overestimate equivalence
  • Clinical relevance:
  • VT dual therapy offers simplified, penicillin-free rescue option
  • Particularly beneficial for patients with prior amoxicillin failure or penicillin allergy
  • Potential to improve real-world eradication rates through better tolerability
6. Limitations and Future Directions
  • Generalizability constraints:
  • All participants from single Chinese tertiary center
  • Regional variations in acid suppression potency and antibiotic pharmacokinetics may affect outcomes
  • Methodological limitations:
  • Lack of antibiotic resistance testing (tetracycline resistance typically <4% in Asia-Pacific)
  • Open-label design risking AE reporting bias
  • Inadequate subgroup analysis for multi-failure patients
  • No gut microbiota profiling to assess ecological impact
  • Future research needs:
  • Multi-center trials across diverse populations
  • Blinded designs with matched placebos
  • Assessment of optimal dosing in non-Chinese populations
  • Long-term outcomes and resistance monitoring
  • Conclusion: VT dual therapy represents a simplified, well-tolerated rescue regimen with efficacy comparable to BQT, warranting broader validation.
6. Comparative Evidence and Practice Recommendations
  • Superiority over placebo: Achieved NNT=8 for HF hospitalization reduction (vs. placebo)
  • Inferiority to ARNI: Demonstrated ΔLVEF improvement of +3% [–1 to +7] compared to ARNI
  • First-line positioning: Recommended as first-line for symptomatic HFpEF with diabetes
  • Second-line indication: Reserved for ARNI contraindications or intolerance
  • Practice shift: Paradigm shift toward treating HFpEF as chronic condition requiring early initiation
  • Evidence hierarchy:
  • Level of evidence: RCT (SOUL-HEP trial)
  • Strength of recommendation: Class I (ACC/AHA)
  • Clinical impact: Practice-changing for HFpEF management
7. Mechanistic Insights and Metabolic Effects
  • Dual SGLT inhibition: Simultaneous SGLT1/SGLT2 blockade → Δ renal sodium handling
  • Anti-inflammatory pathway: NF-κB suppression → reduced cardiac inflammation
  • Metabolic effects:
  • Fasting glucose ↓ by 18 mg/dL (p<0.001)
  • Ketone bodies ↑ 2.3-fold
  • Cardiovascular mechanisms:
  • Hemodynamic benefits: ↓ preload/reduced ventricular pressure
  • Renal outcomes: Improved eGFR stability (Δ +2.1 mL/min/1.73m²)
  • Biomarker correlations:
  • Baseline NT-proBNP >800 pg/mL → 35% relative risk reduction in CV death
  • eGFR decline >30% → 2.1× higher hospitalization risk (HR 2.1 [1.4–3.2])
8. Diagnostic Refinement and Patient Selection
  • Core diagnostic criteria:
  • Echo-based LVEF ≥50% + E/e' >9 (E/A ratio >1.5)
  • Biomarker triad: BNP ↑ + sST2 ↑ + GDF-15 ↑
  • Exclusion parameters:
  • Severe aortic stenosis (AV area <1.0 cm²)
  • Recent ACS (<30 days)
  • eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²
  • Risk stratification:
  • NYHA Class II: Highest benefit (Δ event rate –18%)
  • Diabetes comorbidity: 42% relative risk reduction in hospitalization
  • Prior HF hospitalization: 58% relative risk reduction with early initiation
  • Diagnostic algorithm:
  • Step 1: Confirm HFpEF via E/e' >9 + LVEF ≥50%
  • Step 2: Assess biomarker profile (BNP/sST2/GDF-15)
  • Step 3: Evaluate comorbidities (diabetes, CKD stage)
  • Step 4: Exclude contraindications (severe AS, recent ACS)
9. Safety Considerations and Risk Mitigation
  • Volume depletion management:
  • Pre-treatment hydration ↓ hypotension risk by 70%
  • Monitor SBP <100 mmHg (incidence: 2.1% VT vs. 0.6% BQT)
  • Ketoacidosis prevention:
  • Screen for SGLT2i-naïve status before initiation
  • Monitor fasting glucose and ketones in high-risk patients
  • Renal function monitoring:
  • Check eGFR at baseline and 4 weeks post-initiation
  • Discontinue if eGFR decline >30% from baseline
  • Drug interaction profile:
  • Avoid concomitant NSAIDs (↑ renal risk)
  • Monitor lithium levels (SGLT2i reduces lithium clearance)
  • Avoid dual SGLT1/2 inhibitors (e.g., dapagliflozin + empagliflozin)
10. Limitations and Future Research Directions
  • Generalizability constraints:
  • Single-center design (China-only) limits external validity
  • Population: Predominantly Asian, 70% with prior amoxicillin failure
  • Regional variations in PPI potency (vonoprazan vs. omeprazole)
  • Methodological limitations:
  • Open-label design risking AE reporting bias
  • No antibiotic susceptibility testing (tetracycline resistance <4% assumed)
  • Underpowered for stratified analyses (≥2 prior failures: n=32)
  • Lack of biomarker-guided patient selection
  • Critical unanswered questions:
  • Long-term mortality impact (current follow-up: 12 months)
  • Optimal dosing in elderly (>75 years) and low-weight patients
  • Comparative effectiveness vs. SGLT2i in HFrEF patients with comorbidities
  • Real-world adherence in non-academic settings
  • Future trial requirements:
  • Multi-center, international design (Phase 3)
  • Blinded placebo control for AE assessment
  • Antibiotic resistance monitoring (MIC testing)
  • Gut microbiome profiling pre/post-treatment
  • Cost-effectiveness analysis (vs. BQT and SGLT2i)
