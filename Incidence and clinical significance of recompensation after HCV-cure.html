Incidence and clinical significance of recompensation after HCV-cure
  • Study on recompensation after HCV-cure in patients with advanced chronic liver disease (ACLD).
  • Investigates incidence, impact, and predictors of recompensation after HCV-cure.
  • Retrospective study across 10 European centers.
Methods
  • Retrospective, observational cohort study.
  • Involved 2570 patients from 10 European centers.
  • Follow-up from 2014-2023.
Patient Cohorts
  • Decompensated Cirrhosis Cohort: 361 patients with decompensated cirrhosis achieving SVR after DAA treatment.
  • cACLD Cohort: 2209 patients with compensated ACLD (LSM ≥10kPa) undergoing DAA treatment.
Inclusion Criteria (Decompensated Cirrhosis)
  • History of ascites, variceal bleeding, or overt HE with underlying cirrhosis (LSM ≥15kPa, histology, or imaging).
  • DAA-treatment followed by SVR.
  • Sufficient pre- and post-treatment data.
Exclusion Criteria
  • History of OLT, TIPS, or HCC before treatment.
Data Collection
  • Liver disease severity (clinical, lab parameters) before treatment and at SVR assessment.
  • Clinical data: ascites severity, LVP history, HE severity, variceal status, PVT.
  • Alcohol consumption assessed semi-quantitatively.
Clinical Follow-up
  • Occurrence of LVP, variceal bleeding, HE admissions, TIPS, PVT, HCC, death.
  • Followed until OLT, death, or last follow-up.
  • Ultrasound surveillance for HCC and PVT every 6 months.
Definitions
  • Recompensation: Etiological cure (SVR) + resolution of ascites (off diuretics), encephalopathy (off lactulose/rifaximin) + absence of variceal hemorrhage (≥12 months) + stable improvement of liver function tests (albumin, INR, bilirubin).
  • Further decompensation: Baveno VII criteria, excluding jaundice.
  • Significant diuretic dose: spironolactone ≥100mg, eplerenone ≥100mg, furosemide ≥40mg, or torasemide ≥10mg.
Statistical Analysis
  • R 4.4.1 used for analysis.
  • Continuous variables: median [IQR]. Categorical variables: proportion (%).
  • Mann-Whitney-U or Chi-squared tests for group comparisons.
  • Cox regression for time-to-event analyses, adjusting for center clustering.
Results
  • Median follow-up: 8.4 years [IQR: 6.1-8.8].
  • 137 (40.0%) died, 107 (29.6%) LRD, 26 (7.2%) OLT, 16 (4.4%) TIPS.
  • 132 patients (36.6%) met recompensation definition.
Recompensation Details
  • 28 (7.8%) recompensated at DAA initiation.
  • 104 (28.8%) achieved recompensation after median 1.9 [0.7-3.6] years.
  • Recompensation incidence constant over time.
HCC and PVT Incidence
  • 86 (23.8%) developed HCC.
  • 70 (21.6% of 324) developed PVT, 22 (31.4%) after HCC.
Outcomes After Recompensation (n=132)
  • 25 (18.9%) developed another decompensating event.
  • 24 (18.2%) developed HCC.
  • 15 (12.1% of 124) developed PVT.
  • 3 (2.3%) underwent OLT.
  • 30 (22.7%) died.
Incidence Rates (IR) per 100 PY
  • Death: cACLD 1.5, decompensated 7.2, after recompensation 4.2, before recompensation 8.8.
  • LRD: cACLD 0.6, after recompensation 2.6, before recompensation 7.2.
  • HCC: after recompensation 3.9, before recompensation 5.5.
  • PVT: after recompensation 2.7, before recompensation 5.4.
Association of Recompensation with Mortality
  • Recompensation associated with lower LRD risk (aHR: 0.392 [95%CI: 0.230-0.668]) vs. no recompensation.
  • cACLD had lower risk (aHR vs. decompensated: 0.111 [95%CI: 0.073-0.171]).
  • Significant alcohol, diabetes, PVT also associated with LRD.
Association of Recompensation with HCC and PVT
  • HCC risk after recompensation not significantly different from decompensated (aHR: 0.738 [95%CI: 0.453-1.204]).
  • cACLD had significantly lower HCC risk.
  • Lower risk of PVT after recompensation (aHR: 0.421 [95%CI: 0.224-0.759]), but not comparable to cACLD.
Predictors of Recompensation (n=333)
  • Diabetes (aHR: 0.595 [95%CI: 0.361-0.982]) and albumin levels (aHR per g/L: 1.079 [95%CI: 1.035-1.112]) independently associated with recompensation.
  • De-novo HCC seems to preclude recompensation.
Discussion
  • 37% of decompensated patients achieved recompensation.
  • Recompensation reduced liver-related mortality and PVT risk, but risks remained higher than cACLD.
  • Recompensation did not lower de-novo HCC incidence.
Recompensation Rates Comparison
  • Previous studies: 18% in ALD, 5.6%-60% in HBV, 25% in HCV.
  • Variations may reflect clinical practice or true differences across etiologies.
  • Advanced cohort with higher MELD may explain lower recompensation rate in other studies.
Predictors of Recompensation
  • Low serum albumin and diabetes were negative predictors.
  • Albumin levels >35 g/L linked to regression to CTP-stage A.
  • MELD score not predictive of clinical outcomes after etiological cure.
HCC and Recompensation
  • De-novo HCC after etiological cure impedes recompensation and is a strong predictor of mortality.
  • Recompensation not accompanied by statistically significantly lower HCC risk.
  • HCC surveillance warranted in recompensated patients.
PVT and Recompensation
  • Recompensation accompanied by lower PVT risk.
  • PVT associated with increased liver-related mortality.
  • Potential PVT underdiagnosis.
Limitations
  • Retrospective design.
  • Heterogeneity in completeness of data.
  • Longitudinal assessment of alcohol consumption hampered.
  • Recompensation definition based on expert consensus.
Conclusion
  • Validated prognostic value of Baveno VII criteria for recompensation in decompensated cirrhosis after HCV-cure.
  • Recompensated patients had lower LRD risk, but higher than cACLD.
  • PVT risk intermediate between cACLD and decompensated.
  • HCC risk not lowered by recompensation.
  • Low albumin and diabetes impeded recompensation.
