Understanding Medical Malpractice Lawsuits
  • - Journal: Stroke
  • - Publication Date: March 2023
  • - DOI: 10.1161/STROKEAHA.122.038559
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the name of the judicial doctrine that can preclude liability if a physician's actions are endorsed by a significant portion of their peers, even if not the majority?
  • - Within how many days must a malpractice payment be reported to the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB)?
  • - What is the legal term for a motion made by the defense after the plaintiff has presented their case, arguing that the plaintiff has failed to demonstrate all the necessary elements of negligence?
  • - What percentage of malpractice lawsuits actually reach a judge or jury verdict?
  • - After receiving a Complaint & Summons, within what timeframe must a physician's Answer typically be filed to avoid a default judgment?

Introduction & Physician Risk
  • - Physicians are at increased risk for malpractice if they fail to provide care a minimally competent physician would under similar circumstances.
  • - Stroke care providers have even greater exposure due to high-risk, time-sensitive, and complex decision-making.
Malpractice Claim Statistics
  • - A NEJM study estimated that by age 65:
  • - 75% of physicians in low-risk specialties will face a claim.
  • - 99% of physicians in high-risk specialties will face a claim.

Medical Malpractice Stress Syndrome
  • - A traumatic reaction to a malpractice claim impacting a provider’s emotional and physical health.
  • - Negatively impacts both provider well-being and patient safety during and after litigation.
Mitigation Strategy
  • - A common recommendation is provider empowerment through process knowledge and participation in their own defense.



What is Malpractice? (Legal Theory)
  • - Medical malpractice claims are based on the legal theory of negligence.
Four Elements of Negligence
  • - The patient-plaintiff must prove all four elements by a preponderance of the evidence.
  • - This means it is more likely than not (a >50% probability) that professional negligence occurred.
1. Duty
  • - The physician had a duty to the patient to provide non-negligent care.

2. Breach of Duty
  • - The physician breached that duty by providing negligent care.

3. Proximate Causation
  • - The breach of duty was the proximate cause of the injury.

4. Injury / Damages
  • - The patient suffered an actual injury or damages.


Focus of Most Cases
  • - Duty and injury are often simple to demonstrate.
  • - Most cases hinge on proving Breach of Standard of Care and Causation.


Key Legal Concepts
Breach of Standard of Care
  • - Defined as providing care that a minimally competent physician in the same field would provide under similar circumstances.
Jurisdictional Variation
  • - State governments, not the federal government, primarily establish medical malpractice law.
  • - Definitions of the standard of care can vary by state.

Role of Medical Expert Testimony
  • - Most malpractice cases are heavily reliant on medical experts to explain the professional standard to a layperson jury.
Expert Witness Qualifications
  • - Experts do not need to be in the same specialty as the defendant.
  • - They do not even need to be a physician, as long as they have general experience with the clinical issue.

Temporal Standard
  • - The physician is held to the standard of care that existed at the time of the alleged event, not a newer standard that may exist at the time of the trial.

Basis of Testimony
  • - Testimony must be grounded in a review of the medical record and comparison to the standard of care (e.g., using practice guidelines).
  • - It cannot be based on personal anecdotes.


Respectable Minority Rule
  • - A judicial doctrine in some states that precludes liability when a respectable minority of physicians endorse the course of treatment in question.
  • - This offers some protection when deviating from the majority opinion.


Causation (Proximate Cause)
  • - Requires showing the harm would not have occurred but for the physician's actions.
  • - The harm must also have been a reasonably foreseeable result of the treatment decisions.
Shared Causation
  • - A plaintiff does not need to show the physician’s actions were the sole cause of harm.
  • - However, liability can be reduced if the plaintiff contributed to their own injury by acting unreasonably.



Avoiding Lawsuits & Managing Adverse Events
  • - There is no surefire way to prevent a lawsuit, but tactics exist to reduce its likelihood.
Communication and Resolution Programs (CRPs)
  • - Health system initiatives to lower malpractice premiums and avoid reputational damage.
  • - Formally endorsed by major medical professional groups.
Core Elements of CRPs
  • - Transparency with patients about risks and adverse events.
  • - Action plans to prevent recurrence.
  • - Emotional support for patient, family, and care team.
  • - Proactive resolution: Promptly offering financial/nonfinancial resolution for events caused by unreasonable care.
  • - Educate patients on their right to seek legal representation.
  • - Collaborate with other organizations and insurers.
  • - Constant assessment of the CRP’s effectiveness.



The Lawsuit Process: A Step-by-Step Guide
  • - Malpractice lawsuits are typically state-based civil negligence disputes, not criminal cases.
Pleadings and Initiation
  • - Plaintiff files a Complaint and Summons (C&S).
  • - Defendant files an Answer.
Statute of Limitations
  • - Initiation must occur within the relevant statute of limitations, which is typically 1–3 years.

Initial Physician Actions
  • - Upon receiving a C&S, a physician must immediately contact their malpractice insurer.
  • - The insurer will select and pay for an attorney.

Demand Letter
  • - A pre-lawsuit letter from a patient-plaintiff demanding payment.
  • - Should be brought to an attorney and communicated to the insurance carrier immediately.


Working with Your Attorney
  • - A cooperative and productive relationship is instrumental to litigation success.
Initial Meetings & Deadlines
  • - The physician and attorney will investigate the claim, determine strategy, and draft an Answer to the C&S.
  • - The Answer must be filed within a required period (typically 15–20 days) to avoid a default judgment.

Key Principles for Collaboration
  • - Be open, honest, and forthcoming about all events.
  • - Attorney-client conversations are confidential.
  • - Heed your attorney’s advice.


Pre-Trial Phases
Judgment on the Pleadings
  • - An early motion for rapid resolution based only on the filed pleadings.
  • - Rarely successful as there is usually disagreement on critical facts.

Discovery
  • - The pre-trial phase for examining witnesses and documents.
Interrogatories
  • - Written questions answered in writing, under oath.

Depositions
  • - Oral questions and answers provided under oath.
  • - Standard rules of evidence do not apply, and virtually any question may be asked.

Requests for Fact Admissions
  • - Asking the opposing side to agree to specific, undisputed facts.
  • - If not answered or opposed, the information is considered acknowledged as true for trial.



Trial
  • - Only 5% of malpractice suits reach a judge or jury verdict.
  • - Physicians are likely to prevail in cases that do go to a verdict.
Trial Proceedings
  • - Jury Selection
  • - Opening Statements
  • - Plaintiff's Case (Direct & Cross-Examination)
  • - Defendant's Case (Direct & Cross-Examination)
  • - Closing Arguments
  • - Jury Instructions & Deliberation
  • - Verdict & Judgment

Physician's Role During Trial
  • - Consider attending the trial and taking copious notes.
  • - Assist your attorney in identifying weaknesses in the plaintiff's representations.

Key Motions During Trial
  • - Directed Verdict: A motion the defendant can make after the plaintiff presents their case.
  • - Argues that the plaintiff has failed to demonstrate all required elements of negligence.


Post-Trial and Appeals
Post-Trial Motions
  • - The losing party may move for a Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict (JNOV) or a new trial.

Appeals
  • - A party may generally appeal once as a matter of right.
  • - The appellate court focuses on legal errors, not re-evaluating the facts of the case.
  • - Further appeals (e.g., to a Supreme Court) are discretionary and rarely granted.



Post-Case Reporting: National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB)
  • - A confidential repository detailing US malpractice payments and certain adverse actions.
Reporting Triggers
  • - Any payment made on a physician’s behalf in a malpractice settlement or judgment.
  • - Alterations of a physician’s license or penalties associated with clinical care.

Reporting Process
  • - The payor (e.g., insurer, hospital) must report the payment to the NPDB and the state licensing board within 30 days.
  • - The physician receives a notice when they are reported.

Physician's Rights
  • - Physicians may file factual challenges with the entity that made the report.
  • - If the report is uncorrected after 60 days, the physician may appeal to the Secretary of Health and Human Services (HHS).
Scope of Appeal
  • - The appeal can challenge how the report was submitted or if it inaccurately states the basis for the action.
  • - The appeal cannot review the underlying reasons or merits of the malpractice claim itself.


NPDB Usage
  • - Hospitals must use the NPDB to evaluate new privilege applications.
  • - Hospitals must also query the NPDB biennially (every two years) for current staff.


Conclusions & Takeaways for Stroke Care
  • - It is a near certainty that physicians practicing stroke care will face a malpractice claim at some point.
Primary Cause in Stroke-Related Suits
  • - The 2021 Medscape Malpractice Report found that failure to diagnose (e.g., stroke) was a leading cause of malpractice lawsuits.

Proactive Recommendations
  • - Understand the legal process to manage stress and assist in your defense.
  • - Develop an open, honest, and collaborative relationship with your attorney if sued.
  • - These steps improve patient safety and support physician well-being.
