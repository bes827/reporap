British Society of Gastroenterology practice guidance on the management of acute and chronic gastrointestinal symptoms and complications as a result of treatment for cancer

  • 2. In patients with severe capecitabine/5-FU GI toxicity, what imaging is urgently required and what condition must be excluded?
  • 3. What is the recommended starting dose of Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT) for suspected PEI, in units of lipase?
  • 4. Argon plasma coagulation for radiation proctopathy carries a serious complication rate of up to what percentage?
  • 5. What two patient factors, identifiable before radiotherapy, increase the risk of acute GI toxicity?
Patient Perspectives
  • Emphasizes the importance of the patient's experience and needs.
  • All clinicians have a responsibility to identify unmet needs & signpost patients.
  • Continued holistic assessment is recommended before & after cancer treatment.
Key Recommendations
  • Inform all patients about possible future side effects & how to access help.
  • Use a personalised care and support plan at the end of treatment.
  • Listen to patient concerns & signpost to appropriate services.
Holistic Needs Assessment
  • Highlights problems beyond physical symptoms: emotional, social, occupational, financial.
  • Useful at diagnosis, on treatment completion, and during follow-up.
  • Data from 18,000 patients shows top concerns: questions about diagnosis/treatment, thinking about the future, partner, uncertainty.
Patient Experience
  • Ongoing, unacknowledged symptoms have a negative impact on life.
  • Quotes illustrate impact: 'My bowel issues are the first thing I think about in the morning and the last thing I think about at night.'
  • Patients often feel they are in a 'lonely place' post-treatment.
Trigger Questions for Referral
  • Simple questions to identify patients needing gastroenterology referral.
  • A 'Yes' to any should lead to an offer of referral.
Screening Questions
  • 1. Do you have frequent loose stools?
  • 2. Do you wake up at night needing to poo?
  • 3. Do you have leakage from your bottom?
  • 4. Do you have blood in your stools/rectal bleeding?
  • 5. Is your quality of life reduced owing to your bowel function?
  • 6. Has your mental health been affected by your bowel function?
Pathophysiology Overview
  • Optimal symptom management requires identifying physiological deficits with appropriate testing.
  • The majority of patients developing GI symptoms after cancer treatment have >1 cause.
  • Different treatments often cause the same symptoms via different physiological mechanisms.
Mechanisms of Injury
  • Cancer treatments cause pathological insults → changes in GI physiological processes → abnormal symptoms.
Cytotoxic Chemotherapy
  • Direct effect on rapidly proliferating GI tissues (mucosal barrier, enzyme systems) → inflammation, oedema, cell death.
  • Indirect damage via microvascular injury or visceral nervous system damage.
Targeted Therapies
  • Agents target receptors (VEGF, EGFR, tyrosine kinases) also found in normal GI tissues.
  • GI toxicity, particularly diarrhoea, occurs in 18–95% of treated patients.
Radiotherapy
  • Initial mucosal changes: inflammation & oedema.
  • Subsequent persistent cytokine activation in submucosa → ischaemia, fibrosis, atrophy, loss of stem cells.
  • Damage may be progressive.
Surgery
  • Resectional surgery causes physiological changes from denervation or reconfiguration.
  • Examples: vagotomy, loss of gastric/rectal reservoirs, mechanical shortening, blind loops.
Common Physiological Abnormalities
  • Summarizes the frequency of specific physiological changes caused by different cancer therapies.
During Chemotherapy
  • Lactose intolerance: 5–10%
  • Bile acid malabsorption: Yes (frequency unknown)
  • Small bowel bacterial overgrowth (SIBO): Yes
  • Pancreatic insufficiency (PEI): Yes
During Radiotherapy
  • Lactose intolerance: 50%
  • Bile acid malabsorption: 50%
  • SIBO: 25%
  • Rapid transit: 100%
After Radiotherapy
  • Lactose intolerance: 5–7%
  • Bile acid malabsorption: 1–83%
  • SIBO: 8–45%
  • Rapid transit: 100%
After Upper GI Surgery
  • Malabsorption of disaccharides: Yes
  • Bile acid malabsorption: Yes
  • SIBO: Yes
  • PEI: Yes
  • Rapid transit: Yes
GI Symptoms & Patient-Centred Assessment
  • Use validated symptom questionnaires (e.g., GSRS) or PROMs.
  • In curative-intent patients, recurrence must be considered and ruled out.
  • Consider investigations for conditions not associated with the cancer or its treatment.
Symptom Assessment
  • Individual symptoms & symptom clusters are unreliable for diagnosing the underlying cause.
  • >30 different GI symptoms can develop.
  • Different physiological disorders can cause similar symptoms (e.g., bloating can be from BAD, carbohydrate malabsorption, PEI, or SIBO).
Validated Tools
  • Use of questionnaires ensures the patient's perspective is captured.
  • Examples: Gastrointestinal Symptom Rating Scale (GSRS), EORTC QLQ-CR29, PRO-CTCAE.
  • Visual aids (Visual Analogue Scales, Bristol Stool Chart) support clinical decision-making.
Diagnostic Tests
  • Clinical acumen or symptom patterns are unreliable for diagnosis in this population.
  • Comprehensive investigation is required early if symptoms don't respond to simple, empirical intervention.
  • Early advice from a gastroenterologist should be sought for ongoing or severe toxicity.
Common Treatable Conditions
  • A wide variety of cancer treatments cause the same physiological changes.
  • Frequently include: Bile acid diarrhoea (BAD), carbohydrate intolerance, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (PEI), small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).
  • After immunosuppression, consider bacterial or viral infection.
Timing of Investigations
  • Investigating acutely during chemotherapy/radiotherapy is feasible and beneficial if symptoms impact treatment.
  • Randomised trials show a systematic approach is beneficial in both acute and chronic settings.
Symptom-Specific Test Suggestions
  • A structured approach to investigating common GI symptoms.
Steatorrhoea
  • Test 1: Faecal elastase → if abnormal, trial PERT.
  • Test 2: SeHCAT study → if abnormal, treat for BAD.
  • Test 3: Glucose H2/CH4 breath test / SI aspiration → if abnormal, treat for SIBO.
Diarrhoea (± urgency
)
  • Test 1: Faecal elastase, calprotectin, FIT, stool microbiology, sigmoidoscopy + biopsies → if abnormal, treat accordingly (PERT, microscopic colitis, infection).
  • Test 2: SeHCAT study → if abnormal, treat for BAD.
  • Test 3: Glucose H2/CH4 breath test / SI aspiration → if abnormal, treat for SIBO.
Constipation
  • Test 1: FIT, Abdominal X-ray (AXR) → check for impaction/obstruction.
  • Test 2: Transit study.
  • Test 3: Colonoscopy.
Upper Abdominal Pain
  • Test 1: H. pylori test.
  • Test 2: Glucose H2/CH4 breath test.
  • Test 3: OGD + aspirate, Biliary US.
Bloating/Flatulence/Belching
  • Test 1: Faecal calprotectin, FIT, Faecal elastase.
  • Test 2: Glucose H2/CH4 breath test / SI aspiration.
  • Test 3: Flexible sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy.
Commonly Occurring Conditions: Diagnosis & Treatment
  • In patients on restrictive diets, consider daily long-term trace elements & multivitamin supplements.
  • If long-term dietary interventions are recommended, arrange dietetic review.
  • If multiple diagnoses, introduce treatments one at a time & document symptom response.
Bile Acid Diarrhoea/Malabsorption (BAD/BAM)
  • Common after many oncological treatments.
  • Definitive diagnosis: SeHCAT scan.
Treatment by Severity (SeHCAT 7-day retention)
  • Mild (10–20%): Long-term low-fat diet may be sufficient.
  • Moderate (5–10%): Bile sequestrant.
  • Severe (0–5%): Sequestrant + low-fat diet.
Bile Sequestrants
  • No licensed treatments for BAD/BAM.
  • Options: Colestyramine, colestipol, colesevelam.
  • Colesevelam: more effective, better tolerated, fewer interactions.
  • Start low dose, take with meals, titrate slowly to improve tolerance.
Monitoring
  • Vitamin D deficiency occurs in 20% of patients on sequestrants.
  • Rarely, can develop significant hypertriglyceridaemia and/or vitamin A, E, K deficiency.
Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO)
  • Occurs very commonly during and after cancer treatment.
  • Testing is recommended over empirical treatment to aid antibiotic stewardship.
  • Multiple diagnoses often coincide, making empirical trials confusing.
Diagnosis
  • Glucose or lactulose breath tests can be helpful.
  • Adding methane to hydrogen breath analysis ↑ accuracy.
  • Qualitative small bowel aspiration during endoscopy is a practical option.
Aspirate Technique
  • 1. On intubation, avoid aspirating.
  • 2. Flush 100 mL sterile saline into duodenum.
  • 3. Flush channel with 10 mL air.
  • 4. Aspirate ≥10 mL into a sterile trap.
  • 5. Positive aspirates will grow colonic bacteria.
Treatment
  • Rifaximin 550 mg BID for 1–2 weeks is effective in ≈60–80% of patients.
  • Other options: doxycycline, ciprofloxacin, amoxicillin–clavulanic acid.
  • Recurrent SIBO: low-dose, long-term, cyclical, or recurrent short course antibiotics.
Pancreatic Exocrine Insufficiency (PEI)
  • Best diagnosed by faecal elastase-1 measurement.
  • Faecal elastase <500 µg/g may indicate PEI, untreated coeliac disease, SIBO, or a watery stool sample.
When to Treat Empirically
  • Common in pancreatic cancer & after pancreatico-duodenal resection.
  • Frequently develops after starting somatostatin analogues for NENs.
  • In these situations, a trial of PERT is appropriate without testing.
PERT Dosing
  • A 10-day trial is usually sufficient to assess efficacy.
  • Start at 50,000 units of lipase with meals & 25,000 with snacks.
  • Increase dose if symptoms persist or weight is not maintained.
  • If PERT is not tolerated, it often indicates underlying SIBO.
Carbohydrate Intolerance
  • Lactose intolerance may be induced by chemotherapy/radiotherapy.
  • May resolve within weeks after treatment ends.
  • Requires eliminating only lactose-containing products, not a complete dairy-free diet to avoid nutritional deficits.
Pelvic Floor Dysfunction
  • Evidence for interventions is poor.
  • Management includes bowel habit training, toilet positioning, modifying stool consistency.
  • Faecal incontinence may respond to pelvic floor muscle training & firming stool with loperamide.
Constipation Management
  • Affects 40–90% with advanced cancer or on opioids.
  • Prevention is key: prophylactic laxatives when opioids are prescribed.
  • PAMORAs (peripherally acting mu opioid receptor antagonists) are effective; conventional laxatives are first-line.
  • Consider faecal impaction & overflow as a cause of apparent diarrhoea.
Transanal Irrigation
  • Can be effective when other treatments fail.
  • Especially in passive incontinence, severe constipation, and anterior resection syndrome.
GI Infection
  • Risk is ↑ in immunosuppressed patients.
  • Loperamide can be given safely before microbiology results are available, but reassess for toxic dilatation.
Bacterial
  • C. difficile: Pseudomembranes may not be seen in neutropenic patients. Endoscopic biopsy can diagnose C. difficile colitis in this context.
Viral
  • Multiple GI ulcers should raise suspicion of CMV or HSV.
  • For CMV, immunocytochemistry and PCR of biopsy samples are helpful. Blood-based PCR is insensitive.
Fungal
  • Fungal overgrowth in the small bowel is intermittently identified.
  • May cause SIBO-like symptoms and has been associated with ↑ risk of bowel perforation.
Pain Management
  • Chronic pain after surgery/radiotherapy may be from stricture, adhesions, or fibrosis.
  • Colonic faecal loading and SIBO are under-appreciated causes.
  • New or unexplained pain →
  • consider tumour recurrence.
Acute/Severe Pain
  • Signs of complete intestinal obstruction & severe abdominal pain require emergency surgical assessment.
  • Early CT imaging is required to understand anatomy & exclude recurrence.
Chronic Pain Mechanisms
  • Mechanical obstruction: colicky pain, worse after eating, may be associated with vomiting.
  • Pseudo-obstruction: obstructive symptoms w/o a defined obstruction point on imaging. Often multifactorial (dysmotility, constipation, SIBO, BAD).
  • Neuropathic pain: can be caused by radiotherapy, possibly due to tissue fibrosis and nerve entrapment.
Multidisciplinary Approach
  • Management requires input from gastroenterology, surgery, pain management, and nutrition teams.
  • Always consider localized sepsis or bone insufficiency fracture (may require MRI).
GI Toxicity from Specific Therapies
  • This section details GI side effects related to specific cancer treatment modalities.
Cytotoxic Chemotherapy
  • Frequent physiological disorders: lactose intolerance, SIBO, BAD, PEI.
  • Investigations during treatment are feasible & beneficial.
  • Mortality rate from modern chemo is 1–5%, largely due to sepsis or multiorgan failure with diarrhoea.
Acute Diarrhoea Management
  • Perform stool analysis for infection.
  • It is generally safe to start loperamide while awaiting results.
  • Reassess regularly to exclude toxic dilatation of the colon.
  • Temporarily pause potentially aggravating oral anticancer drugs.
Capecitabine/5-FU Enterocolitis
  • A rare but severe toxicity.
  • Patients presenting with severe GI toxicity require an urgent CT scan to exclude enterocolitis.
  • Associated with dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPD) deficiency (found in 3–5% of population).
  • Requires rapid, intensive intervention: immediate cessation of drug, IV fluids, antibiotics, consider parenteral nutrition.
Immunotherapy
  • Diarrhoea is common and can occur at any time after treatment initiation.
  • Severe diarrhoea (>6 stools/day over baseline) requires admission, IV corticosteroids, and urgent investigation.
Management of Enterocolitis
  • If no response to IV corticosteroids within 2-3 days, start second-line therapy.
  • Second-line: Infliximab or vedolizumab (equally efficacious).
  • Third-line options for refractory cases: tofacitinib, tocilizumab, ustekinumab.
Small Molecules/Targeted Agents
  • Diarrhoea occurs in up to 95% of treated patients.
  • Little research exists on the physiological mechanisms.
Specific Agent Toxicities
  • Sorafenib: PEI is a common cause of GI symptoms, occurring in >10% of patients (median onset 6 months).
  • Lenalidomide: BAD is a common cause of GI symptoms, occurring in 20% of patients (median onset 4 months).
Radiotherapy (RT)
  • Toxicity is limited by tolerance of normal tissues.
  • Severity of acute toxicity predisposes to late toxicity.
Risk Factors for Toxicity
  • Smoking and low BMI (<18.5 kg/m²) predict worse acute toxicity.
  • These should be addressed before RT if possible.
Acute RT-Induced Toxicity Prevention
  • Dietary counselling and/or protein supplementation may reduce risk.
  • Lactobacilli ± bifidobacteria containing probiotics may reduce acute RT-related diarrhoea.
  • A high-fibre diet may reduce toxicity risk during pelvic RT.
Acute Mucositis (Oral & Oesophageal)
  • Exclude HSV, CMV, and extensive candidiasis in persistent cases.
  • Symptomatic treatment: modified WHO analgesia ladder.
  • Consider early referral to nutrition team.
  • Mouth washes, topical analgesics, coating agents may help.
Upper GI Tract Strictures
  • Occur in ≈30% of patients after RT for oesophageal cancer.
  • Do not dilate potentially malignant strictures until recurrence is excluded or MDT approved.
  • Endoscopic dilatation is preferred for benign strictures; success >80%.
  • Avoid high oesophageal stents that impinge on the cricopharyngeus.
Acute Lower GI Symptoms
  • Usually begin in week 2, peak in the last week of treatment.
  • Management is empirical; antidiarrhoeals, antispasmodics may help.
  • Grade 3 or 4 diarrhoea mandates stopping chemotherapy.
Chronic Issues After Cancer Treatment
  • This section covers long-term GI complications following surgery and radiotherapy.
After Upper GI Surgery
  • Symptoms often relate to mechanical reconfiguration & physiological changes.
  • Extensive investigation within 3 months is generally unnecessary as symptoms often settle.
  • Do not attribute new symptoms to IBS until organic causes are excluded.
Common Issues & Management
  • Anastomotic stricture: Endoscopic dilatation is preferred.
  • Acid reflux: Treat with PPIs; prokinetics for up to 6 weeks may help.
  • Bile reflux: Oral sucralfate suspension may be useful.
  • Postprandial pain: Commonly due to eating too much at one sitting.
  • Bowel dysfunction/steatorrhoea: Commonly due to PEI, SIBO, and/or severe BAD. Diagnostic testing is recommended over empirical treatment.
After Pelvic Cancer Treatment (Surgery/RT)
  • Post-pelvic cancer symptoms need to be actively identified and managed.
  • Multimodal treatment carries a higher risk for long-term complaints.
Radiation-Induced Rectal Bleeding
  • Occurs in up to 50% of patients after pelvic RT.
  • Cannot be assumed to be from telangiectasia; requires endoscopic/radiological investigation.
  • Diagnosis is based on typical appearance; biopsy should not be performed due to risk of necrosis/perforation.
Management Strategy
  • Intervention only if bleeding affects quality of life or causes anaemia.
  • Optimising irregular bowel function often reduces bleeding.
  • Stopping anticoagulants/antiplatelet agents (if possible) is effective.
  • Sucralfate enemas can be a useful temporary or long-term treatment.
Interventional Therapies & Risks
  • Argon plasma coagulation: Widely used but carries a serious complication rate up to 26% (stricture, pain, perforation, fistula).
  • Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO): Meta-analysis suggests benefit, but trial data are contradictory.
  • Formalin: Appears effective in observational studies but carries risks of colitis, stricturing, and pain.
Lower GI Functional Symptoms
  • Interventions: bowel habit training, toilet positioning, modifying stool consistency (diet, fluids, loperamide, fibre).
  • A large bowel transit study may help distinguish slow transit constipation from evacuation difficulty.
  • Reserve defaecating proctogram, endoanal ultrasound, anorectal physiology for specialist practice.
Low Anterior Resection Syndrome (LARS)
  • Defined by symptoms like unpredictable bowel function, urgency, frequency, emptying difficulties.
  • Significantly impacts quality of life in 60–90% of patients.
  • Objective testing is not required for diagnosis.
Management
  • Offer supported self-management to all patients post-resection.
  • If symptoms persist >3 months, refer to specialist services.
  • Exclude worsening conditions like BAD, PEI, SIBO.
  • Interventions: Pelvic floor exercises, bulking agents, transanal irrigation, stoma formation.
Fistulae
  • A rare complication; incidence is likely decreasing.
  • Crucial to rule out disease recurrence before assuming radiation injury.
  • Requires a multidisciplinary approach by a regionally based team.
Special Populations & Conditions
  • Management considerations for specific patient groups and complex syndromes.
Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT)
  • GI toxicity predicts post-transplant complications.
  • A multidisciplinary approach is helpful (haematology, dietetics, gastroenterology).
  • For lower GI symptoms, upper GI endoscopy + flexible sigmoidoscopy is safer than colonoscopy and similarly effective.
Diagnosis of Diarrhoea
  • Endoscopic tests with small bowel aspirate & biopsies are helpful.
  • Differentiates between SIBO/fungal overgrowth, infections (C. diff, CMV), and Graft versus Host Disease (GvHD).
  • Wireless capsule endoscopy is not recommended for diagnosing GvHD.
Graft versus Host Disease (GvHD)
  • In patients with typical symptoms, treatment should not be delayed while waiting for biopsy results.
  • Acute GvHD usually occurs in the first 3 months post-transplant.
  • Treatment is determined by severity and includes corticosteroids and other immunosuppressants.
Neuroendocrine Neoplasms (NENs)
  • The majority of GI symptoms in NEN patients do not result from excess hormone production.
  • Surgery and systemic treatments (especially somatostatin analogues) frequently cause GI symptoms.
  • Gastroenterologists should be involved in the NEN MDT.
Common Causes of Symptoms
  • PEI, BAD, and SIBO are common.
  • Starting PERT is appropriate without faecal elastase measurement in those with steatorrhoea after starting a somatostatin analogue.
  • Prophylactic cholecystectomy should be considered during initial surgery to prevent pancreatitis.
Functioning NENs
  • Carcinoid Syndrome: Secretory diarrhoea, flushing. Treat with somatostatin analogues (SSAs).
  • VIPoma: Profound watery diarrhoea. Treat with fluid/electrolyte replacement, SSAs.
  • Gastrinoma (ZES): Peptic ulceration, diarrhoea. Treat with high-dose PPIs.
Mesenteric Fibrosis
  • A complication often seen with NENs, causing shrinkage and fixation of the mesentery.
  • Can lead to small bowel obstruction and/or ischaemia.
  • Abdominal pain can be difficult to manage; requires collaboration with pain/palliative teams.
Management
  • Early and sustained dietetic input is needed.
  • Resection of fibrotic tissue may offer relief but risks short bowel syndrome.
  • Long-term home parenteral nutrition is a valid alternative if surgery is too high-risk.
Palliative and End of Life Care
  • Focus on remote monitoring of PROMs and optimising quality of life.
  • Consider referral to palliative care when there is ≥70% risk of death within 1 year.
  • Prioritise minimising pain and avoiding opioid-induced constipation.
Symptom Management
  • Diarrhoea/bloating: Consider empirical trial of rifaximin (1 week), bile acid sequestrant (10 days), or PERT (10 days).
  • Bleeding: Consider early parenteral iron support.
  • Malignant bowel obstruction: Consider corticosteroids and octreotide. A venting gastrostomy can relieve symptoms.
