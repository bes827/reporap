AGA Clinical Practice Update
Current Role of Blood Tests for Colorectal Cancer Screening
  • Journal: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology
  • Volume: 23, Issue 9
  • Publication Date: August 2025
  • Commentary on the evidence and implications of blood tests for CRC screening.

Background & Rationale
  • Colorectal Cancer (CRC) is the 3rd most common cancer and 2nd leading cause of cancer death in the US.
  • Screening is the most effective strategy to reduce CRC burden but remains underutilized.
  • Advances in technology now allow detection of circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) or cell-free DNA (cfDNA) in blood, leading to new screening tests.

Novel Blood Tests: Performance & Status
  • Two main tests have reported results from population-based clinical validation studies.
Guardant Shield Test
  • Mechanism: Detects cell-free DNA in blood.
  • Status: FDA approved (May 2024), CMS approved, and commercially available.
  • Recommended Interval: Every 3 years per CMS.
Performance Characteristics
  • Overall CRC Sensitivity: 83.1%
  • Overall CRC Specificity: 89.6%
  • Advanced Precursor Lesion (APL) Sensitivity: 13.2%
  • Stage 1 CRC Sensitivity: 55%


Freenome Test
  • Mechanism: Detects methylation markers from cell-free DNA.
  • Status: Expected to seek FDA and CMS approvals.
Performance Characteristics
  • Overall CRC Sensitivity: 79.2%
  • Overall CRC Specificity: 91.5%
  • Advanced Precursor Lesion (APL) Sensitivity: 12.5%
  • Stage 1 CRC Sensitivity: 57.1%



Comparative Test Performance (See Table 1)
  • A summary of diagnostic performance of blood tests compared to established stool tests and colonoscopy.
Colonoscopy (Every 10y)
  • CRC Sensitivity: 96%
  • APL Sensitivity: 95%
  • Specificity: 90%

FIT (Annual)
  • CRC Sensitivity: 74%
  • APL Sensitivity: 24%
  • Specificity: 96%

MT-sDNA (Cologuard, Every 3y)
  • CRC Sensitivity: 92%
  • APL Sensitivity: 42%
  • Specificity: 87%

Blood Tests (Shield & Freenome)
  • CRC Sensitivity: ~79-83%
  • APL Sensitivity: ~13% (critically low)
  • Specificity: ~90-92%


Clinical Implications & Limitations of Blood Tests
  • The performance characteristics of current blood tests have significant clinical consequences.
Reduced Prevention & Early Detection
  • Poor APL detection (~13%) severely limits the potential for cancer prevention.
  • Suboptimal early-stage CRC detection (~55-57% for Stage 1) reduces the chance for curative treatment.

Potential for Improved Adherence
  • A key attraction is bringing currently unscreened individuals into screening programs.
  • One RCT showed adherence up to 11% higher for blood testing compared to stool testing.

Unknown Optimal Interval
  • Published data is for one-time test performance.
  • The optimal repeat interval is currently unknown.


Modeling Studies: Projected Long-Term Outcomes
  • Decision analytic models project long-term benefits and costs, as large-scale RCTs are not feasible.
Projected Risk Reduction vs. No Screening (See Table 2)
  • Models consistently show blood tests are substantially less effective than established screening methods.
Established Tests (FIT, MT-sDNA, Colonoscopy)
  • CRC Incidence Reduction: 40% - 81%
  • CRC Mortality Reduction: 64% - 88%

Triennial Guardant Shield
  • CRC Incidence Reduction: 24% - 47%
  • CRC Mortality Reduction: 53% - 59%


Cost-Effectiveness Analysis
  • At its list price of $1450, Guardant Shield is considered prohibitively costly versus alternatives.
  • It is cost-effective compared to no screening.
  • At a hypothetical cost of $300, it would still be less effective and more costly than FIT or colonoscopy.

Hypothetical Improvements Needed
  • For a triennial blood test to approximate the benefits of annual FIT, it would require:
  • APL Sensitivity ---> 50% - 80% (current is ~13%).
  • Cost ---> <$125 - $140.


Utilization & Programmatic Challenges
  • A test's real-world effectiveness depends on both its efficacy and its uptake.
The Substitution Problem
  • A major concern is that patients will substitute a less effective blood test for more effective options (FIT, colonoscopy), leading to inferior population outcomes.
  • To be as effective as FIT/colonoscopy, blood test participation would need to be at least 35% higher.
  • Population outcomes would be similar if for every 3 people who substitute, 2 new people are screened.

Programmatic Requirements
  • Like all non-invasive tests, blood tests require a programmatic approach.
  • Positive Test ---> Must complete a follow-up colonoscopy.
  • Negative Test ---> Must have repeat testing at the recommended interval.
Challenge: Follow-up Colonoscopy
  • Data from stool-based tests shows that almost 50% of patients do not complete a timely colonoscopy (within 3-6 months) after a positive result.
  • Delayed colonoscopy leads to higher CRC incidence and mortality.


Disparities in Screening
  • It is unknown if blood tests will worsen or lessen disparities.
  • Screening rates are lowest in Hispanics (~50%).
  • Barriers like access to a lab for a blood draw may be more challenging than mailing a stool kit for some populations (e.g., rural).


Multicancer Detection (MCD) Tests
  • These tests aim to detect multiple cancers, including CRC, from a single blood draw.
Example: Galleri Test (Grail)
  • Early validation study showed:
  • Overall Cancer Sensitivity: 51.5%
  • Specificity: 99.5%

Challenges & Concerns
  • Imperfect confirmatory imaging tests.
  • Substantial cost and resources for workup of a positive signal.
  • Unclear if screening for many cancers this way reduces mortality (risk of lead/length time bias).


Conclusions & Key Takeaways
  • Final recommendations on the current role of blood tests in CRC screening.
Primary Recommendation
  • The first available CRC screening blood tests are best viewed as an acceptable option only in persons who decline all established forms of CRC screening (FIT, MT-sDNA, colonoscopy).

Patient Counseling Points
  • Patients must understand that a positive blood test requires a follow-up colonoscopy.
  • Patients should be informed that current blood tests result in lower prevention rates for CRC and CRC death compared to programmatic FIT, Cologuard, or colonoscopy.

Potential for Net Harm
  • Use of novel blood tests could result in net harm if they are substituted for more effective screening tests by patients who would have otherwise completed an established test.
