Liability Risk with Physician Extenders (Chapter 14)
  • Source: Unspecified legal/medical text, Chapter 14.
  • Date: 2025‑12‑25
1. Legal Foundations & Scope of Practice
  • Core framework: state Medical Practice Acts + written collaborative agreements.
State Variability
  • <u>~50%</u> states grant NPs explicit test‑ordering authority.
  • Alaska, New Hampshire, Washington → independent practice w/o physician oversight.
  • Majority of states → require a collaborative agreement spelling out scope & supervision.
Permitted Clinical Functions
  • History & physical exam ✅ for MLPs in all jurisdictions.
  • Diagnosis ✅ permitted in most states.
  • Prescription authority:
  • Many states allow meds w/ physician cosignature.
  • Few states grant unrestricted prescribing (incl. controlled substances) without any physician involvement.
  • Test ordering: half of states explicit for NPs; others via agreement.
2. Physician Liability Exposure
  • Physicians can be sued for MLP actions → name on chart triggers liability.
Direct Liability Scenarios
  • Inadequate Supervision → failure to meet collaborative‑agreement duties (e.g., not available for consult, not reviewing charts) ↑ suit risk.
  • Improper Delegation → assigning tasks beyond MLP’s education/training → liability per Gillis v. Cardio TVP Surgical Associates (520 S.E.2d 767).
  • Scope‑Exceeding Errors by MLPs → e.g., managing hypotensive patient with acute abdomen w/o physician guidance → MLP liable; supervising physician may share liability.
Indirect Liability Mechanisms
  • Vicarious Liability → employer‑employee relationship ⇒ physician employer liable for employee MLP negligence.
  • Apparent Agency → independent contractor presenting as part of practice (e.g., logo coat, “Dr. Welby’s PA”) ⇒ physician may be held liable despite contractor status.
3. Vicarious & Agency Liability Nuances
  • Distinguish employment vs independent‑contractor contexts.
Employment Context
  • Physician employer of MLP → vicarious liability applies.
  • Physician employee of a medical group → not vicariously liable for other physicians’ MLPs.
Independent Contractor Context
  • Generally no vicarious liability, but apparent agency can impose liability if patient perceives contractor as physician’s agent.
  • Mitigation: avoid branding that suggests direct affiliation; use distinct attire/identifiers.
4. Risk Mitigation Strategies & Documentation Practices
  • Goal: reduce likelihood of suit despite inevitable exposure.
Supervisory Structures
  • Define collaborative‑agreement duties:
  • Availability for real‑time consult (phone or in‑person) → minimal supervision.
  • Chart review & signature limited to billing compliance, not clinical endorsement.
  • Use written delegation descriptions where required.
Documentation Tactics
  • When signing MLP chart: add qualifier “Signed for billing purposes only, consistent with state/federal law.”
  • Record date/time of any consult provided.
  • Maintain logs of supervisory interactions (calls, in‑person reviews).
Insurance & Education
  • Ensure each MLP carries adequate malpractice insurance → protects both parties.
  • Conduct regular performance evaluations & continuing education for MLPs.
Billing Considerations
  • Payers require physician signature on all charts → treat as proof of supervision compliance.
  • Clarify to plaintiff’s counsel that signature does not imply direct patient encounter.
5. Strategic Practice Management Decisions
  • Weigh liability risk vs productivity/financial benefits.
State Law Mastery
  • Review & internalize your state’s MLP statutes before delegating authority.
  • Align delegated tasks with each MLP’s documented competence & training.
Organizational Size Impact
  • Larger groups → may absorb litigation costs, favor broader MLP utilization.
  • Smaller groups → may prefer hiring additional physicians over high‑risk MLP delegation.
Cost‑Benefit Analysis
  • Calculate productivity gain (e.g., fast‑track ED throughput) vs potential litigation expense (legal fees, settlements).
  • Factor in malpractice premiums for both physician & MLP.
Decision Framework
  • Low comfort with supervision → limit MLP autonomy; increase direct patient encounters.
  • High confidence in MLP competence & robust agreements → expand independent practice scope.
