Critique of a Study on Pharmacist-Managed POCT and Treatment
  • - Source: Peer review (BSC 731 template) of a research proposal.
  • - Study Title: Evaluation of pharmacist-managed POCT and treatment for UTI, GAS, and COVID-19 vs traditional providers.
  • - Study Design: Retrospective, single-cohort.

üî¨ Research Proposal Overview
  • - Objective: To evaluate the real-world outcomes of pharmacist-managed care for common acute conditions under Board Approved Protocols (BAPs).
Conditions Studied
  • - Uncomplicated Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
  • - Group A Streptococcus (GAS) Pharyngitis
  • - COVID-19

Core Hypothesis
  • - The study aims to show that pharmacist-led care produces "similar outcomes" to care from traditional providers (MDs, NPs, PAs).
  • - This framing implies a non-inferiority or equivalence hypothesis.

Proposed Design
  • - Design: Retrospective, single-cohort study.
  • - Data Source: Electronic Health Record (EHR) data from a single health system.
  • - Study Period: 2022‚Äì2025.


‚≠ê Factor 1: Importance & Innovation
  • - Overall assessment of the research question's relevance and novelty.
‚úÖ Strengths
  • - High Practical Relevance: Addresses a critical policy issue of expanding pharmacist scope of practice to alleviate provider shortages.
  • - Multi-Disease Approach: Evaluating UTI, GAS, and COVID-19 increases the generalizability of findings for pharmacist-driven protocols.
  • - Real-World Evidence: Uses EHR data from an implemented program, providing high external validity for similar practice settings.

‚ö†Ô∏è Weaknesses
  • - Incremental Innovation: Many studies on pharmacist interventions already exist; novelty must be clearly defined (e.g., multi-BAP comparison).
  • - Disconnect between Aims & Design: The aim is to compare against "traditional providers," but the proposed method is a comparison against "previously reported literature values."
  • - This is a major flaw, as using historical external data is less rigorous than a direct internal comparison.


üõ†Ô∏è Factor 2: Rigor & Feasibility (Methodology Critique)
  • - Detailed analysis of the proposed study's methodological flaws and suggested remedies.
1. Study Design & Comparator Group
  • - Weakness: The study uses a single cohort design compared against "literature values" (historical controls).
  • - Problem: This introduces significant confounding and history bias. Care standards, patient populations, and pathogen characteristics (e.g., COVID variants) change over time.
üí° Suggested Fix
  • - Define and extract a contemporaneous control cohort from the same health system (patients treated by non-pharmacists in the same clinics/timeframe).
  • - Use statistical methods to balance covariates between groups, such as:
  • - Propensity score matching
  • - Multivariable adjustment
  • - Inverse probability weighting


2. Outcome Definitions & Follow-up
  • - Weakness: The primary outcome for UTI and GAS, termed "clinical cure," is defined as "no repeat testing/treatment within 7 days" at the same health system.
  • - Problem: This definition lacks Construct Validity and risks measurement error.
Identified Issues
  • - Follow-up too short: A 7-day window may misclassify clinical failures.
  • - Misclassification Risk: Patients may seek follow-up care elsewhere (e.g., another clinic, urgent care, ER), which would be incorrectly counted as a "cure."
  • - Overestimation of Success: The flawed definition is likely to significantly overestimate the true success rate.

üí° Suggested Fix
  • - Extend the primary follow-up window to a more clinically appropriate duration, such as 14 days for UTI and 7‚Äì14 days for GAS.
  • - Add objective secondary outcomes like 30-day return visits, emergency department visits, or hospitalizations.
  • - Re-label the outcome precisely to what is being measured, e.g., "Rate of Treatment Re-escalation within System" instead of "Clinical Cure."


3. Statistical Methods
  • - Weakness: The proposal states that a Student‚Äôs t-test will be used to compare primary outcomes.
  • - Problem: This is statistically incorrect. The t-test is for continuous variables (e.g., blood pressure), while the study's primary outcomes are categorical/binary (e.g., Cure vs. No Cure).
üí° Suggested Fix
  • - For unadjusted comparisons of proportions, use Chi-square or Fisher‚Äôs exact tests.
  • - For adjusted analyses, use logistic regression and report odds ratios.
  • - For time-to-event outcomes (e.g., COVID hospitalization/death), use survival analysis (Kaplan-Meier, Cox proportional hazards) and report hazard ratios.


4. Handling Multiple Encounters
  • - Weakness: The plan includes patients with multiple encounters but does not specify the analytic approach.
  • - Problem: Treating multiple encounters from the same patient as independent observations violates statistical assumptions and can bias variance estimates.
üí° Suggested Fix
  • - Prespecify the unit of analysis (the encounter or the patient).
  • - If encounters are the unit, use models that account for within-person correlation, such as:
  • - Mixed-effects models (with patient as a random effect)
  • - Generalized estimating equations (GEE) with robust standard errors.


5. Selection Bias in COVID-19 Arm
  • - Weakness: The COVID-19 arm only includes patients who accepted treatment (e.g., nirmatrelvir/ritonavir).
  • - Problem: This introduces selection bias, as the sample is skewed towards individuals who are willing and able to receive treatment. It is not representative of all diagnosed patients.
üí° Suggested Fix
  • - Ensure the comparator group is similarly defined (i.e., patients treated by traditional providers who also accepted therapy).
  • - Alternatively, use an intention-to-treat approach if comparing against literature that does so.
  • - Adjust for potential confounders like vaccination status, symptom onset timing, and comorbidities.


6. Non-Inferiority Hypothesis
  • - Weakness: The hypothesis of "similar outcomes" implies non-inferiority, but no formal plan is provided.
  • - Problem: Claiming non-inferiority requires a pre-specified, clinically meaningful margin (delta) and a formal statistical test.
üí° Suggested Fix
  • - Option 1 (Test Non-Inferiority): Prespecify a non-inferiority margin for each outcome and perform sample size/power calculations.
  • - Option 2 (Descriptive/Comparative): Avoid non-inferiority language and frame the study as providing adjusted effectiveness estimates.


7. Other Methodological Issues
  • - A summary of additional identified weaknesses in the research plan.
Temporal Confounding
  • - Issue: The 2022‚Äì2025 study period covers significant changes in COVID-19 (variants, immunity, treatments).
  • - Fix: Adjust for calendar time, vaccination status, and prior infection. Consider stratified analyses by time epoch.

Generalizability
  • - Issue: UTI exclusion criteria (e.g., age >65, diabetics) limit external validity to populations that commonly experience UTIs.
  • - Fix: Justify exclusions and consider a sensitivity analysis with broader criteria.

Power & Sample Size
  • - Issue: No sample size or power calculations were presented.
  • - Fix: Provide a priori power calculations or, if retrospective, report the minimal detectable differences given the available sample size.

Data Quality & Blinding
  • - Issue: No plan for ensuring inter-rater reliability for manual chart review or for blinding assessors.
  • - Fix: Develop a data dictionary, double-code a subset of charts (calculate kappa), and blind chart reviewers/adjudicators to provider type where feasible.



üìù Actionable Recommendations Checklist
  • - A summary of the most critical steps to improve the study's rigor.
Top Priorities
  • - 1. Define Comparator Cohorts: Use a contemporaneous internal control group with propensity score matching or multivariable modeling.
  • - 2. Correct Statistical Tests: Replace t-test with chi-square/Fisher's exact for proportions and logistic regression/Cox models for adjusted/time-to-event outcomes.
  • - 3. Refine Outcomes: Extend follow-up windows to 14‚Äì30 days and use more precise outcome definitions.
  • - 4. Address Repeated Measures: Use mixed-effects models or GEE for multiple encounters per patient.
  • - 5. Adjust for Confounding: Control for temporal trends (calendar time, vaccination status for COVID).

  • - Current State: The study's impact is currently limited by major methodological weaknesses.
  • - Path Forward: If rigor is strengthened, the study can provide valuable real-world evidence to inform policy and clinical workflows.
Final Assessment

  • - With improved analytic rigor, the findings could significantly influence how BAPs are evaluated and implemented in health systems.
