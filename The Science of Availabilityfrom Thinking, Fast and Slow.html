The Science of Availability
from 'Thinking, Fast and Slow'

The Availability Heuristic: Definition & Mechanism
  • - The process of judging frequency by “the ease with which instances come to mind.”
  • - It substitutes a difficult question (e.g., 'What is the frequency of this event?') with an easier one ('How easily can I recall examples?').
  • - Involves both System 1 (automatic impression of ease) and System 2 (deliberate problem-solving strategy).
Instantaneous Impression
  • - An impression of availability can be formed almost instantly, without retrieving any specific instances.
  • - Example: You know that the letters TAPCERHOB offer more word possibilities than XUZONLCJM without generating any words.


Sources of Availability Bias
  • - The heuristic leads to systematic errors because factors other than actual frequency can make retrieval easy.
Salient Events
  • - Events that attract your attention are more easily retrieved from memory.
  • - Example: You are likely to exaggerate the frequency of Hollywood divorces and political sex scandals because they receive so much media attention.

Dramatic Events
  • - A dramatic event temporarily increases the availability of its category.
  • - Example: A plane crash that attracts media coverage will temporarily alter your feelings about the safety of flying.

Personal Experiences & Vivid Examples
  • - Personal experiences, pictures, and vivid examples are more available than incidents that happened to others, mere words, or statistics.
  • - Example: A judicial error that affects you will undermine your faith in the justice system more than a similar incident you read about in a newspaper.


Resisting Bias & Debiasing
  • - Resisting availability biases is possible but tiresome, requiring effortful System 2 engagement.
  • - It involves actively reconsidering impressions and intuitions.
Example: Marital Contributions Study
  • - In a famous study, spouses were asked to estimate their personal contribution to household chores (e.g., keeping the place tidy) in percentages.
  • - The self-assessed contributions consistently added up to more than 100%.
Underlying Mechanism
  • - A simple availability bias: both spouses remember their own individual efforts and contributions much more clearly than those of the other.
  • - This difference in availability leads to a difference in judged frequency.
  • - The bias is not necessarily self-serving; spouses also overestimated their contribution to causing quarrels.

Successful Debiasing
  • - This is a rare case where personal control of bias can be successful.
  • - The mere observation that there is usually more than 100% credit to go around is sometimes sufficient to defuse the situation.



The Psychology of Availability: Fluency vs. Content
  • - Research led by Norbert Schwarz in the 1990s revealed that the experience of retrieval is often more important than the number of items retrieved.
Key Experiment: The Assertiveness Study
  • - Task: Participants were asked to list either 6 or 12 instances of their own assertive behavior and then rate how assertive they were.
Results: The Power of Fluency
  • - People who listed 12 instances (a difficult task) rated themselves as less assertive than people who listed only 6 (an easy task).
  • - The difficulty (low fluency) of recalling the last few instances trumped the number of instances recalled.
  • - The experience of fluent retrieval was the dominant factor.

Paradoxical Extension
  • - Participants asked to list 12 cases where they were not assertive ended up thinking of themselves as quite assertive.
  • - The difficulty of recalling meek behavior led them to conclude they were not meek.


Other Paradoxical Findings
  • - People are less confident in a choice when asked to produce more arguments to support it.
  • - People are less impressed by a car after listing many of its advantages.
  • - Students who listed more ways to improve a course rated it higher.

Disrupting the Heuristic: 'Unexplained Unavailability'
  • - The heuristic relies on a surprise: fluency being worse than expected.
  • - If the difficulty of retrieval is given a spurious explanation, the effect disappears.
Experimental Manipulation
  • - Participants were told background music would affect their memory recall.
  • - Those told the music would make retrieval more difficult did not use fluency as a heuristic.
  • - They rated themselves as equally assertive whether they recalled 6 or 12 instances, as the difficulty was now 'explained'.


System 1 vs. System 2 Engagement
  • - The ease of retrieval (fluency) is a System 1 heuristic.
  • - A focus on the content (number of instances) occurs when System 2 is more engaged.
Case Study: Cardiac Health Risk
  • - Students with a family history of heart disease (high personal involvement) engaged System 2.
  • - They focused on the number of instances recalled, not the ease of recall.
  • - They felt safer after recalling many instances of safe behavior and at greater risk after recalling many instances of risky behavior (the opposite of the standard heuristic).

Conditions Favoring System 1 (Fluency)
  • - When engaged in another effortful task at the same time.
  • - When in a good mood.
  • - If they are knowledgeable novices (in contrast to true experts).
  • - If they score high on a scale of faith in intuition.
  • - If they are made to feel powerful.



Availability, Emotion, and Risk Perception
  • - The ease with which ideas of risks come to mind and the emotional reactions to them are inextricably linked.
Post-Disaster Behavior
  • - Availability effects explain cycles of disaster, concern, and growing complacency.
  • - After a disaster, concern is high, and people take protective measures.
  • - As memories dim, so do worry and diligence.
  • - Protective actions are often designed to be adequate only for the worst disaster actually experienced.

Biased Estimates of Causes of Death
  • - A survey by Paul Slovic et al. showed that estimates of causes of death are warped by media coverage and emotional intensity.
Key Survey Findings
  • - Strokes cause almost twice as many deaths as all accidents combined, but 80% of respondents judged accidental death to be more likely.
  • - Asthma causes 20 times more deaths than tornadoes, but tornadoes were seen as more frequent killers.
  • - Death by disease is 18 times as likely as accidental death, but the two were judged about equally likely.


The Affect Heuristic
  • - A System 1 heuristic where people make judgments and decisions by consulting their emotions: 'How do I feel about it?'.
  • - It substitutes an easy question (about feelings) for a much harder one (about thoughts).
  • - Frightening thoughts that are fluent and vivid exacerbate fear.
The Emotional Tail Wags the Rational Dog
  • - An experiment showed that changing people's emotional appeal towards a technology also changed their perception of its risks and benefits.
  • - People told a technology had high benefits subsequently perceived it as having lower risks, even with no new evidence on risk.
  • - Similarly, people told a technology had low risks developed a more favorable view of its benefits.



Public vs. Expert Perception of Risk
  • - Explores the conflict between public opinion (often guided by emotion and availability) and expert analysis in risk policy.
Paul Slovic's View: The Public's 'Richer' Conception
  • - The public draws finer distinctions than experts (e.g., 'good deaths' vs. 'bad deaths').
  • - Argues that 'risk' is not an objective reality 'out there' but a human concept invented to cope with uncertainty.
  • - Concludes that 'defining risk is thus an exercise in power.'

Cass Sunstein's View: The Experts' Objectivity
  • - Risk regulation should be guided by rational, objective analysis (e.g., number of lives saved, dollar cost).
  • - Believes biased public reactions lead to erratic and misplaced priorities in policy.
  • - Experts should act as a bulwark against 'populist' excesses.

The Availability Cascade
  • - A term invented by Sunstein and Timur Kuran for the mechanism through which biases flow into policy.
  • - A self-sustaining chain of events, starting from a minor media report and leading to public panic and large-scale government action.
Mechanism
  • - 1. A media story about a risk catches public attention.
  • - 2. Public arousal becomes a story itself, prompting more media coverage.
  • - 3. 'Availability entrepreneurs' may deliberately fuel the cycle.
  • - 4. The issue becomes politically important, resetting public priorities as other risks fade into the background.

Example: The Alar Scare (1989)
  • - Media reports claimed Alar, a chemical on apples, caused cancer in rodents at gigantic doses.
  • - This led to public panic, large industry losses, and an FDA ban.
  • - The incident was an enormous overreaction to what was likely a minor problem, and may have been detrimental to public health as fewer apples were consumed.

Probability Neglect
  • - A basic limitation of our minds in dealing with small risks: we either ignore them altogether or give them far too much weight.
  • - We focus on the numerator (the tragic story) and not the denominator (the actual probability).
  • - Terrorism is a prime example, inducing an availability cascade despite statistically low casualty numbers compared to other risks like traffic accidents.



Conclusion: A Messy Democracy
  • - The author finds merit in both perspectives.
  • - Sunstein is right: Availability cascades distort public priorities and resource allocation.
  • - Slovic is right: Widespread public fears, even if irrational, cannot be ignored by policymakers because fear itself is painful and debilitating.
Path Forward
  • - Policymakers must endeavor to protect the public from fear, not only from real dangers.
  • - Psychology should inform the design of risk policies that combine experts' knowledge with the public's emotions and intuitions.


Speaking of Availability...
  • - Practical examples illustrating the concepts.
Availability Bias
  • - 'Because of the coincidence of two planes crashing last month, she now prefers to take the train. That’s silly. The risk hasn’t really changed; it is an availability bias.'

Affect Heuristic
  • - 'She’s raving about an innovation that has large benefits and no costs. I suspect the affect heuristic.'

Availability Cascade
  • - 'This is an availability cascade: a nonevent that is inflated by the media and the public until it fills our TV screens and becomes all anyone is talking about.'
