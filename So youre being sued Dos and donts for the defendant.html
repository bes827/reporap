‚öñÔ∏è What is Malpractice?
  • - An allegation of negligence is only a claimed medical malpractice case until proven.
  • - To be awarded damages, the plaintiff must prove both negligence and that the negligence was the proximate cause of the injury.
Negligence
  • - Definition (US Supreme Court): A physician‚Äôs failure to meet the standard of care, defined as 'that which a reasonable physician would do under the same or similar circumstances.'
  • - In most cases, the plaintiff must present an expert witness to testify that the defendant failed to comply with the standard of care.
Exception: Res Ipsa Loquitur
  • - Latin for 'the thing speaks for itself.'
  • - Defined as something that is 'obvious to the common man.'
  • - In these cases, expert witnesses are not necessary.
  • - Example: If a patient is scheduled for a left foot amputation and the surgeon amputates the right foot instead.


Proximate Cause
  • - Refers to a physician being directly responsible for an adverse outcome by not meeting the standard of care.
  • - Example: A physician does not order appropriate studies for a breast lump, and 6 months later a biopsy confirms cancer. The physician may be liable if they cannot prove the disease would have progressed despite early intervention.

‚ÄúLost Chance‚Äù Doctrine
  • - An increasing number of states, including Ohio, recognize this doctrine.
  • - A cause of action exists if there is even a slight chance that the patient‚Äôs disease would not have progressed with an earlier diagnosis.


üõ°Ô∏è Avoiding a Malpractice Lawsuit
  • - Lawsuits often arise from cases that appear unremarkable.
  • - Try to give equal weight to each patient‚Äôs needs.
‚úÖ Do's
  • - Be available to patients and families after an adverse medical outcome. Most lawsuits are initiated by people who feel their physician did not communicate adequately or treated them in an uncaring manner.
  • - Keep thorough records that reflect your clinical decision-making process (what you did, why you did it, why you didn't do something else).
  • - Focus on the patient you are treating at that moment to avoid missing a crucial diagnosis or failing to order a test.


ü§ù Providing Follow-up Care & Avoiding Implicating Others
  • - Applies when you are a specialist or tertiary care provider.
  • - Goal: The patient should feel that your care is part of a continuum, not an 'unnecessary and expensive detour.'
‚ùå Don'ts
  • - Do not accept patient memories of past care uncritically, as they are not always accurate.
  • - Do not make statements like, 'If I had seen you earlier, I could have helped you a lot more than I can now.' This can lead to a lawsuit and a loss of credibility for all physicians.

üó£Ô∏è Constructive Communication
  • - It is helpful to say, 'I would have done things pretty much the same way' or 'Now that your doctor has ordered these tests, I have a much better idea of what to do next.'

Handling Perceived Mistakes
  • - Never lie or make up excuses for potential errors.
  • - Ask yourself if the mistake would have made a significant difference in the outcome.
  • - Avoid using legal terms such as 'negligence' or 'malpractice.'
  • - Remember that cases must be judged as they presented at the time, not with the 'retrospectoscope.'
  • - Allow an independent expert witness who is not involved in the patient‚Äôs care to present a definitive opinion.


Initiation of a Lawsuit
  • - Most lawsuits begin with a patient who has an adverse outcome and feels dissatisfied with the physician's or institution's response.
Process
  • - The patient or family member visits a plaintiff attorney.
  • - The attorney evaluates the likelihood of winning, as they are typically paid only if they obtain monetary damages.
  • - The attorney consults a medical professional for an opinion on whether the standard of care was met.
  • - If there is a good chance of winning, a lawsuit is filed at the local county courthouse.


The Legal Process
The Summons
  • - A summons is sent to the physician via certified mail. If not accepted, it is served by a process server or through standard mail.
Immediate Actions (Do's)
  • - Notify your insurance carrier or legal department within 30 days. Failure to respond is assumed as an admission to all allegations.
  • - Review records of the case to refresh your memory.
  • - Meet with your appointed attorney and make yourself available, even if it requires giving up personal time.


Discovery
  • - The period between when a lawsuit is filed and when it is tried.
  • - Both sides have the right to find out all they can about the other's case.
‚úÖ Do's
  • - Be candid with your attorney. Discussions between attorneys and clients are privileged, meaning they are not discoverable.

‚ùå Don'ts
  • - Do not engage in pretrial conversation with the plaintiff, their attorney, or anyone else, including colleagues. These conversations are discoverable and admissible in court.
  • - Do not change anything in the medical record. Even correcting a misspelling can lead to the destruction of your credibility.
Note on M&M Rounds
  • - Ohio has some protection for discussion during morbidity and mortality rounds, but it is not as impenetrable as in other states.



The Deposition
  • - A question-and-answer session with the plaintiff‚Äôs attorney to determine what occurred and why.
  • - The attorney is also assessing your potential impact on a jury.
‚úÖ Do's
  • - Review the medical record and anticipate likely questions.
  • - Tell the truth. You are under oath, but remember the questioning attorney is not under oath and may make untrue statements.
  • - Take control of the deposition's timing and comfort (reschedule, take breaks).
  • - Pause before answering to give your attorney a chance to object.
  • - Use 'I don‚Äôt know' or 'I don‚Äôt remember' when they are acceptable and truthful answers.

Communication Strategy
  • - Answer clearly for the court reporter (no 'uh huh' or nods).
  • - Listen carefully and answer only what is asked. Do not elaborate.
  • - Elaborate only if a 'yes' or 'no' is inadequate. For example, when asked about an 'authoritative' textbook, you can ask for a definition of the term.
  • - Handle hypothetical questions carefully. A good response is: 'Are you talking about angina in general or about this particular case?'
  • - Take apart compound questions and answer each part separately.

‚ùå Don'ts
  • - Do not clear up the plaintiff‚Äôs attorney‚Äôs confusion if it does not affect your testimony.
  • - Do not appear arrogant.
  • - Do not get into an argument. A good way to avoid this is to remember that if the plaintiff‚Äôs attorney gets angry, you have won.
  • - Do not allow an attorney to insist that if something is not in the record, it did not happen.
  • - Do not guess the rationale for another professional‚Äôs actions. The best answer to 'Why did the cardiologist order enzyme tests?' is 'I don‚Äôt know. Ask the cardiologist.'


The Trial
  • - Follow the same guidelines as for deposition testimony, with a few significant exceptions.
‚úÖ Do's on the Witness Stand
  • - Engage the jury. Direct your answers to them and look each juror in the eyes.
  • - If jurors seem sleepy or uninterested, get them back by changing your pace, coughing, or talking loudly.
  • - Teach the jury and explain the crux of the case.
  • - Express sympathy for the patient and family. It is acceptable to say, 'I‚Äôm really sorry that this happened to Mrs. Jones; I just don‚Äôt feel that I‚Äôm responsible for it.'

‚ùå Don'ts on the Witness Stand
  • - Do not use medical terminology. If jurors do not understand you, they will assume the patient did not understand you either.



Liability Limits & Settlements
Limits of Liability
  • - Physicians in private practice typically purchase malpractice insurance with a liability limit of not less than $1 million.
  • - A plaintiff could obtain a judgment in excess of this limit.
  • - If you think the judgment could exceed your limits, secure a second attorney whose role is to protect your personal assets.

Out-of-Court Settlements
  • - Most insurance policies allow settling a case within policy limits without a trial, which can limit your personal exposure.
When to Consider Settlement
  • - If you feel your defense is weak.
  • - If taking 2 weeks off from your practice for a trial is not feasible.
  • - Consider your reputation: All settlements are reported to the National Practitioner Data Bank.



Role as an Expert Witness
  • - As an expert witness, your role is to decide whether a caregiver met the standard of care.
‚úÖ Do's
  • - Remember that the standard of care is specialty-specific and does not rely only on published guidelines.
  • - Consider whether suboptimal care would have made a difference in the outcome. For example, would diagnosing metastatic cancer 2 months earlier have changed the patient's outcome?


Final Take-Home Point
  • - In your career, you could have more than 100,000 patient encounters.
  • - Don‚Äôt let the threat that a few might result in a malpractice risk keep you from enjoying your patients and the privilege of practicing medicine.
