Paragraph #2: FDA Approval and Legalization Landscape for AI Medical Tools

  • AI tools present unique regulatory challenges compared to traditional FDA-approved medical equipment due to their adaptive, continuously learning nature and "black box" decision-making processes (Source 1 & 2)
    • Traditional device regulations (510(k), De Novo, PMA) were designed for static hardware, creating tension when applied to evolving algorithms whose performance can shift with new data (Source 2)
    • FDA regulates most AI-enabled tools through traditional premarket pathways, which strain concepts like "substantial equivalence" when the predicate device is itself an evolving algorithm (Source 2)
  • Current situation: FDA has authorized over 1,250 AI-enabled medical devices by mid-2025, with approximately 300 clearances occurring in 2025 alone, despite agency staffing deficits of nearly 2,500 personnel (Source 1)
    • Majority of AI devices (96.7%) continue to be cleared via the 510(k) "substantial equivalence" pathway, which may be suboptimal for novel AI applications (Source 1)
    • FDA has implemented the Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR) harmonizing with ISO 13485 effective February 2, 2026, aligning U.S. quality standards with global norms for AI-driven SaMD (Source 1)
  • AI companies are increasingly utilizing Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) to enable iterative updates without serial new submissions (Source 1 & 2)
    • In 2025, 10.2% of FDA AI device clearances utilized PCCPs, a trend expected to grow with foundation models and large-scale multimodal models (Source 1)
    • FDA PCCP Guidance for AI-Enabled Devices (August 2025) clarifies what changes can occur under approved PCCPs versus those requiring new submissions (Source 2)
  • Proposed legislative approaches to optimize AI legalization process include:
    • Algorithmic Based Healthcare Service (ABHS) Bill creating new classification for AI medical services in outpatient payment systems with minimum 5-year reimbursement certainty (Source 3)
    • Sandbox Act concept providing FDA flexibility to permit AI experimentation and innovation in controlled environments (Source 3)
    • Health Technology Act clarifying AI/ML technologies can qualify as "practitioner eligible to prescribe drugs" (Source 3)
  • Impact of effective legalization frameworks on patient safety:
    • Clear regulatory pathways with lifecycle oversight (like PCCPs) reduce risks associated with unmonitored algorithm drift and performance degradation (Source 1)
    • Harmonized quality standards (QMSR) improve cybersecurity resilience and international consistency for AI medical devices (Source 1)
    • Transparency requirements in emerging frameworks help mitigate bias and build trust in AI-assisted clinical decision-making (Source 2)
  • Role of non-profit organizations like Coalition for Health AI (CHAI):
    • CHAI serves as a collaborative community bridging health systems, industry leaders (75% membership), and expert groups to ensure AI in healthcare is safe, effective, and equitable (Source 2 sidebar)
    • Organization focuses on four pillars: Convening stakeholders, Developing consensus-based standards, Certifying AI tools meet safety benchmarks, and Educating stakeholders on responsible AI practices (Source 2 sidebar)
    • Joint Commission partnered with CHAI to release "Guidance on the Responsible Use of AI in Healthcare" (September 2025), establishing seven core elements for responsible AI deployment including governance structures, local validation, monitoring, risk/bias assessment, workforce training, and transparent communication (Source 2)

Paragraph #3: Liability Framework for AI Healthcare Tools Compared to Other Technologies

  • Current liability status for AI in healthcare remains problematic due to evidentiary opacity and fragmented responsibility across clinicians, institutions, and vendors (Source 2)
    • US liability primarily relies on traditional medical malpractice framework requiring plaintiffs to prove duty, breach, causation, and damages, but AI complicates breach and causation proof when outcomes depend on both human judgments and opaque algorithmic outputs (Source 2)
    • The "Black Box" problem creates difficulty proving specific breaches when algorithms arrive at correct diagnoses through flawed logic or incorrect diagnoses through opaque logic (Source 2)
    • Insurance coverage lags behind exposure as malpractice carriers calibrate policies for AI-related risks, sometimes introducing AI-specific exclusions or requiring provider training as coverage conditions (Source 2)
  • Proposed and newly approved laws are beginning to address these liability challenges:
    • State-level initiatives: Texas Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (TRAIGA) effective January 1, 2026 requires written patient notice if AI used in diagnosis/treatment and prohibits harmful AI systems; Colorado AI Act effective June 30, 2026 imposes "duty of reasonable care" to prevent algorithmic discrimination and mandates annual impact assessments (Source 1)
    • California enacted AB 3030 and SB 1120 in early 2025 requiring disclaimers on AI-generated patient communications and human physician review for medical necessity denials (Source 2)
  • Contrast with European Union liability approach:
    • EU has established a more prescriptive liability regime through the revised Product Liability Directive (PLD) transposed by December 9, 2026, which introduces strict liability for AI systems (no-fault liability) by classifying software as a "product" (Source 1)
    • EU PLD extends liability period for latent personal injuries from 10 to 25 years, abolishes previous €85 million cap on personal injury liability, and introduces claimant-friendly presumptions of defectiveness when developers refuse to disclose technical logs (Source 1)
    • Under EU framework, liability focus shifts from proving negligence (US approach) to demonstrating the "defective" nature of the AI product itself, significantly lowering the evidentiary bar for injured claimants (Source 1)
    • EU AI Act classifies most healthcare AI as "high-risk" requiring third-party conformity assessments under MDR, creating dual compliance burden but establishing comprehensive risk management, data governance, and transparency obligations (Source 1)

Paragraph #4: Reimbursement Approaches for AI Healthcare Services

  • Current billing approaches for healthcare services were designed around discrete, time-based interventions and face fundamental mismatches with AI service delivery models (Source 3)
    • Traditional Medicare Physician Fee Schedule relies on CPT/HCPCS codes tied to specific procedures or services, but AI often functions as ongoing infrastructure or decision support rather than discrete billable events (Source 3)
    • CMS methodology for practice expense (based on 2007-2008 data) poorly captures costs associated with SaaS, analytics, and AI tools, creating disincentives for adoption (Source 2)
    • Heavy reliance on Category III codes (temporary tracking) and carrier pricing by Medicare Administrative Contractors leads to substantial geographic and payer variation in AI service reimbursement (Source 2)
  • AI tools are poorly suited to current reimbursement frameworks because:
    • Many AI services (especially SaaS models) involve ongoing subscription/licensing costs that don't map neatly to per-service, per-time, or per-facility payment structures (Source 2)
    • CMS generally does NOT pay for pure efficiency gains in the Physician Fee Schedule, creating an "efficiency trap" where providers adopting AI to improve workflows may see payment reductions predicated on those very efficiencies (Source 3)
    • Crosswalking problems occur when AI diagnostics get mapped to incorrect code categories (e.g., fully autonomous AI code for diabetic retinopathy crosswalked to contact lens correction rather than cataract surgery), causing unintended payment rate impacts (Source 3)
  • Emerging and proposed approaches to better align reimbursement with AI value:
    • CMS CY 2026 PFS Final Rule explicitly solicits comments on how to value SaaS tools, account for subscription/licensing costs, and incorporate physician work in interpreting AI outputs (Source 2)
    • CPT 2026 code set expansion includes nearly 300 additions targeting AI-assisted diagnostic services and establishes formal taxonomy in Appendix S categorizing AI services as assistive, augmentative, or autonomous (Source 1 & 2)
    • Algorithmic Based Healthcare Service (ABHS) Bill would create outpatient payment classification with minimum 5-year certainty for reimbursement, addressing the current limitation of New Technology APC pathway's 3-year timeline (Source 3)
    • CMS Testing Outcome-Aligned Payments (OAPs) through ACCESS Model: Provides recurring payments to manage patient populations with full payment contingent on clinical improvement relative to baseline, rewarding longitudinal AI value rather than discrete diagnostic data (Source 1)
    • New Technology Add-on Payment (NTAP) pathway provides temporary supplemental payment (up to 3 years) for qualifying new technologies inadequately paid under existing DRGs, with growing adoption for AI SaaS tools (4 SaaS items held NTAP status in 2025) (Source 2)

Paragraph #5: Closing Considerations and Future Directions

  • Current regulatory, reimbursement, and liability frameworks for healthcare AI are evolving but remain fragmented and misaligned with the unique nature of adaptive AI technologies (Sources 1-4)
  • Key priorities for stakeholders include establishing sustainable reimbursement models that recognize AI's longitudinal value, creating liability frameworks with clear risk allocation, and developing regulations that support innovation while ensuring patient safety (Sources 1-4)
  • Clinicians and healthcare organizations should:
    • Engage with ongoing regulatory feedback opportunities (FDA RFIs, CMS comment periods) to ensure policies reflect clinical realities
    • Participate in AI governance structures recommended by frameworks like Joint Commission-CHAI guidance
    • Demand transparency from AI vendors regarding training data, performance metrics, and bias assessments
    • Advocate for payment models that reward improved patient outcomes rather than merely volume of AI-assisted services
  • Policy makers and regulators should:
    • Accelerate finalization of AI-specific guidance (FDA AI DSF, PCCP) to reduce implementation uncertainty
    • Develop reimbursement approaches that account for ongoing AI service costs and value beyond discrete procedures
    • Create liability frameworks that appropriately allocate risk between clinicians, institutions, and vendors while maintaining patient access to redress
    • Foster collaboration between regulatory bodies and medical specialty societies to ensure AI policy addresses specialty-specific needs (e.g., GI)
  • Technology developers and vendors should:
    • Prioritize evidence generation through RCTs and real-world outcome studies to meet payer and regulator demands
    • Implement robust lifecycle management including PCCPs, continuous performance monitoring, and transparent documentation
    • Engage early with healthcare providers to integrate AI tools into clinical workflows with appropriate human oversight mechanisms

Paragraph #2: FDA Approval/Legalization Framework for AI Tools

  • FDA's accelerated authorization trajectory: By mid-2025, FDA had authorized over 1,250 AI-enabled medical devices, with approximately 300 clearances occurring in 2025 alone—accelerating despite staffing deficits of nearly 2,500 personnel^[1]^

    • The agency is increasingly relying on internal AI tools like "Elsa"—a large language model—to summarize technical documents and accelerate review timelines^[1]^

  • AI tools differ fundamentally from other FDA-approved equipment: Traditional medical device review paradigms are "insufficient for the speed and opacity of modern machine-learning models"^[1]^

    • Most AI devices (96.7%) are cleared via the 510(k) "substantial equivalence" pathway, which relies on similarity to predicate devices that may not share the same algorithmic complexities^[1]^
    • This approach can obscure unique risks related to bias or performance drift because the predicate itself may be an evolving algorithm^[1]^

  • Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) enable adaptive AI: The FDA has finalized guidance on PCCPs, allowing manufacturers to pre-specify modifications and significantly reducing the burden of successive 510(k) submissions^[1]^

    • In 2025, 10.2% of devices were cleared with a PCCP—a trend expected to grow as foundation models and large-scale multimodal models become standard in clinical settings^[1]^
    • PCCPs allow for "pre-authorized" iterative updates without serial resubmissions^[1]^

  • Transparency and safety governance requirements: The FDA has implemented the Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR) effective February 2, 2026, harmonizing 21 CFR Part 820 with ISO 13485^[1]^

    • This harmonization is critical for AI developers as it aligns U.S. quality standards with global norms, particularly regarding cybersecurity lifecycle management^[1]^
    • The FDA now expects security to be "built into" the device lifecycle from inception, reissuing guidance in early 2026 to ensure AI-driven SaMD (software as a medical device) is resilient against evolving digital threats^[1]^

  • Proposed legislative solutions to optimize legalization process:

    • Sandbox Act (Senator Cruz): Would create regulatory "sandbox" for experimentation when legacy regulations encumber innovation, giving FDA flexibility to permit AI experimentation in controlled environments^[3]^
    • Algorithmic Based Healthcare Service (ABHS) Bill: Creates new classification for AI medical services in outpatient payment system, with minimum 5-year reimbursement certainty^[3]^
    • Health Technology Act (Mr. Schweikert): Would clarify that AI/ML technologies can qualify as "practitioner eligible to prescribe drugs"^[3]^

  • Impact of good legalization on patient safety:

    • A harmonized regulatory framework that properly addresses AI's unique characteristics enhances patient safety through:
      • Lifecycle-aware regulation rather than static clearance^[1]^
      • Robust post-market monitoring for performance drift^[1]^
      • Standardized bias analysis and mitigation requirements^[2]^
      • Transparent training data characteristics and validation methods^[2]^

  • Role of non-profit organizations—Coalition for Health AI (CHAI):

    • CHAI is a non-profit organization founded to address unique challenges of AI in medicine, serving as bridge between stakeholders^[2]^

    • Four strategic pillars:

      Pillar Objective
      Convene Build massive network (3,000+ organizations) to foster collaboration across health sector
      Develop Create consensus-based standards and guidelines for responsible development/deployment of AI
      Certify Establish oversight mechanisms and evaluation frameworks to ensure AI tools meet safety/quality benchmarks
      Educate Provide resources and training for stakeholders to understand and adopt responsible AI practices^[2]^

    • In September 2025, CHAI partnered with The Joint Commission to release "Guidance on the Responsible Use of AI in Healthcare" (RUAIH)—the first national accreditation-linked framework for health AI governance^[2]^

    • The RUAIH outlines seven core elements for responsible AI deployment: policies/governance, privacy/transparency, data security, ongoing quality monitoring, safety event reporting, risk/bias assessment, and education/training^[2]^

Paragraph #3: Liability for AI Compared to Other Medical Tools

  • Current problem with current liability status: In the U.S., AI-related patient harm is primarily addressed under existing medical malpractice and product-liability frameworks rather than AI-specific federal statutes^[2]^

    • Malpractice law continues to require plaintiffs to prove duty, breach of standard of care, causation, and damages—but AI complicates each element^[2]^
    • "Black box" algorithms create evidentiary opacity: When an algorithm arrives at a correct diagnosis through flawed logic (or incorrect diagnosis through opaque logic), plaintiffs face difficulty proving specific breach^[1]^
    • Liability allocation is unclear when multiple actors (clinician, institution, vendor) may be implicated^[1]^

  • Current status of liability allocation:

    Party Liability Exposure in 2026 Mitigating Factors
    Physicians Liable for "blind reliance" on AI or failure to override clearly erroneous output Documented independent clinical judgment and human-in-the-loop verification^[1]^
    Hospitals Liable for negligent selection, validation, or failure to monitor AI systems for drift Alignment with Joint Commission RUAIH standards and robust bias audits^[1]^
    AI Vendors Increasing exposure to product liability claims for design defects or failure to warn Comprehensive indemnification clauses and adherence to NIST risk management frameworks^[1]^

  • Proposed and newly approved U.S. state laws:

    • Texas (TRAIGA): Effective January 1, 2026; requires practitioners to provide written notice to patients if AI is used in diagnosis or treatment; prohibits AI systems designed to incite self-harm or unlawfully discriminate^[1]^
    • Colorado AI Act: Effective June 30, 2026; imposes "duty of reasonable care" to prevent algorithmic discrimination and mandates annual impact assessments for high-risk systems^[1]^
    • California: Enacted AB 3030 and SB 1120 requiring AI-generated patient communications include disclaimers; mandates that any medical necessity denial in insurance be reviewed by a human physician^[2]^
    • Illinois Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act (2025): Prohibits autonomous AI from providing therapy or making independent therapeutic decisions^[2]^

  • Impact of proposed/state laws:

    • These laws create "safe harbor" for clinicians by clarifying disclosure obligations^[1]^
    • They establish a de facto standard of care that views AI as a powerful but fallible tool requiring documented oversight, governance, and contractual clarity^[2]^
    • Courts are increasingly rejecting attempts to shield organizations from AI-generated harms, affirming that AI has no legal personhood; liability flows upward to human operators^[1]^

  • European contrast in liability framework:

    • The EU has established a more prescriptive liability regime through the revised Product Liability Directive (PLD), transposed by December 9, 2026^[1]^

    • Key differences from U.S. approach:

      Feature United States (2026) European Union (2026)
      Primary theory Negligence/malpractice Strict product liability
      Target of claim Primarily clinician/institution Primarily AI developer^[1]^
      Evidentiary bar High (plaintiff must show breach) Lower (presumptions favor claimant)^[1]^
      Transparency Disclosure via state laws (TX, CA) Mandatory disclosure via AI Act/PLD^[1]^
      Tail risk Standard statute of repose 25-year long-stop for latent injury^[1]^

    • Revised PLD highlights:

      • Software as a Product: AI systems explicitly defined as "products," subject to strict liability (no-fault)^[1]^
      • Presumption of Defectiveness: If claimant presents plausible case of harm and developer refuses to disclose technical logs, AI system is presumed defective^[1]^
      • Extended Liability: Liability period for latent personal injuries extended from 10 to 25 years^[1]^
      • Abolition of Caps: Previous €85 million cap on liability for personal injury removed^[1]^

    • National malpractice regimes continue to govern clinicians and hospitals; strict PLD liability typically attaches to product provider, while fault-based claims remain for professionals who fail to exercise appropriate oversight^[2]^

Paragraph #4: AI Tools Reimbursement for Services

  • Current/recent Medicare approaches to billing:

    • CMS relies on existing payment systems built around traditional models (fee-for-service, per-procedure, per-time) that poorly capture AI's economic reality^[6]^
    • In the outpatient setting, AI-enabled technologies are primarily reimbursed through New Technology Ambulatory Payment Classifications (NTAPs)—temporary add-on payments for qualifying new technologies^[6]^
    • Inpatient settings use New Technology Add-On Payments (NTAP) pathway for AI tools; once added to system, must "fight with every other specialty" about payment rates^[8]^

  • Why current reimbursement models are unsuitable for AI:

    • "Benefit categories haven't been meaningfully updated since the 1970s"—Social Security Act mentions "iron lungs" (obsolete) but doesn't contemplate modern AI^[8]^
    • Medicare Physician Fee Schedule (PFS) practice-expense methodology is based on 2007–2008 data and "poorly captures costs associated with SaaS, analytics, and AI tools"^[6]^
    • CMS explicitly does NOT pay for efficiency gains—the "efficiency adjustment" assumes AI makes physicians more efficient, leading to payment reductions (e.g., -2.5% adjustment in 2026 PFS)^[1]^
      • Paradoxically, this creates an incentive structure where providers are "encouraged to adopt AI to maintain margins while simultaneously facing fee reductions predicated on that very adoption"^[1]^
    • AI tools delivered through subscription or enterprise licenses are misaligned with PFS payment structures built around per-service, per-time, and per-facility costs^[6]^
    • "Crosswalking" problems: AI diagnostics are pigeonholed into wrong code categories (e.g., digital retinopathy AI crosswalked to contact lens correction code), causing price instability^[8]^
    • Most AI-related codes remain Category III (temporary tracking codes), often reimbursed under carrier pricing, leading to substantial geographic and payer variation^[6]^

  • Current NTAP limitations:

    • NTAPs are temporary (typically three years), creating a "reimbursement cliff" for hospitals^[6]^
    • Once NTAP expires, cost of AI tool must be absorbed into base DRG payment^[6]^
    • CMS faces challenges applying NTAP to software-as-service (SaaS) models, as "therapeutic action" of software is considered intangible^[6]^
    • Historical data show actual NTAP spending often falls below projections, raising questions about program sufficiency^[6]^

  • Proposed and approved payment innovations:

    • ABHS Bill: Creates new classification for AI medical services in outpatient payment system with minimum 5-year reimbursement certainty^[3]^
    • CPT 2026 Code Set Expansion: Adds nearly 300 new codes, including "CPT Appendix S" establishing formal taxonomy for AI services (categorized as assistive, augmentative, or autonomous)^[1]^
    • Transitional Coverage for Emerging Technologies (TCET): Finalized in 2024, creates expedited route to national Medicare coverage for FDA breakthrough devices (including AI), aiming for decisions within ~6 months^[6]^
    • Advanced Chronic Care with Effective, Scalable Solutions (ACCESS) Model: CMS launched in December 2025 to test "Outcome-Aligned Payments" (OAPs)—recurring payments contingent on clinical improvement relative to patient baseline, rewarding "high-value AI" delivering longitudinal results^[1]^
    • CMS Solicitation on SaaS Payment: CY 2026 PFS Final Rule explicitly acknowledges shortcomings in current PE methodology for valuing SaaS/AI and solicits stakeholder input on new payment frameworks^[6]^

  • Commercial payer developments:

    • Commercial payers have begun reimbursing certain AI-enabled services, often mirroring Medicare's CPT/HCPCS codes^[6]^
    • Coverage remains highly variable and frequently negotiated in contracts rather than governed by transparent policies^[6]^
    • Insurers increasingly use AI to conduct real-time claims analytics and audits, flagging anomalies for overpayment recovery—increasing financial pressure on providers even when AI doesn't improve clinical reimbursement^[6]^

  • DMEPOS gap: Currently NO coverage for digital technologies used by patients at home under conventional Durable Medical Equipment, Prosthetics, Orthotics, and Supplies (DMEPOS) coverage^[8]^

    • Workaround: Industry reverse-engineers software into physical components (e.g., Abbott sensor packages include a handheld device legally required under DMEPOS, even when patient has smartphone)^[8]^

Paragraph #5: Conclusions and Future Recommendations

  • Key conclusions based on evidence:

    • Reimbursement is the overwhelming #1 barrier to AI adoption in healthcare, consistently ranked as top priority by physician and industry stakeholders^[4]^
    • Current regulatory frameworks are perceived as inadequate for ensuring patient safety, with majority expressing skepticism or uncertainty^[4]^
    • Liability allocation remains unclear in current malpractice framework, with "evidentiary opacity" when AI contributes to errors^[1]^
    • U.S. and EU are developing divergent but increasingly harmonized regulatory philosophies^[1]^
    • State legislation and non-profit guidance (e.g., Joint Commission-CHAI RUAIH) are filling gaps left by federal frameworks^[2]^

  • Recommendations for clinicians:

    • Demand transparent governance: Insist on AI systems aligned with Joint Commission-CHAI RUAIH seven pillars, including documented governance structures and bias monitoring^[2]^
    • Require human-in-the-loop verification: Document independent clinical judgment and reasons for following or overriding AI outputs to mitigate liability risk^[1]^
    • Negotiate AI-aware vendor contracts: Include robust indemnification, no-training clauses (preventing use of patient data to train commercial models), and audit rights^[2]^
    • Stay informed on regulatory changes: "Lack of knowledge by regulators" is a documented concern—actively engage with societies and agencies to ensure AI policy reflects clinical realities^[4]^
    • Participate in bias monitoring and incident reporting: Contribute to non-punitive, de-identified reporting of AI near misses and harms^[2]^
    • Understand their limitations: Courts increasingly expect physicians to understand AI tool limitations—stay current on FDA-cleared intended use and known biases^[1]^

  • Recommendations for health systems administrators:

    • Implement organizational AI governance: Establish multi-disciplinary committees including IT, clinical, and compliance representatives to oversee AI lifecycle^[2]^
    • Adopt risk assessment frameworks: Regularly document how models were evaluated for disparate impact across demographic groups^[2]^
    • Secure comprehensive vendor agreements: Negotiate indemnification for "hallucinations" and IP infringement; require vendors to maintain comprehensive logs^[2]^
    • Plan for reimbursement cliffs: When adopting NTAP-funded AI tools, plan for transition to DRG payment once temporary coverage expires^[6]^
    • Invest in staff training: Required for RUAIH compliance and liability mitigation—ensure clinicians understand intended use, limitations, and black box risks of specific tools^[2]^

  • Recommendations for policymakers:

    • Accelerate reimbursement reform: Prioritize ABHS legislation to create stable, long-term payment pathways for AI-enabled services^[3]^
    • Expand regulatory sandboxes: Support the Sandbox Act to enable controlled experimentation when legacy regulations impede innovation^[3]^
    • Harmonize federal and state approaches: Address patchwork of state laws through federal preemption where appropriate^[3]^
    • Fund real-world evidence generation: Address "widespread use catch-22" by funding RCTs and patient outcome metrics required for payer coverage^[1]^
    • Update benefit categories: Modernize Medicare's statutory benefit categories to contemplate AI and digital health technologies^[8]^

  • Recommendations for researchers:

    • Develop physician-trained models: Incorporate clinician input into AI development as requested by physician respondents ("Physician trained models")^[4]^
    • Focus on GI-specific applications: Address unique demands of gastroenterology that are often not contemplated in general AI legislation^[4]^
    • Share large datasets: Enable multicenter data sharing to improve model validation and generalizability^[4]^
    • Simplify study designs: Reduce administrative burden on physicians by streamlining trial protocols and duration^[4]^

  • Future outlook:

    • "Drips and drabs" of AI legislation currently (~4 bills in 119th Congress) but expect acceleration to ~30 bills in next session^[3]^
    • "Congress is in education mode" on AI basics—members understand potential but lack deep technical understanding^[3]^
    • The 2026 healthcare AI landscape represents "governed growth" with formalized governance, lifecycle-aware regulation, and emerging value-based payment models^[1]^
    • Shadow AI—unvetted tools used by clinicians without institutional oversight—is now a major financial and legal risk requiring formal governance frameworks^[1]^
