Designing Cross-Sectional and Cohort Studies
  • This chapter outlines the design, strengths, and weaknesses of cross-sectional and cohort observational studies.
  • It distinguishes designs based on measurement timing: cross-sectional captures data @ single point for prevalence; cohort follows participants longitudinally for incidence.
  • Explores prospective/retrospective cohort designs, incidence rate measurement (person-time @ risk), and importance of minimizing loss to follow-up.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the primary metric measured in a cross-sectional study, and how does it differ from the metric measured in a cohort study?
  • 2. What is 'immortal time bias' and in which type of observational study is it a significant concern, particularly when evaluating treatments?
  • 3. When analyzing cohort studies, why are 'prevalent users' of a medication problematic, and what is the approximate pooled odds ratio for mortality among good adherers to placebo?
  • 4. What is the key advantage of a nested double-cohort study over a double-cohort study where participants are sampled from entirely separate populations?
  • 5. In the context of cohort studies, what is the primary purpose of calculating 'person-time at risk' in the denominator for incidence rates?
Observational Study Overview
  • Observational studies serve two main purposes: descriptive and analytic.
  • Descriptive: examines variable distributions in a population.
  • Analytic: examines associations among variables.
Categorization Basis
  • Studies categorized by time frame for making measurements.
Cross-Sectional Studies
  • All measurements made @ single occasion / within short time.
  • Investigator samples population, examines variable distributions.
  • Predictors/outcomes designated based on biologic plausibility, historical info, research hypotheses.
Basic Design
  • No follow-up period.
  • Steps: Sample participants (meeting inclusion criteria), exclude (exclusion criteria / no consent), measure current predictor/outcome values (w/ historical info).
Key Metric: Prevalence
  • Proportion who HAVE a disease / condition @ one point in time.
  • Unlike cohort studies, cannot estimate incidence (proportion who develop disease over time).
Prevalence Ratio
  • Compares outcome prevalence in exposed vs. unexposed.
  • Cross-sectional equivalent of risk ratio.
Clinical Utility
  • Help clinicians estimate likelihood of disease (prior probability).
  • Useful for health planners: quantify people w/ diseases for resource allocation.
Age & 'Ever Having' Variables
  • Older age → ↑ opportunity for exposure/disease.
  • Age / factors associated w/ greater opportunity (e.g., duration of employment) must be accounted for in analyses.
Strengths
  • Fast & inexpensive: no waiting for outcome.
  • Avoids loss to follow-up.
  • Can be 1st step in cohort study / clinical trial (low/no added cost).
Additional Strengths
  • Defines baseline demographic/clinical characteristics.
  • Allows exclusion of those w/ outcome from longitudinal studies.
  • Reveals cross-sectional associations.
  • Useful for diagnostic test studies (Chapter 13).
  • Best for associations where characteristics influence each other within short periods (e.g., sleep patterns & cognition).
Weaknesses
  • Difficult to establish causal relationships.
  • Measures prevalence, not incidence → caution w/ inferences about causes, prognosis, natural history.
  • Factor associated w/ prevalence could be cause OR associated w/ disease duration (mortality / resolution).
Example: Obesity & CKD
  • Prevalence of chronic kidney disease (CKD) affected by incidence + survival.
  • Obesity associated w/ greater survival among dialysis patients (9).
  • Cross-sectional study of CKD predictors would overestimate association between obesity & CKD incidence.
Serial Surveys
  • Repeated cross-sectional surveys in same population (e.g., every 5 years).
  • Used to infer changing patterns over time (e.g., NHANES).
Distinction from Cohort Studies
  • Not cohort studies: new sample drawn each time.
  • Cannot assess changes within individuals.
  • Findings influenced by people entering/leaving population (births, deaths, migration).
Example: E-cigarette Use
  • McMillen et al. (10) reported e-cigarette prevalence ↑ from 0% to 14.2% in young adults (18-24 years) between 2010-2013 using annual cross-sectional surveys.
Example: Movie Smoking Exposure
  • Sargent et al. (4) studied association between movie smoking exposure & smoking initiation in 6522 US children (10-14 years).
  • Prevalence of 'ever tried smoking' varied from 2% (lowest quartile exposure) to 22% (highest quartile).
  • After adjusting for age & confounders, differences were significant.
  • Estimated 38% of smoking initiation attributable to movie smoking exposure.
  • Confirmed by subsequent studies (5-7).
Cohort Studies
  • A group of participants ('cohort') specified @ outset & followed over time.
  • Defining characteristic: assembled @ outset & followed longitudinally.
  • No structural difference between predictor of interest (exposure/treatment) & other predictors (covariates).
Prospective Versus Retrospective
  • Often classified as prospective (looking forward) or retrospective (looking backward).
  • Definitions vary; not a sharp dichotomy; continuum exists.
  • Terms best applied to aspects of a study, not all-or-nothing phenomena.
Prospective Features
  • Studies w/ many prospective features are less subject to bias.
  • Tend to be more difficult & expensive; impractical for new investigators.
  • New investigators can use data/specimens from ongoing/completed cohort studies (Chapter 16).
Increasing Validity (Table 8.2)
  • Investigators recruit eligible participants → + control over enrollment criteria; - expense.
  • Check for outcome @ baseline & exclude → + avoids outcome preceding exposure; - expense of screening.
  • Measure exposures/covariates (not relying on previous) → + maximize accuracy/precision, minimize missing data; - time/expense.
  • Exposures measured before outcomes → + prevents outcome affecting exposure measurement.
  • Outcomes measured as they occur/end of follow-up (not relying on previous) → + prevents outcome affecting exposure, greater control over ascertainment, may reduce missing data; - time consuming, expensive.
Ambidirectional Cohort Studies
  • Can look forward @ some variables & backward @ others (12).
Example: Nurses’ Health Study (Mostly Prospective)
  • NHS-II (began 1989, 116,429 female US RNs 25-42 years) studied menstrual cycle regularity & risk of death before 70 years (Wang et al., 2020).
  • Predictor: Menstrual cycle characteristics (recalled @ 14-17, 18-22 years; current @ 29-46 years).
  • Outcome: Mortality ascertained from vital statistics, death index, next of kin.
  • Finding: Women w/ usual cycle length ≥40 days ↑ likely to die before 70 years (adjusted HR 1.34 [95% CI, 1.06-1.69] for 18-22 years; 1.40 [95% CI, 1.17-1.68] for 29-46 years).
  • Comment: Recall of past cycles was retrospective, current cycles prospective. Outcome (total mortality) low bias risk.
Example: Door-to-Needle Time (Mostly Retrospective)
  • Man et al. (14) studied door-to-needle time & 1-year mortality in 61,426 Medicare patients (≥65 years) w/ acute ischemic stroke receiving tPA.
  • Cohort: Medicare beneficiaries @ GTWG-Stroke program.
  • Exposure: Door-to-needle time & covariates collected by hospital personnel.
  • Outcome: Deaths/hospital admissions linked to Medicare claims.
  • Finding: Dose-response relationship: adjusted HR 1.04 (95% CI, 1.02-1.05) per 15-minute ↑ in door-to-needle time.
  • Comment: Causal inference depends on adequate adjustment for measured/unmeasured confounders. Exposure measurement was rigorous. If research team measured & patients consented, might be called prospective.
Prospective Study Details
  • Identifies participants @ risk (excluding those w/ outcome), defines/measures predictors @ baseline.
  • Follows participants w/ strict ascertainment protocol for new occurrence / change in outcome.
  • Example: Blood cadmium levels & peripheral neuropathy development.
Retrospective Predictors in Prospective Studies
  • Nearly all prospective studies measure predictors that occurred before study began (e.g., prior binge drinking history).
  • Though measured retrospectively, measurement @ study onset means outcome knowledge/occurrence could not affect its occurrence/measurement.
New Questions in Prospective Studies
  • Data from prospective studies often used for research questions not considered initially.
  • Example: Study of Osteoporotic Fractures (SOF) studied bone density & stroke risk (15). Bone density measured strictly @ baseline, but strokes not originally adjudicated. Required subsequent validation via medical record review (less rigorous).
Prospective Features in Case-Control Studies
  • Even case-control studies (outcomes occur before study) can have prospective features.
  • Investigators can create rigorous case definition & enroll cases as they occur.
  • Predictors (e.g., driving records) may have been measured before outcomes.
  • ↑ validity: prevents outcome affecting predictor / its measurement.
Defining 'Study Begins'
  • What matters most: when key predictors & outcomes occur, and if/when investigator starts to measure them rigorously.
  • Example: Insomnia & balance. If insomnia not major interest @ baseline, may not be rigorously measured. Absence of baseline balance info → uncertainty if insomnia preceded balance difficulty.
Range of Complexity (Table 8.3)
  • Cohort studies vary from simple to most complex, impacting measurement, follow-up, and analysis.
Simple Cohort Studies
  • Measurement of Exposure/Covariates: At baseline only.
  • Measurement of Outcomes: At end of study.
  • Loss to Follow-up: Must be minimal for validity; sensitivity analyses.
  • Measure of Incidence: Cumulative incidence; incidence proportion (e.g., cases per 100 at risk).
  • Measures of Association: Risk ratio, odds ratio.
  • Multivariable Analysis: Logistic regression.
  • Example: Predictors of survival to hospital discharge in early COVID-19 pandemic (16).
Moderately Complex Cohort Studies
  • Measurement of Exposure/Covariates: At baseline only.
  • Measurement of Outcomes: As they occur or determined periodically.
  • Loss to Follow-up: Participants censored when lost to follow-up (f/u); sensitivity analyses.
  • Measure of Incidence: Incidence rate (e.g., cases per 100 person-years at risk).
  • Measures of Association: Incidence rate ratio or hazard ratio.
  • Multivariable Analysis: Poisson or Cox regression.
  • Accounts for differential follow-up time & loss to follow-up by using person-time @ risk.
Complex Cohort Studies
  • Measurement of Exposure/Covariates: At baseline w/ periodic updating; exposure & covariate levels may change over time, but covariates not affected by exposure of interest.
  • Measurement of Outcomes: As they occur or determined periodically.
  • Loss to Follow-up: Patients censored when lost to f/u; sensitivity analyses.
  • Measure of Incidence: Incidence rate (e.g., cases per 100 person-years at risk).
  • Measures of Association: Hazard ratio.
  • Multivariable Analysis: Cox model w/ time-dependent covariates.
  • Analysis compares risk of events in exposed vs. unexposed person-time. Challenging if exposure effects delayed.
Example: Costly Error (Hormone Therapy)
  • NHS study on postmenopausal hormone therapy & longevity (17).
  • Issue: Compared prevalent users to nonusers.
  • Error: Exposure updated every 2 years. Events in 1st year of hormone use misclassified as non-user events.
  • Misclassification was intentional to maintain 'prospective' nature (exposure precedes outcome).
  • Problem: Hormone use ↑ coronary events mainly in 1st year/two.
  • Resolution: Re-analysis (like target trials) comparing initiators vs. non-initiators revealed early adverse effect (19).
Most Complex Cohort Studies
  • Measurement of Exposure/Covariates: At baseline w/ periodic updating; exposure & covariate levels may change over time, AND covariates may be affected by exposure of interest.
  • Measurement of Outcomes: As they occur or determined periodically.
  • Loss to Follow-up: Patients censored when lost to f/u; sensitivity analyses.
  • Measure of Incidence: Incidence rate (e.g., cases per 100 person-years at risk).
  • Measures of Association: Hazard ratio.
  • Multivariable Analysis: Advanced (and difficult) methods such as marginal structural models.
  • Consider simpler design if possible.
Double-Cohort & Multiple-Cohort Studies
  • Involve two or more separate cohorts w/ different levels of exposure.
  • May be sampled from different populations or (preferably) same population (nested).
Double-Cohort Design
  • Steps: Select two cohorts from populations w/ different exposure levels (but same inclusion/exclusion criteria).
  • Measure other predictors @ baseline.
  • Measure outcome variables as they occur / @ end of follow-up.
  • Can oversample unexposed for power (e.g., 2:1 or 3:1 ratio).
  • Matching on variables (age, sex) sometimes done.
  • Exposure level defines follow-up groups → generally simple/moderately complex; no repeated covariate measures needed.
  • Not to be confused w/ case-control: cohort groups chosen by exposure, case-control by outcome.
Multiple-Cohort Design
  • Similar to double-cohort but >2 cohorts.
  • Allows same exposed cohort vs. ≥2 unexposed, or >1 exposed vs. single unexposed.
  • Variation: Compare outcome rate in exposed cohorts w/ external controls (census, registry, vital statistics data).
Example: Jaundice & Infant Feeding (JIFee)
  • Nested triple-cohort study (UCSF & KPNC) on neonatal jaundice/dehydration & neurodevelopment (21, 22).
  • Cohorts: Max total serum bilirubin ≥25 mg/dL (N=147), readmitted for dehydration (N=197), randomly selected controls (N=428).
  • Outcomes: Neurologic disorder diagnoses from databases, neurodevelopmental exams @ age 5 (blinded).
  • Finding: Neither hyperbilirubinemia nor dehydration associated w/ subtle adverse outcomes.
Example: Physician Suicide Rates
  • Studies compared suicide rates of male/female physicians to general population.
  • Female physician suicide rates ↑, male physician suicide rates ↓ vs. general population (23).
Strengths & Weaknesses
  • Strengths: Only feasible approach for rare exposures (occupational/environmental hazards).
  • External controls (census/registry) are population-based & economical.
  • Nested multicohort studies (exposed/unexposed from same population) are more desirable → ↓ selection bias risk.
  • Efficient for rare exposures if outcome ascertainment difficult/expensive.
Concern: Selection Bias
  • If cohorts from separate populations, ↑ risk of selection bias.
  • Cohorts may differ in important ways (beyond exposure) that influence outcomes.
  • Differences (age, race) can be matched/adjusted, but other differences (geographic location) may make groups incomparable.
Example: Azithromycin & Cardiac Death (Nested Double-Cohort)
  • Zaroff et al. (20) investigated azithromycin & sudden cardiac death risk vs. amoxicillin.
  • Cohort: Patients in Kaiser Permanente (1998-2014) w/ electronic medical records.
  • Exposure: Outpatient prescriptions for azithromycin (~1.7M) or amoxicillin (~6.1M). Excluded those w/ serious underlying conditions.
  • Outcomes: Cardiovascular death 0-5 days & 6-10 days from prescription (adjudicated by cardiologists).
  • Finding: Azithromycin ↑ risk of cardiovascular death within 5 days (adjusted HR 1.82; 95% CI, 1.23-2.67), but not 6-10 days.
  • Caution: Similar association w/ noncardiovascular mortality (adjusted HR 2.17; 95% CI, 1.44-3.26) → causality unclear.
  • Comment: Retrospective design captured rare events from large population over 16 years, providing power. Exposure/outcome likely accurate.
Cohort Studies of Treatments / Interventions
  • Fundamental structure same as risk factor studies, but key differences exist.
  • Treatments generally should be studied w/ randomized trials.
  • Cohort studies (especially retrospective) still have secondary role if trials unethical/infeasible.
Emulating a Target Trial
  • Helpful to design cohort study to emulate a randomized trial ('target trial') (25).
  • Consider inclusion/exclusion criteria of target trial: if unethical to randomize, exclude from cohort study.
  • Clear starting point for follow-up: date of randomization in trial. In cohort, a 'triggering event' can serve as starting point (e.g., seizure, 2nd high A1c).
Avoiding Bias
  • Once eligibility established, subsequent events should not alter defined exposure groups ('Once randomized, always analyzed').
  • Example: Antibiotics & hospital stay for asthma (26). Initial design allowed antibiotic start after admission & required ≥2 days treatment. Patients discharged before 2 days excluded from antibiotic group.
  • Result: Antibiotics associated w/ 1-day longer median stay.
  • Bias: Patients in exposed group 'immortal' to discharge until ≥2 days treatment.
  • Re-analysis (antibiotics started day 1, no min duration) eliminated difference (28).
Immortal Time Bias
  • Bias where exposed participants credited for surviving periods when study design made death impossible (27, 29, 30, 31).
  • Target trial framework helps identify & prevent this and 'other self-inflicted injuries' (32).
Problems with Prevalent Users
  • People already receiving treatment @ study onset are problematic (33-36).
  • 1. Adherence bias: Adherent individuals overrepresented. Good adherers to placebo have more favorable outcomes (pooled OR for mortality 0.56 [95% CI, 0.43-0.74]) (37, 38). → Makes any medication look beneficial.
  • 2. 'Healthy user' effect: Participants may receive treatment for unmeasured reasons (interest in prevention, close clinician relationship) that also affect outcome risk.
  • Example: Osteoporosis drug users have 25-60% ↓ overall mortality vs. untreated, but no such benefit in RCTs (39).
  • 3. Misses early adverse effects: Important if adverse effects (including deaths) occur early (e.g., hormone replacement therapy ↑ cardiovascular disease mainly in 1st year) (19, 40).
  • 4. Complicates confounding control: Confounding variable values may already be affected by treatment @ study start.
  • Example: Statin study controlling for LDL-C. Baseline LDL-C already affected by statin → some treatment benefit hidden.
  • 5. No target trial mapping: Cannot map prevalent user comparison to a target trial. Target trials compare initiators vs. non-initiators, or continuers vs. stoppers.
Strengths
  • Allows calculation of incidence (rate/proportion developing condition over time).
  • Longitudinal approach: examines changes in continuous outcomes (e.g., blood pressure) over time.
  • Establishes time sequence (predictor before outcome) → strengthens causal inference.
Additional Strengths
  • May be only feasible way to estimate efficacy of some treatments (e.g., rare condition, strong belief in efficacy w/ few/no other options).
  • Some argue 'real world' evidence from cohort studies (including those ineligible for trials) provides better info on effectiveness/safety, particularly for adverse effects in older/comorbid patients.
Retrospective Cohort Strengths
  • Much less costly & time consuming.
  • Participants already assembled, baseline measurements made, follow-up completed.
Weaknesses
  • Causal inference challenging; interpretation muddied by confounding variables (Chapter 10).
  • Prospective: Expensive & inefficient for studying rare outcomes (requires large N, long follow-up).
  • More efficient for common/immediate dichotomous outcomes & continuous outcomes.
Retrospective Cohort Weaknesses
  • Limited investigator control over sampling, follow-up, nature/quality of measurements.
  • Existing data may be incomplete, inaccurate, or not ideal for research question.
Caution on 'Real World' Evidence
  • Favorable reports of effectiveness in 'real world' cohort studies prone to many biases → must be considered w/ skepticism.
Approach to Analyzing Cohort Studies
  • Involves measures of outcome frequency and measures of association.
Measures of Outcome Frequency
  • Estimates of dichotomous outcome frequency in followed participants.
  • Share same numerator: # participants developing outcome.
  • Implicit concept: 'at risk' (did not have outcome @ study start).
Risk
  • # people outcome occurred / # at risk.
  • Easiest to understand (everyday familiarity).
  • Example: 100/1000 = 10% risk of lung cancer in 2 years.
Odds
  • # people outcome occurred / # people outcome did not occur.
  • Also: risk / (1 – risk).
  • Harder to grasp intuitively.
  • Similar to risk for rare outcomes.
  • Example: 100/900 = 0.111 odds of lung cancer.
  • 10% risk is upper limit for similarity between risk & odds.
Incidence Rate
  • # outcomes / sum of person-time at risk.
  • Accounts for loss to follow-up, deaths, or events precluding outcome ascertainment.
  • Person-time: time each participant contributes from entry until outcome / censored (loss to follow-up / death).
  • Example: 100 cases / 1900 person-years = 5.3% per year.
  • Rate can ↑ over time if cases constant & people at risk ↓.
Multiple Outcomes
  • If most participants have 0 or 1 outcome: dichotomous variable (0 vs. ≥1 episode) gains simplicity.
  • If many participants have multiple outcomes: calculate average count of outcomes per person
  • , or average counts divided by person-time at risk.
Measures of Association
  • Quantify the relationship between exposure and outcome.
Risk Difference
  • Difference in risk between groups.
  • Customarily: risk in exposed group – risk in unexposed group.
  • If exposure prevents outcome: negative risk difference; absolute value called absolute risk reduction.
Risk Ratio (Relative Risk, RR)
  • Quotient of risks in two groups.
  • Customarily: risk in exposed / risk in unexposed.
Odds Ratio (OR)
  • Ratio of odds of outcome in exposed to odds in unexposed.
  • Similar to risk ratio when outcome is rare.
  • Best avoided in cohort study analysis (odds confusing), EXCEPT when using logistic regression (quantifies associations via ORs).
Rate Ratio
  • Quotient of rates in people w/ and w/o a risk factor.
  • Cox proportional hazard model: multivariable analysis for 'time to event' data; estimates hazard ratios (similar to rate ratios), commonly used in cohort studies.
Maximizing Follow-Up
  • Crucial for validity; prospective studies should take steps to achieve high follow-up (Table 8.5).
  • Good relationships between investigators, staff, participants are essential.
  • Positive, engaging study visits improve retention.
Strategies During Enrollment
  • Build relationships: Make baseline/follow-up visits interesting, enjoyable, engaging.
  • Exclude those likely to be lost: Planning to move, uncertainty about willingness to return, ill health/fatal disease unrelated to research question.
  • Obtain tracking info: Address, phone (mobile useful), email of participant; SSN/Medicare # (w/ permission); name/contact of close friends/relatives (non-cohabiting); physician contact.
Strategies During Follow-up
  • Periodic contact: Collect info, provide results, be supportive.
  • Methods: Telephone (evenings/weekends), mail (repeated mailings, stamped/self-addressed cards), newsletters, birthday cards, token gifts.
  • For unreached: Contact friends/relatives/physicians. Request forwarding addresses (postal service). Seek addresses via public sources (directories, internet, credit bureau search w/ permission).
  • Medicare participants: Collect hospital discharge data from Social Security Administration.
  • Determine vital status: State health department or National Death Index.
General Principle
  • Treat participants w/ appreciation, kindness, respect. Help them understand research question to foster partnership.
Sensitivity Analyses for Loss to Follow-up
  • If follow-up <100%, examine if conclusions are sensitive to assumptions about outcomes of those lost.
  • Example: Redo analyses assuming none of exposed & all of unexposed lost to follow-up had outcome, or vice versa.
  • If conclusions are not sensitive, study is more convincing.
