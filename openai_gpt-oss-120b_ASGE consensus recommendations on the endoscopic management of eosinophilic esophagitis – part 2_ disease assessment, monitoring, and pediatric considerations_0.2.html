ASGE consensus recommendations on the endoscopic management of eosinophilic esophagitis – part 2: disease assessment, monitoring, and pediatric considerations
  • Source: Gastrointest Endosc 2025 (Vol ■, No. ■)
  • Topic: Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE) – endoscopic approach, pediatric issues, disease assessment & monitoring
  • Author(s): Evan S. Dellon MD MPH et al.
1. Introduction & Rationale
  • EGD essential for diagnosing suspected EoE, assessing endoscopic findings, obtaining biopsies, performing dilation.
  • Prior ASGE consensus covered diagnosis, grading (EREFS), and dilation; one monitoring statement lacked agreement.
  • New Delphi focused on pediatric considerations, disease assessment, monitoring → 28 statements with ≥80 % expert agreement.
2. Methods – Modified Delphi Process
  • Core group of 6 EoE experts reviewed guidelines, literature, drafted patient‑centered statements (pediatric, assessment, monitoring).
  • Systematic PubMed/Embase/Cochrane search: Jan 1 1993–Sep 21 2024; English only; excluded animal studies, reviews, conference papers.
  • 21 additional adult/pediatric experts voted online (scale 1‑9); ≥80 % scoring 7‑9 accepted.
  • First round: 27/28 statements met threshold; one revised and approved after virtual discussion (second round).
3. Pediatric Considerations – Consensus Statements 1–4
3.1 Indications for Endoscopy in Infants ≤18 months
  • Perform endoscopy when chronic feeding difficulties, failure to advance textures, malnutrition, or poor weight gain are present.
  • High clinical suspicion needed; infants cannot self‑report dysphagia or pain.
3.2 Indications for Endoscopy in Children >18 months
  • Consider endoscopy for protracted vomiting/reflux, abdominal pain, failure of feeding therapy, dysphagia, or food impaction.
  • Symptoms markedly affect quality of life; early diagnosis prevents strictures and growth failure.
3.3 General High‑Risk Indicators (any age)
  • Endoscopy warranted with high suspicion based on esophageal symptoms plus atopic comorbidities or family history of atopy/EoE.
  • ≈80 % of EoE patients have atopic disorders (asthma, allergic rhinitis, eczema, food allergy).
3.4 Biopsy Protocol in Suspected Pediatric EoE
  • Obtain esophageal, gastric, and duodenal biopsies.
  • Minimum 6 esophageal specimens from ≥2 levels (proximal & distal) to capture patchy eosinophilia.
  • Gastric/duodenal samples sufficient to evaluate EGIDs, celiac disease, H pylori, autoimmune enteropathy.
3.5 Monitoring Biopsies in Known Pediatric EoE (Statement 5)
  • During surveillance endoscopies, limit biopsies to the esophagus if prior gastric/duodenal samples were normal, current stomach/duodenum appear endoscopically normal, and no new upper‑GI symptoms exist.
3.6 Documentation of Endoscopic Severity (Statement 6)
  • Record EREFS (Edema, Rings, Exudates, Furrows, Strictures) at every pediatric procedure to enable baseline & longitudinal severity assessment.
3.7 Role of Less‑Invasive Monitoring Tools (Statement 7)
  • Reserve esophageal string testing and unsedated transnasal endoscopy (uTNE) for monitoring disease status only; do not use them for initial EoE diagnosis.
3.8 Indications for Esophageal Dilation in Children (Statement 8)
  • Consider dilation in patients with symptomatic strictures or narrow‑caliber esophagus identified on esophagram/endoscopy, irrespective of current inflammatory activity.
4. Disease Assessment – Consensus Statements 13–22
4.1 Initial Diagnostic Endoscopy Off Treatment (Statement 13)
  • Perform the first EGD off any therapy that could mask eosinophilic inflammation (PPIs, steroids, elimination diets).
4.2 Comprehensive Standard EGD for Suspected EoE (Statement 14)
  • Conduct a standard EGD allowing full assessment (including gastric/duodenal biopsies when indicated) and dilation of symptomatic strictures if needed.
4.3 Mandatory Esophageal Biopsies in All Relevant Cases (Statement 15)
  • Obtain esophageal biopsy specimens whenever symptoms or endoscopic findings suggest EoE, regardless of age.
4.4 Functional Luminal Imaging Planimetry (FLIP) – Current Role (Statement 16)
  • Routine use of functional luminal imaging planimetry in EoE care remains undetermined; not recommended for all patients presently.
4.5 Integrated Disease Activity Assessment (Statements 17‑20)
  • Assess disease activity by jointly considering symptoms, endoscopic findings (EREFS), and histologic features (peak eosinophil count + other markers).
  • In adults, maintain high suspicion for strictures; endoscopic detection of narrowing is not sensitive.
4.6 Techniques to Measure Esophageal Diameter (Statement 22)
  • Esophageal caliber can be evaluated via esophagography, FLIP, or “sizing” with balloon/bougie dilators.
5. Disease Monitoring – Consensus Statements 23–28
5.1 Treatment‑Specific Monitoring Intervals (Statement 23)
  • Diet elimination: repeat assessment 6‑8 weeks after initiation.
  • PPIs & topical steroids: reassess 8‑12 weeks post‑start.
  • Biologics: initial evaluation up to 24 weeks; earlier checks allowed for severe disease or frequent dilation needs.
5.2 Biopsy Protocol During Monitoring Endoscopies (Statement 24)
  • Use identical protocol as diagnostic EGD: ≥6 esophageal biopsies from ≥2 levels.
5.3 Washout Considerations After Therapy Change (Statement 25)
  • Schedule monitoring endoscopy after sufficient wash‑out of prior treatment to isolate efficacy of the new regimen.
5.4 Use of Less‑Invasive Modalities for Monitoring (Statements 26‑27)
  • uTNE, esophageal string test (EST), sponge/balloon devices may be employed if expertise & availability permit, but not when dilation is required or for initial diagnosis.
5.5 Structured Clinical & Endoscopic Follow‑Up (Statement 28)
  • Implement organized monitoring programs to prevent gaps in care that heighten fibrostenotic risk.
6. Pediatric Therapeutic & Procedural Guidance (Statements 5–11)
  • Statement 5: If prior gastric/duodenal biopsies were normal and no new GI symptoms arise, monitoring endoscopies may sample the esophagus only, reducing unnecessary risk, cost, anesthesia time.
  • Statement 6: Document EREFS in all children with suspected or known EoE to permit initial & longitudinal severity scoring (total score 0‑9).
  • Statement 7: Assess readiness & preprocedure anxiety in older adolescents/young adults; address needle phobia with numbing cream, inhalation induction, or uTNE.
  • Statement 8: Implement a structured transition plan from pediatric to adult care; 100 % of voters agreed on necessity for proactive hand‑off.
7. Disease Assessment – Endoscopic & Histologic Framework (Statements 13‑22)
7.1 Off‑Treatment Diagnostic EGD (Statement 13)
  • Initial EGD must be performed off any therapy that could suppress eosinophilic inflammation (e.g., PPIs, elimination diets).
7.2 Comprehensive Standard EGD (Statement 14)
  • Include gastric/duodenal biopsies when indicated; dilate symptomatic strictures during the same session if needed.
7.3 Universal Esophageal Biopsy Requirement (Statement 15)
  • All patients with compatible symptoms or endoscopic signs must have esophageal biopsies obtained for definitive diagnosis & longitudinal monitoring.
7.4 Histologic Scoring Beyond Peak Eosinophil Count (Statements 16‑20)
  • Peak eosinophil count interpreted alongside lamina propria fibrosis, basal zone hyperplasia, and other histologic features; incorporated in I‑SEE severity index.
  • Symptoms of dysphagia must be evaluated with context of food avoidance, behavioral modifications, and prior dilation status.
7.5 Stricture Suspicion & Detection Sensitivity (Statements 20‑22)
  • Adult endoscopists should maintain high suspicion for strictures; detection sensitivity low (≈33 % for ≤13 mm rings vs. esophagram).
  • Esophageal diameter measurable by barium swallow, FLIP, or “sizing” with balloon/bougie dilators.
8. Disease Monitoring – Consensus Statements 23–28
8.1 Treatment‑Based Monitoring Intervals (Statement 23)
  • Diet elimination: reassess at 6‑8 weeks.
  • PPIs & topical steroids: repeat evaluation 8‑12 weeks after start.
  • Biologics: initial monitoring up to 24 weeks; earlier checks allowed for severe disease or rapid symptom change.
8.2 Consistent Biopsy Strategy During Surveillance (Statement 24)
  • Same protocol as diagnostic EGD: ≥6 esophageal biopsies from ≥2 distinct levels, targeting active furrows/exudates when present.
8.3 Wash‑out Timing After Therapy Change (Statement 25)
  • Interval must allow complete washout of prior agent (PPIs ≥1–2 months; steroids ≈90‑120 days; dupilumab 4‑6 months due to long half‑life).
8.4 Role of Less‑Invasive Monitoring Tools (Statements 26‑27)
  • uTNE, string test, sponge/balloon devices can be considered for disease activity assessment if dilation is not anticipated and local expertise exists.
  • These modalities must not replace sedated upper endoscopy when strictures are present or initial diagnosis is required.
8.5 Structured Clinical & Endoscopic Surveillance (Statement 28)
  • Adopt organized monitoring programs to avoid care gaps that accelerate fibrostenosis; combine clinic evaluation with intermittent endoscopic reassessment.
