1. Introduction to Database-Oriented Research
  • Randomized clinical trials provide highest level of evidence but may not be feasible for fellows/junior investigators and may take years to publish
  • Database-oriented research overcomes barriers: low barrier to entry, can jump-start clinical research career, develops epidemiologic analysis skills
  • Offers ability to test clinically meaningful hypotheses and provide data impacting clinical decision making
Getting Started Steps
  • Find mentor with database research experience; mentorship can be split between GI clinical researcher and epidemiologist/biostatistician
  • Identify clinical area of interest: cancer screening/prevention, motility, hepatology, IBD
  • Use iterative approach: explore database first to generate hypotheses, then conduct thorough literature review to avoid duplicate work
2. Survey Data Databases
National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES)
  • Access: Publicly available at https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/index.htm
  • Cost: Free
  • Data Type: National survey with interview, physical exam, and laboratory data
  • Sample Size: ~5000 individuals per year
  • GI Research Use: Investigate disease associations with medications, physical exam findings, laboratory data
  • Example Study: Kim et al used NHANES to show reduced thyroid function predicts mortality in patients with nonalcoholic fatty liver disease
  • Key Consideration: Requires statistical knowledge for sample weighting to make answers representative of larger population
National Health Interview Survey (NHIS)
  • Access: Publicly available at https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nhis/index.htm
  • Cost: Free
  • Data Type: Interview-based survey focused on health care utilization and access
  • Sample Size: ~30,000 households per year
  • GI Research Use: Investigate associations between diseases and health care access/barriers; includes cancer-related questions every 5 years
  • Example Studies:
  • Nguyen et al evaluated food insecurity, social support, and financial toxicity in patients with IBD
  • Millien et al assessed adherence to colorectal cancer screening guidelines in African Americans
3. Registry Databases
Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) Program
  • Access: Publicly available; restricted data requires application via SEER Research Plus
  • Cost: Free
  • Data Type: Cancer registry with patient demographics, cancer incidence, mortality, tumor stage, morphology
  • Software: Requires SEER*STAT software (free tutorials available; responsive helpline)
  • Additional Tools: JoinPoint software for trend analysis
  • GI Research Use: Assess cancer incidence, mortality, survival, and limited duration prevalence
  • Example Study: Hur et al found increase in esophageal adenocarcinoma incidence and mortality from 1975 to 2009
  • Limitation: Certain demographic and cancer data restricted to SEER Research Plus
GI Quality Improvement Consortium (GIQuIC)
  • Access: Application process required; fee-based
  • Cost: Fee
  • Data Type: Registry for quality improvement measures including patient demographics, provider characteristics, ASA status, anticoagulation use, endoscopic procedure measures
  • Time Period: Data beginning from 2010
  • GI Research Use: Describe endoscopy and colonoscopy measures, adenoma detection rates
  • Example Studies:
  • Bilal et al used GIQuIC to describe polyps and neoplasia in patients aged 45-49, informing adenoma detection rates for new screening guidelines
  • Trivedi et al analyzed prevalence and predictors of young-onset colorectal neoplasia
4. Electronic Health Records Databases
Veteran Health Affairs (VA) Records
  • Access: Affiliation required; collaboration with VA staff needed
  • Cost: Free
  • Data Type: Electronic health records including entire medical charts
  • Unique Feature: Longitudinal data spanning several decades, providing unique long-term patient tracking
  • Data Included: Diagnostic and procedural codes, laboratory data, vital signs, imaging, pathology reports
  • GI Research Use: Evaluate longitudinal care of large cohort of patients; assess disease progression and outcomes
  • Example Study: Published in Hepatology 2021, described clinical characteristics and outcomes of veterans with and without cirrhosis who tested positive for SARS-CoV-2; included 3306 patients with cirrhosis out of 88,747 tested for COVID-19
  • Advantages: Large population enabled rapid study publication during pandemic; comprehensive patient records
  • Limitations:
  • Findings may not be generalizable due to predominantly male and English-speaking population
  • Missing information if care was provided outside the VA system
5. Claims Data Databases
MarketScan
  • Access: Publicly available via IBM
  • Cost: Fee required
  • Data Type: Family of databases with insurance claims from participating providers
  • Population Covered: Employees and employee dependents from employer-sponsored insurance, employer-sponsored Medicare supplement, and Medicaid in 11 states
  • Data Scope: Inpatient, outpatient, and prescription drug claims plus expenditure data
  • Supplemental Data Available:
  • Workplace and disability measures
  • Weather patterns
  • Benefit plan design
  • Inpatient drug use
  • GI Research Use: Examine treatment patterns, patient adherence, and natural history of disease across both inpatient and outpatient care
  • Example Study: Kulaylat et al used MarketScan to evaluate postoperative complications in patients with ulcerative colitis who received preoperative anti-tumor necrosis factor therapy
  • Limitations:
  • Incomplete follow-up if patients change employers
  • Includes only employed patients of working age
SEER-Medicare Linked Database
  • Access: Application process required via NCI
  • Cost: Fee for data purchase
  • Data Type: Combined registry and claims data linking SEER cancer registry with Medicare claims
  • Data Included:
  • SEER registry data (patient demographics, cancer incidence, tumor characteristics)
  • Inpatient, outpatient, and medication claims
  • Measures of comorbidities
  • Receipt of screening and evaluation tests
  • Detailed treatment data
  • Special Feature: Includes random 5% sample of patients without cancer to serve as controls for population-based analyses within SEER registry areas
  • GI Research Use: Perform epidemiological and health services research in patients with cancer; assess screening, treatment patterns, and outcomes
  • Example Study: Rustgi et al found rise in use of endoscopic ultrasound to diagnose pancreatic cancer from 2000 to 2015, along with survival benefit
  • Limitations:
  • Includes only older patients (age 65+)
  • Significant cost for data purchase
  • Extensive application and data use agreement required, approved by NCI
  • Time from application to data receipt can be many months unless researcher already has database access
National Inpatient Sample (NIS) / Nationwide Inpatient Sample
  • Access: Application process required via AHRQ
  • Cost: Fee with discounts available for students
  • Data Type: Combined registry and claims data on inpatient stays, ambulatory surgery, and readmissions
  • Scope: Largest publicly available all-payer inpatient database
  • Sample Size: 7 million inpatient stays per year (35 million with weighting)
  • Purpose: Produce estimates of inpatient use, access, cost, quality, and outcomes
  • Data Included:
  • Patient demographics
  • Hospital characteristics
  • International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes
  • Charges
  • Discharge status
  • Length of stay
  • Severity and comorbidity measures
  • GI Research Use: Evaluate inpatient care patterns, costs, procedures, and outcomes
  • Example Study: Joo et al used NIS to describe hospital costs and utilization of palliative care consults and procedures for patients with gastric cancer over 10-year period (2009-2018)
  • Advantages: Large national sample enables robust population-level estimates; all-payer approach captures diverse patient populations
6. Comprehensive Data Sets
All of Us Research Program
  • Access: Two access types available
  • Anonymized aggregate data: publicly available
  • More comprehensive individual data: requires registration as researcher
  • Cost: Free
  • Data Type: Prospectively enrolling registry with multiple data sources
  • Unique Features:
  • Survey data collected at time of entry
  • Biosamples for future genomic studies
  • Linked to health records for subset of patients
  • Data includes both prospective and retrospective components
  • Data Included:
  • Patient health information from surveys
  • Electronic health records
  • Biosamples
  • Wearable device data (for some participants)
  • Primary Goals: Promote health equity, improve wellness and health outcomes, inform earlier diagnosis of disease in diverse patient population
  • GI Research Use: Evaluate health of large, diverse cohort of patients; includes access to biosamples for genomic studies
  • Example Study: Renedo et al used novel definitions of underrepresented groups (beyond race and ethnicity) to demonstrate disparities in revascularization after stroke
  • Limitations:
  • Potential selection bias as all participants volunteer to participate
  • May not represent general population

8. Practical Recommendations for GI Fellows and Junior Investigators
Selecting Appropriate Database
  • Consider clinical question first, then match to database that contains relevant data elements
  • Assess whether database population matches target patient population for research question
  • Evaluate required statistical expertise: survey data requires weighting knowledge; large claims databases need complex analytical skills
Cost Considerations
  • Several databases are free: NHANES, NHIS, SEER, VA records (with affiliation)
  • Some databases cost several thousand dollars: MarketScan, SEER-Medicare, NIS
  • All of Us is free but has limited GI-specific data currently
Collaboration Strategies
  • Partner with other fellows and junior faculty to share database costs
  • Seek mentorship from both GI clinical researcher (for clinical question) and epidemiologist/biostatistician (for analytical approach)
  • Consider split mentorship model if needed
Practical Tips for Success
  • Start by thoroughly exploring database structure before formulating hypotheses
  • Conduct comprehensive literature review early to avoid duplicating existing work
  • Be flexible and willing to modify research direction based on database capabilities
  • Account for limitations when interpreting and presenting results
9. Conclusion
  • Database-oriented research offers low barrier to entry for GI fellows and junior investigators seeking to develop clinical research careers
  • Multiple database options available: national surveys (NHANES, NHIS), cancer registries (SEER), quality improvement registries (GIQuIC), electronic health records (VA), claims data (MarketScan, SEER-Medicare, NIS), and comprehensive registries (All of Us)
  • Each database type has distinct advantages, limitations, cost considerations, and specific data elements suited to different research questions
  • Success requires finding appropriate mentorship, selecting database matching clinical question, and being flexible in approach
  • Collaboration with peers can offset costs and lead to more productive research endeavors
  • Database research provides opportunity to generate meaningful data that can impact clinical decision making while developing epidemiologic analysis skills
