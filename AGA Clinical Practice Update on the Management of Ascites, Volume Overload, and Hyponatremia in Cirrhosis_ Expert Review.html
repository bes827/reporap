Overview & Pathophysiology
  • - Volume overload is a defining feature of decompensated cirrhosis.
  • - Manifestations: ascites, hepatic hydrothorax (HH), peripheral edema, anasarca.
  • - Complications: Decreased quality of life, hospitalizations, infections, hepatorenal syndrome, increased mortality.
  • - Hyponatremia worsens hepatic encephalopathy (HE) and ascites, associated with mortality.
Mechanism of Volume Overload
  • - Portal hypertension ---> increased intrahepatic vascular resistance & portal blood flow ---> mesenteric & systemic vasodilation.
  • - Kidney responds with neurohormonal activation (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System) ---> renal sodium & water reabsorption ---> intravascular & interstitial volume expansion.
  • - Ascites: Excess fluid leaks into peritoneal cavity due to exceeding hepatic lymphatic capacity, reduced oncotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia), increased hydrostatic pressure (portal hypertension), increased capillary permeability.
  • - Hepatic Hydrothorax (HH): Diaphragmatic defects allow ascitic fluid to enter the pleural space.
  • - Hypervolemic Hyponatremia: Progressive circulatory dysfunction & effective intravascular hypovolemia ---> increased renal water retention.


Best Practice Advice 1: General Management of Ascites, HH, Volume Overload
  • - Manage with dietary sodium restriction and diuretics at the lowest effective dose.
  • - Dose escalation guided by: symptoms, weight, urine output, electrolyte/renal monitoring.
  • - Provide education and referral to a dietitian for dietary management.
  • - Identify and address triggers of liver decompensation (e.g., alcohol, medications, toxins, hemodynamic changes, infection).
Dietary Management
  • - Sodium restriction: ideally <2000 mg/d.
  • - Dietitian guidance: label reading, cooking techniques, sodium education.
  • - Avoid overly restrictive diets to prevent caloric or protein deficiency.
  • - Dietary advice: â‰¥35 kcal/kg/d (non-obese), micronutrient repletion, protein intake 1.2â€“1.5 g/kg.
  • - Fluid restriction is not needed in the absence of significant hyponatremia.

Diuretic Therapy
  • - First-line medications: Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRA) and loop diuretics.
  • - Combined furosemide and spironolactone (40:100 ratio) enhances natriuresis and diuresis while maintaining normokalemia.
  • - Spironolactone: Typically started at 100 mg daily (range 50â€“400 mg), ideally as a once-daily morning dose.
  • - Alternatives for spironolactone intolerance: amiloride, eplerenone (less effective).
  • - Furosemide: Usually starts at 40 mg daily (range 20â€“160 mg).
  • - Titration: To the lowest effective dose based on weight, symptoms, urine output, and laboratory results.
  • - Targeted weight loss: 0.5 kg/d without edema, 1 kg/d with edema.
  • - Mild, asymptomatic ascites: May be managed with low-dose diuretics or sodium restriction alone.


Best Practice Advice 2: Diagnostic Paracentesis
  • - Indication: New-onset ascites, or hospital admission for symptoms related to ascites or encephalopathy.
  • - Timing: As soon as possible.
  • - Testing: Serum ascites albumin gradient (SAAG), cell count, Gram stain, and culture.
  • - Paracentesis within 12â€“24 hours of admission is associated with lower hospital mortality.
SAAG Interpretation
  • - SAAG â‰¥1.1 g/dL strongly suggests portal hypertension.
  • - SAAG <1.1 g/dL warrants evaluation for other causes.
  • - Patients with heart failure typically have SAAG â‰¥1.1 g/dL and high ascitic fluid protein (>2.5 g/dL).

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) Diagnosis
  • - SBP is diagnosed when the neutrophil count exceeds 250/mm3, with or without culture positivity, and without evidence of secondary peritonitis.
  • - Cultures should be obtained by bedside inoculation into blood culture bottles.
  • - For low SAAG or nonâ€“portal hypertension causes, consider further testing (cytology, amylase, mycobacterial studies).


Best Practice Advice 3: Therapeutic Thoracentesis for Hepatic Hydrothorax (HH)
  • - Indication: Patients with HH with dyspnea and/or hypoxemia.
  • - Purpose: Both symptom relief and expansion of the underlying lung.
  • - HH is a poor prognostic sign, associated with a >4-fold increase in mortality compared with refractory ascites.
  • - First-line therapies: Sodium restriction and diuretics.
  • - Thoracentesis is indicated for symptomatic patients or those with recurrent effusions despite treatment.
Spontaneous Bacterial Empyema (SBE) Diagnosis
  • - Hospitalized patients with HH-related symptoms, HE, or unexplained liver decompensation should undergo thoracentesis to evaluate for SBE.
  • - SBE is diagnosed when pleural fluid neutrophils exceed 250/mm3, with or without culture positivity, and without an alternative infectious source.


Best Practice Advice 4: Liver Transplantation Evaluation
  • - All patients with ascites and/or HH should be considered for liver transplantation evaluation, regardless of their Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) score.
  • - Ascites is the most common form of cirrhosis decompensation and a prerequisite for SBP and hepatorenal syndrome, which carry 90-day mortality rates up to 50%.
  • - Once ascites develops, annual mortality rises from <5% to 20%.
  • - HH is independently associated with significantly higher mortality regardless of MELD, with a 1-year mortality rate of 20%â€“50%.
MELD Score & HH Exceptions
  • - MELD has been revised to include sodium, sex, and albumin.
  • - Ascites remains an independent predictor of mortality beyond MELD.
  • - Patients with ascites and low MELD scores may access transplants via living donors or extended criteria donors.
  • - Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) guidance offers no standard MELD exception for HH.
  • - Nonstandard exceptions for HH may be granted individually for patients meeting criteria:
  • - â‰¥1 thoracentesis >1 L weekly for 4 weeks.
  • - Transudative fluid.
  • - No heart failure.
  • - Negative cultures and cytology.
  • - Contraindications to TIPS.


Best Practice Advice 5: Management of Refractory Ascites and/or Hydrothorax
  • - Manage with therapeutic paracentesis and/or thoracentesis, respectively, with the frequency guided by recurrence.
  • - Refractory ascites: Ascites that cannot be mobilized or the early recurrence of which cannot be satisfactorily prevented by medical therapy (See Table 1).
  • - Refractory HH: Unresponsive to sodium restriction and diuretic therapy and requires therapeutic thoracentesis (See Table 1).
Refractory Ascites Subcategories
  • - Diuretic-resistant: Nonresponse to sodium restriction and diuretics.
  • - Diuretic-intractable: Complications that prevent effective diuretic dose escalation.

Therapeutic Paracentesis Considerations
  • - Safe, effective, offers faster symptom relief and fewer complications (e.g., HE, electrolyte abnormalities, AKI) compared to intensive diuretic therapy.
  • - Large-volume removal can cause postparacentesis circulatory dysfunction (PCD), increasing risks of kidney injury, electrolyte imbalances, HE, and death.
  • - Postparacentesis bleeding is rare; prophylactic transfusions are not advised.

Therapeutic Thoracentesis Considerations for HH
  • - More challenging than ascites due to lower fluid thresholds for symptoms.
  • - Carries higher risks (e.g., bleeding, infection, pneumothorax, re-expansion edema) than for other effusion causes.
  • - Bleeding from chest wall portosystemic collaterals can be catastrophic.
  • - Risks of complications and death rise with repeated thoracenteses.
  • - Chest tube placement for HH carries high morbidity and mortality and should be avoided.
  • - Indwelling tunneled pleural catheters: Show promise, relatively low complication rates; may serve as palliative options or a bridge to transplantation.


Best Practice Advice 6: Albumin Administration Post-Paracentesis
  • - When the volume of ascites removed is >5 L, 20%â€“25% intravenous albumin 6â€“8 g per every total liter removed should be administered.
  • - Purpose: Prevent postparacentesis circulatory dysfunction (PCD), which reduces electrolyte derangements and mortality.
  • - For patients with hypotension, renal insufficiency, or electrolyte abnormalities, albumin should also be considered for removal of smaller volumes.
  • - A more liberal albumin strategy (lower-volume thresholds, higher doses) may be considered case by case in this population, with care to avoid iatrogenic volume overload.

Best Practice Advice 7: Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS)
  • - Indication: Well-selected patients with refractory ascites, hepatic hydrothorax, volume overload, or hyponatremia.
  • - Effectiveness: Highly effective for portal hypertension, more effective than paracentesis in controlling ascites, and associated with lowermortality.
  • - Impact on patient-reported outcomes is less clear; symptom relief may be offset by HE and TIPS dysfunction risk.
Patient Selection & Contraindications
  • - Absolute contraindications: Congestive heart failure, severe pulmonary hypertension, uncontrolled HE, and sepsis.
  • - Avoid in patients with: Ejection fraction <50%, severe diastolic/valvular dysfunction, or right ventricular systolic pressure >45 mm Hg.
  • - HE occurs in up to 50% post-TIPS. Risk factors: prior HE, impaired liver function, older age, renal dysfunction, hyponatremia, large gradient reduction, sarcopenia.
  • - Caution advised for patients older than 70 years or with MELD score >18.
  • - Cardiac decompensation may occur in up to 20% within 1 year of TIPS.
  • - Preprocedural evaluation: Echocardiography for cardiac systolic/diastolic function and right ventricular systolic pressure.

TIPS Timing & Alternatives
  • - Optimal TIPS timing remains undefined; most studies focus on refractory ascites and HH.
  • - Some expert panels suggest TIPS after 3 large-volume paracenteses in 12 months, especially with other indications (e.g., prior variceal bleeding).
  • - For refractory ascites patients not eligible for TIPS:
  • - Indwelling peritoneal catheters: Limited evidence, potentially high complication rates.
  • - Alfapump: Improved quality of life in trials, but carries infection risk.
  • - Peritoneovenous shunt: Limited by concerns regarding shunt patency, DIC, heart failure.


Best Practice Advice 8: Diagnostic Workup for Hyponatremia
  • - Etiology of hyponatremia in cirrhosis: Serum overdilution from fluid retention caused by vasopressin secretion, increased water reabsorption, and enhanced sodium reabsorption.
  • - Workup should include:
  • - Dietary and medication history (diuretics, bowel regimen).
  • - Review of electrolyte and kidney function.
  • - Gastrointestinal bleeding assessment.
  • - Infectious workup, including diagnostic paracentesis.
  • - Evaluation of secondary causes (thyroid or adrenal dysfunction).
  • - Hyponatremia (serum sodium <130 mEq/L) is associated with significant morbidity and mortality.
  • - Pseudohyponatremia: Consider if serum osmolality is not low (>280 mOsm/L).

Best Practice Advice 9: Outpatient Management of Asymptomatic Hypervolemic Hyponatremia
  • - Indication: Asymptomatic hypervolemic hyponatremia in liver cirrhosis.
  • - Management entails:
  • - Both sodium and water restriction (aiming for 1â€“1.5 L of daily fluid intake).
  • - Modification of diuretics and laxatives.
  • - Monitoring of electrolytes.
  • - If asymptomatic with mild hyponatremia (serum sodium >120 mEq/L), fluid intake can be limited to 1â€“1.5 L.
  • - Diuretics often need to be reduced or discontinued; can resume/maintain once sodium stabilizes >125 mEq/L.
  • - Patient education on lactulose/laxative dosing is important to avoid excessive stooling, which can lead to volume depletion and worsening hyponatremia.
  • - Ambulatory albumin infusions may help, but access is limited.

Best Practice Advice 10: Inpatient Management of Severe or Symptomatic Hypervolemic Hyponatremia
  • - Indication: Patients with symptoms related to hyponatremia or serum sodium falls below 120 mEq/L.
  • - Management includes:
  • - Both sodium and water restriction.
  • - Modification or discontinuation of diuretics and laxatives.
  • - Additional measures: intravenous albumin (based on volume assessment) or oral vasoconstriction therapy (e.g., midodrine).
  • - Symptoms can include cognitive changes, dizziness, seizures, cardiopulmonary alterations, nausea, and vomiting.
  • - Hypokalemia should be corrected, especially with encephalopathy.
  • - Fluid intake should be restricted to be less than urine output.
  • - In hypotension (systolic BP <90 mm Hg), nonselective beta-blockers may need to be held.
  • - For life-threatening hyponatremia or pre-transplant correction: Cautious 3% hypertonic saline use, avoiding rapid correction to prevent osmotic demyelination syndrome.
  • - Selective vasopressin antagonists (vaptans): Use has declined due to adverse events (hepatic injury, mortality); reserved mainly for short-term use in severe hyponatremia awaiting liver transplantation.
  • - In severe kidney injury, hemodialysis may help correct hyponatremia.

Best Practice Advice 11: Recurrent or Refractory Hyponatremia Management
  • - Indication: Recurrent or refractory hyponatremia in cirrhosis.
  • - Management should involve a multidisciplinary approach (including liver transplantation team).
  • - Therapeutic options: Intravenous vasoconstrictor therapy (e.g., terlipressin or norepinephrine), infusion of hypertonic saline, use of vasopressin receptor antagonist (vaptans), or use of renal replacement therapy.
  • - Recurrent hyponatremia: Redevelopment after a prior episode.
  • - Refractory hyponatremia: Persistence despite maximal therapy.
  • - Hyponatremia recurs in >50% of cases and is associated with poor survival.
  • - If transplantation is not an option, palliative care and hepatology teams can help address goals of care and symptom management.

Best Practice Advice 12: Inpatient Management of Volume Overload
  • - Indication: Anasarca with or without ascites or HH, especially when progressing despite outpatient diuretics or with persistent signs of anasarca.
  • - Management includes:
  • - Reversal of aggravating factors (e.g., alcohol, NSAIDs).
  • - Dietary sodium restriction.
  • - Medication review (including anti-hypertensives, especially RAS inhibitors).
  • - Diuresis: Escalation or trial of intravenous loop diuretics (furosemide or bumetanide) in bolus (2â€“3 times per day) or continuous fashion.
  • - Cautious escalation: Every 2â€“3 days with monitoring of volume status, kidney function, daily weights, and symptoms.
  • - IV furosemide is approximately twice as potent as oral; bumetanide may have 1:1 oral-to-intravenous equivalence.
  • - The role of concurrent intravenous albumin is unclear; targeting a serum albumin level does not reduce infection, kidney dysfunction, or death in patients not admitted specifically for volume overload.

Best Practice Advice 13: Advanced Strategies in Refractory Anasarca
  • - Indication: Inadequate response to initial diuretic therapy.
  • - Strategies should be coordinated with nephrology.
  • - Considerations:
  • - Diuretics in the setting of contraction alkalosis (e.g., addition of acetazolamide).
  • - Second agent with alternate mechanism of action (e.g., thiazide diuretics, such as metolazone).
  • - Need for ultrafiltration.
  • - Adverse events that may limit diuresis: Contraction alkalosis and electrolyte dysfunction.
  • - Elevation in serum creatinine is common but may not necessarily reflect worsening kidney function.
  • - In diuretic-resistant anasarca (no improvement despite maximal diuretics and persistently low urine sodium), advanced strategies include pressors to augment diuresis in the ICU.
  • - Ultrafiltration may be considered in select cases, especially for liver transplantation candidates.
  • - Transitional care on discharge (medication management, patient/caregiver education, timely follow-up) is important.

Conclusions and Future Directions
  • - Liver transplantation remains the only curative option.
  • - Strategies are needed to bridge patients to transplantation or improve quality of life for those without timely access.
  • - Investigational therapeutic trials are especially needed for patients with advanced steatotic liver disease.
  • - Further refinement of disease definitions is needed for earlier recognition and appropriate management.
  • - Gastroenterologists and hospitalists must know when and how to provide optimal care and timely hepatology referral to prevent higher mortality, readmissions, and poor outcomes.
