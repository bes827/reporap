ACG Clinical Guideline: Diagnosis and Management of Eosinophilic Esophagitis
  • Source: American College of Gastroenterology, Am J Gastroenterol 2025;120:31‑59
  • Authors: Evan S. Dellon MD, MPH, FACG et al.
1. Overview & Epidemiology
  • Chronic immune‑mediated esophageal disease → symptoms + ≥15 eos/hpf.
  • Incidence & prevalence ↑ rapidly (global meta‑analysis 2023).
  • Affects GI, allergy, ED, primary‑care settings; common cause of food impaction.
  • Diagnostic delay → “tip of the iceberg” of undetected cases.
1.1 Historical Shift
  • 2013 guideline required PPI trial → now eliminated (PPI‑REE no longer a diagnostic criterion).
  • New consensus (AGREE, 2018) defines EoE without PPI response requirement.
1.2 Risk Factors & Comorbidities
  • Atopic diseases (asthma, eczema, allergic rhinitis) present in 60‑80% of patients.
  • Family history ↑ risk; clustering observed in relatives.
  • Environmental/early‑life exposures (antibiotics, acid suppressants, rural living) linked to disease onset.
1.3 Clinical Burden
  • High health‑care utilization: endoscopies, biopsies, emergency visits for impactions.
  • Significant impact on quality of life, nutrition, growth (children).
2. Pathophysiology & Disease Mechanisms
  • Type 2 immune response triggered by food allergens → epithelial barrier breach.
  • Eosinophils are key effectors but not sole drivers; mast cells, Th2 cells, iNKT, basophils also infiltrate.
2.1 Cellular & Molecular Landscape
  • ↑ eotaxin‑3 (CCL26) expression → eosinophil recruitment (PPI may down‑regulate).
  • IL‑4/IL‑13 signaling via IL‑4Rα central; targeted by dupilumab.
  • STAT6, TGF‑β1, and periostin pathways contribute to fibrostenosis.
2.2 Tissue Remodeling & Fibrostenosis
  • Chronic inflammation → basal zone hyperplasia, dilated intercellular spaces, lamina propria fibrosis.
  • Esophageal wall thickening (submucosa + muscularis) leads to strictures/narrow caliber.
  • Phenotypic shift: inflammatory → fibrostenotic phenotype in majority without treatment.
2.3 Biomarkers & Histologic Scoring
  • Peak eosinophil count ≥15 eos/hpf diagnostic threshold (≈60 eos/mm²).
  • EoE Histologic Scoring System (EoEHSS): grades 8 features (eosinophils, basal hyperplasia, etc.).
  • Emerging markers: mast cell density, IgG4 levels, epigenetic methylation signatures (research stage).
3. Diagnosis of Eosinophilic Esophagitis
3.1 Core Diagnostic Criteria (≥ Low‑quality evidence; Strong recommendation)
  • Symptoms of esophageal dysfunction (age‑specific).
  • Peak eosinophil count ≥15 eos/hpf on esophageal biopsy.
  • Exclusion of other causes of esophageal eosinophilia (GERD, infections, etc.).
3.2 Symptom Assessment (Adults & Adolescents)
  • Dysphagia & food impaction most common; heartburn/chest pain may coexist.
  • “IMPACT” behaviors to elicit: Imbibe fluids, Modify foods, Prolong meals, Avoid hard textures, Chew excessively, Turn away pills.
3.3 Pediatric Symptom Nuances
  • Infants/young children: poor growth, feeding intolerance, prolonged meal times, vomiting/regurgitation.
  • School‑age: abdominal pain, reflux‑like symptoms; may mask dysphagia.
3.4 Endoscopic Evaluation (Strong recommendation)
  • Perform systematic endoscopy with EREFS scoring at every procedure.
  • Typical findings (≥1 of 5): edema, rings, exudates, furrows, strictures → not pathognomonic but raise suspicion.
3.4.1 EREFS Details
  • Scoring range 0‑9; each feature graded 0‑3 (worst area).
  • Validated for diagnosis & monitoring; correlates with treatment response.
3.5 Biopsy Protocol (Strong recommendation)
  • Obtain ≥6 biopsies from ≥2 esophageal levels (proximal + distal).
  • Target areas with endoscopic abnormalities when possible.
  • Diagnostic sensitivity approaches ≈100% with ≥6 biopsies; patchy eosinophilia otherwise missed.
3.6 Histologic Quantification (Strong recommendation)
  • Quantify peak eos/hpf on every biopsy set; report absolute count & density (eos/mm²).
  • Recognize variability by microscope field size; consider reporting density for consistency.
3.7 Ancillary Diagnostic Tools (Key concepts)
  • Clinical prediction models (e.g., IMPACT, I‑SEE) aid pre‑biopsy risk stratification.
  • Endoscopic ultrasonography & FLIP can assess submucosal thickening & distensibility but not routine.
3.8 Exclusion of Mimickers
  • Rule out GERD, eosinophilic gastroenteritis, infections, drug reactions, hypereosinophilic syndromes.
  • PPI trial no longer required; PPIs now a treatment option rather than diagnostic filter.
4. Initial Management Framework (Introductory Overview)
  • Goal: control inflammatory and fibrostenotic components simultaneously.
  • First‑line anti‑inflammatory choice based on shared decision‑making (patient preference, comorbidities, cost).
4.1 Therapeutic Options (Evidence grades)
Modality Evidence Quality Recommendation Strength
Proton Pump Inhibitors Low Conditional suggestion (“high‑dose” PPI)
Swallowed Topical Steroids (budesonide, fluticasone) Moderate Strong recommendation
Empiric Food Elimination Diets (1‑6 FED) Low Conditional suggestion
Dupilumab (≥12 y) Moderate Conditional suggestion
Esophageal Dilation (strictures) Low Conditional suggestion
  • Feeding therapy adjunct for children with feeding dysfunction or aversion.
4.2 Monitoring & Follow‑up (Strong recommendation)
  • Assess symptoms, endoscopy (EREFS), and histology at 8‑12 weeks after initiating any new therapy.
  • Use combined outcome (“deep remission”) when feasible: symptom resolution + EREFS ≤ 2 + eos < 15/hpf.

5. Pharmacologic Therapy
5.1 Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)
  • Indication: All ages, as anti‑inflammatory or adjunct.
  • Dosing: Adults → double standard reflux dose (e.g., omeprazole 40 mg BID).
  • Children → 2 mg/kg/day (or 1 mg/kg BID).
  • Mechanisms of Action
  • ↓ eotaxin‑3 expression → eosinophil recruitment.
  • Improves epithelial barrier integrity.
  • Modulates transcriptional homeostasis in esophageal epithelium.
  • Efficacy Data
  • Small RCTs: esomeprazole ↓ eos/hpf from ≈46 → 22 (eosinophil count).
  • Meta‑analysis (33 studies): clinical response ~61 %; histologic remission ~51 %.
  • Twice‑daily dosing superior to once‑daily (55.9 % vs 49.7 %).
  • Predictors of Response
  • Younger age, lower BMI, higher baseline eosinophils → nonresponse.
  • CYP2C19 rapid metabolism may reduce efficacy; dose escalation can overcome.
  • Presence of allergic rhinitis, STAT6 polymorphisms associated with poorer response.
  • Safety Profile
  • Long‑term data: no increased risk for CKD, dementia, pneumonia, fractures, COPD, diabetes.
  • ↑ risk of Clostridioides difficile infection only notable adverse event.
5.2 Swallowed Topical Steroids (STCs)
  • Formulations
  • Budesonide oral suspension (BOS) 2 mg BID (FDA‑approved 2024).
  • Budesonide orodispersible tablet (BOT) 1 mg BID (EMA‑approved 2018).
  • Fluticasone propionate inhaler powder swallowed from MDI (off‑label).
  • Administration Recommendations
  • Take after meals; avoid food/drink for 30–60 min → maximize esophageal dwell time.
  • For children, use slurry or viscous preparation rather than inhaler device.
  • Efficacy
  • Histologic remission (≤15 eos/hpf) 62‑95 % across phase 3 trials.
  • Symptom improvement variable; PROs often limited by placebo effect.
  • Meta‑analysis: overall histologic response ~65‑70 %.
  • Comparative Studies
  • RCT (OVB vs fluticasone MDI): histologic remission 71 % vs 64 % (no significant difference).
  • No head‑to‑head trials of BOS vs BOT; both achieve high remission rates.
  • Safety & Adverse Effects
  • Oral/esophageal candidiasis: BOS 3.8 %; BOT 23.7 % (often asymptomatic).
  • Management: intermittent topical antifungals, dose reduction, or temporary cessation.
  • Adrenal insufficiency rare (<5 % in induction trials); consider monitoring with long‑term high‑dose use.
  • Predictors of Nonresponse
  • Extremely narrow esophageal caliber, prior dilation, TGF‑β1 SNPs, methylation signatures.
5.3 Biologic Therapy – Dupilumab
  • Target Population
  • Age ≥12 y (adult/adolescent) or 1–11 y (pediatric) nonresponsive to PPIs.
  • Mechanism: IL‑4Rα blockade → inhibits IL‑4 & IL‑13 signaling (type 2 pathway).
  • Dosing
  • ≥40 kg: 300 mg SC weekly (adults).
  • 15–30 kg: 200 mg SC every other week (peds).
  • 30–40 kg: 300 mg SC every other week (peds).
  • Clinical Trial Outcomes
  • Histologic remission: 60 % dupilumab vs 5 % placebo (Phase 3, 24 wks).
  • Dysphagia score ↓21.9 points vs 9.6 placebo; significant improvement.
  • Endoscopic severity (EREFS) and histologic scores improved across dosing regimens.
  • Safety
  • Injection‑site reactions most common; rare arthralgias.
  • No increase in infections; no TB, HIV, hepatitis screening required.
  • Positioning in Algorithm
  • Consider early if comorbid atopic disease meets dupilumab indication (asthma, AD, CRSwNP).
5.4 Other Biologics & Small Molecules (Conditional Recommendations)
Agent Target Evidence Quality Recommendation
Cendakimab (anti‑IL‑13) IL‑13 Low/Phase 2 positive No recommendation (experimental).
Benralizumab, Lirentelimab (anti‑IL‑5Rα, anti‑Siglec‑8) Eosinophil depletion Low/Phase 2 histologic response ↑; symptom benefit modest Not recommended currently.
Omalizumab (anti‑IgE) IgE Low/No efficacy in RCTs Suggest against use.
Cromolyn, Montelukast (mast‑cell stabilizer, leukotriene antagonist) Various inflammatory mediators Very low/limited trials Suggest against use.

6. Dietary Elimination Strategies
6.1 Rationale & Mechanistic Basis
  • Food antigens drive type 2 inflammation in >90 % of patients.
  • Removal → ↓ eosinophilic infiltration, symptom relief, mucosal healing.
6.2 Empiric Elimination Diets (FED)
Diet Foods Excluded Efficacy (Histologic remission)
1‑Food Elimination (1FED) Dairy only 35‑45 %
2‑Food Elimination (2FED) Dairy + wheat 40‑45 %
4‑Food Elimination (4FED) Dairy, wheat, egg, soy 40‑50 %
6‑Food Elimination (6FED) Dairy, wheat, egg, soy, nuts/peanut, fish/seafood 40‑70 %
Elemental Formula Amino‑acid based hypoallergenic formula ~90 % (if adherent)
  • Step‑up approach: Start with least restrictive (1FED/2FED); progress to 4FED/6FED if no response.
  • Reintroduction Protocol
  • Add back one food/group every 6–8 weeks.
  • Perform endoscopy + biopsy after each reintroduction to assess eos/hpf.
  • “Washout” period (2‑6 wks) required after contamination before repeat scope.
6.3 Practical Implementation
  • Dietitian involvement mandatory for nutritional adequacy, label reading, meal planning.
  • Feeding therapist referral if avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder suspected.
  • Adherence challenges: Palatability (especially elemental), cost, multiple endoscopies.
  • “Diet holidays” may be employed during vacations or social events; resume medical therapy concurrently.
6.4 Allergy‑Testing–Guided Diets (Not Recommended)
  • Skin prick, patch, serum IgE/IgG4 tests have low sensitivity/specificity for identifying triggers.
  • Meta‑analyses: empiric FED superior to test‑guided diets.
  • Use allergy testing only for non‑EoE atopic comorbidities or when considering oral immunotherapy.
7. Esophageal Dilation
7.1 Indications
  • Symptomatic dysphagia due to strictures or narrow caliber esophagus confirmed endoscopically or radiographically.
  • Adjunctive to anti‑inflammatory therapy (does not treat underlying inflammation).
7.2 Technique & Protocol (Consensus Recommendations)
  • Goal diameter: ≥16 mm in adults/adolescents; individualized based on baseline lumen.
  • Approach: “Start low, go slow” – begin with dilator size slightly smaller than target, incrementally increase until mucosal disruption (“dilation effect”) observed.
  • Methods: Bougie (Savary‑Gilliard) or balloon dilation acceptable; choice per endoscopist preference and stricture characteristics.
7.3 Outcomes & Safety
  • Clinical improvement in ≈95 % of cases.
  • Perforation rate ~0.5 %; hospitalization <1 %.
  • Dilation reduces need for subsequent procedures when inflammation controlled (histologic remission).
7.4 Monitoring Post‑Dilation
  • Repeat endoscopy if dysphagia persists or recurs.
  • Continue anti‑inflammatory therapy to prevent re‑narrowing.
8. Maintenance Therapy & Long‑Term Monitoring
8.1 Rationale for Maintenance
  • EoE is chronic; cessation of treatment → near universal relapse (clinical, histologic, endoscopic).
  • Ongoing inflammation promotes fibrostenosis; maintenance mitigates progression.
8.2 Maintenance Strategies
8.2.1 Pharmacologic Maintenance
  • Topical Steroids
  • Continue induction dose or taper to lowest effective dose confirmed by repeat endoscopy/biopsy.
  • Randomized withdrawal trials: budesonide 0.25 mg BID maintains remission in ≈36 % vs 0 % placebo at 50 weeks.
  • PPIs
  • “High‑dose” regimen (double reflux dose) effective long‑term in 70‑85 % of patients (1‑3 y follow‑up).
  • Dupilumab
  • Sustained histologic & symptom remission up to 52 weeks; real‑world data show similar durability.
8.2.2 Dietary Maintenance
  • Continue successful elimination diet indefinitely or until trigger identified and permanently excluded.
  • For patients achieving remission after reintroduction, single‑food avoidance of identified triggers may suffice.
8.3 Monitoring Schedule (Strong recommendation)
Timepoint Assessment Components
Baseline (pre‑treatment) Symptoms, EREFS, peak eos/hpf, I‑SEE severity index.
8–12 weeks post‑initiation Repeat endoscopy + biopsies; symptom PROs; adjust therapy as needed.
Every 6–12 months (stable disease) Clinical review, consider surveillance endoscopy if dose changes or symptoms recur.
After dose reduction or treatment change Endoscopy within 8–12 weeks to confirm continued remission.
  • Symptom monitoring: Use validated PROs (e.g., Dysphagia Symptom Questionnaire, adult EoE‑QoL).
  • Endoscopic scoring: Aim for EREFS ≤ 2; complete normalization (EREFS 0) ideal but not required.
  • Histologic target: <15 eos/hpf (≈60 eos/mm²); consider stricter cutoffs (<5 eos/hpf) in research settings.
8.4 Non‑Invasive Monitoring Options (Emerging)
  • Transnasal unsedated endoscopy – feasible in children, reduces anesthesia exposure.
  • Cytosponge – sensitivity 75 %, specificity 86 % for active disease; useful for diet reintroduction monitoring.
  • Esophageal String Test – measures eotaxin‑3 & major basic protein; AUC 0.83 for active vs inactive disease.
  • Mucosal impedance & EndoFLIP – correlate with eosinophilia and distensibility; currently limited to specialized centers.
8.5 Monitoring in Special Populations
8.5.1 Pediatric Patients
  • Growth (height, weight, BMI) tracked alongside disease activity.
  • Feeding therapy essential for children with aversive eating behaviors or ARFID.
  • Endoscopic surveillance performed under sedation; consider transnasal approach when feasible.
8.5.2 Adults with Fibrostenotic Phenotype
  • Baseline esophagram or FLIP to assess lumen diameter before dilation.
  • More frequent endoscopic assessment (every 6‑9 months) due to higher stricture risk.

9. Pediatric‑Specific Considerations
9.1 Diagnostic Nuances in Children
  • Symptom Spectrum varies by age:
  • Infants → poor growth, feeding intolerance, prolonged meals.
  • Toddlers/Preschool → vomiting, regurgitation, food refusal.
  • School‑age → abdominal pain, dysphagia, heartburn.
  • IMPACT Behaviors (Table 4) essential to uncover concealed dysphagia; children may mask symptoms via avoidance.
9.1.1 Endoscopic Evaluation
  • Perform under general anesthesia or unsedated transnasal scope (preferred for repeated monitoring).
  • Obtain ≥6 biopsies from proximal & distal esophagus; targeting visual abnormalities when present.
9.1.2 Imaging Adjuncts
  • Esophagram recommended in dysphagia to detect strictures not apparent endoscopically (detected in 55 % of cases with normal endoscopy).
  • EndoFLIP may be used in specialized centers for assessing distensibility, especially when radiation avoidance is desired.
9.2 Growth, Development & Nutrition Goals
  • Primary endpoints: linear growth, weight gain, and BMI trajectory alongside symptom control.
  • Dietitian involvement mandatory for all children on elimination diets to prevent macro‑/micronutrient deficiencies.
  • Monitor serum ferritin, pre‑albumin, vitamin D, PTH periodically; supplement as needed.
9.3 Feeding Therapy & Behavioral Interventions
  • Up to 16.5 % of pediatric EoE patients have feeding disorders; 93.9 % exhibit maladaptive behaviors (e.g., prolonged meals, excessive chewing).
  • Multidisciplinary program: gastroenterology + dietetics + speech‑language pathology + psychology.
  • Positive reinforcement techniques improve oral intake; avoid negative stimuli (pain, dysphagia) during therapy sessions.
9.4 Pharmacologic Therapy in Children
9.4.1 Swallowed Topical Steroids
  • Budesonide oral viscous slurry (1–2 mg/day) or fluticasone MDI (110‑880 µg/day) effective; dosing weight‑adjusted.
  • Monitor for oral candidiasis; treat with topical antifungals if symptomatic.
9.4.2 Proton Pump Inhibitors
  • High‑dose regimen (double standard dose) used when steroids contraindicated or as adjunct.
9.4.3 Dupilumab (Ages 1–11)
  • FDA‑approved for ≥15 kg; dosing every other week based on weight bands.
  • Demonstrated histologic remission ≈68 % (high dose) and growth improvement over 52 weeks.
9.5 Dietary Management in Children
9.5.1 Empiric Elimination Diets
  • 1‑FED (dairy) often first line due to high prevalence of milk trigger; remission rates ≈35‑45 %.
  • 2‑FED (dairy + wheat) modestly higher response; consider if 1‑FED fails.
  • 4‑FED / 6‑FED reserved for refractory disease or when multiple triggers suspected.
9.5.2 Elemental Formula
  • Reserved for infants, severe refractory cases, or when oral intake impossible.
  • Requires feeding tube in many patients; high cost and adherence challenges.
9.5.3 Reintroduction Protocol
  • After 6‑8 weeks of diet, reintroduce foods sequentially (single food per interval).
  • Perform endoscopy after each reintroduction to assess eos/hpf; if remission persists, the food is deemed non‑triggering.
9.6 Monitoring & Follow‑up in Pediatrics
Interval Assessments
Baseline Height/weight/BMI percentiles, symptom questionnaire, EREFS, biopsies.
8–12 weeks post‑therapy start Repeat endoscopy + biopsies; growth parameters; diet adherence check.
Every 6 months (stable) Clinical review, growth tracking; consider non‑invasive monitoring (transnasal scope).
After dose reduction or therapy change Endoscopy within 8–12 weeks to confirm continued remission.
  • Adrenal insufficiency rare but monitor cortisol if high‑dose steroids >6 months.
  • Vaccination considerations: live vaccines deferred during systemic steroid bursts; otherwise routine schedule maintained.
10. Monitoring of Treatment Response & Outcome Metrics
10.1 Composite “Deep Remission” Concept
  • Definition: Symptom resolution + EREFS ≤ 2 + peak eos < 15/hpf (≈60 eos/mm²).
  • Achievable in ~9 % of patients; associated with lower relapse rates but requires intensive therapy.
10.2 Symptom Assessment Tools
Tool Population Domains
Dysphagia Symptom Questionnaire (DSQ) Adults/Adolescents Frequency & severity of dysphagia, food impaction.
Adult EoE‑QoL Adults Health‑related quality of life across 4 domains.
Pediatric Quality of Life Inventory – EoE module Children ≥5 y Symptom burden, emotional impact, social functioning.
IMPACT behavior checklist All ages Specific dysphagia‑related coping behaviors.
  • PROs should be collected at each visit; correlate modestly with endoscopic/histologic findings (r≈0.3‑0.4).
10.3 Endoscopic Scoring – EREFS
  • Scoring components: Edema, Rings, Exudates, Furrows, Strictures (0‑3 each).
  • Interpretation: Total 0–9; response threshold often set at ≤2 or ≥2‑point reduction from baseline.
10.4 Histologic Evaluation
Parameter Threshold for Remission
Peak eos/hpf <15 (≈60 eos/mm²)
EoEHSS total grade ↓ by ≥1 point in ≥50 % of features
Basal zone hyperplasia, lamina propria fibrosis Absent or minimal (optional for deep remission).
  • Report absolute eos count and density; note any additional histologic features (e.g., basal hyperplasia) that may indicate ongoing activity despite low eos.

10.6 Timing of Monitoring
  • Initial response: endoscopy + biopsy at 8‑12 weeks after therapy initiation (PPIs, STCs, FED).
  • Biologic agents (dupilumab): consider 12‑24 weeks for first assessment due to longer onset.
  • Maintenance phase: repeat endoscopy every 12‑24 months if stable; sooner if symptoms recur or dose changes occur.
11. Algorithmic Treatment Pathway (Figure 3 Summary)

1. Confirm Diagnosis → Symptoms + ≥15 eos/hpf + exclusion of other causes.

2. Assess Phenotype: Inflammatory vs fibrostenotic (stricture, narrow caliber).

3. Shared Decision‑Making → Choose anti‑inflammatory modality:

  • PPIs (high‑dose) OR
  • Swallowed Topical Steroid (BOS/BOT or fluticasone) OR
  • Empiric Elimination Diet (1FED→2FED→4FED→6FED).

4. Concurrent Management of Fibrostenosis:

  • If stricture present → endoscopic dilation (start low, go slow).

5. 8‑12 Week Assessment → Symptoms, EREFS, eos/hpf.

  • Responder: Continue therapy; consider dose taper if stable.
  • Partial/Non‑Responder: Switch modality (e.g., STC ↔ PPI ↔ diet) or add dupilumab.

6. Maintenance Therapy → Ongoing anti‑inflammatory agent at lowest effective dose + periodic monitoring.

12. Future Directions & Knowledge Gaps
Gap Research Priority
Comparative effectiveness of first‑line therapies (PPI vs STC vs diet) Head‑to‑head RCTs with standardized outcomes.
Predictors of treatment response (genetic, epigenetic, microbiome) Development of personalized algorithms incorporating biomarkers.
Phenotype/endotype classification (inflammatory vs fibrostenotic) Validation of I‑SEE and FLIP metrics for risk stratification.
Non‑invasive monitoring tools Large‑scale validation of Cytosponge, string test, mucosal impedance.
Long‑term safety of biologics in pediatric population Extended follow‑up registries for growth, immunogenicity, infection risk.
Cost‑effectiveness analyses across treatment modalities Incorporate drug costs, diet adherence resources, endoscopy frequency.
Role of combination therapy (e.g., PPI + STC) Trials to assess additive benefit vs monotherapy.
Novel therapeutic targets (TSLP, IL‑15, mast‑cell stabilizers) Phase 2/3 trials for emerging agents (tezepelumab, barzovolimab).
  • Implementation Science: Need strategies to reduce diagnostic delay, educate primary‑care providers, and standardize biopsy protocols.
  • Patient‑Centric Outcomes: Emphasize quality‑of‑life metrics, feeding behavior interventions, and shared decision‑making frameworks.
