Definition, Diagnosis, and Treatment of Primary Biliary Cholangitis - Autoimmune Hepatitis (PBC-AIH) Variant: An International Expert Delphi Consensus
  • Source: Hepatology - American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases, Vol. XX
  • Topic: Autoimmune Liver Disease - PBC-AIH Variant Syndrome Diagnosis and Management
  • Author(s): Gerussi A, Sebode M, Nofit E, Stoelinga A, Bernasconi D, Leburgue A, Invernizzi P, Schramm C, Van Hoek B, Tiniakos D, Terracciano L, Carbone M, Lohse AW
1. Overview and Methodology
  • 74 hepatologists + 14 liver pathologists participated across 3 Delphi rounds
  • Geographic distribution: Europe (51), Asia (16-19), North America (13), Oceania (3-4)
  • 88 contributions in first round, 82 in second round, 51 in third (nomenclature) round
  • Consensus threshold: >80% agreement on 9-point Likert scale (1=disagree to 9=agree)
  • Disagreement index calculated using RAND/UCLA methodology
  • Endorsed by ERN RARE-LIVER, Global PBC Study Group, IAIHG, in partnership with European Society of Pathology
1.1 Key Consensus Achieved
  • PBC-AIH represents a variant of either PBC or AIH (not overlap)
  • Features may present simultaneously or sequentially
  • Diagnosis requires multidimensional approach (biochemistry + serology + histology)
  • Liver biopsy mandatory in specific diagnostic contexts
  • PBC-AIH has worse prognosis than PBC alone unless treated with immunosuppression
2. Definition and Nomenclature
  • Final consensus term: "PBC-AIH" (Primary Biliary Cholangitis - Autoimmune Hepatitis)
  • 64% voted for this term in third dedicated round
  • Alphabetical ordering not intended to give primacy to either disease
  • Panel rejected: "overlap syndrome," "continuous spectrum," "simultaneous presence of both diseases"
2.1 Conceptual Framework
  • Overlap --> patient meets criteria for multiple distinct conditions (e.g., mixed connective tissue disease)
  • Variant --> subtype of single disease where one condition predominates but presents atypically
  • Continuous spectrum --> challenges rigid classifications, proposes gradual transition between disease states
  • Clinical practice shows either PBC or AIH dominates with some features of the other
3. Presentation Patterns
  • Features of AIH and PBC may present simultaneously or sequentially
  • Sequential presentation: patient initially diagnosed with one disease develops features of the other over time
  • Periodic reassessment of diagnosis is essential as phenotype may evolve
3.1 Suspicion Triggers in PBC Patients
  • Primary trigger: Incomplete response to UDCA after 12 months + disproportionate elevation of transaminases vs. cholestasis
  • Agreement: 82.9% rated this appropriate (first round consensus)
  • No consensus on suspicion criteria at initial PBC diagnosis
  • Hepatologists showed 79.5% agreement vs. only 50% among pathologists for diagnostic criteria at diagnosis
3.2 Suspicion Triggers in AIH Patients
  • Positive AMA, gp210, or sp100 + disproportionate ALP elevation --> suspect PBC-AIH
  • 84.8% rated this appropriate (consensus achieved)
  • ALP elevation must be disproportionate to other markers
3.3 R Ratio for Biochemical Classification
  • R ratio = (ALT ÷ ULN) ÷ (ALP ÷ ULN)
  • R >5 --> hepatocellular injury pattern
  • R <2 --> cholestatic injury pattern
4. Diagnostic Criteria and Approach
  • 95.1% consensus: Diagnosis should be based on combination of laboratory, serological, AND histological findings
  • Single-parameter or strict criteria approaches not approved
  • Previous diagnostic criteria (Paris Criteria, Zhang Criteria) did not achieve consensus
4.1 Diagnostic Parameters Required
  • Concurrent elevation of transaminases AND cholestatic markers
  • Increased IgG levels
  • Presence of relevant autoantibodies (ANA/SMA/SLA-LP in AIH context; AMA/gp210/sp100 in PBC context)
  • Compatible histopathological features
4.2 Rejected Diagnostic Approaches
  • Clinical judgment alone --> not approved
  • Histology alone --> not approved
  • Response to therapy as diagnostic criterion --> 58.8% hepatologists + 50% pathologists approved (below threshold)
  • AIH diagnostic criteria for PBC-AIH diagnosis --> high disagreement
5. Liver Biopsy - Mandatory Role
  • 93.5% consensus: Liver biopsy mandatory in patients with established PBC + clinical/biochemical features suggestive of PBC-AIH
  • 100% of pathologists and 92% of hepatologists agreed
5.1 In Established PBC Patients
  • Confirms diagnosis and guides management decisions
  • Mandatory when suspicion raised by incomplete UDCA response + disproportionate transaminases
5.2 In Established AIH Patients
  • 87.8% consensus: Re-assessment of original liver biopsy should be performed BEFORE new biopsy
  • Context: disproportionate chronic cholestasis + AMA/gp210/sp100 positivity
  • Avoids unnecessary procedures
  • Determines if PBC features were present but previously overlooked
5.3 Pathologist vs. Hepatologist Disagreement
  • For re-biopsy in AIH patients: 14/14 pathologists judged appropriate vs. only 58.8% of hepatologists
  • Highlights need for multidisciplinary discussion
6. Diagnostic Histological Features - Pathologist Consensus
  • All statements in Table 3 reached consensus among pathologists only
  • Hepatologists showed high disagreement on specific histological thresholds
6.1 In Patients with Established AIH (Pathologist Consensus)
  • Florid bile duct lesions (non-suppurative destructive granulomatous cholangitis): supportive but NOT required
  • Portal granulomas: supportive but NOT required
  • Bile duct loss: supportive but NOT required
  • Periportal copper accumulation (chronic cholestasis): supportive but NOT required
  • Keratin 7-positive intermediate periportal hepatocytes: supportive but NOT required
6.2 In Patients with Established PBC (Pathologist Consensus)
  • Interface hepatitis grade ≥moderate (≥2/4 Ishak HAI) required for diagnosis
  • 85.7% consensus among pathologists
  • Severe interface hepatitis (≥3/4 Ishak HAI) did NOT reach consensus as required threshold
  • Any grade of interface hepatitis insufficient for diagnosis (78.6% rated inappropriate)
  • Absence of interface hepatitis alone does NOT exclude variant syndrome
6.3 Lobular Hepatitis Grading (Pathologist Consensus)
  • Grade ≥moderate required for diagnosis in PBC patients
  • Defined as: ≥2/4 focal lytic necrosis AND/OR ≥2/6 confluent necrosis (Ishak HAI)
  • 71.4% of pathologists agreed
  • Severe lobular hepatitis (≥3/4 or ≥4/6) did NOT reach consensus
7. Treatment Considerations
  • 93.5% consensus: Benefit must outweigh risk/side effects considering disease severity, stage, demographics, comorbidities, patient preferences
  • No strict timing endorsed for immunosuppression initiation
7.1 Indication for Immunosuppressive Therapy in PBC
  • Severe interface hepatitis (≥3/4 Ishak HAI) --> indication for immunosuppression
  • 80.5% consensus
  • Moderate interface hepatitis (≥2/4 Ishak HAI) --> NOT consensus as threshold
  • Any grade of interface hepatitis --> both categories deemed inappropriate to start immunosuppression
  • Pathologists showed 92.9% agreement that any grade is insufficient; hepatologists more divided (13.2% appropriate, 17.6% uncertain)
7.2 Treatment Endpoints
  • Should align with most recent AIH guidelines --> 90.2% consensus
  • Should align with most recent PBC guidelines + local practice --> 95.1% consensus
7.3 Choice of Immunosuppressive Drug
  • Should follow AIH guidelines + local practice --> 85.9% consensus
  • Only statement with statistically significant hepatologist vs. pathologist difference (p<0.023)
  • 89.7% of hepatologists supported vs. only 64.3% of pathologists
7.4 Clinical Decision Factors
  • Severity of interface hepatitis (ideally confirmed by experienced hepatopathologist)
  • Disease stage
  • Age
  • Comorbidities
  • Patient preference
8. Prognosis and Natural History
  • 91.5% consensus: Untreated PBC-AIH has worse prognosis than PBC alone
  • Higher rates of fibrosis progression
  • Greater risk of hepatic decompensation
  • Untreated inflammatory AIH component contributes to accelerated liver damage
8.1 Treatment Impact
  • No consensus reached on role of treatment on prognosis (Statements 33-34)
  • Recent data: PBC with moderate-to-severe interface hepatitis shows prognostic advantage with UDCA + immunosuppression combination
  • Complete biochemical response (CBR) as treatment endpoint
9. Clinical Workflow Summary
  • See Figure 2 (AIH-dominant presentation) and Figure 3 (PBC-dominant presentation)
9.1 AIH-Dominant Pathway
  • Suspicion at diagnosis or follow-up
  • Trigger: disproportionate ALP elevation + AMA/gp210/sp100 positivity
  • If AIH not yet diagnosed --> liver biopsy mandatory
  • If AIH established --> re-review original biopsy before new biopsy
  • Compatible histology --> diagnosis confirmed
  • Start UDCA + immunosuppression
  • Re-evaluate diagnosis over time
9.2 PBC-Dominant Pathway
  • Suspicion at diagnosis or follow-up
  • Trigger: disproportionate AST/ALT vs. cholestatic markers
  • Criteria at diagnosis still lacking (poor international agreement)
  • After UDCA 12 months: incomplete response + disproportionate transaminases
  • Liver biopsy always required
  • Compatible histology --> diagnosis confirmed
  • Start UDCA + immunosuppression
  • Re-evaluate diagnosis over time

11. International Guideline Comparison
  • EASL AIH 2025: Variant terminology endorsed; Paris criteria may be helpful but strict
  • EASL PBC 2017: Variant terminology endorsed; 8-10% estimated prevalence
  • AASLD AIH 2020: Overlap terminology used; Paris criteria may underestimate prevalence
  • AASLD PBC 2018: Overlap terminology used; liver biopsy if transaminases >5×ULN
  • BSG AIH 2011: 8% of AIH; no formal definition
  • BSG PBC 2018: True overlap based on Paris criteria rare (<2%)
  • APASL AIH 2021: Overlap terminology; most cases require UDCA + IS
  • APASL PBC 2022: Paris criteria too stringent for Asia-Pacific; IgG cutoff reduced to 1.3×ULN
12. Research Agenda (Unmet Needs)
  • Validation of diagnostic criteria in diverse populations
  • Biomarker development (non-invasive markers for PBC-AIH)
  • Prognostic predictors stratified by histology and biochemistry
  • Optimal treatment duration
  • Preferred immunosuppressive agents
  • Role of second-line PBC therapies (PPAR/FXR agonists) in variant syndrome
13. Clinical Implications
  • Multidisciplinary discussion required (hepatologists + pathologists + patients)
  • Management preferably in specialized centers
  • Early recognition and timely intervention improves outcomes
  • Periodic reassessment essential as features can occur sequentially
  • Treatment must balance inflammatory component (AIH) with cholestatic component (PBC)
