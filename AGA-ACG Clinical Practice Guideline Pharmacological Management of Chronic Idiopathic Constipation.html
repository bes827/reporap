Executive Summary & Introduction
  • - Chronic Idiopathic Constipation (CIC) is a common disorder of gut-brain interaction affecting 8%–12% of the US population.
  • - Associated with infrequent/incomplete defecation without structural abnormalities.
  • - Medical costs are estimated between $2,000 and $7,500 per patient per year.
Initial Management
  • - Nonpharmacological therapies are often the initial steps.
  • - Includes dietary changes (increased fluid and fiber) and behavioral changes (exercise).


Fiber Supplementation
  • - Recommendation 1: Suggests the use of fiber supplementation over no supplements.
  • - Strength: Conditional
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Low
Implementation Considerations
  • - Can be used as first-line therapy, especially for individuals with low dietary fiber intake.
  • - Among evaluated supplements, only psyllium appears effective.
  • - Flatulence is a commonly observed side effect.
  • - The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics recommends 14 g/1,000 kcal intake per day.

Types of Fiber
  • - Soluble fiber: Traps water, softens stool (e.g., psyllium, inulin, oats).
  • - Insoluble fiber: Increases stool bulk (e.g., wheat bran, methylcellulose).
Bran
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Very Low
  • - Based on one small crossover study (n=29).
  • - May increase Spontaneous Bowel Movements (SBMs) per week, but with a wide confidence interval (MD 1.30).

Inulin
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Very Low to Low
  • - Had little to no effect on SBMs per week (MD 0.75).
  • - A minor side effect of flatulence occurred more frequently in the inulin group.

Psyllium
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Low
  • - May lead to an increase in SBMs per week (MD 2.32).
  • - May increase global relief symptoms (RR 1.86).
  • - Studied dose was 5 g twice daily.



Osmotic Laxatives
  • - These agents work by creating an osmotic gradient, drawing water into the intestinal lumen.
Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)
  • - Recommendation 2: Recommends the use of PEG.
  • - Strength: Strong
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Moderate
Implementation & Dosing
  • - Response to PEG has been shown to be durable over 6 months.
  • - Standard dose is 17 g daily.
  • - Side effects include abdominal distension, loose stool, flatulence, and nausea.

Efficacy
  • - Likely results in an increase in Complete Spontaneous Bowel Movements (CSBMs) per week (MD 2.90).
  • - Increases SBMs per week (MD 2.30).
  • - Associated with a higher responder rate (RR 3.13).


Magnesium Oxide (MgO)
  • - Recommendation 3: Suggests the use of MgO.
  • - Strength: Conditional
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Very Low
Implementation & Dosing
  • - The panel suggests starting at a lower dose (e.g., 400–500 mg daily).
  • - Avoid use in patients with renal insufficiency due to risk of hypermagnesemia.

Efficacy
  • - Studied dose was 1.5 g/d for 4 weeks.
  • - May increase CSBMs per week (MD 4.29) and SBMs per week (MD 3.59).
  • - Achieved a higher treatment response compared to placebo (RR 3.93).


Lactulose
  • - Recommendation 4: Suggests use in adults who fail or are intolerant to other OTC therapies.
  • - Strength: Conditional
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Very Low
Implementation & Dosing
  • - Bloating and flatulence are dose-dependent and common side effects, which may limit its use.
  • - FDA-approved dose is 10–20 g (15–30 mL) daily, may be increased to 40 g daily.

Efficacy
  • - May have little to no effect on SBMs per week.
  • - May be associated with a large increase in global relief (RR 2.42).



Stimulant Laxatives
  • - These agents stimulate colonic peristalsis and secretion.
Bisacodyl or Sodium Picosulfate (SPS)
  • - Recommendation 5: Recommends use for short-term or as rescue therapy.
  • - Strength: Strong
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Moderate
Implementation & Dosing
  • - Short-term use is defined as daily use for 4 weeks or less.
  • - Common side effects: abdominal pain, cramping, diarrhea.
  • - Panel suggests starting at a lower dose (e.g., 5 mg orally).

Mechanism & Onset
  • - Both are converted to the same active metabolite, BHPM.
  • - Onset of action: 6–12 hours for oral tablet, 30–60 minutes for rectal suppository.

Efficacy
  • - Leads to a large increase in CSBMs per week (MD 2.54) and SBMs per week (MD 4.04).
  • - Higher responder rates (RR 2.60).
  • - May increase diarrhea compared to placebo (RR 8.76).


Senna
  • - Recommendation 6: Suggests the use of senna.
  • - Strength: Conditional
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Low
Implementation & Dosing
  • - The dose evaluated in the single trial (1 g) is higher than commonly used doses.
  • - Panel suggests starting at a lower dose.
  • - Abdominal pain and cramping may occur with higher doses.

Efficacy
  • - Based on one placebo-controlled RCT.
  • - Participants had higher CSBMs per week (MD 7.60) and SBMs per week (MD 7.6).
  • - Response rate was higher in the senna group (RR 5.25).
  • - Notably, 83% of participants randomized to senna reduced their daily dose during the trial.



Secretagogues
  • - These agents increase intestinal fluid secretion, accelerating GI transit.
  • - Recommended for patients who do not respond to OTC agents.
Lubiprostone
  • - Recommendation 7: Suggests the use of lubiprostone.
  • - Strength: Conditional
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Low
Mechanism & Dosing
  • - Acts on chloride channel type 2.
  • - Dose for CIC: 24 mg BID.
  • - Dose for IBS-C: 8 mg BID.

Implementation & Side Effects
  • - Nausea is the most common adverse event, observed in 35% of individuals.
  • - Nausea led to discontinuation of therapy in only 5% of individuals.
  • - Risk of nausea is dose-dependent and lower when taken with food and water.

Efficacy
  • - Resulted in an increased number of SBMs per week (MD 1.98).
  • - May increase responder rates (RR 1.67).
  • - Increased risk of diarrhea leading to discontinuation (RR 5.30).


Linaclotide
  • - Recommendation 8: Recommends the use of linaclotide.
  • - Strength: Strong
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Moderate
Mechanism & Dosing
  • - Acts on guanylate cyclase-C.
  • - Dose for CIC: 72 mg or 145 mg daily.
  • - Dose for IBS-C: 290 mg daily.

Implementation & Side Effects
  • - Should be taken at least 30 minutes before the first meal of the day.
  • - May be associated with diarrhea leading to discontinuation.

Efficacy
  • - Increases CSBMs per week (MD 1.37) and SBMs per week (MD 1.97).
  • - May lead to a large increase in responder rates (RR 3.14).
  • - Patients are 3 times more likely to have diarrhea leading to discontinuation (RR 3.35).


Plecanatide
  • - Recommendation 9: Recommends the use of plecanatide.
  • - Strength: Strong
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Moderate
Mechanism & Dosing
  • - Acts on guanylate cyclase-C.
  • - Dose for CIC and IBS-C: 3 mg daily.
  • - Can be taken with or without food.

Efficacy & Side Effects
  • - Increases CSBMs per week (MD 1.1) and SBMs per week (MD 1.66).
  • - Increased responder rates (RR 1.78).
  • - Higher rates of diarrhea leading to discontinuation (RR 5.39).



5-HT4 Agonist
  • - Recommended for patients who do not respond to OTC agents.
Prucalopride
  • - Recommendation 10: Recommends the use of prucalopride.
  • - Strength: Strong
  • - Certainty of Evidence: Moderate
Mechanism & Dosing
  • - A selective, high-affinity serotonin type 4 (5-HT4) agonist.
  • - Recommended dose is 2 mg once daily.
  • - Dose is reduced to 1 mg daily in individuals with severe renal impairment (creatinine clearance <30 mL/min).

Side Effects & Safety
  • - Most frequent side effects are headache, abdominal pain, nausea, and diarrhea.
  • - These side effects are generally transient, occurring within the first week and resolving in a few days.
  • - Label cautions about unusual changes in mood/behavior and suicidal ideation, but a causal link is unclear.

Efficacy
  • - Resulted in an increased number of CSBMs per week (MD 0.96).
  • - Responder rates were higher in the prucalopride group (RR 2.37).
  • - Improved quality of life scores (PAC-QOL).
