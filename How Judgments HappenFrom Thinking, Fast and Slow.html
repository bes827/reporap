How Judgments Happen
From 'Thinking, Fast and Slow'
  • - This mind map summarizes the cognitive mechanisms behind intuitive judgments, focusing on the interplay between System 1 and System 2.

Core Concepts: System 1 & System 2
  • - System 1: Operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control.
  • - Continuously generates assessments (basic assessments) without specific intention.
  • - System 2: Allocates attention to effortful mental activities, including complex computations.
  • - Receives or generates questions, then directs attention and memory search to find answers.
The Heuristics and Biases Approach
  • - The essential idea is that basic assessments from System 1 are easily substituted for more difficult questions posed to System 2.


Basic Assessments by System 1
  • - System 1 provides a continuous, effortless assessment of the environment to answer crucial survival questions.
  • - Examples: Is there a threat? Is everything normal? Should I approach or avoid?
  • - In humans, this manifests as assessments of safety and familiarity, which are linked to good mood and cognitive ease.
Judging Strangers: The Todorov Study
  • - We have an evolved ability to evaluate a stranger's face at a glance for two key facts:
  • - 1. Dominance (potential threat) ---> Cued by face shape (e.g., a 'strong' square chin).
  • - 2. Trustworthiness (intentions) ---> Cued by facial expression (e.g., smile or frown).
Political Implications: Facial Competence
  • - Todorov found that ratings of competence were highly predictive of voting outcomes.
  • - In about 70% of races for senator, congressman, and governor, the winner was the candidate whose face was rated higher in competence.
  • - This finding was replicated in Finland, England, Australia, Germany, and Mexico.
Defining 'Competence' in Faces
  • - People judge competence by combining two dimensions: strength and trustworthiness.
  • - Competent-looking faces combine a strong chin with a slight, confident-appearing smile.

Voter Susceptibility
  • - The effect of facial competence on voting is about three times larger for politically uninformed voters who watch a great deal of television.
  • - This shows that the relative importance of System 1 in decisions varies among individuals.



Assessments of Sets & Prototypes
  • - System 1 excels at computing averages and prototypes automatically, but is poor at computing sums.
Example: Average vs. Total Length
  • - People can instantly and accurately assess the average length of a set of lines (Figure 8).
  • - This is done automatically by System 1, even when cognitively busy.
  • - However, calculating the total length requires laborious effort from System 2 (estimate average x count lines).

Example: The Exxon Valdez Study
  • - Participants were asked their willingness to pay to save migratory birds from oil ponds.
  • - Willingness to pay to save 2,000, 20,000, or 200,000 birds was $80, $78, and $88 respectively.
  • - This demonstrates neglect of quantity (a sum-like variable).
  • - People reacted to the prototype image (a single drowning bird), not the number of birds.



Key System 1 Mechanisms
  • - These are underlying processes that enable heuristics and intuitive judgments.
Intensity Matching
  • - System 1 has the ability to translate values across different dimensions based on an underlying scale of intensity.
  • - This allows matching concepts like crime severity to colors (murder = deep red) or loudness (mass murder = fortissimo).
Example: 'Julie's Precocity'
  • - The statement: "Julie read fluently when she was four years old."
  • - People can intuitively match this level of precocity to other intensity scales:
  • - A man's height (e.g., more than 6' but less than 7').
  • - A corresponding income level or GPA.
  • - This matching is a key component of how we make predictions, even if it's statistically wrong.


Mental Shotgun
  • - An intention by System 2 to answer a specific question automatically triggers excess computations in System 1.
  • - It's difficult for System 1 not to do more than it's asked to do, like a shotgun scattering pellets.
Experiment: Rhyming Words
  • - Task: Press a key if two heard words rhyme.
  • - Pairs: VOTE-NOTE (rhyme, same spelling) vs. VOTE-GOAT (rhyme, different spelling).
  • - Result: Participants were distinctly slower to identify rhyming words with discrepant spellings.
  • - Conclusion: The irrelevant attribute (spelling) was computed automatically and interfered with the main task (sound comparison).

Experiment: Literal vs. Metaphorical Truth
  • - Task: Judge if sentences are literally true or false.
  • - Sentences: "Some jobs are snakes" and "Some jobs are jails" are metaphorically true but literally false.
  • - Result: People took longer to identify these as false compared to a sentence like "Some roads are snakes."
  • - Conclusion: The irrelevant metaphorical meaning was computed and created conflict, disrupting performance.



Substitution: Answering an Easier Question
  • - A core mechanism for generating intuitive opinions on complex matters.
  • - If a satisfactory answer to a hard question is not found quickly, System 1 finds a related, easier question and answers that instead.
Terminology
  • - Target Question: The assessment you intend to produce (the hard question).
  • - Heuristic Question: The simpler question that you answer instead.

The Process of Substitution
  • - 1. A difficult Target Question is posed.
  • - 2. The Mental Shotgun automatically generates answers to easier, related questions.
  • - 3. Intensity Matching maps the answer from the easy question onto the scale of the target question.
  • - 4. A lazy System 2 often endorses this heuristic answer without much scrutiny.
Examples of Substitution (Table 1)
  • - Target: How much to contribute to save a species? ---> Heuristic: How much emotion do I feel about dying dolphins?
  • - Target: How happy are you with your life? ---> Heuristic: What is my mood right now?
  • - Target: How far will this political candidate go? ---> Heuristic: Does this woman look like a political winner?



Specific Heuristics Based on Substitution
  • - Concrete examples of how substitution leads to predictable biases in judgment.
The 3-D Heuristic
  • - When viewing a 2-D image with perspective cues, our perceptual system automatically interprets it as a 3-D scene.
  • - This leads to a powerful illusion where judgment of 2-D size is dictated by the impression of 3-D size.
Example: Figures in a Corridor (Figure 9)
  • - Target Question: As printed on the page, is the figure on the right larger than the figure on the left?
  • - Heuristic Question: How tall are the three people in the 3-D scene?
  • - Result: The figure on the right appears much larger, even though on the page they are the exact same size.
  • - The bias is that objects appearing more distant are perceived as larger on the page.


The Mood Heuristic for Happiness
  • - When asked about general happiness, people may substitute an answer about their current mood or a salient aspect of their life.
Experiment: German Student Survey
  • - Order 1: 1. How happy are you? 2. How many dates did you have?
  • - Result: The correlation between happiness and dating was about zero.
  • - Order 2: 1. How many dates did you have? 2. How happy are you?
  • - Result: The correlation was as high as correlations between psychological measures can get.
  • - Conclusion: The emotion aroused by the dating question (the heuristic) was substituted for an evaluation of overall life happiness (the target).


The Affect Heuristic
  • - Proposed by Paul Slovic, this is when people let their likes and dislikes (affect) determine their beliefs about the world.
  • - It represents the dominance of conclusions over arguments, especially when emotions are involved.
Mechanism & Examples
  • - If you like an activity (e.g., current health policy), you believe its benefits are substantial and its costs are manageable.
  • - If you dislike something (e.g., nuclear power, red meat), you believe its risks are high and its benefits are negligible.
  • - Information about lower risks can also change your view of the benefits (for the better), even if benefits weren't mentioned.

Role of System 2
  • - In the context of attitudes, System 2 acts more as an apologist for System 1's emotions than a critic.
  • - It engages in a biased search for information that is consistent with existing beliefs, rather than examining them critically.



Summary: Characteristics of System 1
  • - A list of features and activities attributed to the automatic system.
Core Operations
  • - Generates impressions, feelings, inclinations.
  • - Operates automatically and quickly with no sense of voluntary control.
  • - Creates a coherent pattern of activated ideas in associative memory.
  • - Links cognitive ease to illusions of truth and pleasant feelings.

Biases & Tendencies
  • - Infers and invents causes and intentions.
  • - Neglects ambiguity and suppresses doubt.
  • - Is biased to believe and confirm.
  • - Exaggerates emotional consistency (halo effect).
  • - Focuses on existing evidence and ignores absent evidence (WYSIATI).

Computational Features
  • - Represents sets by norms and prototypes; does not integrate (poor with sums).
  • - Matches intensities across scales (intensity matching).
  • - Computes more than intended (mental shotgun).
  • - Substitutes an easier question for a difficult one (heuristics).
  • - Is more sensitive to changes than to states.

Risk & Decision Making
  • - Overweights low probabilities.
  • - Shows diminishing sensitivity to quantity (psychophysics).
  • - Responds more strongly to losses than to gains (loss aversion).
  • - Frames decision problems narrowly, in isolation.
