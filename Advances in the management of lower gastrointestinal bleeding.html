Advances in the management of lower gastrointestinal bleeding

  • - DOI: 10.1111/apt.17859

Epidemiology & Aetiology
  • - Lower Gastrointestinal Bleeding (LGIB) is defined as bleeding originating from the colon or anorectum (below the ligament of Treitz).
  • - Small bowel bleeding is now termed 'mid-gastrointestinal' bleeding.
Incidence & Mortality
  • - Incidence is increasing, currently 33–87 per 100,000 population.
  • - This rise is linked to an ageing population and increased use of antithrombotics.
  • - Overall mortality is 3.4%–8.8%, but rises to 18%–20% in already hospitalized patients.
Mortality Details
  • - Most deaths are related to underlying comorbidities.
  • - Death from severe bleeding itself accounts for <1% of cases.
  • - Risk factors for mortality include advanced age, male sex, comorbidities (Charlson Comorbidity Index ≥2), long-term anticoagulant use, and hypovolemia.


Common Aetiologies
  • - The most common cause of LGIB, especially in patients >60 years, is diverticulosis.
  • - In up to 25% of cases, no specific aetiology is identified.
Prevalence of Causes (See Table 1)
  • - Diverticulosis: 25%–65%
  • - Colonic ischaemia: 5%–20%
  • - Haemorrhoids: 5%–20%
  • - Colorectal polyps or cancer: 2%–15%
  • - Angiodysplasia: 5%–10%
  • - Post-polypectomy bleeding: 2%–7%



Initial Assessment & Management
  • - Initial management involves a focused history, physical exam, and lab evaluation performed concurrently with hemodynamic resuscitation.
Initial Evaluation
  • - History: Focus on symptoms (abdominal pain, weight loss), comorbidities (cardiovascular disease), and medications (antithrombotics, NSAIDs).
  • - Physical Exam: Assess vital signs (including orthostatic changes), and perform abdominal and digital rectal examinations.
Upper GI Bleed (UGIB) Consideration
  • - Haematochezia can occur with massive UGIB in up to 15% of patients.
  • - Suspect UGIB in patients with hemodynamic instability, portal hypertension, or a history of peptic ulcer disease.
  • - An elevated BUN-to-creatinine ratio (>30:1) is highly suggestive of a UGIB source but lacks sensitivity.


Risk Stratification
  • - Initial triage is based on hemodynamic stability using the Shock Index (SI).
  • - SI = Heart Rate / Systolic Blood Pressure.
Unstable Patients (SI > 1)
  • - Patients with a shock index >1 are considered unstable.
  • - These patients should proceed directly to CT Angiography (CTA).

Stable Patients (SI < 1)
  • - Stable patients should be further risk-stratified using a dedicated LGIB risk score.
Oakland Score
  • - The Oakland score is the most discriminative tool for predicting safe discharge.
  • - A score of ≤8 correctly identifies patients for safe discharge in 95% of cases.

Other Scores
  • - The Strate score is best for predicting the need for hemostasis at endoscopy.
  • - Other scores include NOBLADS and BLEED.



Resuscitation & Transfusion
  • - The primary goal is to preserve tissue perfusion and prevent organ damage.
  • - Secure two large-bore IV lines and administer crystalloid fluids.
Blood Transfusion Strategy
  • - Recommendations are largely extrapolated from UGIB literature.
Restrictive Strategy (Stable Patients)
  • - Transfuse packed red blood cells when hemoglobin is <7 g/dL.
  • - The target hemoglobin level is 9 g/dL.

Liberal Strategy (High-Risk Patients)
  • - Consider for patients with underlying cardiovascular disease or hemodynamic instability.
  • - Transfuse when hemoglobin is <8 g/dL, with a target of 10 g/dL or greater.

Platelet Transfusion
  • - Consider platelet transfusion only if the count is <50 × 10⁹/L and urgent endoscopic hemostasis is required.




Antithrombotic Management
  • - The primary goal is to balance the risk of worsening bleeding against the risk of thromboembolic events.
  • - Reversal agents are reserved for life-threatening bleeding.
Life-Threatening Bleeding Definition
  • - Bleeding resulting in hypovolemic shock or requiring pressors/surgery.
  • - Associated with a hemoglobin decrease of >5 g/dL.
  • - Requiring transfusion of ≥5 units of packed red blood cells.

Antiplatelet Agents
  • - Platelet transfusion is not recommended for patients on antiplatelets without thrombocytopenia due to lack of benefit and potential harm.
Aspirin Management
  • - Primary Prevention: Stop aspirin and reassess. USPSTF 2022 guidelines state risks outweigh benefits in patients >60 years.
  • - Secondary Prevention: Do not stop aspirin. If held, resume as soon as hemostasis is achieved.

Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)
  • - Aspirin: Should not be held.
  • - P2Y12 inhibitor: Can be resumed within 5 days to reduce thrombotic risk.


Anticoagulant Agents
  • - For stable LGIB, the anticoagulant should be held on admission.
Vitamin K Antagonist (VKA)
  • - Vitamin K administration is not recommended due to its slow onset.
  • - PCC or FFP should be reserved for life-threatening bleeds only.

Direct Oral Anticoagulant (DOAC)
  • - Hold the DOAC on admission for stable LGIB.
  • - Use PCC or a drug-specific reversal agent only for life-threatening bleeds if the drug was ingested in the last 24 hours.

Resumption of Anticoagulation
  • - Restarting VKA and DOAC is suggested within 7 days after bleeding cessation to decrease thromboembolic risk.



Diagnostic Modalities
  • - Diagnosis is challenging due to the intermittent nature of LGIB.
  • - Approximately 80% of LGIB episodes, especially diverticular, stop spontaneously.
Radiology: CT Angiography (CTA)
  • - Indication: First-line test for hemodynamically unstable patients.
  • - Performance: Sensitivity 90%, Specificity 92%.
  • - Can detect bleeding rates as low as 0.3 mL/min.

Nuclear Medicine: Scintigraphy
  • - Has a limited and declining role in LGIB diagnosis.
  • - Limitations: Long preparation time, low diagnostic rate (<50%), and inaccurate localization of bleeding sites (10%–33% error rate).
  • - A study showed a diagnostic rate of only 38%.

Endoscopy: Colonoscopy
  • - Randomized trials and meta-analyses show no clinical benefit from early colonoscopy (<24h).
  • - Recommendation: Perform colonoscopy at the next elective timeslot or during hospital admission for stable patients.
Considerations for Colonoscopy
  • - Colonoscopy may be forgone if bleeding has stopped, the patient has known diverticulosis, and had a high-quality colonoscopy in the prior 12 months.
  • - However, there is a high attrition rate for outpatient colonoscopy scheduling (up to 45%).
  • - Bowel Prep: Split-dose or low-volume preps are acceptable. Unprepped colonoscopies are discouraged.



Treatment Modalities
  • - A multidisciplinary approach is crucial.
  • - The majority of bleeding stops spontaneously, so many patients do not require acute intervention.
Radiology: Transcatheter Embolization
  • - Indication: First-line therapy for unstable LGIB with a bleeding source identified on CTA.
  • - Timing: Should be performed urgently after a positive CTA. Angiography within 90 minutes of a positive CTA significantly increases therapeutic yield. ESGE recommends within 60 minutes.
Outcomes & Complications
  • - Clinical Success: 63%–96%.
  • - Re-bleeding: Up to 50%, depending on aetiology (lower for diverticular, higher for angiodysplasia).
  • - Major Complication: Bowel ischemia in 1%–4% of cases.


Endoscopic Therapy
  • - Performed in stable patients with active bleeding or stigmata of recent hemorrhage (SRH).
  • - A distal attachment transparent cap can improve visualization and therapy delivery.
Diverticular Bleeding
  • - Endoscopic therapy reduces the risk of rebleeding compared to conservative treatment.
  • - However, rebleeding rates remain high: early rebleeding 17.4%, late rebleeding 32.0%.
Treatment Options
  • - Through-the-scope (TTS) clips: Direct clipping of the vessel is superior to indirect clipping.
  • - Endoscopic Band Ligation (EBL): May be the preferred method. Associated with less early rebleeding (9%) compared to TTS clips (19%) and thermal coagulation (21%).
  • - Thermal Therapy: Avoid at the base of the diverticulum due to perforation risk.
  • - Injection Monotherapy: Not recommended due to high rebleeding rates.


Vascular Lesions
  • - For angiodysplasia and radiation proctitis, Argon Plasma Coagulation (APC) is safe and effective.
  • - A submucosal injection is recommended before thermal therapy in the thin-walled right colon to reduce perforation risk.

New Hemostatic Tools
  • - Over-the-scope-clips (OTSC): Show high success rates for diverticular, post-polypectomy, and anastomotic ulcer bleeding.
  • - Topical Hemostatic Agents (e.g., Hemospray™): Useful for diffuse bleeding. Achieves immediate hemostasis in 95% of cases, with a 30-day rebleeding rate of 14.3%.


Surgery
  • - Reserved for patients who fail endoscopic or radiological therapy.
  • - Emergency surgery is rare, required in only 0.2% of patients.
  • - Segmental resection is preferred over subtotal colectomy as it has lower morbidity and mortality.

Pharmacological Therapy
  • - Has a limited role, mainly for non-acute management of angiodysplasia.
  • - Somatostatin analogues and thalidomide have shown some promise but have limitations.
  • - Tranexamic acid is not recommended; the HALT-IT trial showed no benefit and an increased risk of thromboembolic events.


Recurrence & Secondary Prophylaxis
  • - Recurrent LGIB is a major challenge in management.
Recurrent Bleeding
  • - Definition: Bleeding after 24 hours of clinical stability or readmission within 1 week.
  • - Rates: In-hospital rebleeding is 4.4%. 1-year recurrence is 13%–19%, rising to 46% at 5 years.
  • - Recurrence arises from the same site in only 37% of patients.
Risk Factors for Rebleeding
  • - Age ≥65 years (HR: 1.7)
  • - Use of NSAIDs (HR: 2.0)
  • - Non-aspirin antiplatelet drugs (HR: 1.8)


Secondary Prophylaxis
  • - Antiplatelet use is associated with recurrent LGIB (Monotherapy HR: 3.57, DAPT HR: 5.3).
  • - Use of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) in patients on low-dose aspirin did not reduce the risk of LGIB.
  • - It is recommended to discontinue non-aspirin NSAIDs when possible.


Post-Polypectomy Bleeding (PPB)
  • - A common complication of polypectomy, accounting for 2%–7% of all LGIB cases.
Epidemiology & Risk Factors
  • - Incidence: 0.2%–2.2% overall, but can be as high as 6.5% for polyps >2 cm.
  • - Delayed PPB: Can occur up to 14 days after resection.
Key Risk Factors (See Table 2)
  • - Polyp-related: Size >10 mm, right-sided location, pedunculated morphology, hot snare polypectomy.
  • - Patient-related: Older age, cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, use of anticoagulants or antiplatelets.


Treatment
  • - The source of bleeding is known, so acute colonoscopy has a greater role than in other LGIB cases.
  • - However, bleeding stops spontaneously in 43%–55% of patients.
  • - A selective approach to colonoscopy is beneficial in only 22% of cases.
Predictors of Active Bleeding at Colonoscopy
  • - Left-sided polyp removal
  • - Use of electrocautery
  • - Use of anticoagulants
  • - Pedunculated polyp morphology


Prophylaxis
  • - Prophylactic clipping: Can reduce delayed PPB risk for large (>2 cm) and proximal polyps.
  • - Cold snare polypectomy: Associated with a lower risk of delayed PPB compared to hot snare.
  • - Self-assembling peptide (PuraStat®): Shown to reduce delayed PPB risk after advanced endoscopic resection.


Future Directions & Conclusions
  • - High-quality studies are needed to guide the management of LGIB.
Research Gaps
  • - Risk assessment scores like the Oakland score require further prospective validation.
  • - Comparative efficacy studies (e.g., embolization vs. endoscopic therapy) are needed.
  • - The role and outcomes of reversal agents for antithrombotics remain undefined.

Key Takeaways
  • - LGIB is a common emergency with increasing incidence and worse outcomes than UGIB.
  • - Diverticulosis is the most common cause.
  • - Initial Management: Focus on hemodynamic stabilization and risk stratification (Oakland score).
  • - Unstable Patients: CTA is the preferred diagnostic and triage tool, followed by radiological embolization.
  • - Stable Patients: Elective colonoscopy is the standard of care.
  • - A multidisciplinary approach is essential for optimal outcomes.
