How to Become a Better Learner
  • - Two key tips for improving learning based on brain science.
Tip 1: Physical Exercise
  • - The best gift you can give your brain.
  • - By far more effective than any drug on the market today to help you learn better.
Mechanism: Neurogenesis
  • - New neurons are born every day in a few brain areas.
  • - One key area is the Hippocampus, which is very important for learning new things.

Neuron Function & Survival
  • - New neurons are recruited to help with tasks like pattern separation (e.g., distinguishing a flower from an airplane).
  • - These new neurons will die if you don't use them.
  • - New experiences and exercise help new neurons survive.

Critique of Modern Education
  • - It is unfortunate that schools are dropping gym and recess for more instruction time.
  • - Gym and recess are arguably the most important parts of the curriculum for learning.


Tip 2: Practice & Critical Periods
  • - Practice makes perfect, but only when your brain is prepared.
Critical Periods
  • - Specific times in development when sudden improvements occur in specific abilities.
Examples
  • - First language acquisition: Critical period extends up to puberty.
  • - Binocular depth perception (Stereopsis): Matures during the first two years of life.


Stereopsis (3D Vision)
  • - The brain interprets the slight shift between images in the two eyes as a difference in depth.
Stereo Blindness
  • - Affects over 5% of the population.
  • - Caused by improper eye alignment during the critical period (first 2 years).
  • - Results in neurons in the visual cortex failing to properly strengthen inputs, leading to permanently impaired depth perception.

Recovery is Possible
  • - Dogma suggested the impairment was permanent.
  • - However, Sue Barry was able to recover stereo vision through eye exercises later in life.
  • - This demonstrates that practice can repair and train the brain, but it takes much longer past the critical period.



Brain Maturation & The Prefrontal Cortex
  • - Learning, planning, and language are skills that make us human.
Prefrontal Cortex
  • - Involved in complex analysis, social behaviors, decision making, and planning.
  • - It is the last part of the cortex to mature.

Case Study: EVR
  • - Suffered a stroke in the social parts of his prefrontal cortex.
  • - Maintained a high IQ but was ruined by making bad financial and social decisions.
  • - Demonstrates that good judgment takes a long time and a lot of experience to acquire.



Renaissance Learning & Unlocking Potential
  • - This section covers metaphors, teamwork, and test performance.
The Non-Linear Process of Learning
  • - Learning doesn't progress logically; you can hit a wall and things can suddenly seem confusing.
Knowledge Collapse
  • - This phenomenon occurs when your mind is restructuring its understanding to build a more solid foundation.
  • - Language learners may experience periods where the foreign language seems incomprehensible.

Embrace the Struggle
  • - Periods of feeling like you're taking a step backwards are natural.
  • - It means your mind is wrestling deeply with the material.
  • - When you emerge, your knowledge base often takes a surprising leap forward.



Metaphors & Analogies
  • - One of the best things you can do to not only remember but understand concepts.
  • - Often, the more visual the better.
Technique & Examples
  • - A metaphor is realizing one thing is somehow similar to another.
  • - Pretend you are the concept (e.g., 'put yourself in an electron's slippers').
Examples
  • - Electrical current ---> Water
  • - Electrical voltage ---> Pressure
  • - Cation ---> A cat with 'paws' is pawsitive
  • - Anion ---> An onion is negative because it makes you cry


Benefits
  • - Helps overcome Einstellung (being blocked by thinking about a problem in the wrong way).
  • - Helps glue an idea into your mind by making a connection to neural structures that are already there.


No Need for Genius Envy
  • - Revisiting chunking and the relationship between practice, intelligence, and creativity.
Chunking & Repetition
  • - Smooth repetition creates 'muscle memory' for the brain, turning complex steps into a single chunk.
  • - People learn by trying to make sense of information themselves, not just by being told.

Intelligence vs. Creativity
  • - Being 'smarter' often equates to having a larger working memory (e.g., holding 9 things in mind instead of 4).
Downside of a Large Working Memory
  • - Can make it more difficult to be creative due to Einstellung.
  • - A superb working memory can hold its thoughts so tightly that new thoughts can't easily peek through.

Upside of a Smaller Working Memory
  • - You can more easily generalize your learning into new, creative combinations.
  • - Your focus doesn't lock everything up, allowing input from other brain areas (e.g., sensory cortex).
  • - You may have to work harder, but once something is chunked, you can be more creative with it.


The Power of Practice
  • - Deliberate practice on the toughest aspects of the material can help lift average brains into the realm of those with more natural gifts.

The Imposter Syndrome
  • - The feeling that you're an incompetent fluke and your successes are accidental.
  • - This feeling is extraordinarily common, and many people secretly share it.


Change Your Thoughts, Change Your Life
  • - Lessons from the life of Santiago RamÃ³n y Cajal, the Father of Modern Neuroscience.
Key to Success: Perseverance
  • - Cajal, a former juvenile delinquent, attributed his Nobel Prize-winning success to perseverance ('the virtue of the less brilliant') and the flexibility to admit errors.

Neural Development & Practice
  • - We can make significant changes in our brain by changing how we think.
  • - Practicing thoughts helps build the myelin sheath over neurons, strengthening connections.
  • - Myelin may not finish developing until the twenties, which may explain teenage impulsivity.

Dealing with Criticism
  • - Cajal warned that others may criticize or undermine your achievements, often because they feel threatened.
  • - It's important to learn to switch on a 'cool dispassion' to tune out people who try to undercut you.


The Value of Team Work
  • - Using others to overcome our own cognitive blind spots and errors.
Brain Hemispheres & Perspective
  • - Research gives intriguing hints about differences between the brain's two hemispheres.
Right Hemisphere
  • - Helps us step back for a big-picture perspective.
  • - Crucial for 'ah-ha' insights and reality checks.
  • - Damage can lead to an inability to recognize nonsensical conclusions (e.g., a hot dog stand losing a billion dollars).

Left Hemisphere
  • - Tries to cling tenaciously to the way things were.
  • - Associated with the focused mode, which can lead to rigidity, dogmatism, and egocentricity.


Checking Your Work
  • - Not checking your work is like refusing to use your right hemisphere's ability to see the big picture.
  • - Richard Feynman: 'The first principle is that you must not fool yourself. And you are the easiest person to fool.'

Benefits of Collaboration
  • - Friends and teammates can serve as a larger-scale, external 'diffuse mode' to catch what you missed.
  • - Explaining concepts to friends helps build your own understanding.

Warning for Study Groups
  • - Study groups are effective for learning, but not if they turn into socializing occasions.
  • - Red flags: meetings start late, members are unprepared, conversation veers off topic.
  • - If this happens, it's best to find another group.


A Test Checklist
  • - Testing is a powerful learning experience.
  • - You will retain and learn far more from one hour of taking a test than from one hour of studying the same material.
Dr. Richard Felder's Checklist
  • - The goal is to be able to answer 'Yes' to most of these questions before a test.
Preparation Questions
  • - Did you make a serious effort to understand the text?
  • - Did you work with classmates on homework or at least check your solutions with others?
  • - Did you attempt to outline every homework problem solution before working with classmates?
  • - Did you consult with the instructor when you were having trouble?

Review Questions
  • - Did you understand all your homework solutions when they were handed in?
  • - If you had a study guide, did you carefully go through it and convince yourself you could do everything on it?
  • - Did you go over the study guide with classmates and quiz one another?
  • - Did you attend the review session and ask questions?

Final Step
  • - Did you get a reasonable night's sleep before the test?
  • - If the answer is no, your answers to all the preceding questions may not matter.



Test Strategy: Hard Startâ€”Jump to Easy
  • - A counterintuitive but highly effective technique for test-taking.
The Flaw in the 'Easy First' Approach
  • - The classic approach is to do easy problems first to build confidence.
  • - This is often counterproductive because tough problems need the most time and the creative powers of the diffuse mode.

The Technique
  • - 1. When the test is handed out, take a quick look over the whole thing.
  • - 2. Start with what appears to be the hardest problem.
  • - 3. If you get stuck, pull away within the first minute or two.
  • - 4. Switch to an easy problem and complete it.
  • - 5. Alternate between hard and easy problems, making small bits of progress on the hard ones.

Why It Works
  • - Starting hard loads the difficult problem into your mind, then switching attention allows the diffuse mode to work on it in the background.
  • - It helps you avoid Einstellung (getting stuck on the wrong approach).
  • - It guarantees you will have at least some work done on every problem, which is good for partial credit.

Key Requirement
  • - You must have the self-discipline to pull yourself off a problem once you find yourself stuck for a minute or two.


Final Helpful Hints for Tests
  • - Practical tips for managing stress and logistics on test day.
Managing Test Anxiety
  • - The body releases chemicals like cortisol under stress, causing physical symptoms.
Reframe Your Mindset
  • - How you interpret the symptoms makes all the difference.
  • - Shift your thinking from 'This test has made me afraid' to 'This test has got me excited to do my best.'
  • - This simple shift can significantly improve performance.

Deep Breathing
  • - Counteracts the 'fight or flight' response.
  • - Relax your stomach, place your hand on it, and slowly draw a deep breath so your hand moves out.
  • - Practice this for a minute or two in the weeks before the test to make it a natural response.


Test Day Logistics
  • - Advice for the day before and during the test.
The Day Before
  • - Do a quick, final look over the materials, but don't push your brain too hard.
  • - Conserve mental energy for the test.
  • - Crucially: Not getting enough sleep can negate any other preparation you've done.

During the Test
  • - Double check answers: Blink, shift your attention, and askyourself, 'does this really make sense?'
  • - Check answers from different perspectives if possible.
  • - In science, ensure units of measurement match on each side of an equation.
Checking Order
  • - When checking your work, try starting from the back of the test and working towards the front.
  • - This can give your brain a fresher perspective that helps you catch errors more easily.
