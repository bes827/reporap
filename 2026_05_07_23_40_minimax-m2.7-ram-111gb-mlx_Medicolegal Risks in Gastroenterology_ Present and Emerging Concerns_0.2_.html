Stark Law Compliance for Private Practice
What is Stark Law?
  • Federal civil penalty statute regulating physician self-referrals for "designated health services" (DHS) billed to Medicare
  • Applies when physician has financial relationship with entity receiving referral for DHS
  • Violation --> return of payments + exclusion from Medicare/Medicaid + civil monetary penalties
What Qualifies as DHS?
  • Inpatient hospital services
  • Outpatient hospital services
  • Lab services
  • Diagnostic imaging
  • Other designated service types
  • Example: CPT code 9110 (capsule endoscopy) = DHS under Stark
Key Stark Exceptions
  • Employee Exception: Covers employed physicians
  • In-Office Ancillary Services (IOAS) Exception: Most popular exception for group practices
  • Excludes financial relationships from Stark prohibition
  • Covers ownership or compensation arrangements
In-Office Ancillary Services (IOAS) Exception Requirements
Focus 1: Who Furnishes the Service
  • Must be furnished personally by:
  • Referring physician, OR
  • Physician who is member of same group practice as referring physician, OR
  • Individual supervised by referring physician or another physician in group
  • Supervision must comply with all applicable Medicare payment and coverage rules
  • Compliance tip: Easier to use full-time employees than independent contractors for DHS
Focus 2: Location of Service Provision
  • Simplest safe approach: Perform services in medical office open full-time for other patient services
  • Other arrangements require consultation with health law attorney
Focus 3: Billing of Services
  • Must be billed by physician performing/supervising service OR the group practice
  • Most efficient --> bill using tax ID number assigned to group practice
Group Practice Requirements (for IOAS Exception)
Basic Requirements
  • Must be single legal entity
  • Must have at least 2 physicians who are group practice members
  • "Members of the group practice" = employees, not independent contractors
Shared Resources Requirement
  • Each physician must provide most of their typical patient care services using:
  • Shared office space
  • Shared facilities
  • Shared equipment
  • Shared staff (collaboratively)
  • "Substantially" = 75% threshold
  • Consequence: Gastroenterologist cannot be group practice member and offer only one type of service elsewhere
Revenue Distribution Requirement
  • 75% of patient care services must be:
  • Furnished through the group practice
  • Billed through billing number assigned to group
  • Amounts received --> treated as receipts of the group
  • Wholly owned subsidiaries for billing:
  • Cannot exceed 25% of overall services of group practice
Pre-Established Compensation Methods
  • Group practice must distribute:
  • Overhead
  • Expenses
  • Income
  • Using methods set before receiving payment for services
  • "Group practice" cannot operate without compensation set in advance
Physician Compensation Rules Under Group Practice Requirements
  • No physician --> directly compensated based on their referral of Medicare or Medicaid patients for DHS
  • Detailed regulations provide options for sharing profits (including overall profit shares)
  • Common Stark-compliant method: Share revenue from ancillary services classified as DHS based on each physician's ownership percentage
  • This ensures income distribution is not tied to volume or value of referrals for DHS services
Ambulatory Surgery Center (ASC) Investment Compliance
Federal Antikickback Statute (AKS) Basics
  • Generally --> felony to pay or receive anything of value for Medicare/Medicaid business referrals
  • Gastroenterologist referring Medicare patients to ASC where physician has investment --> potentially violates AKS
  • Violation consequences:
  • Felony with potential jail time
  • Substantial civil monetary penalties
  • Exclusion from Medicare program
AKS Safe Harbor Protection
  • Financial arrangements protected from prosecution if they meet all safe harbor requirements
  • Failure to meet one or more aspects --> does not automatically constitute crime
  • Government examines facts and circumstances to determine intent for referral
ASC Safe Harbor: 5 Common Requirements

1. ASC must treat Medicare patients in non-discriminatory manner

2. Investment opportunity terms for physician investors --> not related to volume or value of referrals

3. No loans or loan guarantees to investors allowed

4. All ancillary services must be directly and integrally related to primary procedures at ASC

  • None may be separately billed to Medicare

5. Patients must be informed of physician's ownership

ASC Safe Harbor: 4 Types Covered

1. Surgeon-owned ASCs

2. Single-specialty ASCs

3. Multi-specialty ASCs

4. Hospital/physician ASCs

Volume Requirements for Safe Harbor Compliance
  • Single-specialty, multi-specialty, and surgeon-owned ASCs:
  • At least 1/3 of each physician investor's medical practice (based on all sources from previous fiscal year or 12-month period) --> must come from performing procedures requiring ASC or hospital surgical setting
  • Multi-specialty ASCs additional requirement:
  • At least 1/3 of procedures required to be at ASC or hospital surgical setting --> must be performed at the ASC
  • Ongoing compliance requirement: Gastroenterologists must track and meet these thresholds continuously
Private Equity Investment in Gastroenterology Practices
Corporate Practice of Medicine Doctrine
  • Most US states have this doctrine
  • Prohibits unlicensed people from owning directly in medical practice
  • Creates need for complex structures for private equity investment
Typical Private Equity Structure
  • Private equity invests in Management Services Organization (MSO)
  • MSO takes much of practice's value via management fees
  • Gastroenterologists may or may not have opportunity to invest in:
  • The practice itself
  • The MSO
Clinical Control Requirements
  • Physicians must control clinical aspects of patient care
  • Some states restrict private equity companies from controlling:
  • Use of medical devices
  • Pricing decisions
  • Care protocols
  • Other patient care-related issues
Areas for Negotiation
  • Compensation arrangements
  • Bonuses
  • Investment opportunities in:
  • The practice itself
  • Practice management company (MSO)
  • Real estate ventures
  • Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs)
Restrictive Covenant Considerations
  • Some states --> restrictive covenants not permitted
  • In states where legal --> gastroenterologist may negotiate less restrictive terms
  • Negotiations depend on state-specific laws
Key Takeaways for Gastroenterologists
  • Private equity companies remain interested in gastroenterology practices
  • Compliance is essential
  • Physicians must protect:
  • Business interests
  • Clinical interests when entering investment arrangements
  • Recommendation: Seek both business and health law advice before entering private equity arrangements
Summary: Compliance Essentials for Gastroenterologists
Stark Law
  • Regulates physician referrals for designated health services (DHS) billed to Medicare
  • Key exceptions: Employee Exception, IOAS Exception
  • Violation --> return payments + exclusion + civil penalties
IOAS Exception (3 Main Focuses)

1. Who furnishes service --> must be referring physician, group member, or supervised individual

2. Location --> preferably in medical office open full-time

3. Billing --> through performing physician or group practice tax ID

Group Practice Requirements for IOAS
  • Single legal entity + minimum 2 physician members (employees, not contractors)
  • 75% threshold for shared resources and revenue distribution
  • Pre-established compensation methods required
  • No direct compensation based on DHS referrals
ASC Investment Compliance
  • Must meet AKS safe harbor requirements
  • 5 common requirements including non-discrimination and no referral-based terms
  • Volume thresholds: 1/3 of physician practice from ASC-appropriate procedures
  • Multi-specialty ASCs --> also 1/3 of procedures performed at the ASC
Private Equity Considerations
  • Corporate practice of medicine creates complex structures (MSO model)
  • Physicians must retain clinical control
  • Negotiate compensation, bonuses, and investment opportunities
  • Restrictive covenants vary by state
  • Legal and business advice essential before entering arrangements
