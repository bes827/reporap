Overall Impact & Impression
  • - High clinical importance: Addresses a common, debilitating problem (PMPS) with limited effective treatments.
  • - Moderate-to-high innovation: Applies a plausible biologic intervention (RPNI) in a prospective study.
  • - Current major weakness: Methodological ambiguities, especially the lack of control/comparator groups, limit causal inference and dampen the study's potential impact.
Potential
  • - If methodological issues are fixed, the study could provide robust evidence to change surgical practice and pain management after mastectomy.

Current Risk
  • - As planned, the study is promising but at high risk of bias, making results difficult to interpret.


Factor 1: Importance & Innovation
  • - Evaluates the significance of the research question and its novelty.
Strengths
  • - Addresses a clinical gap: PMPS is a significant problem, and RPNI offers a physiological solution (neuroma prevention).
  • - Translational value: Includes two complementary aims: prevention (prophylactic) and treatment (therapeutic).
  • - Feasibility: Multi-site recruitment within a single health system supports generalizability.

Weaknesses
  • - Incremental Innovation: Prior case series exist, so novelty depends on the rigor of this prospective study.
  • - Unclear Scope: Proposal should clarify how this study is a significant step up from the 'couple of case series' already published (e.g., first large-scale prospective study).


Factor 2: Rigor & Feasibility (Approach)
  • - This section details the major methodological flaws and provides actionable recommendations for improvement.
1. Comparator / Causal Inference
  • - CRITICAL FLAW: The study design, as proposed, cannot establish causality due to the lack of appropriate control groups.
Aim 1 (Prevention) Weakness
  • - Plans to enroll 100 patients who receive RPNI but has no defined contemporaneous control group of mastectomy patients who did not.
  • - This makes it impossible to determine if lower pain rates are due to RPNI or other factors like selection bias.
Recommended Fix
  • - Add a contemporaneous control cohort of similar mastectomy patients without RPNI from the same sites and time frame.
  • - Ideal Design: Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT).
  • - Alternative Designs: Prospective cohort with propensity score matching or multivariable adjustment for confounders (age, reconstruction type, etc.).


Aim 2 (Treatment) Weakness
  • - The pre/post within-subject design is stronger but lacks a comparator for placebo effects, natural recovery, or regression to the mean.
Recommended Fix
  • - Consider a wait-list or delayed-treatment control group.
  • - If not feasible, emphasize within-subject change but rigorously adjust for concomitant interventions (pain meds, physical therapy).



2. Sample Size & Power
  • - Weakness: Justification is minimal (single effect size of 0.39 cited without source/variance).
  • - Weakness: Proposal fails to account for patient dropouts (attrition).
Recommended Fix
  • - Define the Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID) for the chosen pain scales (SF-MPQ-2, BREAST-Q).
  • - Calculate sample size based on MCID, using mean/SD estimates from prior literature.
  • - Inflate the recruitment target by 15-20% to ensure the final analyzed sample size is met after anticipated dropouts.


3. Statistical Methods
  • - Weakness: The planned use of simple t-tests is inadequate for the data structure and potential confounders.
Recommended Fix
  • - For Aim 2 (pre/post): Use linear mixed-effects models or repeated measures ANCOVA to handle repeated measures and subject variability.
  • - For Aim 1 (comparative): Use logistic or linear regression; consider propensity score methods to balance covariates.
  • - Pre-specify the primary endpoint to limit issues with multiple comparisons.


4. Measurement & Bias Reduction
  • - Strengths include using validated instruments (SF-MPQ-2, BREAST-Q) and objective measures.
Blinding
  • - Weakness: Surgeon and patient cannot be blinded, but outcome assessors can be.
  • - Fix: Ensure clinical evaluators performing Tinelâ€™s sign and pressure algometry are blinded to the patient's group and time point.

Standardization
  • - Weakness: RPNI technique may vary between surgeons, confounding results.
  • - Fix: Use a detailed operations manual to standardize the surgical technique. Document intraoperative variables (nerve resected, graft size) and surgeon experience.

Follow-up Timing
  • - Weakness: A single endpoint at 6 months may miss important pain trajectories.
  • - Fix: Collect data at multiple time points (e.g., baseline, 1, 3, 6, and 12 months) to characterize pain onset, persistence, and durability of effect.


5. Recruitment & Selection Bias
  • - Weakness: Recruitment relies on flyers, emails, and patient interest, leading to Self-Selection Bias.
  • - Patients with very severe or very mild symptoms may be more likely to participate, making the sample not representative.
Recommended Fix
  • - Utilize a systematic screening of all eligible mastectomy records at the study sites.
  • - Aim for a consecutive sample to improve representativeness and reduce selection bias.


6. Data Management & Quality
  • - Weakness: Data entry, coding, and quality control steps are not detailed.
Recommended Fix
  • - Use a dedicated data management system like REDCap with audit trails.
  • - Create a data dictionary, train abstractors, and perform double-coding on a subset of data to ensure reliability.
  • - Blind statisticians to group allocation for primary analyses.


7. Ethics & Consent
  • - Strength: Offering the procedure as standard of care regardless of study participation prevents coercion.
  • - Weakness: The distinction between clinical consent for surgery and research consent for data collection needs to be clearer.
Recommended Fix
  • - Create separate consent forms for clinical care (the RPNI surgery) and research participation (surveys, extra assessments).
  • - Ensure compensation does not create coercion.


8. Reporting & Protocol Registration
  • - Weakness: No mention of protocol registration or adherence to reporting guidelines.
Recommended Fix
  • - Pre-register the study protocol on a site like clinicaltrials.gov.
  • - Follow established reporting guidelines: SPIRIT for the protocol, and CONSORT (for RCT) or STROBE (for cohort) for the final report.



Actionable Checklist (Summary)
  • - Key steps to improve the research proposal.
1. Solidify Study Design
  • - Choose and justify the design for Aim 1: RCT or a prospective matched cohort with a contemporaneous non-RPNI comparator.

2. Refine Endpoints & Sample Size
  • - Pre-specify primary endpoint(s) and the MCID for pain instruments.
  • - Re-calculate sample size accounting for MCID, realistic standard deviations, and dropout.

3. Standardize Procedures
  • - Create an operations manual for the surgical technique.
  • - Develop a training plan for assessors and measure inter-rater reliability (e.g., kappa/ICC).

4. Develop Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)
  • - Detail model choices (e.g., mixed models), covariate handling, and missing data strategy (e.g., multiple imputation).

5. Register & Report
  • - Register the protocol and pre-specify the analysis plan.
  • - Prepare to report according to SPIRIT/CONSORT/STROBE guidelines.

6. Implement Data & Safety Plans
  • - Set up data management in REDCap.
  • - Define a safety monitoring plan and a process for adjudicating adverse events.


Inclusion & Regulatory Items
  • - Considerations for patient populations, safety, and ethics.
Patient Demographics
  • - Exclusion of Males: The proposal excludes males. This limits generalizability (external validity).
  • - Suggestion: Unless there is a strong physiological reason, this exclusion should be removed.

Human Subjects Protection
  • - Appears appropriate, but requires clear separation of clinical vs. research consent.
  • - A Data & Safety Monitoring Plan is needed to report and adjudicate adverse events (e.g., wound issues, infection).
