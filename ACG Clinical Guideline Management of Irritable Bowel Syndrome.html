ACG Clinical Guideline: Management of Irritable Bowel Syndrome
  • Journal: The American Journal of Gastroenterology
  • Publication Date: January 2021 (published online December 14, 2020)
  • Authors: Brian E. Lacy, et al.
  • Methodology: Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE).

  • 2. In patients with IBS-D, what percentage are estimated to have bile acid malabsorption (BAM) based on SeHCAT testing?
  • 3. What is the recommended starting dose for amitriptyline when used as a neuromodulator for IBS?
  • 4. What is the pooled prevalence of post-infection IBS following a GI infection?
  • 5. For which specific patient population is alosetron recommended, and what is the primary risk associated with its use?
IBS: Introduction & Definition
  • IBS is a chronic, highly prevalent disorder of gut-brain interaction.
  • Characterized by recurrent abdominal pain & disordered defecation.
  • Prevalence in US, UK, Canada ≈ 4.4%–4.8%; most common in women & individuals < 50 years.
Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria
  • Recurrent abdominal pain ≥ 1 day/week in the last 3 months.
  • Associated w/ ≥ 2 of the following:
  • - Related to defecation
  • - Associated w/ a change in stool frequency
  • - Associated w/ a change in stool form (appearance)
Timing Criteria
  • Criteria must be fulfilled for the last 3 months.
  • Symptom onset must be ≥ 6 months before diagnosis.
Patient Impact
  • Significantly reduces quality of life (QoL).
  • Majority of patients would give up 10–15 years of life for an instant cure.
  • Patients would accept a median 1% risk of sudden death for a curative medication.
Economic Burden
  • Direct medical costs in the US estimated at $1.5–$10 billion per year (excluding medications).
  • High healthcare resource utilization & unnecessary testing contribute to costs.
Diagnostic Strategies
  • Focuses on key questions regarding diagnostic testing in patients with suspected IBS.
1. Celiac Disease (CD) Screening
  • Recommendation: Perform serologic testing to rule out CD in patients w/ IBS & diarrhea symptoms.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate.
Rationale & Prevalence
  • CD symptoms (pain, bloating, altered bowels) can mimic IBS.
  • Prevalence of biopsy-proven CD in patients w/ IBS symptoms: 3.3% (95% CI 2.3%–4.5%).
  • Pooled Odds Ratio (OR) for biopsy-proven CD in IBS vs controls: 4.48 (95% CI 2.33–4.60).
Prevalence by IBS Subtype
  • Highest prevalence in IBS-D: 5.7% (EMA or tTG positive).
  • IBS-M: 3.4%.
  • IBS-C: 2.1% (lowest).
Screening Recommendations
  • Recommended test: Immunoglobulin A (IgA) tissue transglutaminase (tTG).
  • Also check quantitative IgA level to rule out IgA deficiency.
  • If endoscopy is performed: obtain 6 duodenal biopsies, including the bulb.
Clinical Importance
  • Untreated CD → significant consequences (e.g., neuropsychiatric disease, other autoimmune diseases, GI malignancies).
  • Early diagnosis is cost-effective & allows for highly effective treatment.
2. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) Exclusion
  • Recommendation: Check fecal calprotectin (or fecal lactoferrin) & C-reactive protein (CRP) in patients w/o alarm features & suspected IBS-D to rule out IBD.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate for CRP/fCal; Very low for fecal lactoferrin.
Rationale
  • Symptoms alone cannot always distinguish IBS-D from IBD.
  • Pre-test probability of IBD in IBS is low (0.5%–1.2%), but incidence is 2.6–5 times higher than controls after 5 years of symptoms.
Biomarker Performance
  • CRP: A level ≤0.5 mg/dL yields a 1% probability of IBD.
  • Fecal Calprotectin (fCal): Sensitivity 93%, Specificity 96% for IBD vs endoscopy.
  • Fecal Lactoferrin (FL): Sensitivity 67%–86%, Specificity 96%–100% for active IBD vs IBS.
Key Point
  • Negative Predictive Value (NPV) of fCal is superior to CRP & ESR.
  • These tests are for ruling out IBD, not for ruling in IBS.
3. Enteric Pathogen Testing
  • Recommendation: Against routine stool testing for enteric pathogens in all patients w/ IBS.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Post-Infection IBS (PI-IBS)
  • IBS can arise after bacterial, viral, or parasitic GI infections.
  • Estimated pooled prevalence of PI-IBS: 11% (95% CI 8.2–15.8).
  • Risk is 4.2 times higher in exposed vs non-exposed individuals.
Risk Factors for PI-IBS
  • Female gender.
  • Antibiotic exposure during infection.
  • History of anxiety or depression.
Role of Giardia
  • Giardia is the most common parasitic pathogen in the US associated w/ PI-IBS.
  • Relative risk of developing IBS after Giardiasis: 3.4 (95% CI 2.9–3.9).
  • Prevalence of any parasitic infection in IBS is low (<2%).
When to Test for Giardia
  • Testing is indicated only in patients w/ specific risk factors:
  • - Travel to endemic areas
  • - Poor water quality exposure (camping, hiking)
  • - Daycare exposure
  • - Close contact w/ infected person
4. Colonoscopy Utility
  • Recommendation: Against routine colonoscopy in patients w/ IBS symptoms < 45 years old w/o warning signs.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Alarm Features
  • Warrant consideration for colonoscopy:
  • - Hematochezia, melena
  • - Unintentional weight loss
  • - Older age of onset
  • - Family history of IBD, colon cancer, or other significant GI disease.
Diagnostic Yield
  • In patients w/o alarm features, yield is low.
  • Common findings: hemorrhoids, diverticulosis, polyps.
  • Rate of polyps may be lower in IBS vs controls (7.7% vs 26.1%).
Special Considerations
  • Microscopic colitis: Consider colonoscopy in women > 60 years w/ new-onset, intense diarrhea.
  • Colon cancer screening: Should be performed based on age-appropriate guidelines, independent of IBS symptoms.
5 & 6. Positive vs. Exclusionary Diagnostic Strategy
  • A positive diagnostic strategy involves a careful history, physical exam, use of standard criteria (e.g., Rome IV), & limited diagnostic tests.
Improve Time to Therapy
  • Recommendation: Use a positive diagnostic strategy to improve time to initiate appropriate therapy.
  • Strength: Consensus recommendation (unable to assess using GRADE).
  • Rationale: Reduces delay, engages patients in shared decision-making, allows earlier access to evidence-based therapies.
Improve Cost-Effectiveness
  • Recommendation: Use a positive diagnostic strategy to improve cost-effectiveness.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: High.
Cost Data
  • Providers using an 'exclusion' strategy order nearly 2x the tests & consume $400 more per patient.
  • In one study, a positive strategy was ~40% cheaper ($3,160 vs $5,075 annually) w/ no difference in symptoms or satisfaction.
  • In IBS-D, ~80% of healthcare costs in the first 2 years are associated w/ a diagnosis of exclusion approach.
7. IBS Subtype Categorization
  • Recommendation: Categorizing patients by accurate IBS subtype improves patient therapy.
  • Strength: Consensus recommendation (unable to assess using GRADE).
  • Rationale: Pharmacological therapies usually target specific subtypes (IBS-D, IBS-C).
How to Subtype
  • 1. Use Bristol Stool Form Scale (BSFS).
  • 2. Assess predominant stool consistency only on days w/ abnormal bowel movements (off therapy).
  • 3. Apply Rome IV criteria based on % of abnormal BMs.
Rome IV Subtype Definitions
  • IBS-C: >25% BSFS 1-2 & <25% BSFS 6-7.
  • IBS-D: >25% BSFS 6-7 & <25% BSFS 1-2.
  • IBS-M: >25% BSFS 1-2 & >25% BSFS 6-7.
  • IBS-U: Cannot be determined.
Subtype Fluctuation
  • >50% of patients change predominant subtype over a 1-year period.
  • Clarification of subtype should be performed routinely.
8. Food Allergy & Sensitivity Testing
  • Recommendation: Do not test for food allergies & food sensitivities in all patients w/ IBS unless there are reproducible symptoms concerning for a food allergy.
  • Strength: Consensus recommendation (unable to assess using GRADE).
Food Allergy vs. Intolerance
  • Food Allergy: Immune-mediated (IgE, non-IgE, or mixed). Uncommon in adults (1-3%). Patients w/ IBS are not more likely to have them.
  • Food Intolerance/Sensitivity: Not immune-mediated. More common in IBS (up to 50% report adverse food reactions).
Testing Limitations
  • Skin prick test: Positive only 50% of the time in true food allergies.
  • Serum IgE: Low sensitivity; can miss up to 25% of significant reactions.
  • Serum IgG panels: Not validated & not recommended.
  • Indiscriminate testing → many false positives.
9. Anorectal Physiology Testing
  • Recommendation: Perform anorectal physiology testing in patients w/ IBS & symptoms suggestive of a pelvic floor disorder and/or refractory constipation.
  • Strength: Consensus recommendation (unable to assess using GRADE).
Prevalence & Symptoms
  • Anorectal dysfunction occurs in all IBS subtypes; prevalence up to 40% in tertiary care.
  • Symptoms alone cannot distinguish IBS from dyssynergic defecation (DD).
  • Symptoms suggesting DD: straining, incomplete evacuation, digital disimpaction.
Diagnostic Tests
  • Anorectal manometry (ARM).
  • Balloon expulsion test (BET).
  • Defecography.
  • Diagnosis of DD requires abnormalities on 2 of 3 tests.
Clinical Utility
  • Identifies patients who may benefit from biofeedback therapy.
  • Biofeedback can improve both bowel symptoms & abdominal pain in IBS patients w/ DD.
  • 44% of female patients w/ IBS-C have prolonged BET, suggesting DD.
Management & Treatment Strategies
  • Focuses on therapeutic options for patients with IBS, including dietary, pharmacologic, and psychological interventions.
Dietary & Lifestyle Interventions
  • Recommendations on dietary modifications for managing IBS symptoms.
10. Low FODMAP Diet
  • Recommendation: A limited trial of a low FODMAP diet to improve global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Very low.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • FODMAPs → ↑ GI water secretion & fermentation → luminal distension & symptoms.
  • Meta-analysis showed significant reduction in global IBS symptoms (Risk Ratio 0.69; 95% CI 0.54–0.88).
  • Benefits noted for abdominal pain & bloating.
Implementation (3 Phases)
  • 1. Substitution: Replace high FODMAP foods w/ low FODMAP choices (2-6 weeks).
  • 2. Reintroduction: Gradual reintroduction of individual FODMAPs to determine sensitivities.
  • 3. Personalization: Liberalize diet based on individual tolerance.
Clinical Considerations
  • Requires proper patient instruction, ideally by a trained GI dietitian.
  • Long-term over-restriction may lead to micronutrient deficiencies.
  • Need for high-quality, long-term data on efficacy, adherence, & harms.
11. Fiber Supplementation
  • Recommendation: Use soluble, but not insoluble, fiber to treat global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate.
Types of Fiber
  • Soluble fiber: Psyllium (ispaghula), oat bran, barley, beans.
  • Insoluble fiber: Wheat bran, whole grains.
Efficacy
  • Soluble fiber (ispaghula): Showed significant benefit for IBS symptoms (RR of not improving = 0.83).
  • Number Needed to Treat (NNT) = 7 (95% CI 4–25).
  • Insoluble fiber (bran): Provided no significant benefit.
Adverse Events
  • AEs reported in 36% of patients on fiber vs 25.1% on placebo (not statistically significant).
  • Fibers that ferment in the colon can aggravate bloating & flatulence.
Pharmacotherapy - General & Symptom-Based
  • Medications used for global IBS symptoms, not specific to one subtype.
12. Antispasmodics (US Market)
  • Recommendation: Against the use of antispasmodics currently available in the US for global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Available Agents & Evidence
  • Dicyclomine: 2 small, older trials w/ methodological flaws; showed some benefit but high side effect rates (69%).
  • Hyoscyamine: 1 small trial; response comparable to placebo w/ significantly higher AEs (87% vs 7%).
  • Hyoscine (Scopolamine): 3 trials outside US w/ mixed results; one showed benefit (76% vs 64% placebo) but data is weak.
Conclusion
  • Data is decades-old, of poor quality, w/ small sample sizes & non-standardized criteria.
  • Side effects are common, especially in the elderly.
13. Peppermint Oil
  • Recommendation: Suggest the use of peppermint to provide relief of global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • Mechanism: L-menthol blocks calcium channels → smooth muscle relaxation. Also modulates visceral sensation & has antimicrobial effects.
  • Meta-analysis (12 RCTs): RR for overall symptom improvement was 2.39 (95% CI 1.93–2.97) vs placebo.
  • NNT = 3 for overall symptoms; NNT = 4 for abdominal pain.
Safety & Formulation
  • AEs similar to placebo (9.3% vs 6.1%).
  • Heartburn can occur; enteric-coated formulations may mitigate this.
  • Only a small number of commercial supplements have been rigorously tested.
14. Probiotics
  • Recommendation: Suggest against probiotics for the treatment of global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Very low.
Evidence Challenges
  • Myriad of different products (single vs. multi-strain).
  • Studies are small, single-center, & lack rigorous endpoints.
  • Meta-analyses are difficult to interpret due to significant heterogeneity.
Meta-Analysis Findings
  • Combination probiotics showed a significant pooled effect (RR 0.79), but w/ high heterogeneity & publication bias.
  • Single species (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Saccharomyces) did not show significant pooled benefit.
  • No category of probiotic showed benefit for bloating.
23. Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
  • Recommendation: Use TCAs to treat global symptoms of IBS.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • Act as neuromodulators, improving visceral & central pain.
  • Anticholinergic effects can slow GI transit, helping w/ diarrhea.
  • RR of symptoms not improving = 0.65 (95% CI 0.55–0.77) vs placebo.
  • NNT = 4.5 (95% CI 3.5–7).
Dosing & Agent Selection
  • Start low & titrate slowly (e.g., amitriptyline or desipramine 10 mg).
  • Secondary amines (desipramine, nortriptyline): less sedation/constipation.
  • Tertiary amines (amitriptyline, imipramine): more antihistaminic/anticholinergic effects.
Safety & Adverse Events
  • AEs occur at a greater rate than placebo (RR 1.59).
  • Common AEs: dry mouth (36%), drowsiness, constipation (23%), insomnia (24%).
  • NNH = 9 (for any AE); NNH = 18 (for AEs prompting discontinuation).
Pharmacotherapy - IBS with Constipation (IBS-C)
  • Medications specifically evaluated for IBS-C.
15. Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)
  • Recommendation: Suggest against PEG products to relieve global IBS symptoms in IBS-C.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Rationale
  • PEG is an effective osmotic laxative for chronic constipation (NNT=3).
  • However, RCTs have failed to show it improves overall symptoms or pain in IBS-C.
  • It improves stool frequency & consistency, but not the key symptom of abdominal pain.
16. Chloride Channel Activators (Lubiprostone)
  • Recommendation: Use chloride channel activators to treat global IBS-C symptoms.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • Locally acting secretagogue; activates type-2 chloride channels → ↑ intestinal secretion & peristalsis.
  • Dose: 8 mcg twice daily for adult women w/ IBS-C.
  • Overall responder rate: 17.9% vs 10.1% placebo (P=0.0001).
  • NNT = 12.5.
Safety & Adverse Events
  • Most common AEs: Nausea (8-19%) & diarrhea (6-14%).
  • Nausea may be mitigated by taking w/ food.
  • Overall discontinuation rates are low (1.2%).
17. Guanylate Cyclase-C (GC-C) Activators
  • Recommendation: Use GC-C activators to treat global IBS-C symptoms.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: High.
Agents & Mechanism
  • Linaclotide (290 mcg daily) & Plecanatide (3 mg daily).
  • Activate GC-C receptors → ↑ intestinal fluid secretion & peristalsis.
  • Preclinical data suggests reduced activation of visceral nociceptive neurons.
Efficacy
  • Linaclotide: Overall responder rate ~34%; NNT = 6.
  • Plecanatide: Overall responder rate ~26%; NNT = 9.
  • Significant separation from placebo occurs by week 1 for both agents.
Safety & Adverse Events
  • Most common AE is diarrhea.
  • Linaclotide: ~20% diarrhea rate; 5% withdrew due to diarrhea.
  • Plecanatide: ~4.3% diarrhea rate; 1.2% withdrew due to diarrhea.
18. 5-HT4 Agonists (Tegaserod)
  • Recommendation: Suggest use in women < 65 years w/ ≤1 cardiovascular (CV) risk factor who have not adequately responded to secretagogues.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • Stimulates 5-HT4 receptor → initiates peristaltic reflex, accelerates GI transit.
  • Meta-analysis (11 trials): Patients on tegaserod were less likely to have persistent IBS-C symptoms vs placebo (RR 0.85; 95% CI 0.80–0.90).
  • Pivotal trials: 44-50% of patients on tegaserod met the primary endpoint (SGA) vs 37-39% on placebo.
Safety & CV Risk
  • Most common AE: Diarrhea (6% vs 2% placebo).
  • Withdrawn in 2007 due to a small excess of CV events.
  • Re-analysis found events occurred in patients w/ pre-existing CV risk factors.
Current Status
  • Re-approved in 2019 for a restricted population.
  • Indicated for women < 65 years w/ IBS-C & ≤1 CV risk factor.
  • Contraindicated in patients w/ >1 CV risk factor.
Pharmacotherapy - IBS with Diarrhea (IBS-D)
  • Medications specifically evaluated for IBS-D.
19. Bile Acid Sequestrants
  • Recommendation: Do not suggest the use of bile acid sequestrants to treat global IBS-D symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Very low.
Rationale & Prevalence of BAM
  • Bile Acid Malabsorption (BAM) may cause diarrhea in a subset of IBS-D patients.
  • Prevalence of BAM in IBS-D based on SeHCAT testing is 28.1% (CI 22.6%–34%).
  • Testing for BAM (SeHCAT, serum C4) is not widely available in the US.
Evidence
  • No placebo-controlled trials exist for global IBS-D symptoms.
  • One open-label study of colestipol showed 55.5% of subjects were responders.
  • Given lack of controlled trials, routine use is not recommended, but empiric therapy is a reasonable option if BAM is suspected.
20. Rifaximin
  • Recommendation: Use rifaximin to treat global IBS-D symptoms.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • Non-absorbed antibiotic targeting an abnormal gut microbiome.
  • Pivotal trials: After a 2-week course, 40.8% had improvement in pain & stool consistency vs 31.7% placebo.
  • Retreatment is safe & effective for symptom recurrence (approved for up to 2 retreatments).
  • NNT = 9.
Safety & Predictors
  • Excellent safety profile (NNH = 8,971).
  • No stable bacterial resistance or significant microbiome disruption noted.
  • Positive lactulose breath test may predict a higher response rate (56% vs 25%).
21. Alosetron
  • Recommendation: Use alosetron to relieve global IBS-D symptoms in women w/ severe symptoms who have failed conventional therapy.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Low.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • 5-HT3 antagonist; slows intestinal transit.
  • Meta-analysis: RR of symptom persistence = 0.79; NNT = 7.5.
  • Global symptom improvement in severe IBS-D was 12.2% to 32% greater than placebo.
Safety & REMS Program
  • Associated w/ rare but serious risk of ischemic colitis & complicated constipation.
  • Available only under a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) program.
  • Use is restricted to women w/ chronic (>6 months), severe IBS-D who have failed other therapies.
Risk Rates
  • Post-REMS incidence of ischemic colitis: 1.03 cases/1,000 patient-years.
  • Constipation is the most common AE; NNH = 10.
22. Mixed Opioid Agonists/Antagonists (Eluxadoline)
  • Recommendation: Suggest use of mixed opioid agonists/antagonists to treat global IBS-D symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Moderate.
Mechanism & Efficacy
  • Peripherally acting mixed mu- & kappa-opioid agonist, delta-opioid antagonist.
  • Significantly improved composite endpoint of pain & stool consistency vs placebo (e.g., 31.0% vs 19.5% at 26 weeks for 100mg dose).
  • NNT = 9-14.
Safety & Contraindications
  • Most common AE: Constipation (8%).
  • Serious AEs: Pancreatitis (0.4%) & Sphincter of Oddi spasm (0.5%).
  • Contraindicated in patients w/o a gallbladder, history of pancreatitis, alcohol abuse (>3 drinks/day), or severe hepatic impairment.
Central Neuromodulators & Psychotherapies
  • Interventions targeting the brain-gut axis.
24. Gut-Directed Psychotherapies (GDPs)
  • Recommendation: Suggest gut-directed psychotherapies be used to treat global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Very low.
Rationale & Types
  • Targets cognitive-affective drivers of symptoms (e.g., pain catastrophizing, stress sensitivity).
  • Types include: Cognitive-Behavior Therapy (CBT), Gut-Directed Hypnotherapy (GDH), Mindfulness.
  • Less effective in patients w/ comorbid mental health conditions; these patients need referral to non-GI mental health professionals.
Efficacy & Clinical Use
  • Level 1 evidence shows efficacy & durability, especially for CBT.
  • NNT = 4 (using IBS-Symptom Severity Scale).
  • Low risk, long-term benefits, and can be used for any IBS subtype (IBS-M, IBS-U).
Investigational & Other Therapies
  • Emerging or non-recommended therapies.
25. Fecal Microbiota Transplant (FMT)
  • Recommendation: Against the use of fecal transplant for the treatment of global IBS symptoms.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation.
  • Evidence Quality: Very low.
Rationale & Evidence
  • Conceptually appealing due to the role of the microbiome in IBS.
  • Meta-analysis showed no significant difference in global symptoms vs placebo (RR 0.93).
  • Results are conflicting & of very low quality.
Conclusion
  • Cannot be recommended at present.
  • Requires large, well-designed, placebo-controlled trials to determine any potential role, optimal donor, and best delivery technique.
