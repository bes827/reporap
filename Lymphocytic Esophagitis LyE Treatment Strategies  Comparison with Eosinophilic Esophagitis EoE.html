Treatment of Lymphocytic Esophagitis (LyE)
  • No consistent consensus for LyE treatment.
  • Regimens mainly based on EoE experience → clinical similarities.
  • Large studies proving efficacy of each treatment are lacking.
Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)
  • Efficacy debated; some studies show improvement.
  • Cochen et al. [18]: 60% LyE patients improved w/ PPIs.
  • Nguen et al. [29] & Sloan et al. [30]: confirmed PPI efficacy.
Variable Efficacy & Concerns
  • Lee et al. [31]: 24% clinical improvement, 17% endoscopic improvement w/ PPIs.
  • Lee et al. [31]: PPIs not best option → may delay more effective treatments.
  • Genta [32]: PPIs ineffective in some; question if improvement is PPI-dependent.
Positive Outcomes
  • Zaver et al. [13]: 86% clinical improvement in LyE patients following PPI treatment.
  • Improvement achieved w/ lower drug doses vs. EoE patient population.
Topical Corticosteroids
  • Fairly effective treatment option.
  • Pasricha et al. [17]: observed improvement in patient treated w/ swallowed fluticasone.
  • Kasirye et al. [33]: resolution of dysphagia in LyE patient after swallowed fluticasone (220 micrograms, 3x daily regimen).
Other Treatments in Pasricha Study
  • Improvement also achieved in patients after administration of PPIs, systemic prednisone, and a 'GI cocktail' (maalox, donnatal, lidocaine).
Endoscopic Esophageal Dilatation
  • Necessary intervention for esophageal strictures.
  • Habbal et al. [14]: 12.9% of LyE patients develop esophageal strictures.
Case Series by Jideh et al. [19]
  • One patient required endoscopic esophageal dilatation @ 12-month intervals + concurrent PPI therapy.
  • Another patient experienced spontaneous resolution of symptoms.
  • Third patient achieved minimum symptom relief after PPI + topical corticosteroid therapy.
Disease Course & Prognosis
  • LyE is a benign chronic disease.
  • No malignant transformations observed to date [9].
  • Cochen et al. [9]: some patients' symptoms resolved both w/ and w/o treatment (mainly PPIs).
Research Gap
  • Large studies to assess natural course of disease + effectiveness of treatment are still lacking.
Comparison of Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE) and Lymphocytic Esophagitis (LyE)
  • Clinical course of LyE very similar to EoE.
  • Dysphagia: leading pathological sign in both LyE + EoE patients.
Similarities
  • Endoscopic features remarkably similar.
  • Features range from unremarkable esophagus to fissures, white exudates, trachealization, and strictures.
Key Differences
  • Demographics: EoE mainly affects younger male patients.
  • Demographics: LyE more common in older females.
  • Histopathological presentation: Main difference.
Histopathological Distinctions
  • EoE: eosinophilic infiltrate (>15 eosinophils/HPF) in esophageal mucosa.
  • LyE: lymphocytic infiltrate.
Etiological Factors
  • EoE: Atopy is an important etiological factor; disease is multifactorial.
  • LyE: Etiological factors largely unclear; role of atopy debated but not proven [8,34].
Phenotypes of Esophagitis (Rubio et al. [35] Study)
  • Identified four phenotypes of esophagitis based on histopathological + immunohistochemical findings.
  • Evaluated biopsies from 311 patients using dual staining procedure (H&E-CD3).
Diagnostic Criteria in Study
  • LyE: >40 lymphocytes/HPF.
  • Lymphocytic infiltration: <39 lymphocytes/HPF.
  • EoE: >15 eosinophils/HPF.
Study Findings
  • 28.3% (n=88) of patients diagnosed w/ LyE.
  • 21.2% (n=66) of patients diagnosed w/ EoE.
  • 10.6% (n=33) of patients diagnosed w/ concomitant EoE + LyE.
  • 39.9% (n=124) of patients diagnosed w/ lymphocytic infiltration.
Immunohistochemical Observations
  • No immunohistochemical differences found between lymphocytes present in EoE + LyE.
  • Possible that lymphocytic infiltrate initiates eosinophilic inflammation [35].
Role of T Cells in EoE
  • Pasha et al. [36]: T cells play an important role in development of EoE.
  • Particularly significant in the active form of the disease.
