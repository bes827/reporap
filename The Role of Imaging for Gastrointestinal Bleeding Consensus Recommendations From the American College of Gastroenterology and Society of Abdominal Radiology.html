The Role of Imaging for Gastrointestinal Bleeding: Consensus Recommendations From the American College of Gastroenterology and Society of Abdominal Radiology
  • Journal: The American Journal of GASTROENTEROLOGY
  • Publication Date: MARCH 2024
  • DOI: 10.14309/ajg.0000000000002631
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the minimum bleeding rate detectable by modern multidetector CT Angiography (CTA)?
  • 2. For a 99mTc-RBC scan, a Time to Positive (TTP) of less than how many minutes is associated with a significantly higher diagnostic yield on subsequent catheter angiography?
  • 3. What is the technical success rate of embolization for acute lower GI bleeding, and what is the approximate re-bleeding rate?
  • 4. In adults, what is the reported sensitivity of a Meckel scan for detecting a Meckel diverticulum, and how does it compare to the sensitivity in children?
  • 5. What is the preferred oral contrast agent for CT Enterography (CTE) when evaluating for suspected small bowel bleeding, and why?
Overview of GI Bleeding
  • GI bleeding is the most common GI diagnosis → hospitalization in the US.
  • Prompt diagnosis & treatment are critical to improve outcomes.
Upper GI Bleeding (UGIB)
  • Definition: Bleeding from esophagus, stomach, or duodenum.
  • Accounts for ≈80% of bleeding events.
Lower GI Bleeding (LGIB)
  • Definition: Bleeding distal to the ileocecal valve.
  • Accounts for ≈15%–30% of all GI bleeding events.
Small Bowel (Midgut) Bleeding
  • Definition: Bleeding between the ligament of Treitz & the ileocecal valve.
  • Accounts for ≈5%–10% of GI bleeding events.
Diagnostic Testing in Overt LGIB
  • Evaluation of active bleeding originating from the colon.
CT Angiography (CTA)
  • Primary goals: Determine location & assess intensity of bleed.
  • Secondary goal: Identify cause of bleeding.
Technique
  • Multiphase CT technique required to confirm contrast extravasation.
  • - Noncontrast phase: Identifies high-attenuation material that can mimic bleeding.
  • - Late arterial phase: ≈25–35 seconds post-injection.
  • - Portal venous (60-70s) or late venous (70-90s) phase.
Key Technical Points
  • Bolus tracking is suitable for patients w/ varying cardiac outputs.
  • Oral contrast should be avoided → delays scanning & can obscure bleeding.
  • Post-processed 3D images help guide subsequent angiography.
Consensus Recommendations: Technique
  • 1. Unenhanced images (conventional or virtual) should be acquired in all cases.
  • 2. Images should be acquired during a late arterial phase AND a portal venous or delayed phase.
  • 3. No oral contrast should be administered.
  • 4. 3D CTA images can be generated to guide subsequent conventional angiography.
  • 5. Dual-energy CT may be used to improve visibility of contrast extravasation.
Performance Data
  • Highly sensitive (85%–90%), specific (92%), and accurate (94%–95%).
  • Can detect bleeding rates as low as 0.1 mL/min w/ multidetector CT.
  • Larger extravasation volumes on CTA → associated w/ higher transfusion needs & need for hemostatic therapy.
Prognostic Value
  • A negative CTA is associated with a ↓ rate of rebleeding and need for intervention.
Advantages
  • Noninvasive, short examination time, widely available.
  • Well-suited for hemodynamically unstable patients.
  • Provides a vascular & enteric anatomical roadmap for planning subsequent procedures (endoscopic, IR, or surgical).
Limitations
  • Requires IV contrast administration.
  • Involves ionizing radiation, which can be higher than standard CT due to multiple phases.
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • 1. Should be performed as the first diagnostic study in hemodynamically unstable patients.
  • 2. Could be considered the first-line study in stable patients w/ high suspicion of active bleeding.
  • 3. Not indicated as a first-line test in stable patients where bleeding has subsided.
Nuclear Medicine: 99mTc-RBC Scan
  • Preferred radiopharmaceutical: 99mTc-labeled red blood cells (99mTc-RBCs).
  • Long intravascular half-life allows continuous imaging for several hours.
Technique
  • Dynamic imaging for a minimum of 1 hour.
  • Reviewing images in 10-15 minute sequences may ↓ time to catheter angiography.
  • Single-photon emission CT (SPECT)/CT can clarify indeterminate findings.
Consensus Recommendations: Technique
  • 1. The in vitro RBC labeling method is preferred due to highest labeling efficiency.
  • 2. Imaging should be continued for 1 hour if no bleeding is detected.
Performance Data
  • Sensitivity: 93%; Specificity: 95%.
  • Can detect bleeding rates as low as 0.1 mL/min (clinical) or 0.04 mL/min (experimental).
  • Time to positive (TTP) < 9 minutes → associated w/ a 6x greater likelihood of a positive catheter angiography (CA).
  • TTP > 9 minutes → accurately predicted negative CA in 94% of patients.
Comparison to CTA
  • One study showed 99mTc-RBC scans had lower accuracy (55.4%) vs CTA (96%).
  • SPECT/CT may improve localization accuracy compared to planar imaging alone.
Advantages
  • High sensitivity for intermittent bleeding due to prolonged imaging time (up to 24 hours).
  • Lower radiation dose compared to CTA.
Limitations
  • Only for hemodynamically stable patients.
  • Long preparation & imaging times.
  • Incorrect localization reported in 10%–33% of cases.
  • Labeling efficiency can be ↓ by low hematocrit, recent transfusions, hemoglobinopathies, and some medications (e.g., heparin).
Common Pitfalls
  • False positives can occur from physiologic activity or other conditions:
  • - Ureters, penile activity
  • - Splenosis, pancreatic pseudocysts
  • - Non-enteric bleeding/hematoma
  • *True GI bleeding changes in intensity and moves over time.
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • 1. Perform in a hemodynamically stable patient with:
  • - Evidence of ongoing LGIB
  • - Negative evaluation with colonoscopy
  • - CTA is negative, contraindicated, or not available.
Catheter Angiography (CA)
  • Invasive procedure for diagnosis & treatment (embolization).
  • Most commonly performed for unstable patients w/ active LGIB who are not candidates for endoscopy.
Technique
  • Ideally performed after CTA to identify the vessel territory.
  • Access via common femoral or left radial artery.
  • Superselective angiogram of the vasa recta is performed, followed by embolization.
Embolization Goal & Agents
  • Goal: ↓ blood flow to achieve hemostasis while maintaining collateral perfusion to prevent bowel ischemia.
  • Agents: Microcoils or glue (N-butyl 2-cyanoacrylate).
Consensus Recommendations: Technique
  • 1. Can be performed through common femoral or radial artery access.
  • 2. Use permanent agents (microcoils, glue) to embolize vasa recta at the bleeding site.
  • 3. Embolization should NOT be performed without active extravasation on angiography.
Performance Data
  • Technical success of embolization: >95%.
  • Recurrent bleeding may occur in up to 25% of patients.
  • Glue embolization may have a better impact on recurrent bleeding rates than microcoils.
Advantages
  • Ability to both diagnose and definitively treat at the same time.
  • High technical success rate with relatively low rate of recurrent bleeding.
Limitations
  • Invasive procedure with risks: hematoma, dissection, AV fistula.
  • Bowel ischemia risk: up to 10% (most asymptomatic), more common with glue.
  • Only effective if bleeding is active at the time of the procedure.
  • Requires iodinated contrast and significant radiation exposure.
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • 1. If CTA is negative for GI bleeding, CA is not indicated in most cases.
  • 2. In unstable patients w/ active extravasation on CTA, CA w/ embolization can be used as the primary treatment.
  • 3. Provocative CA can be performed for recurrent intermittent LGIB if all other modalities have failed.
Diagnostic Testing in Suspected Small Bowel Bleeding
  • Evaluation of bleeding originating between the ligament of Treitz and the ileocecal valve.
CT Enterography (CTE)
  • Designed to optimize evaluation of the small bowel wall.
  • Requires ingestion of a large volume (≈1.5 L) of neutral oral contrast to distend the bowel.
Technique
  • Neutral oral contrast is preferred: hyperenhancing lesions (vascular, inflammatory, neoplastic) are more conspicuous against low-density fluid.
  • IV contrast is required.
  • Can be single-phase or multiphase.
Phase Selection
  • Single-phase (enteric or portal venous): Adequate for inflammation & most masses.
  • Multiphase (arterial + enteric/portal venous ± delayed): Improves detection & characterization of vascular lesions.
Consensus Recommendations: Technique
  • 1. Use multiphase technique in patients >40 years old (vascular lesions are common).
  • 2. Multiphase CTE should include at least arterial, and enteric or portal venous phases.
  • 3. 'Multiphase CTE' is the recommended term for this indication.
  • 4. Single phase is adequate for inflammatory conditions (Crohn's, radiation enteritis) & most malignancies.
  • 5. Administer neutral enteric contrast in divided doses starting 1 hour before CTE.
Performance Data
  • Data is heterogeneous; most studies include patients with overt bleeding.
  • Occult bleeding: Sensitivity 30.9%, Specificity 69.4%.
  • Overt bleeding: Sensitivity 39.5%, Specificity 73.9%.
  • Diagnostic yield is lower in occult bleeding (0%–33.3%) vs. overt bleeding (22.4%–66.7%).
Advantages
  • Greater sensitivity for small bowel masses, especially mural-based ones.
  • Allows visualization of extraintestinal structures (mesentery, vessels).
  • Can be first-line study if risk of video capsule retention is high (e.g., prior surgery, Crohn's disease).
Limitations
  • Use of ionizing radiation and IV contrast.
  • Neutral oral contrast may theoretically dilute active contrast extravasation.
  • Incompletely distended bowel may obscure or mimic pathology.
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • 1. Perform in stable patients w/ ongoing suspected small bowel bleeding after negative EGD/colonoscopy.
  • 2. If bleeding is brisk w/ hemodynamic instability, perform CTA instead of CTE.
  • 3. First-line test if patients are at ↑ risk for video capsule retention.
  • 4. First-line study if small bowel neoplasm is suspected.
  • 5. Can be performed if capsule endoscopy is negative but suspicion for ongoing bleeding remains.
Meckel Scan
  • Uses IV 99mTc pertechnetate, which accumulates in ectopic gastric mucosa found in a Meckel diverticulum.
Performance Data
  • Less sensitive in adults (63%) compared to children (85%).
  • Most symptomatic Meckel diverticula are found in children and young adults.
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • 1. Can be considered for unexplained intermittent GI bleeding in children & adolescents after negative endoscopic evaluation and cross-sectional imaging.
Diagnostic Testing in Nonvariceal UGIB
  • Evaluation of bleeding from the esophagus, stomach, or duodenum not related to varices.
CT Angiography (CTA)
  • Paucity of data compared to LGIB.
  • May be helpful if endoscopy cannot identify the source or is not feasible.
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • In the setting of ongoing bleeding, CTA can be considered:
  • - If patient is not suitable for EGD or no in-house emergency GI coverage is available.
  • - After a negative EGD or if EGD is unable to identify the bleeding site.
Catheter Angiography (CA)
  • Indicated when endoscopy is unsuccessful in achieving hemostasis or for recurrent bleeding.
  • May be first-line treatment in select cases (e.g., hepatic pseudoaneurysm).
Technique
  • Target the bleeding vessel identified on prior imaging/endoscopy.
  • Interrogate both celiac and superior mesenteric arteries.
  • Superselective embolization is preferred; endoscopic clips can help guide localization.
Outcomes
  • Technical success rate: up to 95%.
  • Clinical success rate: ≈67%.
  • Rebleeding rate: ≈33% on the first attempt.
  • Complication rate: up to 10% (access site issues, renal damage, non-target embolization).
Consensus Recommendations: Role/Indications
  • 1. Indicated when an EGD is unsuccessful in achieving initial hemostasis.
  • 2. Indicated if the patient experiences recurrent bleeding after a successful initial EGD and a repeat EGD is unsuccessful or not recommended.
