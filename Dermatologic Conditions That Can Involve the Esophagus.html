Dermatologic Conditions That Can Involve the Esophagus
  • Gastroenterology & Hepatology, April 2025, Volume 21, Issue 4.
  • Author: David Katzka, MD, Professor of Medicine, Esophagology and Swallowing Center, New York-Presbyterian, Columbia University Irving Medical Center.
  • Focus: Etiologies, clinical features, diagnosis, management, and surveillance of dermatologic conditions affecting the esophagus.
Etiologies of Esophageal Dermatoses
  • Underlying etiologies are divided into several categories:
  • Autoimmune disorders: Lichen planus, pemphigoid disease.
  • Inherited disorders: Epidermolysis bullosa dystrophica, tylosis.
Autoimmune Etiologies
  • Includes disorders where the body's immune system attacks its own tissues.
  • Examples: Lichen planus and pemphigoid disease.
Lichen Planus (Autoimmune)
  • Autoimmune disorder that can affect the skin and mucous membranes, including the esophagus.
  • Characterized by inflammation and potential scarring.
Associated Conditions
  • May be associated with hepatitis C infection.
  • Gastroenterologists should check for underlying disorders.
Pemphigoid Disease (Autoimmune)
  • Autoimmune blistering disease that can affect the skin and mucous membranes.
  • Esophageal involvement is possible.
Associated Conditions
  • May be associated with malignancy.
  • Gastroenterologists should check for underlying disorders.
Inherited Etiologies
  • Genetic disorders that are passed down through families.
  • Examples: Epidermolysis bullosa dystrophica and tylosis.
Epidermolysis Bullosa Dystrophica (Inherited)
  • Genetic disorder causing fragile skin and mucous membranes.
  • Esophageal involvement can lead to strictures and scarring.
Tylosis (Inherited)
  • Rare genetic disorder characterized by thickening of the skin on the palms and soles.
  • High risk of esophageal squamous cell carcinoma.
Infectious Etiology
  • Mucosal Candidiasis: Occurs in immunocompromised patients.
Mucosal Candidiasis
  • Fungal infection of the mucous membranes, including the esophagus.
  • Common in individuals with weakened immune systems.
Clinical and Endoscopic Features
  • Vary depending on the specific condition.
  • Lichen planus: White or lacy appearance, sloughing, strictures.
  • Tylosis and pemphigus: Esophageal mucosa may appear normal on endoscopy.
Lichen Planus (Clinical/Endoscopic)
  • Commonly presents with a white or lacy appearance on endoscopy.
  • Sloughing is observed on biopsy.
  • Strictures are common.
Scarring
  • Can lead to scarring, manifesting as ring formation.
  • Similar to rings seen in eosinophilic esophagitis, but can occur with any scarring process.
Tylosis and Pemphigus (Clinical/Endoscopic)
  • Esophageal mucosa may appear completely normal on endoscopy.
  • Difficult to differentiate from other causes of strictures.
Pemphigus and Epidermolysis Bullosa Dystrophica (Biopsy)
  • Severe sloughing with biopsies is common, except in pemphigus and epidermolysis bullosa dystrophica.
  • Biopsies can create strictures in these conditions.
Importance of Considering Dermatologic Disorders
  • Diagnosis is not always obvious due to bland endoscopic appearance.
  • Necessary to keep dermatologic disorders in mind during evaluation.
Correlation Between Skin and Esophageal Disease Activity
  • Generally, disease activity in the skin mirrors that in the esophagus.
  • Exceptions: Pemphigus may present with initial esophageal involvement; lichen planus may present with dysphagia as the initial symptom.
General Correlation
  • For most diseases, the activity in the skin generally reflects the activity in the esophagus.
Pemphigus (Initial Presentation)
  • May present with initial esophageal involvement.
Epidermolysis Bullosa Dystrophica (Diagnosis)
  • Diagnosed in infancy by skin manifestations before esophageal involvement.
Tylosis (Presentation)
  • Usually presents in teenage years with hypertrophic skin on palms and soles before esophageal involvement.
Lichen Planus (Initial Symptom)
  • Patients may present with dysphagia as their initial symptom.
  • Determine if primary disorder of esophagus or extraesophageal signs are present.
Prevalence of Esophageal Dermatoses
  • Most are rare, particularly inherited disorders.
  • Lichen planus may be more common than realized, often underscored in patients with strictures of unclear etiology.
Rarity
  • Most esophageal dermatoses are rare, especially the inherited disorders.
Lichen Planus (Prevalence)
  • May be more common than previously thought.
  • Often unrecognized in patients with esophageal strictures.
Lichen Planus (Clinical Experience)
  • In practice, proximal strictures in middle-to-older-age women are often caused by lichen planus.
  • Rarer than reflux disease or eosinophilic esophagitis, but more common than appreciated.
Challenges in Diagnosing Esophageal Dermatoses
  • Thinking about them, nonspecific endoscopic findings, and reliance on classic biopsy findings.
  • Diagnosis often requires combining clinical presentation, endoscopic signs, and nonspecific biopsy findings.
Awareness
  • Given their rare status, esophageal dermatoses are not always considered.
  • Important to keep them in mind when evaluating esophageal abnormalities or strictures.
Endoscopic Findings
  • Often not specific; may only see a bland stricture.
  • Seek classic signs like sloughing or endoscopic Nikolsky sign with pemphigus.
Biopsy Findings
  • Classic findings (e.g., Civatte bodies in lichen planus) or specific antibodies are often not present.
  • Diagnosis requires combining clinical, endoscopic, and nonspecific biopsy findings.
When to Consider Esophageal Lichen Planus
  • Recurrent strictures unresponsive to PPIs and dilation, sloughing, and white lacy appearance of mucosa.
  • Consider in patients with recurrent strictures of unclear etiology.
Classic Presentation
  • Proximal esophageal stricture in a woman with a history of genital or oral lichen planus.
Isolated Esophageal Lichen Planus
  • Can occur in any part of the esophagus and can affect men as well.
Typical History
  • Numerous dilations of esophageal stricture with rapid recurrence.
  • Lack of response to proton pump inhibitors.
  • Nonspecific biopsy findings.
Endoscopic Suspicion
  • Sloughing with biopsy.
  • White lacy appearance of the mucosa.
Key Consideration
  • Recurrent stricture with no other clear etiology.
  • Does not respond well to dilation.
Esophageal Dermatoses Requiring Surveillance
  • Lichen planus, tylosis, and epidermolysis bullosa dystrophica are premalignant.
  • Lichen planus: Surveillance every 2-3 years. Tylosis: Surveillance starting at age 20 years. Epidermolysis bullosa dystrophica: Surveillance in the 20s, every couple of years.
Lichen Planus (Surveillance)
  • Incidence of cancer is about 100 times more than in the general population.
  • Surveillance every 2 or 3 years.
Tylosis (Surveillance)
  • Carries up to a 95% risk of esophageal squamous carcinoma by age 65 years.
  • Surveillance starting at age 20 years.
Epidermolysis Bullosa Dystrophica (Surveillance)
  • Reported with cancer.
  • Surveillance is controversial due to risk of scarring from biopsies.
  • Start surveillance in the 20s, every couple of years.
Management of Dermatosis with Esophageal Involvement
  • Managed in conjunction with a dermatologist.
  • Gastroenterologists can administer topical corticosteroids. Other topical therapies (tacrolimus) may be tried.
Collaboration
  • Gastroenterologists must manage in conjunction with a dermatologist.
  • Dermatologists are more familiar with systemic therapies.
Gastroenterologist Role
  • Administer topical corticosteroids, similar to eosinophilic esophagitis management.
  • Consider other topical therapies like tacrolimus.
Therapeutic Options
  • Often, patients have few therapeutic options other than systemic therapy.
  • Topical therapies are worth trying, although data are limited to case series.
Areas for Further Research
  • Understanding why some patients with systemic dermatoses develop esophageal involvement.
  • Better endoscopic and histologic markers for definitive diagnosis. Education of gastroenterologists.
Etiology
  • Need to understand why some patients with systemic dermatoses develop esophageal involvement while others do not.
Diagnostic Markers
  • Better endoscopic and histologic markers are needed to make a definitive diagnosis.
Education
  • Education of gastroenterologists is key.
  • Many disorders, particularly lichen planus, remain undiagnosed for years.
Impact of Early Diagnosis
  • Early diagnosis and treatment can effectively control dysphagia and improve patient outcomes.
