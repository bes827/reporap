5 Prompting Secrets AI Engineers Use (That Most People Don’t Know)

Core Problem: Generic AI Responses
  • Most users treat AI like a search engine (e.g., Google).
  • This often leads to incomplete, generic answers.
  • AI engineers achieve superior results by prompting differently: cleaner, sharper.
Comparison: Casual vs. Engineer Prompting
  • Casual users: Type a question, hope for useful output.
  • Engineers: Structure prompts to elicit expert-level, specific responses.
Observed Trend
  • Testing major LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok) reveals a consistent pattern.
  • Effective users employ specific prompting 'tricks'.
Secret 1: Give the AI a Job Title
  • Assign a specific role to the AI before asking a question.
  • This immediately shifts the AI's persona from a 'helpful assistant' to an expert.
Mechanism
  • AI model remains the same 'under the hood'.
  • Role assignment primes the AI to access and present information from a specialized perspective.
Example (General)
  • Instead of: 'How do I price my SaaS?'
  • Try: 'You’re a SaaS pricing strategist who’s worked with 100+ B2B companies. How should I price my project management tool?'
Application for Physicians
  • Instead of: 'What are the treatment options for hypertension?'
  • Try: 'You’re a board-certified cardiologist specializing in refractory hypertension. What are the latest evidence-based treatment options for a patient with uncontrolled blood pressure despite 3-drug therapy?'
Benefit
  • Answers feel like they originate from a field expert.
  • ↑ relevance, ↑ specificity of information.
Secret 2: Stack Your Context
  • Place all important contextual information at the beginning of the prompt.
  • Avoid burying critical details at the end.
Mechanism
  • AI processes information sequentially.
  • Leading with context sets the stage for the entire response.
  • Prevents 'broken' or vague outputs.
Example (General)
  • Instead of: 'Write me a cold email. Oh, and it’s for enterprise clients, and we’re B2B, and it’s about our new feature…'
  • Try: 'Context: B2B SaaS, targeting enterprise CTOs, launching a new AI feature. Write a cold email.'
Application for Physicians
  • Instead of: 'What's the differential for chest pain? Patient is 65, has CAD, and ECG shows ST elevation.'
  • Try: 'Patient context: 65 y/o male, hx of CAD, presenting with chest pain, ECG shows ST elevation in leads V2-V4. Provide differential diagnosis for acute chest pain.'
Benefit
  • Sets the stage upfront.
  • ↓ back-and-forth, ↑ usable draft on the first try.
Secret 3: Tell It How to Answer
  • Explicitly define the desired format or structure of the AI's output.
  • Prevents lengthy, unstructured, or 'essay-like' responses.
Mechanism
  • Analogous to defining 'return types' in programming code.
  • Guides the AI to shape its output according to user needs.
Example (General)
  • Instead of: 'Analyze my landing page copy.'
  • Try: 'Analyze my landing page copy. Format it as: 1) What’s working, 2) What’s broken, 3) Specific fixes with examples.'
Application for Physicians
  • Instead of: 'Summarize the new diabetes guidelines.'
  • Try: 'Summarize the latest ADA guidelines for Type 2 Diabetes management. Format as: 1) Key diagnostic criteria, 2) First-line pharmacotherapy recommendations (with specific drug classes), 3) Non-pharmacological interventions, 4) Monitoring parameters.'
Benefit
  • Output is structured, to the point, and immediately useful.
  • ↑ clarity, ↑ actionability.
Secret 4: Show, Don’t Just Tell
  • Provide concrete examples of the desired output style, tone, or content.
  • This acts as direct 'training data' within the prompt.
Mechanism
  • AI learns from patterns.
  • Examples guide the AI to reflect a specific style rather than guessing.
  • Especially effective for creative or nuanced content.
Example (General)
  • Instead of: 'Write product descriptions.'
  • Try: 'Write product descriptions like these: Example 1: 'This sleek water bottle keeps drinks cold for 24 hours.' Example 2: 'A lightweight backpack designed for weekend getaways.' Now write one for: Smart standing desk.'
Application for Physicians
  • Instead of: 'Draft a patient education handout for flu.'
  • Try: 'Draft a patient education summary for Type 2 Diabetes, using this tone and complexity: 'Example 1: Diabetes is a condition where your body has trouble using sugar for energy. It's important to manage it to stay healthy.' Now draft one for: Managing osteoarthritis, maintaining a similar tone and simplicity.'
Benefit
  • AI reflects the shown style.
  • ↓ guessing, ↑ accuracy in desired output characteristics.
  • Works well for patient communication, consent forms, or research summaries.
Secret 5: Force It to Think Out Loud
  • Instruct the AI to detail its decision-making process or reasoning steps before providing a final answer.
  • Don't just ask for the conclusion; ask for the journey.
Mechanism
  • Encourages a 'whiteboard session' approach.
  • AI lays out its logic, allowing users to understand the rationale.
  • Crucial for complex decisions or analyses.
Example (General)
  • Instead of: 'Should I pivot my startup?'
  • Try: 'I’m considering pivoting my startup. Walk through your decision process step by step: 1) Analyze current metrics, 2) Identify problems, 3) Evaluate alternatives, 4) Give me your recommendation with reasoning.'
Application for Physicians
  • Instead of: 'What's the diagnosis for this patient?'
  • Try: 'Patient presents with acute abdominal pain, fever, and elevated WBC. Walk through your diagnostic process step by step: 1) Initial assessment and red flags, 2) Key differential diagnoses (with supporting/refuting points), 3) Recommended investigations (imaging, labs), 4) Potential management strategies based on likely diagnoses, 5) Provide your most likely diagnosis with reasoning.'
Benefit
  • AI lays out the logic before the conclusion.
  • ↑ transparency, ↑ helpfulness for big decisions.
  • Allows for critical evaluation of AI's reasoning.
Summary of Effective Prompting Habits
  • These five habits transform AI interaction from basic querying to skilled 'prompt engineering'.
  • Analogous to moving from a quick script to production-ready code.
Key Habits
  • 1. Give it a role.
  • 2. Lead with context.
  • 3. Define the format.
  • 4. Drop in a couple of examples.
  • 5. Make it think step by step.
