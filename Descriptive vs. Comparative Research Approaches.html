Descriptive Approaches
(Qualitative Description)
  • Focus: Understanding & portraying phenomena as experienced by participants.
  • Goal: Minimize external interpretation.
Goals & Characteristics
  • Primary Aim: Describe participant experiences in their own language w/ minimal interpretation.
  • Goal: Discover interviewee's own framework of meanings; avoid imposing researcher's assumptions.
  • Analysis: Inductive → categories & explanations emerge gradually from the data.
Common Outcome
  • Most common result of a purely descriptive approach is concept development or model building.
Example: Conventional Content Analysis
  • Use Case: To describe a phenomenon, particularly when existing theory or research literature is limited.
Process
  • Researchers immerse themselves in the data.
  • Codes are derived directly from the text data.
  • Categories & their names flow naturally from the information gathered.
Application Example
  • Study: Emotional reactions of terminally ill patients in hospice care.
  • Method: Use conventional content analysis w/o a pre-existing theoretical framework.
  • Goal: Identify & describe emotions based entirely on participants’ unique perspectives.
Example in SICP Study
  • Methodology: Qualitative description.
  • Objective: To explore clinicians’ experiences w/ the Serious Illness Care Program (SICP) one year after implementation.
Descriptive Findings
  • Reported how SICP supported changes in clinician behavior.
  • Showed a shift in the focus of goals-of-care conversations.
  • Detailed how SICP influenced clinicians personally & professionally.
Comparative & Validation Approaches
  • Involves evaluating data against external benchmarks, existing theories, or using quantification (counting).
  • Goal: Validate or extend prior knowledge.
Comparison in Research Questions
  • Qualitative research addresses questions that are often different from quantitative research.
Quantitative vs. Qualitative Comparison
  • Quantitative Approach: Measures statistical correlates of outcomes; compares prescribing rates between groups.
  • Qualitative Sampling: Does not normally seek statistical representativeness.
Applied Qualitative Research
  • Framework Approach: Often used in applied policy research w/ short timescales.
  • Design: Analysis can be linked w/ quantitative findings.
Analytical Approaches w/ Comparative Elements
  • Two main approaches incorporate comparative or validation elements: Directed & Summative Content Analysis.
1. Directed Content Analysis
(Theory Validation/Extension)
  • Guided by existing theory or prior research.
  • Inherently comparative against that framework.
Goal
  • Primary Goal: To validate or extend conceptually a theoretical framework or theory.
Process: Deductive Category Application
  • Analysis starts deductively.
  • Uses existing theory to define key concepts as initial coding categories.
Findings & Comparison
  • Findings offer supporting & non-supporting evidence for the guiding theory.
  • Example: Compare incidence of codes from a pre-existing model (e.g., Kübler-Ross’s stages of grief) vs. newly identified reactions.
  • May use rank order comparisons of frequency.
2. Summative Content Analysis
(Quantification & Contextual Comparison)
  • Integrates basic quantification (counting) to provide a foundation for contextual interpretation & comparison.
Process
  • Step 1 (Manifest Analysis): Begins by identifying & quantifying certain words or content in the text.
  • Step 2 (Latent Analysis): Followed by interpreting the underlying meaning of the words or content.
Comparison Example
  • Count the frequency of explicit terms for death vs. euphemisms.
  • Analysis then compares usage patterns based on context or speaker characteristics.
  • Example: Compare frequencies of euphemisms vs. direct terms across different types of speakers or demographics.
