A Roadmap for AI in GI Fellowship Training
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, Vol. 121 (2026), pp. 813–817
  • Topic: Integrating Artificial Intelligence into Gastroenterology Fellowship Education
  • Author(s): Justin L. Sewell, MD, PhD, MPH; Neeral L. Shah, MD, FACG; Amit Gupta, MD, MHPE; Alexandra T. Strauss, MD, PhD
1. Why AI Matters in GI Training
  • AI technology is evolving rapidly
  • Many program directors feel urgency to adopt AI
  • Programs that avoid AI may become less desirable
  • GI fellows are increasingly facile with AI each year
  • AI solutions must be implemented with attention to risk
  • Few GI educators have computer science or AI training
2. AI Competencies for GI Fellows
2.1. Six Core Competency Domains
  • Basic knowledge of AI and its applications
  • Social, ethical, and legal implications
  • Practical use of AI applications in clinical workplace
  • Interpretation and application of AI tools and output for patient/population care
  • Practice-based learning and improvement using AI tools
  • AI as tool for scholarship and research
2.2. Entrustable Professional Activities (EPAs)
  • EPAs = identifiable units of work trainees can reliably perform
  • AI-related EPAs have not yet been articulated
  • EPAs are a promising way to conceptualize fellow skills with AI
3. AI as Tool for Clinical Decision-Making
3.1. Types of Large Language Models (LLMs)
3.1.1. General Purpose LLMs
  • Examples: ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity
  • Significant limitations for clinical questions
  • Often produce irrelevant or inaccurate answers
  • Can create hallucinations with references that do not exist
  • Answers are not evidence-based
3.1.2. Data Extraction LLMs
  • Example: ChatRWD
  • Uses data from millions of real patients
  • Recognizes patterns in diagnosis and treatment
  • Provides structured answers to clinical questions
  • Can do hypothesis generation by imputing results and statistical calculations
3.1.3. Evidence Retrieval-Augmented LLMs
  • Example: OpenEvidence — most commonly used tool currently
  • 358,000 users in a month during 2024
  • 8.5 million clinical queries monthly in 2025
  • Has collaborations with: NEJM, JAMA
  • Provides hyperlinked articles and actual article figures within output
  • Generates answers 86% of the time based on articles/guidelines
  • Answers are at least partially relevant to posed question
  • DeepConsult = additional tool for in-depth investigation
3.2. Recommendations for LLM Use
  • Prohibit general purpose LLMs as stand-alone clinical decision-making tools
  • Encourage careful use of data extraction and evidence retrieval-augmented LLMs
  • All providers must use these tools within clinical context
  • Always ensure accuracy and relevance by referring to primary or secondary literature
  • Never make decisions based on LLM output alone
4. AI in Clinic and Inpatient Settings
4.1. Ambient Scribe Technology
  • Captures provider-patient conversations automatically
  • Generates visit transcript and draft documentation
  • Creates opportunity for cognitive apprenticeship
4.1.1. Teaching Benefits
  • Faculty can demonstrate careful editing of AI-generated notes
  • "Modeling" and "scaffolding" of note-writing skills
  • Faculty can review transcript/audio of trainee-patient encounters
  • Provides targeted feedback on: empathy, explanation style, diagnostic framing
  • "Fading" = gradually encourage independent documentation as skills improve
4.1.2. Practical Uses
  • Trainees can rehearse patient communication for challenging scenarios
  • Example: explaining need for early delivery to pregnant patient with acute liver disease
  • Faculty can quickly outline teaching pearls or chalk talks tailored to actual cases
4.2. EHR-Embedded AI Technologies
  • Predictive models embedded in EHR
  • Example: predicting risk of readmission for inflammatory bowel disease patients
  • Educators can teach: when to use AI tools, how to interpret results (e.g., accuracy)
  • Allows personalized support based on trainee's zone of proximal development
4.3. Risks and Challenges
4.3.1. Outsourcing Cognition
  • Risk: learners accept AI output uncritically
  • May undermine diagnostic acumen and learning
  • LLMs may introduce: hallucinations, implicit biases, recommendations ignoring patient complexity
4.3.2. Mitigation Strategies
  • Faculty should use AI outputs as teaching moments
  • Ask trainees to critically appraise suggestions
  • Justify divergences from AI recommendations
  • Recognize when algorithmic recommendations warrant skepticism
  • Model critical thinking and discuss limitations explicitly
4.3.3. Implementation Recommendations
  • GI fellow access to ambient scribe technology should match other trainees at institution
  • Training and competence confirmation required before use among fellows
  • Patients must be informed and provide assent for use
  • Fellows and faculty responsible for reviewing and editing AI output
  • Fellow notes should be regularly reviewed to confirm accuracy
5. AI in the Endoscopy Suite
5.1. Computer-Assisted Detection (CADe) and Diagnosis (CADx)
  • Improve detection of adenomas and other pathology
  • Especially helpful for less experienced endoscopists
  • Trainees experience greater difficulties with polyp detection due to cognitive recognition errors
5.1.1. When to Introduce CADe/CADx
  • Delay introduction until basic endoscopy skills are solidified (after first-year)
  • Minimizes cognitive overload early in training
  • Balances concern for dependency on AI and deskilling through cognitive shortcuts
  • Second-year or third-year use must be intentional with dedicated curriculum
5.1.2. Faculty Control Recommendations
  • Faculty should initially control when to turn on/off CADe or CADx
  • Gradually taper to give fellows more autonomy as they near graduation
  • Periodic procedures with system completely off recommended for faculty and fellows
5.2. Cognitive Skills Development with AI
  • Fellows can assess own ability to identify anatomic structures
  • Recognize pathology: polyps, Barrett's esophagus, esophageal neoplasia, early gastric cancer, ulcerative colitis activity
  • Detect abnormalities on video capsule endoscopy
  • Rapidly solidify cognitive circuitry for image recognition via immediate validation
5.3. Future AI Applications in Endoscopy
  • On-screen guide for lesion demarcation
  • Targeting for hemostasis
  • Lesion sampling and size analysis
  • Tool selection
  • Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) imaging augmentation
5.4. Patient Perspective
  • AI can narrow gap between trainee and experienced endoscopist
  • Improves examination quality and patient confidence
5.5. AI for Assessment and Feedback
  • Makes assessment more: timely, personalized, formative, anonymous
  • Automatically calculate metrics:
  • Landmark recognition
  • Cecal intubation time
  • Withdrawal time
  • Adenoma detection rate
  • Streamlines competency tracking with real-time data
  • Mobile app powered by generative AI can standardize evaluation and feedback
  • Helps fellows self-identify areas for improvement
6. Roadmap for AI Integration in GI Fellowship
6.1. Key Principles
  • Thoughtful, rational, and cautious integration
  • Ensure responsible and ethical use of AI by all
  • Enhance training and education
  • Improve GI care for patients
6.2. Professional Society Recommendations
  • Develop and maintain curated set of AI-related competencies for GI programs
  • Provide guidance on CADe/CADx use
  • Support EPA framework for articulating AI skills
6.3. Fellowship Program Responsibilities
  • Articulate AI-related policies: which tools permitted, circumstances, appropriate use
  • Specify policies for LLM use (data extraction and evidence retrieval-augmented)
  • Ensure trainees trained in AI tool use with confirmed competence
  • Regular review of fellow notes for accuracy
6.4. Research Needs
  • Dedicated studies of AI use among fellows
  • Assess impact of ambient scribe technologies
  • Study equity for patients who do not speak English
  • Impact of LLM use in clinical decision-making
7. Quick Reference: AI Tools and Settings
Tool Type Setting(s) Key Points
CADe / CADx Endoscopy suite Not mandatory; use after first-year
Ambient scribes Clinic, inpatient Match trainee access; patient consent required
EHR-embedded AI Clinic, inpatient Train and confirm competence
General purpose LLMs All settings Prohibited for clinical decisions
Evidence retrieval LLMs All settings Use with literature verification
8. Summary of Key Recommendations
For Program Directors and Faculty
  • Develop familiarity with AI tools to train GI fellows
  • Use AI outputs as teaching moments for critical appraisal
  • Start with faculty control over CADe/CADx, then taper autonomy
  • Provide regular feedback on AI-assisted documentation
For Trainees
  • Use data extraction and evidence retrieval-augmented LLMs within clinical context
  • Always verify LLM output against primary/secondary literature
  • Never make decisions based on LLM output alone
  • Develop critical thinking about AI limitations
For Systems
  • Professional societies should define AI competencies
  • More research needed on AI use in endoscopy training
  • Monitor future AI tools for mucosal characterization and EUS imaging
