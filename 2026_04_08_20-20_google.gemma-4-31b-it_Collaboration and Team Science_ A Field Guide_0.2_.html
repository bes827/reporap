Collaboration and Team Science: A Field Guide
  • Source: Collaboration and Team Science: A Field Guide / Writing the NIH Grant Proposal (2018)
  • Topic: Preparing for, leading, and maintaining scientific collaborations and team science.
  • Author(s): Not explicitly listed in text (Reference to Gerin, Kinkade, & Page in source snippet).
1. Preparing Yourself for Team Science
  • Transition: Shift from solo-investigator culture $\rightarrow$ collaborative culture.
  • Core Requirements:
  • Ability to communicate and exchange constructive feedback.
  • Collaborative spirit: willingness to share data, credit, and decision-making.
  • Psychological Foundations:
  • Personal insight and self-awareness.
  • Consciousness of one's impact on others.
1.1. Mental and Emotional Preparation Tips
  • Acknowledge that others may have different perceptions/understandings.
  • Explore multiple possibilities for how others perceive an experience.
  • Understand that differing perspectives do not threaten identity or relationships.
2. Leadership in Team Science
  • Nature of Leadership: No single formula; varies by team and leader.
  • Essential Leader Strengths:
  • Clear, decisive communication of vision and information.
  • Modeling collaborative behavior to motivate others.
  • Empowering members $\rightarrow$ assigning roles and delegating responsibilities.
  • Managing expectations and selecting members who fit the team culture.
2.1. Leadership Roles and Impact
  • Primary Functions:
  • Coordinating resources to launch projects.
  • Building personal and scientific trust.
  • Serving as a conduit to senior organizational leadership.
  • Fostering mutual respect and containing conflict.
  • Styles:
  • Directive: Command and charge; clearly identified as the head.
  • Non-directive: "Driving from the back of the bus."
2.2. Dysfunctional Leadership Styles (Derailers)
  • Absentee: Unavailable or insufficiently involved.
  • Inhibited: Conflict-avoidant; reluctant to handle difficult people/situations.
  • Defensive: Resistant to feedback; projects blame outward.
  • Hostile: Actively promotes competition and conflict within the lab.
2.3. Situational Leadership Model (Hersey & Blanchard)
  • Key Factors:
  • Maturity: Evolution of team/individual from dependence $\rightarrow$ independence.
  • Role Extension: Shift from Telling (task-focused, low relationship) $\rightarrow$ Delegating (monitoring, high autonomy).
  • Progression Stages:

1. Train/Direct: Leader directs work.

2. Dialogue/Interaction: Individual gains ability; leader provides direction via interaction.

3. Shared Decision-making: Leader less involved in directing $\rightarrow$ individual gains confidence.

4. Independence: Mutual respect and trust $\rightarrow$ individual works independently.

3. Communication and Mentoring
  • Communication Needs:
  • Clear outlines of roles, resource commitments, and credit sharing.
  • Tools: Listservs, teleconferencing, Web-based tools, e-mail.
  • Face-to-face: Workshops and retreats to broaden networks.
  • Language: Necessity of learning different "scientific languages" across disciplines.
3.1. The Role of the Mentor
  • Importance: Absence of mentors $\rightarrow$ frustration, uninformed decisions, and behaviors that undermine projects.
  • Characteristics of Great Mentors:
  • Identify growth potential in newer scientists.
  • Help navigate organizational/disciplinary politics.
  • Assist with networking and identifying opportunities.
  • Share formative successes and failures.
3.2. Seeking a Mentor
  • Value: Serves as a sounding board regardless of career stage.
  • Strategy: If no mentor exists, use supervisors as resources to identify potential mentors.
4. Self-Reflection and Emotional Intelligence
  • Connection: Scientific thinking and self-reflection both rely on inferential reasoning (observing data $\rightarrow$ drawing inferences $\rightarrow$ testing).
  • Goal: Overcome naïve realism (the belief that we see events exactly as they are).
4.1. Personality Assessment Tools
  • Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI):
  • Attitude: Extraversion [E] vs. Introversion [I].
  • Functioning: Sensing [S] vs. Intuition [N]; Thinking [T] vs. Feeling [F].
  • Lifestyle: Judging [J] vs. Perceiving [P].
  • Result: 16 personality types (e.g., ESTJ).
4.2. Emotional Intelligence (EI)
  • Definition: Self-awareness, building healthy relationships, and understanding emotional impact.
  • Impact on Leadership: EI may be more important than raw intelligence for effective leadership.
  • Negative Impacts of Unaware Emotional Reactions:
  • $\downarrow$ Vision and creativity.
  • $\downarrow$ Curiosity, openness, and playfulness.
  • $\downarrow$ Ability to recognize nuances.
  • Distorted perceptions.
5. Conflict Resolution
  • Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument: Identifies natural styles of resolving conflict.
  • Five Conflict Styles:

1. Competing: Using power to win one's position.

2. Accommodating: Neglecting own concerns to satisfy others.

3. Avoiding: Sidestepping the conflict entirely.

4. Collaborating: Finding a solution that fully satisfies both parties.

5. Compromising: Finding an expedient, mutually acceptable partial solution.

5.1. Receiving and Encouraging Feedback
  • Challenges: Power imbalances make it difficult for subordinates to provide candid feedback.
  • 360-Degree Evaluations: Feedback from peers, supervisors, and subordinates (often anonymous).
  • Creating a Feedback Culture:
  • Welcome disagreement and constructive criticism.
  • Avoid defensiveness, ridicule, or punishment $\rightarrow$ leads to silence.
  • Meet disagreement with receptivity $\rightarrow$ improves team function.
6. Sharing Recognition and Credit
  • Criticality: Impacts tenure, grants, promotions, and awards.
  • Timing: Decisions must be made before work begins or as early as possible.
6.1. Strategies for Fair Credit
  • Negotiate roles and responsibilities unambiguously.
  • Establish clear criteria for authorship in a written collaborative agreement.
  • Create approachable channels to raise credit concerns early.
  • Explicitly acknowledge team contributions in public presentations.
6.2. Institutional Shifts (Examples)
  • NIH: Modified tenure guidelines (2006) and created multiple-PI grant mechanisms (2007).
  • NCI Center for Cancer Research: Revised site-visit guidelines to reward substantial team science contributions.
  • Journals: Requiring delineated contributions (e.g., who designed vs. analyzed data).
7. Strengthening Team Dynamics
  • Definition: Interactions that influence attitudes, behavior, and group functioning.
  • Key Components: Communication, conflict management, leadership, shared goals/recognition, and trust.
7.1. Methods to Improve Dynamics
  • Schedule regular "function checks" (meetings).
  • Integrate individual career needs with team goals.
  • Address personnel issues swiftly.
7.2. The Five Dysfunctions of a Team (Lencioni)

1. Absence of trust.

2. Fear of conflict.

3. Lack of commitment.

4. Avoidance of accountability.

5. Inattention to results.

7.3. Positive Team Mood Ingredients
  • Can-do attitude, Hopefulness, Curiosity, Perseverance, Attitude of abundance, Playfulness, and Zest.
8. Developing and Maintaining Collaborations (NIH Grant Context)
  • Benefits: Brainstorming, complementary skills, increased "staying power" during stressful grant writing, and higher quality via multiple reviewers.
8.1. Selecting Collaborators
  • Sources: Friends in the field, gap analysis of needed expertise, or contacting authors from journals/conferences.
  • Approach: E-mail is preferred over phone calls for initial contact (allows organized thought).
8.2. Maintenance Principles
  • Long-term View: Avoid short-term advantages (e.g., extra first-authorship) that destroy trust.
  • Ethics: Be punctual and proactive; a bad reputation spreads quickly in small scientific communities.
  • Professionalism: Quality of work $\rightarrow$ precedence over individual egos. Demand critical "slashing" of prose to improve quality.
  • Interpersonal Discipline: Avoid sarcasm or nastiness under stress. Apologize for lapses to show strength.
8.3. Practical Grant Tips
  • Draft science $\ge 8$ weeks before submission for colleague appraisal.
  • Discuss authorship order at the first meeting.
