Chapter 1: Designing Your Perfect Lawn
  • - This chapter helps you decide what you want your lawn to do for you and your landscape.
What Is a Lawn?
  • - A lawn is a crowded city of grasses forming a resilient, soft, even carpet of green.
Benefits of Lawns
  • - Ideal ground cover for walking and playing.
  • - Soothes harsh environments: absorbs CO2, produces oxygen, traps dust, and cools the air.
  • - Aesthetically pleasing: ties the house and landscape together.

Downsides of Lawns
  • - Significant consumers of limited resources like fertilizers, pesticides, and water.
  • - Can be high maintenance, requiring time and energy for mowing, watering, etc.
  • - Potential for pollution if chemicals are misapplied and enter water sources.


Lawn Decisions
  • - Involves practical decisions that influence cost, ease of care, and maintenance.
Key Considerations
  • - Cost: Varies with size and planting method (seed is cheaper, sod is instant).
  • - Climate: Influences grass choice and watering needs (e.g., a permanent irrigation system adds cost but saves time).
  • - Maintenance: Time and expense increase with lawn size.

How large a lawn do you really need?
  • - Reducing lawn size is the best way to conserve time, water, and resources.
  • - For a small play or lounging area, 600 to 800 square feet is often sufficient.
  • - For games like volleyball, a rectangle of 45 x 80 feet (3,600 sq ft) is plenty.
  • - In areas with common summer rainfall, an average lawn is 7,000 to 10,000 square feet.

Lawn Shape
  • - Non-square shapes (circular, kidney-shaped) are often more visually interesting.
  • - Caution: Overly complex shapes can make mowing and watering difficult.

Lawn Placement
  • - Backyard: A natural spot for play and relaxation, offering privacy.
  • - Front yard: Enhances curb appeal, but consider its actual use. If only used for mowing, a ground cover might be a better, lower-maintenance choice.


Designing a Low-Maintenance Lawn
  • - Incorporate design techniques to make your lawn easier to care for.
Key Techniques
  • - Mowing strips: Cement or wood borders that allow mower wheels to run up to the edge, eliminating hand trimming.
  • - Edgings: Thinner strips that keep grass in bounds and create a clean edge.
  • - Avoid awkward areas: Don't plant grass in narrow strips (e.g., between street and sidewalk) that are hard to mow and water.
  • - Avoid steep slopes: Dangerous to mow and difficult to water without runoff.
  • - Avoid heavy shade: Grass struggles for light, and tree roots compete for water and nutrients.



Chapter 2: Watering Your Lawn
  • - Proper watering is the most important aspect of caring for your lawn.
The Basics of Lawn Watering
  • - Healthy roots are the key to a healthy lawn. They need a balance of moisture and air.
Watering Strategy
  • - Deep watering ---> encourages deep roots.
  • - Deep roots ---> access a larger soil reservoir of water.
  • - Larger reservoir ---> lawn needs to be watered less frequently.

Common Watering Pitfalls
  • - Overwatering: Waterlogged soil lacks air, leading to shallow roots that can drown.
  • - Shallow watering: Wetting only the top few inches of soil also creates shallow roots, making the lawn dry out quickly.


Factors Affecting Watering Needs
  • - Several conditions influence how much water your lawn requires.
Soil Conditions
  • - Different soil types have different water-holding capacities and absorption rates.
Sandy Soils
  • - Hold the least moisture and dry out the fastest.
  • - Need more frequent watering, but with less water per application.
  • - To wet to 6-8 inches: requires 0.5 inch of water, takes approx. 30 minutes.

Clay Soils
  • - Hold water tightly; slow to absorb and slow to dry.
  • - Need less frequent watering, but with more water per application.
  • - To wet to 6-8 inches: requires 1 to 1.5 inches of water, takes approx. 5 hours.
  • - Water slowly in cycles to prevent runoff.

Loam Soil
  • - An ideal mixture with good aeration and moisture retention.
  • - To wet to 6-8 inches: requires 1 inch of water, takes approx. 2 hours.


Other Factors
  • - Weather: More water needed in hot, sunny, windy conditions.
  • - Shade: Grass in shade needs less water, UNLESS shaded by trees, whose roots can dry the soil quickly.
  • - Cultural Practices: Lawns mown short or heavily fertilized need more water.
  • - Type of Grass: Warm-season grasses (Bermuda, Zoysia) are generally more drought-tolerant than cool-season grasses (Kentucky bluegrass).
Grass Water Needs (See Table 2-2)
  • - Least Water: Buffalo grass, Bermuda grass, Zoysia grass.
  • - Most Water: Kentucky bluegrass.



Watering Best Practices
  • - In midsummer, most lawns need 1 to 2 inches of water per week, typically split into two applications.
Key Principles
  • - Water to the proper depth: Moisture should penetrate 6 to 8 inches deep.
  • - Allow partial drying between waterings: Look for signs of thirst (footprints remain, color dulls to grayish-blue) to ensure good soil aeration.
  • - Avoid runoff: On dense soils, water in short intervals of 10-15 minutes, allowing water to soak in between cycles.
  • - Water in the morning: This is the best time due to less evaporation and wind. It also allows grass to dry before nightfall, reducing disease risk.


The Can Test
  • - A simple method to measure how much water your sprinklers apply over a given time.
Procedure
  • - Place 5-7 straight-sided cans randomly in the sprinkler's coverage area.
  • - Run sprinklers for a set time, like 10 or 15 minutes.
  • - Measure the water in each can to determine the application rate.
  • - Important: Use the can that has accumulated the least amount of water to calculate your total watering time to ensure the whole lawn gets enough.


Lawn Care During Drought
  • - Strategies to conserve water and help your lawn survive dry periods.
Conservation Techniques
  • - Mow higher to encourage deeper roots and shade the soil.
  • - Fertilize less to slow growth and reduce water needs.
  • - Water less frequently, but still water deeply when you do.
  • - Let the lawn go brown: Many grasses can go dormant for two or three weeks or longer and recover when rains return.



Chapter 3: Fertilizing Your Lawn
  • - A well-fed lawn allows grasses to thrive and grow close together, keeping it green and reducing weeds.
Nitrogen Counts the Most
  • - Of the 16 essential nutrients, Nitrogen (N) is the most important for lawn health and is needed in the largest amounts.
Role of Nitrogen
  • - Keeps grass green and vigorous.
  • - Deficiency signs: Pale/yellow color, slow growth, thinning lawn.
  • - Excess application: Can burn the lawn, cause weak/lush growth prone to disease, and pollute water runoff.

Forms of Nitrogen in Fertilizers
  • - Quick-release: Immediately available for rapid green-up but is used up quickly (e.g., urea, ammonium nitrate).
  • - Slow-release: Provides more consistent, sustained growth over a longer period (e.g., sulfur-coated urea, organic materials).


How Much Fertilizer to Apply
  • - Recommendations are based on the amount of actual nitrogen a lawn needs per year.
Factors Determining Amount
  • - Type of Grass: Different grasses have different needs. High-need grasses include Kentucky bluegrass and hybrid Bermuda grass (4-6 lbs N/1000 sq ft), while low-need grasses include Buffalo grass and Centipede grass (0-2 lbs N/1000 sq ft). (See Table 3-1)
  • - Length of Growing Season: Use the lower end of the recommended range for short seasons and the upper end for long seasons.
  • - Lawn Clippings: Leaving clippings on the lawn can reduce fertilizer needs by 25% or more.


When to Fertilize
  • - Apply fertilizer during seasons of active growth. Never apply more than 1 pound of actual nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft with each application.
Warm-Season Grasses (e.g., Bermuda)
  • - Feed from late spring to early fall.
  • - Avoid early spring feeding (promotes weeds) and late fall feeding (reduces winter hardiness).

Cool-Season Grasses (e.g., Fescue)
  • - Most important feeding times are fall and spring.
  • - Fall feeding is critical for building reserves for a quick green-up in spring.
  • - Avoid fertilizing during the heat of midsummer.


How to Apply Fertilizer
  • - Use a spreader for even application to avoid uneven greening or burning the grass.
Application Steps & Safety
  • - 1. Set spreader according to fertilizer label.
  • - 2. Fill spreader on a hard surface and sweep up spills.
  • - 3. Apply to edges first, then move back and forth, slightly overlapping paths.
  • - 4. Water thoroughly after fertilizing to wash nutrients into the soil.
  • - 5. Clean spreader over the lawn area when finished.

Critical Pitfalls to Avoid
  • - Never apply fertilizer to a dry lawn.
  • - Do not fertilize during very hot weather.
  • - If you spill fertilizer, clean it up as best you can and flood the area with water to prevent chemical burns.


Fertilizing without Polluting
  • - Responsible application protects groundwater, lakes, and streams.
Best Practices
  • - Use slow-release forms of nitrogen, as they are less likely to leach.
  • - Calibrate spreaders correctly to avoid over-application.
  • - Avoid getting fertilizer on hard surfaces like driveways or streets where it can wash into storm drains.
  • - Sweep up any spills immediately.



Chapter 4: Mowing Your Lawn
  • - Proper mowing is one of the most important practices for keeping your lawn healthy.
Why Mowing Matters
  • - Clipping the growing tips of grass causes the plants to branch out and become denser.
  • - This process is what turns thousands of individual grass plants into a tightly woven turf.

Lawn Mower Safety
  • - Power lawn mowers can be dangerous. Always follow safety precautions.
Key Safety Tips
  • - Check the lawn for rocks or debris before mowing.
  • - Keep pets and children away while mowing.
  • - On gentle slopes, mow across the slope, not up and down.
  • - Wear heavy shoes and long pants for protection.


How High to Mow
  • - Each grass type has an ideal cutting height range. Taller grass generally means deeper roots and a healthier lawn.
The One-Third Rule
  • - This isa critical guideline for lawn health: never cut away more than one-third of the grass blade in any one mowing.
Handling Overgrown Grass
  • - If grass gets too long, raise the mower to its highest possible setting and mow.
  • - Rake up the long clippings if they are too heavy.
  • - A few days later, mow again at the normal cutting height.


Recommended Mowing Heights
  • - Cool-season grasses (Fescues, Kentucky bluegrass) are generally cut higher.
  • - Warm-season grasses (Bermuda, Zoysia) are cut lower.
Specific Heights
  • - Tall Fescue: 2 to 3 inches
  • - Kentucky Bluegrass: 1.5 to 2.5 inches
  • - Bermuda grass (common): 1 to 1.5 inches
  • - Zoysia grass: 1 to 2 inches


Mowing Strategy for Stressful Conditions
  • - Mow at the upper end of the recommended range during hot weather or drought.
  • - Taller grass shades the soil, keeping it cooler and reducing evaporation.
  • - Also cut higher in shady areas to maximize light absorption.

Protecting Trees from 'Lawn-Mower Disease'
  • - Repeatedly hitting a tree trunk with a mower or weed whip can seriously damage or kill it, especially young trees.
  • - The damage can girdle the trunk, making it weak and prone to snapping.
Prevention
  • - Create a grassless ring at least 3 feet wide around the trunk.
  • - Cover this area with a thick layer (3 to 6 inches) of organic mulch, keeping it a few inches from the trunk base.
  • - Alternatively, use plastic trunk protectors from a garden center.



Setting Mower Height
  • - A simple procedure to ensure you're cutting at the correct height.
Procedure
  • - 1. Place the mower on a hard, flat surface (e.g., driveway).
  • - 2. Use a ruler to measure the distance from the mower blade to the ground.
  • - 3. Adjust the wheels/lever to the desired height.
  • - 4. Make a test pass on the lawn and measure the cut grass to confirm the height.


Knowing How Often to Mow
  • - Frequency depends on how quickly the grass grows, which is influenced by fertilization, watering, and time of year.
The Guiding Rule
  • - Mow when the grass reaches about one-third higher than your set mowing height.
  • - Example: If you cut your lawn to 2 inches, you should mow it when the grass is about 3 inches high.

Dangers of Mowing Overgrown Grass
  • - Sunburn: The lower, previously shaded parts of the grass blades get exposed to sun and can burn.
  • - Root Shock: Whacking off a large portion of the top cuts off the food supply and hurts the roots.
  • - Messy Clippings: Extra-long clippings can smother the grass below and look terrible.
