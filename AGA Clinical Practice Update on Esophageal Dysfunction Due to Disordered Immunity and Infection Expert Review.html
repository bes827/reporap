AGA Clinical Practice Update on Esophageal Dysfunction Due to Disordered Immunity and Infection: Expert Review
  • Authors: Chanakyaram A. Reddy, Emily McGowan, Rena Yadlapati, and Kathryn Peterson
  • Journal: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology
  • Publication Date: 2024
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the estimated relative risk of Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE) in patients with achalasia?
  • 2. In Esophageal Lichen Planus (ELP), what histological finding is characteristic, and in what percentage of biopsies is it typically found?
  • 3. What is the preferred first-line treatment and typical duration for Candida esophagitis according to available guidelines?
  • 4. While both HSV and CMV can cause viral esophagitis, how do their typical endoscopic appearances differ?
  • 5. What percentage of patients with Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA) test positive for ANCA?
Best Practice Advice Statements
  • Summary of key clinical recommendations.
BPA 1 & 2: Awareness & Suspicion
  • - Be aware of esophageal manifestations of systemic immunologic/infectious diseases to ↓ diagnostic delay.
  • - Identify risks for inflammatory/infectious causes.
  • - If infection is found, assess for signs of systemic immunocompromise & consult infectious disease experts.
BPA 3: Refractory Symptoms
  • - If symptoms persist after infectious esophagitis therapy, evaluate for refractory infection or other underlying causes of esophageal/immunologic dysfunction.
BPA 4: EoE & Motility
  • - In EoE patients with persistent symptoms despite remission, be aware of potential motility disorders (e.g., achalasia) & consider further evaluation.
BPA 5: Lymphocytic Esophagitis (LyE)
  • - For patients with histologic/endoscopic features of LyE, consider treatment with PPIs, swallowed topical corticosteroids, and esophageal dilation as needed.
BPA 6: Hypereosinophilia
  • - In patients with esophageal symptoms & AEC >1500 cells/uL, work up non-EoE eosinophilic GI disease, HES, and EGPA.
  • - Consult allergy/immunology for guidance.
BPA 7: Rheumatologic Diseases
  • - In SSc, MCTD, SLE, or Sjogren’s, be aware that esophageal symptoms can occur from muscle layer involvement → dysmotility/LES incompetence.
  • - Dysfunction is often significant in SSc and MCTD.
BPA 8: Crohn's Disease
  • - A minority of Crohn's patients can develop esophageal involvement (inflammatory, stricturing, fistulizing).
  • - Tends to occur in individuals with active intestinal disease.
BPA 9: Dermatologic Diseases
  • - In lichen planus or bullous disorders, dysphagia can occur from visible esophageal mucosal involvement.
  • - Esophageal lichen planus (ELP) can occur w/o skin involvement.
BPA 10: Secondary Achalasia
  • - During initial achalasia evaluation, consider infectious (COVID, Chagas) and inflammatory (eosinophilic disease) causes.
Infectious Etiologies
  • - Most common: Candida, Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV), Cytomegalovirus (CMV).
  • - Can occur in both immunosuppressed and immunocompetent hosts.
Candida Esophagitis
  • - Most common infectious esophagitis.
  • - Presents with dysphagia or odynophagia.
Diagnosis
  • - Endoscopy: White nummular lesions; may carpet the entire esophagus in severe cases.
  • - Histology: Cytobrush or biopsies identify fungal forms.
Risk Factors
  • - Recent antibiotics.
  • - Local/systemic steroids or immunosuppression.
  • - Malignancy, PPI use, older age, chronic alcohol use, CKD, diabetes.
  • - Motility disorders → esophageal stasis.
Treatment
  • - Preferred: Fluconazole 200-400 mg/day for 14-21 days.
  • - Prophylaxis: Nystatin may be used for high-risk patients.
  • - Asymptomatic Cases: Treatment is unclear; one study showed asymptomatic candidiasis rarely becomes symptomatic.
Viral Esophagitis
  • - Common presentation: Dysphagia or severe odynophagia.
Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)
  • - Endoscopy: Multiple small, shallow, well-circumscribed ulcers.
  • - Histology: Intra-nuclear eosinophilic inclusions.
  • - Treatment: Acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir.
  • - Immunocompetent: Up to 10 days.
  • - Immunosuppressed: Up to 21 days.
Cytomegalovirus (CMV)
  • - Endoscopy: Few large, cratered ulcers.
  • - Histology: Large eosinophilic or basophilic intranuclear inclusions.
  • - Treatment: Ganciclovir or valganciclovir for up to 6 weeks.
HIV
  • - Endoscopy: Can present with a few discrete esophageal ulcerations.
Immune Dysfunction of the Esophagus
  • Esophageal symptoms can be a manifestation of immune dysfunction, either localized to the esophagus or as part of a systemic disease.
Localized Immune Dysfunction
  • Involves local immune responses within the esophagus.
Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)
  • - Clinicopathologic condition: Esophageal dysfunction + ≥15 eosinophils/hpf.
  • - Trigger: Food or aeroallergens → Type-2 (allergic) response.
Presentation & Diagnosis
  • - Symptoms: Vary by age; dysphagia, chest pain, food impactions.
  • - Endoscopy: Edema, rings, exudates, furrows, strictures; can appear normal.
  • - Biopsy Protocol: Multiple biopsies from ≥2 esophageal levels due to patchy inflammation.
Pathophysiology & Complications
  • - Untreated inflammation → transmural remodeling, smooth muscle hypertrophy.
  • - Result: ↓ esophageal wall compliance & contractility.
  • - Motility Issues: Persistent symptoms despite remission may signal subtle strictures or motility disorders.
  • - Achalasia Risk: ↑ risk for achalasia.
Treatment
  • - Diet: Elimination diets.
  • - Medical: PPIs, swallowed topical corticosteroids (Budesonide oral suspension is FDA-approved).
  • - Biologic: Dupilumab (anti-IL-4 receptor alpha antibody) is FDA-approved.
Lymphocytic Esophagitis (LyE)
  • - Histology: Dense peripapillary lymphocytic infiltrate & spongiosis w/o significant eosinophils/neutrophils.
  • - Prevalence: ≈0.1% in a large biopsy series.
Clinical Features
  • - Demographics: Mostly women > 60 years old.
  • - Symptoms: Dysphagia in ≈2/3 of patients.
  • - Endoscopy: Abnormal in ≈70-75%; rings, esophagitis, stricture are most common.
Diagnosis & Treatment
  • - Diagnosis: Must rule out EoE-like disease (biopsy off PPI for 2-4 weeks) and Crohn's disease.
  • - Treatment: PPI therapy, swallowed topical corticosteroids, esophageal dilation.
Systemic Inflammation Manifestations
  • Esophageal dysfunction as part of a systemic inflammatory or autoimmune process.
Hypereosinophilic Syndromes (HES)
  • - Definition: AEC >1500 cells/uL + organ damage from eosinophilic infiltration.
  • - GI Involvement: Up to 38% of HES patients have GI symptoms; can mimic EoE.
  • - Distinguishing Feature: HES has peripheral hypereosinophilia, rare in isolated EoE.
Eosinophilic Granulomatosis w/ Polyangiitis (EGPA)
  • - Definition: Rare systemic eosinophilic vasculitis of small-medium arteries (formerly Churg-Strauss).
  • - ANCA Status: Positive in only 30-40% of patients.
  • - Esophageal Involvement: Rare; due to eosinophilic infiltration or vasculitic neuropathy.
Autoimmune Diseases
  • Esophageal involvement in various rheumatologic conditions.
Crohn's Disease
  • - Uncommon in adults; more frequent in children (20% in one study).
  • - Features: Superficial/deep ulcers, often with active disease elsewhere.
  • - Treatment: Requires biologic therapy.
Scleroderma (SSc) & MCTD
  • - SSc: Atrophy/fibrosis of smooth muscle → severe hypomotility & incompetent LES.
  • - MCTD: Resembles SSc but can affect both striated (proximal) and smooth (distal) muscle; may be less severe.
Sjogren's & SLE
  • - Sjogren's: Up to 80% of patients complain of dysphagia.
  • - SLE: Dysphagia from dysmotility with unclear mechanism.
Myositis
  • - Conditions: Polymyositis, dermatomyositis, inclusion body myositis.
  • - Involvement: Affects proximal striated muscle, especially cricopharyngeus (CP).
  • - Prevalence: 30-80% experience dysphagia.
  • - Management: CP-directed therapy (dilation, botulinum toxin, myotomy).
Dermatologic Diseases
  • Esophageal involvement can occur even without skin manifestations.
Pemphigus Vulgaris (PV)
  • - Most common bullous disorder.
  • - Endoscopy: Non-specific findings (erythema, red longitudinal lines, erosions, blisters).
  • - Diagnosis: Direct immunofluorescence (DIF) is diagnostic (IgG & C3 deposition).
Lichen Planus (LP)
  • - Most common dermatologic disorder to affect the esophagus.
  • - Esophageal LP (ELP) can be isolated (33% of cases) and has a predilection for middle/older aged Caucasian females.
Features & Diagnosis
  • - Endoscopy: Narrowed caliber (proximal/middle), strictures, pale/edematous mucosa, peeling/sloughing on contact, thick white exudates.
  • - Histology: Civatte bodies are characteristic but found in <50% of biopsies.
  • - DIF: Not diagnostic, but fibrinogen deposition is suggestive.
Management & Risks
  • - Treatment: Swallowed topical corticosteroids (>60% benefit); systemic immunosuppressants for non-responders.
  • - Cancer Risk: ↑ risk of esophageal squamous cell carcinoma.
Diagnostic Pearl (DIF)
  • - For suspected dermatologic involvement, obtain biopsies for DIF from both involved and nearby uninvolved mucosa.
  • - Place samples on saline-soaked gauze for transport.
Achalasia: Immune & Infectious Etiologies
  • Recent evidence suggests infectious and immune-mediated pathways for some achalasia subsets, challenging the purely idiopathic view.
Autoimmune Hypothesis
  • - Theory: Autoimmune disease targeting esophageal myenteric neurons.
  • - Association: Patients with achalasia are more likely to have other autoimmune conditions (Sjogren's, SLE, SSc).
  • - Risk: OR 1.26 to 3.6 for comorbid autoimmune disease.
Allergy-Mediated Association
  • - Histology: Dense eosinophil accumulation found in the muscularis propria of achalasia patients.
  • - EoE Link: Strong association between EoE and achalasia.
  • - Relative Risk: Estimated RR of EoE in achalasia is 32.9 (95% CI, 24.8-42.8), higher in patients ≤40 years old.
  • - Mechanism: Eosinophils/mast cells release products that can cause motility disturbances or neuronal destruction.
Secondary Achalasia: Malignancy
  • - Paraneoplastic phenomenon.
  • - Associated Cancers: Lymphoma, lung cancer, breast cancer, esophageal adenocarcinoma.
Secondary Achalasia: Infections
  • Specific pathogens can trigger achalasia-like motility disorders.
Chagas Disease (Trypanosoma cruzi)
  • - Mechanism: Immune cross-reactivity between T. cruzi antigen and the myenteric plexus → neuronal degeneration.
  • - Result: Loss of peristalsis & impaired LES relaxation.
  • - Prevalence: GI manifestations (megaesophagus) in 10-15% of chronically infected; ≈300,000 cases in the US.
  • - Diagnosis: Serologic tests.
Post-COVID-19 Infection
  • - Mechanism: SARS-CoV-2 may trigger inflammation and ganglionic cell destruction via neuronal ACE2 receptors.
  • - Findings: ↑ viral proteins (N & S) and inflammatory markers (NLRP3, TNF) in esophageal muscle of post-COVID achalasia patients.
  • - Management: Unclear if reversible; myotomy has shown favorable short-term outcomes.
Diagnostic Considerations Summary
  • A structured approach to diagnosis based on patient history and clinical context.
History: Immunosuppression / Recent Antibiotics
  • - Consider: Candidal esophagitis, HSV, CMV, medication-induced ulceration.
  • - Tests: Upper endoscopy with biopsies and brushings.
  • - Endoscopy: White nummular lesions (Candida); shallow ulcers (HSV); deep ulcer(s) (CMV); 1-2 well-marked ulcers (pill-induced).
History: Atopy / Allergy
  • - Consider: Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE).
  • - Tests: Upper endoscopy with biopsies from proximal & distal esophagus (off PPI for 2-4 weeks).
  • - Endoscopy: Edema, rings, exudates, furrows, strictures; can appear normal.
History: Autoimmune Disease (Crohn's, SSc, SLE, Sjogren's, Myositis)
  • - Consider: Esophageal involvement of the systemic disease (inflammatory or dysmotility).
  • - Tests: Upper endoscopy with biopsies, high-resolution manometry.
  • - Findings: Varied; ulcerations (Crohn's), absent contractility (SSc), non-specific edema/stenoses (Sjogren's), cricopharyngeal issues (myositis).
History: Dermatologic Disease (Bullous disorders, Lichen Planus)
  • - Consider: Esophageal involvement of dermatologic disease.
  • - Tests: Upper endoscopy with biopsies for H&E and DIF.
  • - Endoscopy (LP): Narrowed caliber, pale/edematous mucosa, peeling/sloughing, white exudates.
  • - Endoscopy (Pemphigus): Sloughing/blistering, erythema, red longitudinal lines.
History: Recent COVID Infection / Travel to Central/South America
  • - Consider: Post-COVID dysmotility/achalasia, Chagas disease.
  • - Tests: Upper endoscopy, high-resolution manometry, timed barium esophagram. Consider infectious disease consult for Chagas serology.
  • - Endoscopy: Often normal or shows chronic stasis changes.
