The Emotional Toll of a Malpractice Lawsuit
  • - Getting sued is an intensely personal and devastating experience for physicians.
  • - It is described as a 'total affront to everything doctors have trained to do' and can damage the professional psyche.
Immediate Reactions
  • - Shock and Anger.
  • - Feeling of Betrayal by the patient, especially if the relationship was perceived as good.
  • - Guilt and self-doubt, questioning one's own actions.

Psychological Impact
  • - Common effects include anxiety, anguish, and depression.
  • - Physical symptoms: poor sleep, poor appetite, and ill temper.
  • - The emotional distress can be long-lasting, with one OB/GYN still emotional 21 years after winning her case.

Sense of Isolation
  • - Physicians feel a strong sense of isolation and loneliness.
  • - They are legally restricted from discussing case details with anyone except their attorney and insurer.
  • - This prevents seeking support and advice from colleagues.

Loss of Control
  • - Doctors are used to being in control, but a lawsuit places them in an environment with very little control.
  • - Medical school does not prepare physicians for the reality of malpractice litigation.


Navigating the Litigation Process
  • - The process is lengthy and stressful, often lasting years.
  • - One case went on for almost 7 years and reached the state supreme court.
  • - Another case took 7 years from filing to trial.
Dealing with Attorneys
  • - Plaintiff's Attorney: Expect them to be 'merciless', to attack you, and to twist your words.
  • - Defense Attorney: It is crucial to have a trusting relationship. If trust is absent, find a new lawyer.

Depositions & Trial
  • - Depositions: Described as 'awful'; every word is scrutinized.
  • - Key Advice: Do not lose your temper during depositions or trial, as attorneys may try to provoke you.
  • - Trial Demands: It's a 'very bad idea' to work during the trial; it is physically and mentally exhausting.
Trial as 'Theater'
  • - A helpful perspective is to view the process as 'theater' where you are an actor.
  • - This can make personal attacks less painful.

Courtroom Appearance
  • - Spouses may be advised on how to dress to avoid appearing overly wealthy (e.g., no Gucci bags, diamonds).
  • - The goal is a 'conservative and tasteful' appearance.


The Jury
  • - It's difficult to know what jurors are thinking, causing significant anxiety.
  • - However, one doctor described the jury as 'wonderful' and believes in the system after they took less than an hour to find him not liable in a complex case.

Inadmissible Evidence
  • - Juries are often unaware of crucial context that is not admissible as evidence.
  • - Example 1: A plaintiff's prior settlements from workers' compensation.
  • - Example 2: In one case, the jury was not told the patient had already received $8 million from the hospital ($5 million) and other doctors ($1 million each) who settled.


Long-Term Consequences & Practice Changes
  • - The trauma of being sued 'never leaves you' and can resurface years later.
  • - Many doctors experience significant changes in their professional lives and outlook.
Practice of Defensive Medicine
  • - A common reaction is to practice more defensively.
  • - This includes ordering additional, sometimes unnecessary, imaging studies, labs, and second opinions.

Altered Patient Interactions
  • - Seeing new patients as potential litigants.
  • - Feeling that 'we're not on the same team anymore'.
  • - Spending more time explaining risks to patients and families.

Changes in Documentation
  • - Documentation becomes more detailed and burdensome.
  • - 'If it isn't written on the chart, it's like it didn't happen.'
  • - One surgeon now documents 'completely unnecessary information' and notes on every conversation.

Impact on Collegial Relationships
  • - Increased reluctance to offer 'curbside consults' or help colleagues at the last minute.
  • - Fear of involvement in a case without full knowledge of the patient.

Career Changes
  • - The stress can lead physicians to leave practice, retire early, or change careers.
  • - One orthopedic surgeon gave up surgery and became a chief medical officer for health plans after a second lawsuit.


Coping Strategies & Key Advice
  • - 'Getting sued is just a part of doing business in medicine. It doesn't mean you're a bad doctor; it's just your turn.'
Build a Strong Support Network
  • - Crucial: Have someone to talk to, like a supportive spouse or family.
  • - 'I don't know what I would have done without her.'

Utilize Professional Support
  • - Some malpractice carriers (e.g., The Doctors Company, COPIC, ProAssurance) offer support programs.
  • - These include weekend retreats for physicians and spouses.
  • - 'Care for the caregiver' programs connect sued doctors with colleagues who have been through it.

Maintain Self-Care & Perspective
  • - Live your life: Don't let the case consume you. Continue hobbies and time with loved ones.
  • - Physical Health: Eat well, exercise, and get enough sleep.
  • - Trust Yourself: Believe in your training and your team. Remember why you became a doctor.

Post-Trial Recovery
  • - Don't go back to work the day after the trial ends.
  • - Take a vacation, regardless of the outcome, to decompress.
  • - It can take almost a year to see patients in a non-adversarial way again.
