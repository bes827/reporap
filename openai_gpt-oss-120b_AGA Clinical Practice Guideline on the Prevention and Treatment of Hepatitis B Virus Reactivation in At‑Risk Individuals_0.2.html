AGA Clinical Practice Guideline on the Prevention and Treatment of Hepatitis B Virus Reactivation in At‑Risk Individuals
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2025;168:267‑284
  • Topic: HBV reactivation (HBVr) risk assessment, antiviral prophylaxis, monitoring, screening
  • Author(s): Faisal S. Ali et al.
1. Guideline Scope & Methodology
  • Background: HBVr occurs when immune suppression lifts control of latent HBV in HBsAg‑positive or anti‑HBc‑positive/HBsAg‑negative patients.
  • Methods: GRADE framework used; systematic evidence review since 2014 guideline; Evidence‑to‑Decision (EtD) tables incorporated benefits, harms, values, costs, equity.
  • Panel: 7 clinicians (5 hepatologists), 2 GRADE methodologists; conflicts disclosed and managed per AGA policy.
2. Core Recommendations (Risk‑Based Management)
2.1 High‑Risk Individuals (>10% baseline HBVr) – Strong Recommendation (moderate certainty)
  • Antiviral prophylaxis strongly recommended over monitoring alone.
  • Prophylaxis must start before immunosuppressive exposure and continue ≥6 months after therapy cessation (≥12 months for B‑cell–depleting agents).
2.2 Moderate‑Risk Individuals (1%–10% baseline HBVr) – Conditional Recommendation (moderate certainty)
  • Antiviral prophylaxis suggested; patients may opt for active monitoring if they value avoiding long‑term antiviral use and cost.
  • Monitoring: HBV DNA + ALT every 1–3 months during exposure and after discontinuation.
2.3 Low‑Risk Individuals (<1% baseline HBVr) – Conditional Recommendation (moderate certainty)
  • Monitoring alone suggested; antiviral prophylaxis optional for patients preferring maximal safety.
  • Monitoring schedule identical to moderate‑risk group.
2.4 Universal HBV Testing – Strong Recommendation (moderate certainty)
  • Test all adults ≥18 y (CDC guidance): HBsAg, anti‑HBs, total anti‑HBc.
  • No risk‑stratified screening needed; serology guides subsequent risk categorization.

4. Antiviral Prophylaxis Details
  • Preferred agents: high barrier to resistance (entecavir, tenofovir disoproxil fumarate, tenofovir alafenamide).
  • Duration: Minimum 6 months post‑exposure; extend to 12 months for B‑cell–depleting regimens.
  • Efficacy: Pooled RR = 0.18 (95% CI 0.11‑0.30) → 82 % relative reduction in HBVr; absolute risk reduction varies with baseline (e.g., 410 fewer cases/1000 at 50 % baseline).
  • Safety: Minor renal/bone effects with TDF; TAF comparable safety profile; overall adverse‑event RR ≈ 1.08 (95% CI 0.47‑2.49).
5. Monitoring Strategy Without Prophylaxis
  • Frequency: HBV DNA and ALT every 1–3 months while on immunosuppressive therapy; continue same interval for at least 6 months after exposure ends.
  • Trigger for Rescue Therapy: Detectable HBV‑DNA rise ≥10‑fold from baseline OR new HBsAg appearance → initiate antiviral treatment promptly.
  • Evidence: No RCTs directly comparing monitoring vs prophylaxis in low‑risk groups; conditional recommendation based on patient preference and feasibility.
6. Screening Implementation & Interpretation
  • Initial Testing: Perform simultaneous HBsAg, anti‑HBs, total anti‑HBc*.
  • Serologic Algorithms:
  • HBsAg + → classify as HBsAg‑positive; assign exposure‑specific risk (high/moderate).
  • HBsAg‑negative & anti‑HBc + → resolved infection; use anti‑HBc status for baseline risk.
  • Anti‑HBs ≥ 10 mIU/mL alone → indicates immunity, no prophylaxis needed unless other high‑risk exposure present.
  • Viral Load Testing: If HBsAg + or anti‑HBc +, obtain quantitative HBV DNA before starting immunosuppression; repeat per monitoring schedule.
7. Duration of Prophylaxis & Post‑Exposure Management
  • RCTs comparing longer (12 mo) vs shorter (6 mo) continuation after exposure showed no significant difference in HBVr (RR = 0.99, 95% CI 0.76‑1.28).
  • Panel conclusion: Continue prophylaxis ≥6 months; extend to 12 months for agents with documented delayed reactivation (e.g., rituximab, other B‑cell depleters).
  • Case Example: Entecavir prophylaxis stopped at 6 mo after rituximab → HBVr observed >12 mo in several case reports; supports extended duration.
8. Cost‑Effectiveness & Health‑Equity Considerations
  • No published CEAs for low‑ or moderate‑risk categories; one Japanese CEA (baseline risk = 6%) yielded ICER ≈ US$132,048/QALY → not cost‑effective at US willingness‑to‑pay threshold. Adjusting HBVr probability in prophylaxis arm to 1.1% (reflecting 82 % RR reduction) would lower ICER into acceptable range.
  • Antiviral drugs covered by most insurers; out‑of‑pocket costs may impede adherence for some patients → consider patient assistance programs.
  • Equity: No specific disparities identified in evidence review; universal screening aligns with CDC equity goals.
9. Knowledge Gaps & Research Priorities
  • Baseline Risk Data: Need large, prospective cohorts reporting HBV serology and standardized HBVr definitions across emerging agents (e.g., newer TKIs, bispecific antibodies).
  • Corticosteroid Dose‑Response: Precise quantitative relationship between cumulative prednisone equivalents and HBVr remains uncertain.
  • Cost‑Effectiveness Analyses: Formal CEAs for each risk tier (low, moderate, high) required to inform policy.
  • Gut‑Specific Anti‑Integrin Therapy: Insufficient evidence for vedolizumab; registry data needed before risk categorization.
  • Anti‑HBs Protective Role: Pooled RCT anti‑HBs + rituximab subgroup showed baseline HBVr 6.6% (95% CI 5.8‑14.4%); panel concluded anti‑HBs status should not dictate prophylaxis decisions.
5. Outcomes of Antiviral Prophylaxis vs Monitoring
  • HBVr (critical): RR = 0.18 (95% CI 0.11‑0.30); absolute risk ↓ by Δ ≈ baseline × RR (e.g., 410 fewer/1000 at 50 % baseline).
  • Hepatitis flare from HBVr: pooled RR = 0.22 (95% CI 0.10‑0.50) → 78 % relative reduction; absolute benefit mirrors reactivation risk tier.
  • Chemotherapy interruption: pooled RR = 0.31 (95% CI 0.05‑1.84); very low certainty, CI crosses both low‑ and high‑risk thresholds → effect considered imprecise.
6. Safety & Harms of Antiviral Prophylaxis
  • Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF): possible ↑ renal creatinine, ↓ bone mineral density; overall impact trivial.
  • Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF): no significant renal or bone effects vs TDF.
  • Tenofovir‑related adverse events: out‑of‑pocket costs may act as barrier for some patients → shared decision making required.
7. Implementation Considerations & Clinical Decision Support
  • Strong recommendation assumes high‑barrier antivirals; start before risk‑imposing therapy, continue ≥6 mo after exposure.
  • For B‑cell–depleting regimens, extend prophylaxis to ≥12 months post‑therapy because delayed HBVr cases reported up to 18 months.
  • Monitoring alone assumes reliable follow‑up; must ensure patient adherence to 1‑to‑3‑month HBV DNA + ALT checks.
8. Equity, Cost‑Effectiveness & Limitations
  • Equity: No health‑disparities issues identified in the EtD search; universal testing supports equitable care delivery.
  • Cost‑effectiveness: Current models (Fujita et al.) suggest prophylaxis not cost‑effective for low‑risk exposures (ICER ≈ US$132k/QALY > US$45k threshold). Adjusted HBVr probabilities could improve ICER for high‑risk cases.
  • Limitations: Imprecise baseline risk estimates for several drug classes (anti‑IL6, anti‑IL6, JAK inhibitors in HBsAg‑positive cohorts); moderate‑risk categorization sometimes based on class effect rather than drug‑specific data.
9. Evidence Summary Tables (Key Outcomes)
  • HBVr: 879 participants (12 RCTs), certainty ⨁⨁⨁/C, RR = 0.18 (95% CI 0.11‑0.30).
  • HBV flare: 624 participants (9 RCTs), certainty ⨁⨁⨁/C, RR = 0.22 (95% CI 0.10‑0.50).
  • Chemotherapy interruption: 124 participants (2 RCTs), very low certainty; pooled RR = 0.31 (95% CI 0.05‑1.84).
  • Adverse events of antivirals: 141 participants (2 RCTs), low certainty; pooled RR ≈ 1.08 (95% CI 0.47‑2.49).
10. Practical Clinical Decision Flowchart (textual description)

1. Identify at‑risk patient → obtain HBsAg, anti‑HBc, anti‑HBs per universal testing recommendation.

2. Determine exposure risk using Table 6 (exposures & serologic status).

3. Assign risk category: high (>10 %), moderate (1%–10 %), low (<1 %).

4. Apply recommendation:

  • High → antiviral prophylaxis strongly; start before exposure, continue ≥6 mo post‑therapy (≥12 mo for rituximab/ofatumumab).
  • Moderate → antiviral prophylaxis conditional; if declined, implement monitoring every 1–3 months (HBV DNA + ALT).
  • Low → monitoring alone conditional; same interval as moderate risk.
11. Limitations of Current Evidence
  • Baseline‑risk estimates for many newer agents derived from small observational cohorts; imprecision led to downgrading certainty (e.g., anti‑IL6, JAK inhibitors in HBsAg‑negative patients).
  • No direct RCTs evaluating prophylaxis vs monitoring for several moderate‑risk exposures (e.g., TKIs, cytokine/integrin inhibitors); recommendations rely on pooled class effects and expert consensus.
  • Lack of cost‑effectiveness data across all risk tiers; only one CEA (moderate‑risk exposure) identified, limiting policy modeling.
12. Future Updates & Maintenance
  • Guideline scheduled for revision in 5 years (≈2029).
  • Panel recommends establishing a living registry of HBV serologies and reactivation events linked to novel immunotherapies; periodic meta‑analyses to refine baseline risk estimates.
