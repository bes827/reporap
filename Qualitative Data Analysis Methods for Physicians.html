Qualitative Data Analysis Methods for Physicians
  • Source: Provided text on Qualitative Data Analysis.
  • Target Audience: Physicians.
  • Focus: Understanding qualitative research methodologies for clinical application and interpretation.

General Principles of Qualitative Data Analysis
  • Systematic, rigorous, time-consuming process.
  • Involves sifting + interpreting large amounts of textual data (e.g., transcripts, fieldnotes).
  • Goal: make sense of findings.
Analytical Process Timing
  • Often begins *during* data collection (sequential/interim analysis).
  • Allows researcher to refine questions + pursue emerging avenues.
Deviant / Negative Cases
  • Cases running counter to emerging hypotheses.
  • Used to refine propositions.
Quantification vs. Categorization
  • Generally avoids quantifying data for statistical representativeness.
  • Uses analytical categories → describe + explain phenomena.
Category Derivation
  • Categories may be derived inductively or deductively.
Inductive Derivation
  • Categories emerge from the data.
  • Described as Grounded Theory.
Deductive Derivation
  • Categories based on existing theory or pre-set objectives.
Qualitative Content Analysis
  • Widely used method for analyzing text data.
  • Three distinct approaches: Conventional, Directed, Summative.
Conventional Content Analysis
  • Codes + categories derived directly from the data.
  • Consistent w/ qualitative descriptive approach.
  • Used in studies reporting participant experience (e.g., clinicians' perspectives on SICP).
Study Goal
  • To describe a phenomenon.
  • When existing theory/research literature is limited.
Process: Inductive Category Development
  • Researchers immerse themselves in data; read repeatedly → gain sense of whole.
  • Data read word by word → derive codes by highlighting key concepts.
  • Codes sorted into categories (meaningful clusters, ideally 10-15) → often directly from text.
Codes Source
  • Derived directly from the data.
Pros
  • Gains direct information from study participants.
  • W/o imposing preconceived categories/theoretical perspectives.
  • Knowledge generated based on participants' unique perspectives; grounded in actual data.
Cons / Challenges
  • Risk of incomplete understanding of context → findings may not accurately represent data (threat to credibility).
  • Can be confused w/ other methods (e.g., Grounded Theory, Phenomenology).
  • Limited in theory development compared to GTM.
Directed Content Analysis
  • Begins w/ existing theory/framework.
  • Uses theory to guide the analysis.
Study Goal
  • To validate or extend conceptually a theoretical framework or theory.
Process: Deductive Category Application
  • Researchers identify key concepts/variables from existing theory as initial coding categories.
  • Develop operational definitions.
  • Data coded using these predetermined codes.
  • Text not codable w/ initial scheme → new code / identify subcategories.
Codes Source
  • Derived from theory or relevant research findings.
Pros
  • Existing theory can be efficiently supported + extended.
  • Acknowledges researchers unlikely to be working from a naive perspective.
Cons / Challenges
  • Use of theory inherently creates strong bias → researchers more likely to find evidence supportive of theory.
  • Risk: participants may receive cues from targeted questions → ↓ neutrality.
  • Overemphasis on theory can blind researchers to important contextual aspects of phenomenon.
Summative Content Analysis
  • Characterized by counting specific content.
  • Counting occurs *before* moving to interpretation of meaning.
Study Goal
  • Identify + quantify certain words/content (manifest content analysis).
  • Followed by interpretation of underlying context (latent content analysis).
  • Goal: understand contextual use of words.
Process
  • Analysis begins w/ searches + frequency counts of identified keywords/content (often computer-assisted).
  • Quantification step followed by interpretation (latent content analysis).
  • Goal: discover underlying meanings + explore word usage.
  • Counting used to identify patterns + contextualize codes.
Codes Source
  • Keywords derived from researcher interest or a review of literature.
Pros
  • Unobtrusive + nonreactive way to study phenomenon of interest.
  • Can provide basic insights into how words are actually used.
Cons / Challenges
  • Findings may be limited by inattention to broader meanings present in data.
  • Initial phase is essentially quantitative.
  • Interpretation must proceed to latent content analysis for qualitative classification.
Other Systematic Qualitative Analysis Methods
  • Beyond content analysis, other structured approaches exist.
The Framework Approach
  • Developed specifically for applied or policy-relevant qualitative research.
  • Combines inductive + deductive elements.
Process
  • Analysis is more explicit + systematic.
  • Often informed by *a priori* reasoning (deductive) derived from study aims.
  • Still reflects grounded, inductive accounts of participants.
Five Stages of Analysis
  • Structured, sequential steps for rigorous analysis.
1. Familiarisation
  • Immersion in the raw data (tapes, transcripts, notes).
2. Identifying a Thematic Framework
  • Creating a detailed index of key issues, concepts, + themes.
  • Based on *a priori* questions + recurrent issues raised by respondents.
3. Indexing
  • Systematically applying the thematic framework (numerical codes) to all textual data.
4. Charting
  • Rearranging the data according to the framework.
  • Typically by creating charts containing distilled summaries (abstraction + synthesis) of views + experiences.
  • ≠ simple cut-and-paste methods.
5. Mapping and Interpretation
  • Using the charts to define concepts, create typologies, find associations, + provide explanations for findings.
Pros
  • Systematic + rigorous.
  • Analysis designed to be viewed + assessed by people other than primary analyst.
  • Useful for linking findings w/ quantitative data.
  • Works well under short timescales.
Cons / Challenges
  • Data collection tends to be more structured than normal for other qualitative research.
Grounded Theory and Analytic Induction
  • General processes describing how categories + theories are developed.
Grounded Theory (GTM)
  • Inductive process of identifying analytical categories as they emerge from the data.
  • Aim: develop theory 'from the ground or research field upwards'.
Analytic Induction
  • Involves iterative testing + retesting of theoretical ideas using the data.
  • Researcher examines cases → develops hypotheses/constructs.
  • Examines further cases → tests these propositions.
  • Crucial for handling deviant/negative cases → refine propositions.
Constant Comparison
  • Key technique used in inductive analysis.
  • Each item of data checked/compared w/ rest of data → establish coherent + systematic analytical categories.
  • Inclusive process → aims to reflect many nuances in data, ≠ reducing data to few numerical codes.
