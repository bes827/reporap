Content Analysis Approaches in Qualitative Research
  • A comparative overview of three hypothetical research studies demonstrating different content analysis methodologies: Conventional, Directed, and Summative.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the primary difference in the starting point between a conventional and a directed approach to content analysis?
  • 2. Researcher Y's study aimed to test the validity of which famous psychological model related to grief?
  • 3. What was the initial, quantitative step in Researcher Z's summative content analysis of end-of-life communication?
  • 4. Which research approach is most suitable when there is limited existing theory on a topic?
  • 5. In Researcher X's study, what sampling technique was used to ensure a diverse group of hospice patients based on care setting and duration?
Researcher X’s Study: Conventional Approach
  • An exploratory study using an inductive approach to understand the emotional reactions of terminally ill patients in hospice care.
  • This approach is used when existing theory or research literature on a phenomenon is limited.
  • Codes are derived directly and inductively from the raw data.
Research Focus & Question
  • Problem: Little is known about the emotional reactions of terminally ill patients in hospice, possibly due to reluctance to discuss death.
Research Question
  • “What are the emotional reactions of terminally ill patients who are receiving hospice care?”
Study Design & Methodology
  • A qualitative study designed to capture a wide range of patient experiences.
Sampling Strategy
  • Stratified sampling technique to ensure sample heterogeneity.
Target Sample
  • Total N = 20 patients.
By Location
  • - 10 home hospice patients.
  • - 10 inpatient hospice patients.
By Enrollment Time
  • - 5 from each group recruited w/in 48 hours of enrollment.
  • - 5 from each group recruited 7-10 days post-enrollment.
Demographics
  • Sample included both men & women, and both older & middle-aged people.
Data Collection
  • Method: Individual interviews w/ open-ended questions.
  • Example Question: “What has it been like to be in hospice care?” followed by specific probes.
  • All interviews were audiotape-recorded & transcribed verbatim.
Ethical Considerations
  • - Research procedures approved for use w/ human subjects.
  • - Informed consent obtained from all participants.
Data Analysis: Conventional Content Analysis
  • An inductive process where codes emerge from the data.
Step 1: Immersion
  • Reading each transcript from beginning to end to get a sense of the whole.
Step 2: Open Coding
  • - Rereading transcripts carefully.
  • - Highlighting text describing an emotional reaction.
  • - Writing a keyword/phrase in the margin, using the participant’s own words.
Step 3: Code Development
  • - After coding 3-4 transcripts, preliminary codes were decided.
  • - Attempted to limit the number of developing codes as much as possible.
Step 4: Coding & Refinement
  • - Remaining transcripts were coded using the preliminary codes.
  • - Original transcripts were recoded for consistency.
  • - New codes were added for data that did not fit existing codes.
Step 5: Categorization
  • - All data within each code were examined.
  • - Codes were combined or split into subcategories as needed.
  • - Final codes were organized into a hierarchical structure.
Findings & Discussion
  • The study's results described the emotional responses of hospice patients using the identified codes and hierarchical structure.
Comparison to Existing Theory
  • Results were compared and contrasted with Kübler-Ross’s (1969) model to highlight similarities and differences.
Researcher Y’s Study: Directed Approach
  • A deductive study using an existing theory (Kübler-Ross's stages of grief) to guide the analysis of patient experiences with terminal illness.
  • Goal is to validate or conceptually extend a theoretical framework.
Research Focus & Question
  • Problem: Kübler-Ross’s (1969) five stages of grief (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) are widely accepted but not well-confirmed by research.
Research Question
  • “How well does Kübler-Ross’s model describe the emotional passages or journeys of patients who have been diagnosed with a terminal illness?”
Study Design & Methodology
  • A qualitative study designed to test and elaborate on an existing framework.
Sampling Strategy
  • Purposeful sampling designed to maximize the chance of recruiting participants at different theoretical stages.
Target Sample
  • Total N = 18 to 21 participants.
By Treatment Stage
  • - 1/3 receiving “last chance” curative therapy.
  • - 1/3 refused further curative therapy (not in hospice).
  • - 1/3 contemplating or recently entered hospice care.
Demographics
  • Recruited for gender balance & diagnostic diversity (oncology and non-oncology diagnoses).
Data Collection
  • Method: Individual interviews w/ open-ended questions & specific probes based on the theory.
  • Example Question: “What has your emotional journey been since being diagnosed with this illness?”
  • Example Probe: “Have you felt angry since your diagnosis?”
Ethical Considerations
  • - Institutional review board approval obtained.
  • - Informed consent obtained from all participants.
Data Analysis: Directed Content Analysis
  • A deductive process where initial codes are derived from a theory.
Step 1: Pre-determined Codes
  • Developed operational definitions for the five emotional responses from Kübler-Ross’s model (anger, bargaining, etc.).
Step 2: Initial Coding
  • - Reviewed all transcripts, highlighting text describing an emotional response.
  • - Coded all highlighted text using the pre-determined categories wherever possible.
Step 3: Handling Non-fitting Data
  • Text that could not be coded into one of the pre-determined categories was given a new label that captured the essence of the emotion.
Step 4: Refinement & Sub-categorization
  • - Examined data within each category to determine if subcategories were needed.
  • - Example: Anger → Anger toward self, anger toward doctors, anger toward spiritual being.
Step 5: Analysis & Comparison
  • - Reexamined data that couldn't be coded into the five categories to describe different emotional reactions.
  • - Compared the extent to which data supported Kübler-Ross’s theory vs. how much represented different emotional responses.
Findings & Discussion
  • The study report described the incidence of codes representing Kübler-Ross's stages compared with codes for different emotional responses.
Contribution to Theory
  • Summarized how the study validated parts of Kübler-Ross’s model and what new perspectives were added.
Researcher Z’s Study: Summative Approach
  • A study starting with quantification of words/content, then extending to an interpretive analysis of the underlying context.
  • Focuses on identifying and quantifying specific words or content in text.
Research Focus & Question
  • Problem: Use of explicit terms for death (die, dying, death) is often avoided in favor of euphemisms, potentially hindering clinician-patient communication.
Research Question
  • “How are the terms die, dying, and death used in clinician-patient communication when discussing hospice care, and what alternative terms are used?”
Study Design & Methodology
  • A mixed-method study designed to quantify language use and explore its context.
Sampling Strategy
  • Designed to maximize diversity of both clinician and patient/family demographic characteristics.
Sample Characteristics
  • - Patient: gender, age, diagnosis, ethnic background.
  • - Clinician: gender, discipline, area of specialization.
Communication Events
  • N = 50 separate communication events for 50 different clinician-patient/family pairs.
Event Type 1
  • Discharge teaching for hospitalized patients transferring to home hospice, inpatient hospice, or SNFs for EOL care.
Event Type 2
  • Clinician-patient/family conferences (in- or outpatient) to plan EOL care.
Data Collection
  • All 50 clinician-patient conversations were audiotape-recorded and transcribed verbatim.
Ethical Considerations
  • - Approved by institutional review boards.
  • - Informed consent obtained from each participant.
Data Analysis: Summative Content Analysis
  • Begins with quantification (manifest content) and moves to interpretation (latent content).
Step 1: Quantification of Explicit Terms
  • - Computer-assisted searches for occurrences of 'die', 'death', and 'dying'.
  • - Calculated word frequency counts for each term per transcript.
  • - Compared counts to the total length of the communication event.
Step 2: Analysis by Speaker
  • - Coded the identity of the speaker (physician, nurse, patient, family member).
  • - Calculated frequency counts by speaker type and compared to the total number of terms coded.
Step 3: Identification of Alternative Terms (Euphemisms)
  • - Identified alternative terms or expressions used instead of death, die, or dying.
  • - Counted occurrences of these terms, both as a total and for each specific alternative term.
Step 4: Comparative Analysis
  • Compared frequencies of euphemisms vs. direct terms based on:
  • - Type of speaker.
  • - Demographic characteristics of the clinician.
  • - Demographic characteristics of the patient.
Findings & Discussion
  • Major findings described the occurrences of the three explicit terms compared to euphemistic terms in clinician-patient communication.
Interpretation
  • Discussion focused on exploring possible explanations for differences in the use of explicit vs. euphemistic terms for different groups and in different situations.
