The National Institutes of Health (NIH) – Overview
  • Source: NIH Grant Writing Guide (2026)
1. Mission & Goals
  • Mission: science → fundamental knowledge of living systems → extend healthy life, reduce illness/disability.
  • Goal 1: foster creative discoveries, innovative research strategies → boost national health‑protective capacity.
  • Goal 2: develop/maintain scientific human & physical resources → ensure disease‑prevention capability.
  • Goal 3: expand medical knowledge base → enhance economic well‑being & public‑investment return.
  • Goal 4: exemplify highest scientific integrity, accountability, social responsibility.
2. Core Functions & Programmatic Areas
  • Conduct/support research on causes, diagnosis, prevention, cure of human diseases.
  • Study human growth & development, environmental contaminant effects, mental/addictive/physical disorders.
  • Direct programs for information collection, dissemination, exchange (incl. medical libraries, librarian training).
3. Organizational Structure
3.1 Headquarters & Geographic Distribution
  • Main campus: Bethesda, Maryland (surrounding area).
  • Some institutes elsewhere (e.g., National Institute on Aging – Baltimore, MD).
3.2 Leadership & Administration
  • Office of the Director (OD) sets policy, plans, manages, coordinates all NIH components.
  • Current Director (as of writing): Francis Collins, MD, PhD – geneticist, former NHGRI head, nominated by President Obama.
3.3 Institutes & Centers (27 I/Cs)
  • Includes: NCI, NHLBI, NIMH, NIA, NIDDK, NIAMS, NIAAA, NIDA, NEI, NIGMS, NIBIB, NICHD, etc.
  • Each I/C focuses on specific physiologic systems & related disorders.
4. Funding Overview
  • FY 2016 proposed budget: $31,311,349 (Congressional appropriations).
  • NIH funds the majority of U.S. biomedical research and a substantial portion internationally.
5. Selecting an Institute for Grant Proposals (Hint)
  • Identify the I/C most aligned with your scientific focus; title must reflect primary emphasis.
  • Example: hypothesis on inflammatory markers in depressed CHF patients → if focus = depression → NIMH; if focus = CHF → NHLBI.

8. Grant Submission & Review Workflow
  • End‑to‑end process from concept to post‑award management; timing approximations included.
8.1 Pre‑Submission Planning (Months 1–3)
  • Planning: early internal deadline setting, preliminary data collection.
  • Writing: begin manuscript months before due date – recommended for quality.
  • Receipt & Referral: submit via www.grants.gov; CSR routes compliant applications to appropriate Institute/Center (IC).
8.2 Peer Review Stages (Months 4–8)
8.2.1 Initial Scientific Review
  • Scientific Review Officer (SRO) assigns application to a Scientific Review Group (SRG).
  • SRG members evaluate scientific merit → generate Priority Score (available in eRA Commons).
  • Produce Summary Statement (eRA Commons) detailing strengths/weaknesses.
8.2.2 Second Level Review
  • Advisory council or board of the assigned IC reviews SRG scores & statements.
  • Council may modify scores, recommend funding, or request revisions.
8.3 Funding Decision & Award Issuance (Months 9–10)
  • IC Director makes final funding decision based on review outcomes and budget constraints.
  • Grants Management staff conducts final administrative review, negotiates terms.
  • Notice of Award (NoA) sent to applicant institution; project period officially begins.
8.4 Post‑Award Management
  • Ongoing administrative & fiscal monitoring by NIH grants office.
  • Required reporting: annual progress reports, financial statements, compliance documentation.
9. Practical Tips for Applicants (Hints)
  • Institute Matching: verify target I/C on NIH website; consider funding disparities but let scientific interest drive choice.
  • Title Clarity: ensure title reflects primary research focus to guide IC assignment.
  • Multiple‑IC Scenarios: if two or more I/Cs could sponsor, note both in proposal; may influence review panel composition.
  • Funding Landscape Check: browse each I/C’s funding opportunities page for specific announcements, RFAs, and budget caps.

11. Detailed Flow of Submission‑to‑Award Process
  • Visualized in Figure 1.2 (flow chart); textual steps reproduced below.
11.1 Applicant Actions
  • Submit Application: via www.grants.gov; includes all required forms, budgets, biosketches.
  • Write & Plan Early: recommended start several months prior to deadline for data collection and internal reviews.
11.2 NIH Internal Routing
  • Center for Scientific Review (CSR): receives application, checks compliance with NIH policies.
  • Division of Receipt & Referral (CSR): assigns to appropriate Institute/Center (IC) and Scientific Review Group (SRG).
11.3 Review Milestones
Phase Responsible Party Output
Initial Level Review SRG members Priority Score, Summary Statement (eRA Commons)
Second Level Review Advisory Council/Board of IC Funding recommendation, possible score adjustments
Decision IC Director & Grants Management Notice of Award (NoA) issued
11.4 Timeline Summary
  • Months 1–3: Receipt & Referral, planning, writing.
  • Months 4–8: Peer Review (initial + second level).
  • Months 9–10: Funding decision, award issuance.
12. Post‑Award Responsibilities
  • Administrative Monitoring: NIH staff track compliance with federal regulations, fiscal reporting.
  • Fiscal Reporting: quarterly/annual expenditure reports submitted via eRA Commons.
  • Scientific Progress Reports: annual updates on aims, milestones, publications.
  • Closeout Procedures: final financial reconciliation, data sharing statements, archiving of study materials.
13. Additional NIH Support Structures
13.1 Training & Resources
  • NIH Office of Research Training: offers webinars, grant‑writing workshops, mentorship programs.
  • Clinical Center (CC): provides clinical trial infrastructure for translational studies.
13.2 Information Services
  • National Library of Medicine (NLM): maintains PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, and other databases.
  • Center for Information Technology (CIT): supports IT infrastructure for grant applicants (e.g., eRA Commons).
14. Key Take‑aways for Prospective Applicants
  • Align research question tightly with the mission of a specific Institute/Center.
  • Use NIH website to verify current funding announcements, budget caps, and institute priorities.
  • Begin planning early; allocate sufficient time for internal reviews before submission deadline.
  • Familiarize yourself with eRA Commons functionalities (score viewing, NoA receipt).
  • Maintain rigorous post‑award compliance: timely reports, fiscal accountability, data sharing.
