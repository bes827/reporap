Expert Intuition & The Outside View
(from Thinking, Fast and Slow)

  • - Explains when to trust expert intuition and the cognitive biases that cause major forecasting errors.

Expert Intuition: Marvels vs. Flaws
  • - An exploration of the boundary between valid and flawed intuition, based on a collaboration between Daniel Kahneman (skeptic) and Gary Klein (proponent of expert intuition).
The Nature of Intuition
  • - Intuition is not magic; it is simply recognition.
Herbert Simon's Definition
  • - 'The situation has provided a cue; this cue has given the expert access to information stored in memory, and the information provides the answer.'
  • - This view reduces the 'magic' of intuition to the everyday experience of memory.

Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) Model
  • - Developed by Gary Klein from studies of firefighters.
  • - Explains how experts make good decisions without comparing multiple options.
RPD Process
  • - Phase 1 (System 1): An automatic function of associative memory brings a tentative plan to mind.
  • - Phase 2 (System 2): The plan is deliberately and mentally simulated to check if it will work.



Acquiring Intuitive Skill
  • - Expertise is not a single skill but a large collection of 'miniskills'.
Emotional Learning
  • - Can be acquired very quickly, sometimes from a single experience (e.g., food aversion).
  • - Can also be learned from words, not just direct experience (e.g., a soldier learning to identify ambush terrain).

Complex Skills (e.g., Chess)
  • - Takes a very long time to develop.
  • - Requires at least 10,000 hours of dedicated practice to reach the highest levels.
  • - Experts learn to recognize thousands of patterns, like a skilled reader recognizes words.


Evaluating Intuition: When to Trust It?
  • - Key Principle: The confidence people have in their intuitions is not a reliable guide to their validity.
  • - Do not trust subjective confidence, including your own.
Two Basic Conditions for Acquiring Skill
  • - 1. An environment that is sufficiently regular to be predictable.
  • - 2. An opportunity to learn these regularities through prolonged practice.
High-Validity Environments
  • - Situations are complex but fundamentally orderly.
  • - Examples: Chess, bridge, poker, physicians, nurses, firefighters, anesthesiologists.
  • - Experts learn to use highly valid cues, even if they can't name them.

Low/Zero-Validity Environments
  • - Situations are fundamentally unpredictable.
  • - Examples: Stock pickers, political scientists making long-term forecasts.
  • - In these environments, intuitive 'hits' are due to luck or lies.

Wicked Environments
  • - Environments where professionals are likely to learn the wrong lessons from experience.
  • - Example: A physician in the early 20th century who thought he could intuit typhoid, but was actually spreading it by palpating patients' tongues without washing his hands.


Role of Feedback
  • - The quality and speed of feedback are essential for developing expertise.
  • - Ideal conditions: Immediate and unambiguous feedback (e.g., braking a car on a curve).
  • - Poor conditions: Delayed, sparse, or nonexistent feedback.
Medical Examples
  • - Good Feedback: Anesthesiologists see the effects of their actions quickly. If one says 'I have a feeling something is wrong,' it should be taken seriously.
  • - Poor Feedback: Radiologists get little information on the accuracy of their diagnoses. Psychotherapists get poor feedback on long-term patient outcomes.


Limits of Expertise
  • - Experts may not know the limits of their own expertise.
  • - Skill in one task (e.g., a therapist sensing a patient's immediate reaction) does not guarantee skill in another (e.g., long-term forecasting for that patient).
  • - This unrecognized limit helps explain expert overconfidence.



Forecasting & The Outside View
  • - Illustrates two profoundly different approaches to forecasting, highlighting a major cognitive bias.
The Curriculum Project: A Case Study
  • - A team designing a new school curriculum was asked to estimate the project's completion time.
The Inside View
  • - The team's spontaneous approach.
  • - Focused on their specific circumstances, plans, and experiences.
  • - Forecast: 1.5 to 2.5 years.
  • - This view is prone to the planning fallacy and ignoring 'unknown unknowns'.

The Outside View
  • - Prompted by asking an expert about similar past projects (the 'reference class').
  • - Used statistics from a class of similar cases to create a baseline prediction.
The Grim Statistics
  • - A substantial fraction, about 40%, of similar teams failed to finish their project.
  • - For the teams that did finish, it took between 7 and 10 years.
  • - The team's own skill was rated as 'below average' compared to the reference class.


The Outcome
  • - The team ignored the outside view and continued the project.
  • - The project was finally completed in 8 years and was never used.
  • - This demonstrates irrational perseverance and the sunk-cost fallacy (unwillingness to abandon a project after investing effort).


The Planning Fallacy
  • - The tendency to create plans and forecasts that are:
  • - 1. Unrealistically close to best-case scenarios.
  • - 2. Could be improved by consulting the statistics of similar cases (i.e., taking the outside view).
Causes
  • - Cognitive Bias: Focusing on a specific plan (inside view) while neglecting base rates (outside view).
  • - Optimism Bias: Overestimating benefits and underestimating costs.
  • - Motivation: Desire to get a plan approved by superiors or clients.

Examples of Overruns
  • - Scottish Parliament: Initial estimate £40 million ---> Final cost £431 million.
  • - Rail Projects (1969-1998): Planners overestimated passenger numbers by an average of 106%, with an average cost overrun of 45%.
  • - Kitchen Remodels: Homeowners expected to pay $18,658 ---> Actual average cost was $38,769.


Mitigating the Planning Fallacy
  • - The cure is to deliberately adopt the outside view.
Reference Class Forecasting
  • - The technical name for the treatment, developed by Bent Flyvbjerg.
3-Step Method
  • - 1. Identify an appropriate reference class (e.g., large railway projects, kitchen renovations).
  • - 2. Obtain statistics of the reference class (e.g., cost overruns, completion times) to generate a baseline prediction.
  • - 3. Use specific information about the current case to adjust the baseline prediction.


Organizational Strategy
  • - A well-run organization should:
  • - Reward planners for precise execution and accurate forecasts.
  • - Penalize for failing to anticipate difficulties, including 'unknown unknowns'.
