Introduction & Overview
  • - Gastroenterology is a leading specialty for AI innovation, with some of the most translationally mature applications in medicine.
  • - Endoscopy AI is particularly advanced, with regulatory approval for computer-aided detection (CADe) and computer-aided diagnosis (CADx) in colonoscopy.
  • - The clinical efficacy of CADe in colonoscopy is now supported by high-quality evidence from multiple randomized controlled trials (RCTs).
Key Implementation Challenges
  • - Navigating evolving regulatory hurdles for AI as a medical device (AIaMD).
  • - Generating robust real-world clinical evidence to demonstrate impact on patient outcomes.
  • - Ensuring cost-effectiveness and establishing reimbursement models.
  • - Addressing novel challenges from emerging tech like generative AI (genAI).
  • - Managing ethical and medicolegal concerns (data governance, patient harm, liability, bias).


Regulatory Approval Pathways
  • - Global variation exists, but common principles apply in major jurisdictions like the US and EU.
  • - Processes are defined to demonstrate safety and clinical efficacy based on intended use and risk classification.
üá∫üá∏ US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
  • - Devices are categorized into 3 classes based on risk: Class I (low), Class II (medium), and Class III (highest).
510(k) Clearance
  • - Granted to devices that are ‚Äúsubstantially equivalent‚Äù to a predicate device.
  • - Generally requires the least evidence, focusing on bench and performance data.
  • - It is the fastest and least resource-intensive option.
  • - Note: All endoscopy AI systems cleared via 510(k) have been evaluated in RCT settings.

Premarket Approval (PMA)
  • - Required for Class III (highest risk) devices.
  • - Most rigorous pathway, requiring extensive clinical evidence, often from RCTs with US-representative populations.

De Novo Pathway
  • - Targets novel devices without a predicate.
  • - Bridges the gap between 510(k) and PMA, typically relying on pivotal studies.


üá™üá∫ European Union (EU)
  • - The new Medical Device Regulation (MDR) became effective in 2021.
  • - Risk classes are similar to the FDA but with additional subcategories: Class IIa and Class IIb for medium-risk devices.
  • - The class determines the conformity assessment route, which usually requires an independent notified body.

Regulatory Challenges & Future Directions
  • - Regulatory uncertainty for AI as a Medical Device (AIaMD) may delay technological innovation.
  • - Traditional pathways are not well-designed for the dynamic pace of AI.
EU Challenges
  • - The new EU MDR poses significant challenges, especially for small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs).
  • - AIaMD providing information for clinical decision making is classified as at least Class IIa.
MDR Impact
  • - A 2022 survey showed that MDR certificates had not been issued for >85% of previously certified devices.
  • - Up to 30% of SMEs were unable to access an MDR-designated notified body.
  • - Consequence: Many companies are planning to reduce their portfolio or deprioritize Europe for first approval.

EU AI Act
  • - The world's first comprehensive legal framework on AI.
  • - Creates strict rules for high-risk AI systems, requiring comprehensive AI-specific technical documentation (ISO/IEC 420001).


US FDA Challenges & Directions
  • - Currently no AI-specific clearance pathway; most use the 510(k) pathway.
Predicate Creep
  • - A key risk of the 510(k) pathway.
  • - Lenient interpretation of 'substantial equivalence' allows generations of devices to be cleared with iterative changes, resulting in devices dissimilar from original predicates.
  • - Analysis showed almost one-third of FDA-cleared AI/ML algorithms were based on a non-AI/ML initial predicate.

FDA's Proposed Solutions
  • - Total Product Lifecycle Approach: An adaptive regulatory scheme for AI that can continuously learn and improve.
  • - Predetermined Change Control Plans: Allows manufacturers to prespecify and get premarket authorization for intended modifications without new submissions.
  • - Global Collaboration: Working with UK and Canada on Good Machine Learning Practices.


Generative AI (genAI) Challenges
  • - Definition: AI models that generate derived synthetic content (images, text, etc.).
  • - Often built on broad 'foundation models'.
Unique Regulatory Issues
  • - Difficult to define a clearly constrained intended use due to broad capabilities.
  • - Prone to ‚Äúhallucinations‚Äù (generation of erroneous or false content).
  • - Lack of transparency regarding model development, especially when using off-the-shelf software.



Other Barriers to Clinical Implementation
  • - Beyond regulation, several other factors hinder widespread adoption of AI in gastroenterology.
Clinical Trials & Real-World Evidence
  • - Most AI evidence consists of preclinical or small-scale studies.
  • - Gastroenterology is the largest contributor to the 65 published prospective RCTs for AI health technologies.
RCTs vs. Observational Studies
  • - RCTs: Overwhelmingly show improved adenoma detection rate (ADR) with CADe, but may induce a Hawthorne effect (behavior change due to trial participation).
  • - Observational Studies: Found CADe provided less or no efficacy, but are prone to selection bias and lack controlled groups.

Human-AI Interaction
  • - Clinical efficacy is highly dependent on the interaction between the human operator and the AI system.
Behavioral Risks
  • - Overreliance: CADe could lead to unconscious degradation in the quality of mucosal exposure.
  • - Altered Gaze: Eye-tracking studies showed CADe led to less time spent looking at the peripheries of the screen.
  • - Detrimental Interaction: CADx studies showed the highest accuracy was achieved by standalone AI, with human interaction being detrimental to performance.

Physician Sentiment Biases
  • - Automation Bias: Tendency to favor the AI's output over one's own judgment, even when the AI is incorrect.
  • - Algorithmic Aversion: General mistrust of AI, leading to rapid loss of confidence after errors, even if overall performance is superior.


Trial Endpoints & Outcomes
  • - ADR is an established endpoint, but CADe mainly increases detection of diminutive polyps (<5 mm).
  • - The impact of detecting these small lesions on long-term colorectal cancer (CRC) incidence is uncertain.
  • - There is a potential risk of overdiagnosis leading to unnecessary surveillance.


Cost-Effectiveness & Reimbursement
  • - A major current barrier to implementation with very few published economic evaluations.
Cost Analysis
  • - Short-term: Costs may increase with CADe due to more polypectomies, pathology exams, and surveillance.
  • - Long-term: Microsimulation studies estimate significant savings from reduced CRC incidence, with one study projecting yearly savings of $290 million in the US.
CADx Cost Savings
  • - A post-hoc analysis of a clinical trial suggested a CADx-enabled optical diagnosis strategy for diminutive rectosigmoid polyps could lead to an 11% reduction in average colonoscopy costs.
  • - This could potentially save $85.2 million in the US annually.


Reimbursement Models
  • - Currently, there is no specific reimbursement for CADe in colonoscopy in the United States.
  • - Japan's public health insurance announced an add-on payment for a CADe tool in February 2024.
  • - Alternative models proposed include value-based payments, time-limited reimbursements, and advance market commitments.



Ethical and Medicolegal Issues
  • - Implementation of AI raises major concerns regarding data governance, patient harm, accountability, and bias.
Data Governance
  • - AI development requires vast quantities of data, creating privacy challenges (HIPAA, GDPR).
  • - Anonymization: Full anonymization is difficult to achieve, with risks of re-identification, especially for rare diseases.
  • - Consent: Traditional consent models are too narrow for future secondary AI research. Broader consent strategies complemented by robust governance may be needed.

Accountability & Liability
  • - When harm occurs, liability is complex: is it the clinician, hospital, developer, or data labeler?
  • - A key distinction is between improper use of the AI and errors from a flawed algorithm.
Role of AI Autonomy
  • - As AI autonomy increases, the burden of responsibility is likely to shift toward algorithm developers.
  • - The American Medical Association (AMA) policy states that for fully autonomous systems, the developer must accept liability and hold medical liability insurance.
  • - Example: Digital Diagnostics carries medical malpractice insurance for its autonomous AI system for diabetic retinopathy screening.


Lessons from Other Industries
  • - Automotive Industry: SAE's 6-level classification for driving automation (Level 0 to 5) can be adapted for healthcare. Liability is shifting towards corporations (e.g., Mercedes accepting responsibility for accidents).
  • - Manufacturing Industry: Collaborative robots, or "cobots," work alongside humans to enhance precision, a model that could apply to complex procedures like endoscopic submucosal dissection.

Bias & Health Inequalities
  • - AI algorithms may introduce bias and exacerbate health inequalities.
  • - This can occur at multiple stages: problem selection, data collection, algorithm development, and post-deployment.
Mitigation Efforts
  • - Good Machine Learning Practice: Guiding principles from regulatory bodies recommend that data should be representative of the intended patient population.
  • - Transparency Issue: Patient demographic characteristics for training datasets are often not in the public domain for most CADe software.
  • - STANDING Together Initiative: An international, consensus-based exercise to develop recommendations for the composition and reporting of datasets to underpin medical AI and tackle bias.



Conclusions
  • - The path to real-world clinical implementation of AI in gastroenterology is complex and involves overcoming numerous challenges.
  • - Key hurdles include evolving regulatory landscapes, the need for high-quality real-world evidence, and the development of viable reimbursement models.
  • - Addressing issues of bias, accountability, and data governance is critical for patient safety and trust.
  • - Ongoing proactive collaboration between all stakeholders is required to harness the transformative potential of AI to improve patient care.
