1. The Illusion of Understanding
  • - We believe we understand the past, which makes us think the future is predictable.
  • - In reality, we understand the past less than we think we do.
Narrative Fallacy
  • - Coined by Nassim Taleb.
  • - Describes how we create flawed stories about the past to make sense of the world.
  • - These stories shape our view of the future.
Characteristics of Compelling Stories
  • - They are simple and coherent.
  • - They are concrete, not abstract.
  • - They give a large role to talent and intention, and a small role to luck.
  • - They focus on a few striking events that happened, ignoring the countless things that did not happen.

Example: The Google Story
  • - The story of Google's success seems like a clear result of skill and good decisions.
  • - This simple narrative fosters an illusion of inevitability.
  • - It makes us underestimate the huge role that luck played in their success.

The Problem with Narratives
  • - The real test of an explanation is if it could have predicted the event in advance. No story can do this.
  • - The human mind struggles to deal with 'non-events' (things that could have happened but didn't).
  • - WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is) is at work: we build the best story from limited information and believe it.


Hindsight Bias (The 'I-Knew-It-All-Along' Effect)
  • - Once an event happens, we adjust our view of the world to accommodate the surprise.
  • - We lose the ability to remember our old beliefs accurately.
  • - This makes us underestimate how much we were surprised by past events.
Demonstration of Hindsight Bias
  • - Baruch Fischhoff's study before President Nixon's 1972 visit to China.
  • - People were asked to recall the probabilities they had assigned to events before the visit.
  • - Result: If an event occurred, people exaggerated the probability they had assigned to it. If it didn't occur, they recalled thinking it was always unlikely.

Pernicious Effects of Hindsight
  • - Hindsight bias has dangerous consequences, especially for evaluating decisions.
Outcome Bias
  • - We assess the quality of a decision by its outcome, not by whether the process was sound.
  • - A good decision with a bad outcome is blamed unfairly.
  • - A risky gamble with a good outcome is praised excessively.
Example: Duluth Bridge Monitor
  • - A legal case about hiring a bridge monitor to prevent flood damage.
  • - Group 1 (told only facts available at the time): 24% said the city should hire a monitor.
  • - Group 2 (told a flood had occurred): 56% said the city should have hired one, despite being told to avoid hindsight.


Impact on Professionals
  • - Hindsight is especially unkind to physicians, financial advisers, CEOs, and politicians.
  • - It encourages risk aversion and bureaucratic solutions to avoid blame.
  • - For example, doctors may order more tests to protect themselves, not necessarily to benefit the patient.



Recipes for Success (Business Books)
  • - Business books often satisfy our need for simple stories where success is a direct result of good leadership and practices.
  • - This is largely an illusion.
The Role of Luck vs. Skill
  • - The correlation between a firm's success and its CEO's quality is generously estimated at .30.
  • - This means a stronger CEO leads the more successful firm in only about 60% of cases (only 10% better than a random guess).

The Halo Effect in Business
  • - A successful company's CEO is seen as 'flexible' and 'decisive'.
  • - If the company fails, the same CEO is seen as 'rigid' and 'confused'.
  • - We get the cause-and-effect backward: The CEO appears rigid because the firm is failing.

Regression to the Mean
  • - Companies praised in books like 'Built to Last' and 'In Search of Excellence' often perform worse in the years after the book is published.
  • - This is not because they became complacent; it's because their initial success was partly due to luck, and their performance regressed to the average.



2. The Illusion of Validity & Skill
  • - We often feel confident in our judgments even when they are based on weak evidence and are statistically useless.
The Illusion of Validity
  • - The feeling of confidence we have in our judgments, even when we know from global evidence that our predictions are not accurate.
Example: Israeli Army Officer Evaluation
  • - The author evaluated officer candidates using a 'leaderless group challenge'.
  • - The observers felt they could clearly see each candidate's 'true nature' and were very confident in their predictions.
The Reality
  • - Feedback sessions showed their ability to predict actual performance was negligible (almost useless).
  • - The Paradox: Knowing this fact had no effect on their confidence when evaluating the next group of candidates.
  • - This is the Illusion of Validity.


Source of Subjective Confidence
  • - Confidence is a feeling from System 1.
  • - It reflects the coherence of the story we create, not the quality or amount of evidence.
  • - Poor evidence can make a very good, coherent story.


The Illusion of Stock-Picking Skill
  • - A major industry built largely on an illusion of skill.
  • - Most buyers and sellers have the same information but different opinions, each believing they know more than the market.
Individual Investors
  • - A study by Terry Odean of 10,000 brokerage accounts showed poor performance.
  • - On average, shares that investors sold did better than the shares they bought by 3.2 percentage points per year.
  • - The most active traders had the poorest results.

Professional Investors (Fund Managers)
  • - Research over 50 years is conclusive: most fund managers are playing a game of chance, not skill.
  • - Typically, at least two out of three mutual funds underperform the market in any given year.
  • - The year-to-year correlation of performance is near zero, meaning success is mostly luck.
Author's Study at an Investment Firm
  • - Analyzed performance of 25 wealth advisers over 8 years.
  • - Computed 28 year-to-year correlations to check for persistent skill.
  • - The average correlation was .01 (effectively zero).
  • - The firm was rewarding luck as if it were skill, and this fact was ignored by the executives and advisers.



The Illusions of Pundits (Experts)
  • - The illusion that we understand the past fosters overconfidence in our ability to predict the future.
Philip Tetlock's 20-Year Study
  • - Interviewed 284 experts who made a living commenting on political and economic trends.
  • - Gathered over 80,000 predictions.
Devastating Results
  • - The experts performed worse than if they had assigned equal probabilities to the outcomes.
  • - They were worse than dart-throwing monkeys.
  • - The most famous experts were often the most overconfident and least accurate.


Hedgehogs vs. Foxes
  • - A classification of expert thinking styles, from Isaiah Berlin.
Hedgehogs
  • - Know 'one big thing'.
  • - Have a single, coherent theory about the world.
  • - Are very confident and reluctant to admit error.
  • - Make for good television but are poor forecasters.

Foxes
  • - Are complex thinkers who believe reality emerges from many interacting forces, including luck.
  • - Are less confident and more likely to admit error.
  • - They scored best in Tetlock's study, but their performance was still very poor.




3. Intuitions vs. Formulas
  • - Simple, statistical rules (algorithms or formulas) are often superior to the intuitive judgments of experts.
Paul Meehl's 'Disturbing Little Book'
  • - Reviewed studies comparing clinical predictions (expert intuition) with statistical predictions (formulas).
  • - The contest between algorithms and humans has a clear winner.
The Score
  • - In about 60% of ~200 studies, algorithms were significantly more accurate.
  • - The other 40% were roughly a tie, which is a win for the cheaper, faster formulas.
  • - This holds true in diverse fields: medicine, economics, government, etc.

Example: Predicting Bordeaux Wine Prices
  • - Economist Orley Ashenfelter created a formula to predict the future price of wine.
  • - It uses 3 weather variables: summer temperature, harvest rain, and winter rain.
  • - The formula's predictions have a correlation with actual prices of above .90, far more accurate than expert opinions.


Why Formulas Work Better
  • - Humans are inconsistent, while formulas always give the same output for the same input.
Expert Flaws
  • - Experts try to be clever and think 'outside the box', which often reduces accuracy.
  • - They are prone to cognitive biases and context dependency (e.g., mood, weather).

Human Inconsistency
  • - Experienced radiologists who re-evaluate the same X-ray contradict their own previous judgment 20% of the time.
  • - This level of inconsistency is typical for professionals and makes valid prediction impossible.

The 'Broken-Leg Rule'
  • - Meehl's idea that you should only override a formula when you have rare, decisive information that the formula does not know (e.g., a person predicted to go to the movies just broke their leg).

The Power of Simple Formulas
  • - Even simple, equally-weighted formulas often outperform complex regression models and expert judgment.
  • - Example: Marital stability is well predicted by the formula: frequency of lovemaking minus frequency of quarrels.


The Apgar Score: A Real-World Success
  • - A simple checklist developed by Dr. Virginia Apgar in 1953 to assess newborn health.
  • - It standardized assessment and dramatically helped reduce infant mortality.
The 5 Variables
  • - Heart rate
  • - Respiration
  • - Reflex
  • - Muscle tone
  • - Color
  • - Each is scored 0, 1, or 2. A total score of 8 or above is good.


Hostility to Algorithms
  • - Experts and the public often resist the idea that a simple formula can be better than human intuition.
Reasons for Resistance
  • - It contradicts the personal experience of skill that clinicians feel in their daily work (even if that skill is in short-term, not long-term, prediction).
  • - A strong preference for the 'natural' over the 'artificial'.
  • - The cause of a mistake matters emotionally: an error by an algorithm feels worse than an error by a human.


How to Make Better Hiring Decisions
  • - A practical, formula-based approach to hiring, inspired by Meehl.
The Method
  • - 1. Select ~6 key traits for success in the job.
  • - 2. Create factual questions to assess each trait.
  • - 3. Score each trait on a 1-5 scale, one at a time, to avoid the halo effect.
  • - 4. Add up the scores for a final result.
  • - 5. Hire the candidate with the highest score, even if you intuitively like someone else better.
