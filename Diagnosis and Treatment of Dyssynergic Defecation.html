Introduction to Dyssynergic Defecation
  • - An acquired behavioral problem due to the inability to coordinate abdominal and pelvic floor muscles for stool evacuation.
  • - Affects up to one half of patients with chronic constipation.
  • - The term dyssynergic defecation is endorsed by the Rome criteria as it aptly describes the core pathophysiology of incoordination.
Alternative Terminology
  • - This condition has been described by several terms, which are now less preferred:
  • - Anismus
  • - Pelvic floor dyssynergia
  • - Obstructive defecation
  • - Paradoxical puborectalis contraction
  • - Pelvic outlet obstruction
  • - Spastic pelvic floor syndrome


Epidemiology & Impact
  • - The global prevalence of chronic constipation in the community ranges from 11% to 18%.
  • - Dyssynergic defecation is detected in 27-59% of patients with chronic constipation.
Demographics
  • - More common in women, with a female:male ratio of 2.2:1.
  • - Prevalence increases with age, particularly after age 65.
  • - Higher prevalence in African Americans, lower socioeconomic status, pregnancy, and neurological diseases (e.g., Parkinson's, multiple sclerosis).

Economic & Social Impact
  • - Significant healthcare resource utilization.
  • - In the US, direct per-patient expenditure ranges from $1912 to $7522 per year.
  • - Results in 13.7 million days of restricted activity.
  • - Impairs ability to work in 60% of patients.

Psychological Distress & Quality of Life
  • - Associated with increased anxiety, depression, somatization, and obsessive-compulsiveness.
  • - Patients with dyssynergia show greater psychological distress than those with slow transit constipation (STC).
  • - A history of abuse is common:
  • - Sexual abuse: reported by 22-48% of subjects.
  • - Physical abuse: reported by 31-74% of subjects.
  • - Health-related quality of life is significantly impaired, often more so than in STC.


Etiology & Pathophysiology
  • - The etiology is often unclear, but it is considered an acquired condition.
Onset of Condition
  • - A prospective survey found the problem began:
  • - During childhood in 31% of patients.
  • - After a specific event (pregnancy, trauma, back injury) in 29%.
  • - With no identifiable cause in 40%.
  • - Excessive straining to expel hard stools over time may lead to dyssynergia.

Core Pathophysiology
  • - The primary problem is incoordination (dyssynergia) of the muscles involved in defecation, not simply anal spasm.
  • - Myectomy or Botulinum toxin injections, which target spasm, have shown minimal improvement (10-30% and minimal, respectively).
Mechanisms of Dyssynergia
  • - Failure of rectoanal coordination consists of:
  • - Inadequate propulsive force from abdominal muscles.
  • - Paradoxical anal contraction during attempted defecation.
  • - Inadequate anal relaxation.
  • - Impaired rectal sensation is also present in 50-60% of patients.

Phenotypes of Dysfunction
  • - A large study identified 3 distinct phenotypes:
  • - 1. High anal sphincter pressure at rest and during defecation.
  • - 2. Inadequate propulsive force.
  • - 3. A hybrid of both disturbances.



Clinical Evaluation & Diagnosis
  • - The first step is to exclude underlying metabolic or pathologic disorders and structural abnormalities (e.g., rectoceles, fissures, prolapse).
Clinical Features & Symptoms
  • - Symptoms alone cannot reliably differentiate between slow transit constipation (STC) and dyssynergic defecation.
Common Symptoms
  • - Excessive straining: 85%
  • - Feeling of incomplete evacuation: 75%
  • - Passage of hard stools: 65%
  • - Stool frequency < 3/week: 62%
  • - Use of digital maneuvers to facilitate defecation: 66%

Associated Conditions
  • - A substantial number of patients have overlapping IBS features.
  • - 85% of patients with suspected levator ani syndrome without constipation have physiologic features of dyssynergic defecation.
  • - Many patients with solitary rectal ulcer syndrome also exhibit dyssynergia.

Psychological Factors
  • - Obsessive-compulsive disorder (e.g., belief that a daily bowel movement is necessary).
  • - Phobia for painful defecation.
  • - Association with bulimia or anorexia nervosa.


Diagnostic Procedures
  • - A combination of clinical evaluation and objective testing is required for a definitive diagnosis.
Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)
  • - Often the most revealing part of a clinical evaluation.
  • - Assesses for stricture, spasm, tenderness, and stool presence (awareness of which suggests normal rectal sensitivity).
  • - Key Maneuver: Ask patient to push/bear down ---> examiner should feel relaxation of the external anal sphincter/puborectalis and perineal descent.
  • - Diagnostic Accuracy: Sensitivity of 75% and specificity of 87% for identifying dyssynergia.

Anorectal Manometry
  • - Essential for a diagnosis of dyssynergic defecation.
  • - Assesses rectal sensation, reflexes, and coordination during attempted defecation.
Manometric Patterns of Dyssynergia (Fig. 1)
  • - Type I: Adequate propulsive force with paradoxical anal contraction.
  • - Type II: Inadequate propulsive force with paradoxical anal contraction.
  • - Type III: Adequate propulsive force with inadequate (< 20%) anal relaxation.
  • - Type IV: Inadequate propulsive force with inadequate anal relaxation.

Clinical Considerations
  • - Dyssynergic patterns can be seen in asymptomatic controls, partly due to testing in a non-physiologic (left lateral) position.
  • - Proper coaching can sometimes normalize manometric findings.
  • - Measures the Defecation Index (ratio of intrarectal pressure to anal residual pressure) as a quantitative measure of coordination.


Balloon Expulsion Test (BET)
  • - An essential component of physiologic testing.
  • - Patient is asked to expel a balloon filled with 50 mL of warm water.
  • - Normal individuals can expel the balloon within one minute.
  • - It is a useful screening test with high specificity (80-90%) but low sensitivity (50%).

Defecography & MR Defecography
  • - Dynamic imaging studies that visualize the evacuation process.
  • - Useful for identifying structural changes like rectoceles, prolapse, and intussusception.
  • - MR defecography offers excellent soft tissue resolution without radiation exposure.
  • - Often used as an adjunct test when manometry and BET results are equivocal.

Colonic Transit Study
  • - Measures the time it takes for stool to move through the colon using radio-opaque markers or a wireless motility capsule (WMC).
  • - Up to two-thirds of patients with dyssynergic defecation also have delayed colonic transit.
  • - Recommendation: Evaluate and treat dyssynergia first, as colonic transit often improves after successful treatment of the outlet dysfunction.


Diagnostic Criteria (Proposed Rome IV)
  • - Diagnosis requires satisfying criteria from all three categories below:
1. Symptom Criteria
  • - Patient must satisfy the diagnostic criteria for functional constipation and/or constipation-predominant IBS (IBS-C).

2. Manometric Criteria
  • - Patient must demonstrate a dyssynergic pattern of defecation (Types I-IV) during repeated attempts on manometry, imaging, or EMG.

3. Corroborative Test Criteria
  • - Patient must satisfy one or more of the following:
  • - Inability to expel a 50 mL water-filled balloon within 1-2 minutes.
  • - Inability to evacuate or ≥ 50% retention of barium during defecography.
  • - Prolonged colonic transit time (e.g., >5 markers retained at 120 hours).



Treatment of Dyssynergic Defecation
  • - Treatment should be customized, considering symptoms, pathophysiology, age, and patient expectations.
Standard Treatment & Lifestyle
  • - Includes correction of coexisting issues and behavioral modifications.
General Measures
  • - Avoid constipating medications.
  • - Ensure adequate fiber (up to 25 g per day) and fluid intake.
  • - Regular exercise.

Timed Toilet Training
  • - Educate patient to attempt a bowel movement at least twice a day, usually 30 minutes after meals.
  • - Instruct to push at 50-70% of maximum effort.
  • - Strain for no more than 5 minutes per attempt.

Fiber Supplements
  • - Soluble fiber (e.g., psyllium) is beneficial for mild-to-moderate constipation.
  • - Patients with dyssynergic defecation or slow colonic transit respond poorly to fiber supplementation alone.


Pharmacologic Approaches
  • - Laxatives and newer drugs have not been systematically evaluated in dyssynergic defecation but can be used as adjunctive treatment with biofeedback therapy.
  • - Newer drugs include Linaclotide, Lubiprostone, and Prucalopride.

Biofeedback Therapy
  • - The mainstay of treatment for dyssynergic defecation.
  • - An instrument-based 'operant conditioning' technique.
  • - Assigned a Grade A recommendation by ANMS and ESNM.
Goals of Biofeedback
  • - 1. To correct the incoordination of abdominal and pelvic floor muscles during evacuation.
  • - 2. To improve perception of rectal filling in patients with impaired rectal sensation.

Efficacy & Outcomes (See Table)
  • - Treatment efficacy is 70-80% in randomized controlled trials (RCTs).
  • - More effective than sham therapy, polyethylene glycol (PEG), or diazepam.
  • - Efficacy is maintained long-term, for more than 2 years after treatment.
  • - Symptom improvement correlates with correction of the underlying pathophysiology.

Treatment Protocol
  • - Typically requires 4 to 6 sessions, each lasting 60 minutes, scheduled about 2 weeks apart.
  • - Phase I: Evaluation & Education (explaining physiology, diaphragmatic breathing).
  • - Phase II: Active Therapy (visual/auditory feedback training).
  • - Phase III: Reinforcement (sessions at 6 weeks, 3, 6, and 12 months).

Biofeedback Techniques
  • - Training is ideally performed while the patient is seated on a commode.
1. Correcting Dyssynergia
  • - Rectoanal Coordination: Patient uses visual feedback from a manometry or EMG monitor to learn how to generate an abdominal push while simultaneously relaxing the anal sphincter.
  • - Simulated Defecation: Patient practices expelling a 50 mL water-filled balloon to reinforce the correct technique.

2. Rectal Sensory Training
  • - Goal is to improve awareness of rectal filling and the urge to defecate.
  • - A balloon is progressively inflated in the rectum, and the patient learns to recognize smaller volumes, using visual cues to re-establish sensory thresholds.
  • - Barostat-assisted sensory training is superior to syringe-assisted training.



Other Measures
  • - These methods are generally less effective than biofeedback therapy.
  • - Botulinum toxin injection: Mixed results, with improvement in less than half of patients and a risk of incontinence.
  • - Myectomy or Surgery: Generally not recommended as primary therapy.


Summary & Conclusion
  • - Dyssynergic defecation is a common, diagnosable, and treatable cause of chronic constipation.
  • - Diagnosis relies on a careful history, DRE, and anorectal physiologic tests (manometry, BET).
  • - Biofeedback therapy is the first-line treatment, proven to be highly effective in both the short and long term by correcting the underlying pathophysiology.
