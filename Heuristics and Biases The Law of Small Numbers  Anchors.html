Heuristics and Biases: The Law of Small Numbers & Anchors

  • - In the Campbell's soup experiment, what was the average number of cans purchased when a 'limit of 12 per person' sign was present?
  • - What did a statistical analysis of rocket bomb hits in London during WWII reveal?
  • - What is the 'hot hand' in basketball considered to be?

The Law of Small Numbers
  • - The incorrect belief that the principles of large samples apply to small samples as well.
  • - Our minds, particularly System 1, have a strong bias toward believing that small samples closely resemble the population from which they are drawn.
Illustrative Example: Kidney Cancer Incidence
  • - An analysis of 3,141 US counties revealed a strange pattern.
The Observation & Causal Trap
  • - Counties with the LOWEST incidence of kidney cancer were mostly: rural, sparsely populated, and traditionally Republican.
  • - Counties with the HIGHEST incidence of kidney cancer shared the exact same characteristics.
  • - System 1 jumps to create causal stories (e.g., clean rural living vs. poverty of rural living), but these explanations are contradictory and wrong.

The Statistical Truth
  • - The key factor is not lifestyle but small population size.
  • - Extreme outcomes (both very high and very low) are statistically more likely to be found in small samples than in large ones.
  • - The observed cancer rates are an artifact of the sample size, not a true fact to be explained.


Mind's Relationship with Statistics
  • - System 1: Automatically identifies causal connections, even spurious ones. It is inept with 'merely statistical' facts.
  • - System 2: Required for statistical thinking, which is effortful and less intuitive.
Two Equivalent Statements
  • - 1. 'Large samples are more precise than small samples.' ---> Intuitively true.
  • - 2. 'Small samples yield extreme results more often than large samples do.' ---> Less intuitive, but means the same thing.


A Bias of Confidence Over Doubt
  • - We naturally focus on the story (the message's content) rather than the reliability of its source (e.g., sample size).
  • - System 1 suppresses ambiguity and constructs coherent stories, an effect known as WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is).
  • - System 2 can sustain doubt, but this is harder work than sliding into certainty.

Cause and Chance: Misinterpreting Randomness
  • - Our associative machinery (System 1) seeks causes and sees patterns, even in random events.
  • - A sequence like BGBBGB appears more random and likely than GGGGGG, even though all specific sequences of six births are equally likely.
Real-World Example: WWII London Bombing
  • - The public believed bombing patterns were not random due to conspicuous gaps on the map, suspecting spies.
  • - A careful statistical analysis revealed the distribution was typical of a random process.
  • - Key insight: 'To the untrained eye, randomness appears as regularity or a tendency to cluster.'

Real-World Example: The 'Hot Hand' in Basketball
  • - The belief that players get 'hot' is a massive and widespread cognitive illusion.
  • - Analysis of thousands of shots shows that the sequence of successes and misses satisfies all tests of randomness.
  • - The 'hot hand' is entirely in the eye of the beholders, who are too quick to perceive order and causality.

Real-World Example: Successful Schools Research
  • - The Gates Foundation invested $1.7 billion based on research showing the most successful schools are often small.
  • - The critical flaw: the worst schools also tend to be smaller than average.
  • - The truth: Small schools are not better on average; they are simply more variable.


Implications for Researchers
  • - Researchers' intuitions about sample size are often flawed.
  • - They commonly choose samples so small they face a 50% risk of failing to confirm a true hypothesis.
  • - Recommendation: Regard statistical intuitions with proper suspicion and replace impression formation with computation whenever possible.


Anchoring
  • - An effect that occurs when people consider a particular value (the 'anchor') for an unknown quantity before estimating that quantity.
  • - The final estimate tends to stay close to the anchor, even if the anchor is arbitrary and uninformative.
The Two Mechanisms of Anchoring
  • - Anchoring is produced by two different mechanisms, involving both System 1 and System 2.
1. Anchoring as Adjustment (System 2)
  • - A deliberate, conscious, and effortful process.
  • - Strategy: Start from an anchor number and adjust away from it.
  • - The adjustment is typically insufficient because people stop when they are no longer certain they should move farther.
  • - This is a failure of a 'weak or lazy' System 2.

2. Anchoring as a Priming Effect (System 1)
  • - An automatic, involuntary process with no corresponding subjective experience.
  • - It is a form of suggestion, where the anchor selectively evokes compatible evidence and thoughts.
  • - System 1 tries to construct a world in which the anchor is the true number, a manifestation of associative coherence.
Supporting Evidence
  • - A high temperature anchor (20°C) made it easier to recognize summer words (sun, beach).
  • - A high price anchor for cars selectively primed names of luxury brands (Mercedes, Audi).



Measuring the Anchor: The Anchoring Index
  • - The anchoring effect can be measured as a percentage.
  • - Formula: (Difference between estimates) / (Difference between anchors)
  • - In an experiment about redwood tree height, the anchoring index was 55%.

Real-World Power of Anchors
  • - Anchors are powerful even when they are obviously random and uninformative.
Real Estate Agents
  • - When assessing a house, professional agents were asked to consider a high or low asking price.
  • - They insisted the price had no effect, but they were wrong: the anchoring index was 41%.

Charitable Donations
  • - When asked to donate to save seabirds:
  • - No anchor ---> average donation was $64.
  • - $5 anchor ---> average donation was $20.
  • - $400 anchor ---> average donation was $143.

German Judges
  • - Judges rolled a loaded die (getting a 3 or 9) before sentencing a shoplifter.
  • - Judges who rolled a 9 gave an average sentence of 8 months.
  • - Judges who rolled a 3 gave an average sentence of 5 months.
  • - The anchoring effect was 50%.


Uses, Abuses, and Defenses
  • - Anchoring is widely used in marketing and negotiations.
Marketing & Sales
  • - Arbitrary Rationing: A 'limit of 12 per person' sign for Campbell's soup led shoppers to buy an average of 7 cans, twice as many as when there was no limit.

Negotiations
  • - Moving first by setting a list price is an advantage.
  • - Defense Strategy: If the other side makes an outrageous proposal, do not counter with another outrageous offer. Instead, make it clear you will not negotiate with that number on the table.
  • - Another defense is to deliberately 'think the opposite' by activating System 2 to search for arguments against the anchor.


Anchoring and the Two Systems
  • - System 2 makes the final judgment, but it works on data retrieved by System 1.
  • - System 1's retrieval is automatically and involuntarily biased by the anchor.
  • - System 2 has no control over this effect and often no knowledge of it.
  • - Takeaway: Assume that any number on the table has had an anchoring effect on you. If the stakes are high, mobilize your System 2 to combat the effect.
