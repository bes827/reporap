High-resolution Manometry in Treated Achalasia: Scoping Review and Novel Nomenclature
  • This scoping review + expert recommendations provide guidance for HRM interpretation in treated achalasia.
  • It introduces novel descriptive terms for pathophysiology post-treatment.
  • Conventional Chicago Classification (CC) should NOT be used for treated achalasia description.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the recommended IRP cutoff for Medtronic systems to describe adequately treated achalasia (ATA)?
  • 2. Which provocative maneuver is particularly relevant in assessing residual obstruction in treated achalasia?
  • 3. What novel term is proposed for treated achalasia when features of EGJ obstruction or obstructive esophageal body spasms are present?
  • 4. What is the significance of 'delta-IRP' in evaluating treatment response in achalasia?
  • 5. Beyond HRM, what other diagnostic tools are crucial for assessing clinical-manometric discordance and anatomical alterations in treated achalasia?
Background & Problem Statement
  • Chicago Classification (CC) is standard for primary motility disorder diagnosis, esp. achalasia.
  • CC lacks recommendations for HRM in treated achalasia (post-surgery/endoscopic interventions).
  • This gap leads to challenges in interpreting HRM findings post-treatment.
HRM Evolution & CC 4.0
  • HRM revolutionized esophageal motility disorder diagnosis.
  • CC 4.0 improved specificity for diagnosis + achalasia subtyping.
  • HRM guides personalized management & treatment for naïve achalasia.
Advanced HRM Metrics
  • Intra-bolus pressurization (IBP) + secondary position measurements → improved differential diagnosis.
  • Helps distinguish achalasia from esophagogastric junction outflow obstruction (EGJOO).
Clinical Need for Treated Achalasia Guidance
  • Achalasia treatments (PD, LHM, POEM) are widespread.
  • Increasingly encountered in clinical practice.
  • Need for interpretation advice, optimal protocol, & personalized management for treated patients.
Methodology
  • Systematic scoping review of literature (Pubmed/MEDLINE, Embase, Web of Science) from inception to Dec 2024.
  • 12 esophageal motility experts provided recommendations.
  • Proposed novel nomenclature & management flowchart.
Search Strategy & Selection
  • Adhered to PRISMA-ScR guidelines.
  • 2 research questions guided keyword selection (PICO format).
  • Initial search yielded 9043 reports; 40 studies included after screening.
Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria
  • Included: Articles on HRM findings in treated primary achalasia.
  • Excluded: Pretreatment-only evaluations, studies w/o extractable HRM parameters, secondary/pseudo-achalasia.
Expert Consensus Process
  • Statements/advice developed from systematic review + expert discussion.
  • Formal rating of evidence quality/recommendation strength NOT performed.
  • No IRB approval needed (published data review).
Novel Nomenclature & Key Recommendations
  • Conventional CC should NOT be used in treated achalasia description.
  • New terms proposed to describe pathophysiology post-treatment.
  • Guidance for HRM interpretation in treated achalasia.
Adequately Treated Achalasia (ATA)
  • Proposed term for treated achalasia w/o elevated IRP + no obstructive features in esophageal body.
  • IRP <15 mmHg (Medtronic) / <22 mmHg (Diversatek/Laborie).
  • Avoids confusion from 'absent contractility' in CC.
Treated Achalasia with Outflow Obstruction (TAOO)
  • Proposed term for treated achalasia w/ EGJ obstruction features OR obstructive esophageal body spasms.
  • IRP ≥15 mmHg (Medtronic) / ≥22 mmHg (Diversatek/Laborie).
  • Also includes RDC-IRP ≥10 mmHg, RDC-IBP (20 mmHg isobaric contour), repetitive PEP (30 mmHg), distal spasm, LES shortening.
Delta-IRP
  • Difference between post-treatment & pretreatment IRP.
  • Valuable tool to profile successfully treated achalasia.
  • Availability of pretreatment HRM measurement is advised.
Predictive Value
  • Significantly ↓ in clinical success (23 mmHg) vs failure (14 mmHg; P < .001).
  • Cutoff of 16.5 mmHg predicted clinical success w/ ≥70% sensitivity & specificity.
  • Objectively quantifies manometric improvement post-myotomy.
Residual Spastic Activity in Treated Type III Achalasia
  • Term for spasms in esophageal body + clinical activity, ONLY if pretreatment diagnosis was spastic-type achalasia.
  • Requires retreatment, often w/ POEM (Supplementary Figure 4).
  • Indicates suboptimal clinical outcomes from PD or short myotomy.
Peristaltic/Contractility Reappearance
  • Presence of remnant peristalsis/contractility in esophageal body must be reported.
  • Term 'reappearance' used as body contractility could be uncovered by LES-targeted treatments.
  • Correlates w/ radiographic success or treatment inefficacy.
Patterns of Reappearance
  • True peristaltic 'recovery' is unlikely due to neuronal depletion.
  • Partial reappearance described in 28-60% of series.
  • Compartmentalized wave front (DCI >450 mmHg) in type II achalasia → ↓ barium column height on TBE.
  • More common pattern in treated type II achalasia is IEM (mean DCI <400 mmHg.cm.s).
Recommendations Against
  • Avoid 'absent contractility' for treated achalasia (esp. type I) → fails to classify achalasia as primary motility disorder.
  • Avoid achalasia subtyping (types I, II, III) after treatment.
  • Pretreatment subtype features (e.g., PEP) are fundamental for guiding retreatment.
HRM in Esophagogastric Junction (EGJ) Assessment
  • IRP & LESP significantly ↓ after successful achalasia treatment.
  • No major discordance between POEM, LHM, or PD in ↓ IRP/LESP.
  • Mean post-treatment IRP: 13 mmHg (vs 33 mmHg pretreatment).
IRP Cutoffs & Clinical Success
  • IRP <15 mmHg (Medtronic/Given) / <22 mmHg (Diversatek/Laborie) / <26 mmHg (Starlet) → successful treatment.
  • <15 mmHg IRP (Medtronic/Given) effective for clinical responders (<10% discordance).
  • IRP ≥15 mmHg (or 22 mmHg) → OR 2.1 for predicting symptom persistence.
  • Sensitivity >87% in predicting clinical response after LES myotomy.
Impact of Treatment Modality on EGJ Physiology
  • PD results in lesser ↓ IRP & LESP vs POEM/LHM.
  • PD correlates w/ clinical improvement, but higher median IRP in symptomatic persistence (15.5 mmHg) vs clinical success (9.0 mmHg).
  • Myotomy (LHM/POEM) generally reliable for IRP <15/22 mmHg.
Management of Elevated Post-treatment IRP
  • Elevated post-treatment IRP can be managed conservatively w/ good outcomes if symptoms limited.
  • LES-directed retreatment can achieve efficient results in ≈60% of cases.
  • Age & esophageal anatomy (sigmoid vs non-sigmoid) unlikely to affect EGJ relaxation post-treatment (esp. POEM).
Esophagogastric Junction-Contractile Integral (EGJ-CI)
  • EGJ-CI is higher in achalasia patients vs controls.
  • Declines after LES-targeted treatment.
  • EGJ-CI <30 mmHg associated w/ clinical & radiographic success.
  • Possible role in post-POEM follow-up due to association w/ post-treatment reflux burden.
Provocative Maneuvers: Rapid Drink Challenge (RDC)
  • RDC (continuous rapid swallowing of 200 cc water) is relevant for treated achalasia assessment.
  • Symptomatic treated patients more frequently show IBP >20 mmHg, higher max IBP, higher IRP (>10-12 mmHg), & higher trans-EGJ gradient.
  • Negative predictive value of normal RDC-IRP (≤10 mmHg) was 94% (97% w/ normal trans-EGJ gradient).
Correlation with Radiologic Obstruction
  • RDC findings correlated w/ radiologic obstruction on Timed Barium Esophagogram (TBE) after multiple LES-targeted treatments.
  • Performing RDC as part of HRM protocol is valuable in treated achalasia.
HRM Assessment of Esophageal Body
  • CC is NOT applicable for distinguishing achalasia subtype after treatment.
  • Lack of concordance of EGJ metrics & body contractility patterns pre- & post-treatment.
  • Evaluation of body contractility provides valuable info on clinical & radiologic outcomes.
Treated Achalasia Subtypes & Contractility
  • Treated type I achalasia often shows absent contractility + low IRP (<15 mmHg) if successful.
  • Type II/III achalasia often show PEPs/IBP w/ spastic (DL ≤4.5 sec) contractility + high pretreatment IRP.
  • May show remnant esophageal body contractility even after successful treatment.
Persistent Obstruction Indicators
  • Retained PEP or IBP w/ elevated pressure (>20 mmHg) → suggests remnant obstruction.
  • Especially if associated w/ elevated IRP.
  • Progressive pressure build-up in distal esophagus over multiple swallows → reflects persistent outflow resistance @ EGJ.
Type III Achalasia Specifics
  • Often characterized by higher pre- & postoperative IRP.
  • Clinical success ↓ vs other subtypes, even w/ ↓ IRP post-myotomy.
  • PD & LHM less effective in type III achalasia.
Reasons for Reduced Efficacy
  • PD is LES-limited treatment.
  • Standard myotomy only targets LES, may NOT ablate entire esophageal body spastic segment.
  • Suboptimal outcomes + distinctive manometric pattern ('residual spastic activity in treated type III achalasia') → requires retreatment (POEM).
Tailored Management
  • Management of type III achalasia is nuanced.
  • Should be tailored to individual patient after thorough phenotyping.
  • Radiographic & manometric characteristics determine optimal myotomy length.
Repeated Treatment of Previously Treated Achalasia
  • Less is known about HRM in retreated achalasia.
  • LESP & IRP are ↓ in previously treated achalasia vs treatment-naïve patients, even if unsuccessful.
  • PD is most frequent treatment w/ symptom relapse → affects IRP values.
Re-staging & Assessment
  • In patients w/ dysphagia, absent esophageal peristalsis, IRP ≤15 mmHg → treated achalasia w/ persisting/relapsing symptoms.
  • Important to restage disease using HRM to explore relapse mechanisms.
  • Supplement w/ TBE & FLIP.
Efficacy of Retreatment Modalities
  • Largest trial comparing PD & POEM after unsuccessful LHM → higher Δ IRP & LESP in POEM group.
  • Correlated well w/ clinical & radiological endpoints.
  • HRM assessment after repeated treatments similar to post-initial treatment, w/ similar criteria.
Clinical-Manometric Discordance & Adjunctive Tools
  • HRM parameters do NOT always correlate w/ relapsing symptoms.
  • Discordance stems from anatomic alterations (end-stage achalasia, distal diverticulum, blown out myotomy (BOM)).
  • Radiological assessment (TBE) is fundamental to complement clinical, manometric, & FLIP assessment.
Functional Lumen Imaging Probe (FLIP)
  • FLIP assessment is complementary (if not superior) to HRM.
  • Higher sensitivity in predicting clinical response to treatment (up to 95%).
  • Identifies subtle ↓ distensibility not evident on HRM/endoscopy, esp. in altered anatomy/post-treatment.
  • FLIP + HRM considerably improves EGJ physiology assessment post-LES treatments.
EGJ Distensibility & Prediction
  • EGJ distensibility measured during FLIP correlated well w/ radiographic retention on TBE.
  • Sensitivity of 78% for EGJ-DI <2.8 mm²/mmHg in patients w/ altered anatomy.
High-Resolution Impedance Manometry (HRIM)
  • HRIM provides further insights in identifying bolus retention.
  • Bolus flow time (BFT) from HRIM better predicted composite outcome (clinical + radiological success) w/ AUC 0.77 (vs 0.65 for EGJ-CI).
  • More complex HRIM metrics (clearance ratio, retention volume, 4D-HRIM) show potential.
4D-HRIM Metrics
  • Combination of pressure & impedance calculations.
  • High correlation (0.7-0.8) & inter-rater agreement (0.7-0.9) w/ standard HRM, FLIP, & radiological results.
  • May improve HRIM accuracy, esp. in non-expert centers where FLIP is less available.
Timed Barium Esophagogram (TBE)
  • Essential to assess esophageal emptying post-treatment.
  • Detects anatomic alterations (BOM, epiphrenic diverticula).
  • Forecasts treatment failure or sheds light on clinical-radiological discordance.
  • Remains a complementary tool; routinely considered in treated achalasia evaluation.
pH-Impedance Study & Reflux Symptoms
  • Reflux symptoms post-treatment may NOT always indicate true GERD.
  • Can result from stasis-related acidification of retained food.
  • If HRM/TBE are uninformative, perform EGD & 24-hour pH-impedance study.
  • Complemented by FLIP to assess disguised EGJ distensibility alteration.
Management Flowchart for Treated Achalasia
  • Clinical assessment should precede HRM after achalasia treatment.
  • If symptoms persist (dysphagia/reflux), perform complete manometric assessment.
  • Integrate HRM data w/ distensibility assessment (FLIP) & TBE for comprehensive evaluation.
Initial Steps
  • Always consider TBE or barium contrast X-ray.
  • Evaluate EGJ morpho-physiology, esophageal body contractility, & provocative maneuvers.
Identifying TAOO
  • Elevated IRP (<15/22 mmHg), high LESP, delta-IRP <16.5 mmHg.
  • High EGJ-CI.
  • RDC: IBPs @ 20 mmHg, IBP max pressure >30 mmHg, IRP >10-12 mmHg, ↑trans-EGJ gradient.
  • Presence of residual spasm (DL<4.5s) or PEP (30 mmHg) or IBP (>20 mmHg).
  • If confirmed obstruction → retreatment.
Identifying ATA
  • Absence of TAOO features.
  • If FLIP available, use as first choice.
  • Consider altered anatomy (BOM) if present.
Symptom-Specific Management
  • If dysphagia prevalent: FLIP, TBE, ↓BFT @ HiRM (if available).
  • If reflux symptoms prevalent: EGD & 24-h pH-impedance study to confirm acidic stasis.
  • Retreatment for 'residual spastic activity in treated type III achalasia' → POEM of remnant spastic segment.
Conservative Management
  • If only slightly elevated IRP w/o other clear obstruction signs → initial conservative management.
  • Should NOT prompt immediate LES-targeted retreatment.
Areas Warranting Further Research
  • Lack of adequate patient-reported questionnaires specifically for treated achalasia w/ persisting symptoms.
  • Need for studies focusing on HRIM metrics in treated achalasia.
  • Development of novel, easy-to-use, reproducible HRM provocative metrics.
HRIM Metrics
  • Clearance ratio & retention volume (already developed in naïve patients).
  • 4D HRIM metrics (already developed in naïve patients).
  • Particularly relevant as HRIM may be more available than FLIP in non-expert centers.
Provocative Maneuvers
  • Solid meal testing (w/ consensus on meal type/consistency/quantity).
  • Correlation of HRM outcomes of provocative maneuvers w/ radiology & FLIP results.
  • Prediction of treatment outcome in patients w/ recurrent symptoms.
Symptom Assessment
  • Development of validated patient-reported outcomes for treated achalasia.
  • Quality of life questionnaires focusing on dietary & nutritional status post-treatment.
Reproducibility of HRM Data
  • Evaluating reproducibility of HRM metrics in different measurements for treated achalasia.
  • Focus on provocative maneuvers & concordance between primary/secondary position for EGJ metrics & obstructive features.
