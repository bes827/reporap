United European Gastroenterology (UEG) and European Society for Neurogastroenterology and Motility (ESNM) consensus on gastroparesis
  • Authors: Jolien Schol, Lucas Wauters, Ram Dickman, et al.
  • Journal: United European Gastroenterology Journal
  • Publication Date: 2021
  • DOI: 10.1002/ueg2.12060
  • Focus: European expert consensus on definition, symptoms, pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management of gastroparesis.

1. Definitions and Symptom Descriptors
  • Gastroparesis: Symptoms + delayed GE in absence of mechanical obstruction.
  • Nausea and vomiting are cardinal symptoms.
  • Dyspeptic symptoms (postprandial fullness, early satiation, epigastric pain, bloating, belching) often present.
1.1 Definition of Gastroparesis
  • Symptoms associated w/ delayed GE in absence of mechanical obstruction.
  • Overall agreement: 100% (A+ 60%, A 40%).
  • GRADE A.
1.2 Disturbed Gastric Motor Function
  • Symptoms associated w/ severely disturbed gastric motor function in absence of mechanical obstruction.
  • Overall agreement: 85% (A+ 48%, A 38%, A−13%).
  • GRADE B.
1.3 Cardinal Symptoms
  • Nausea and vomiting are cardinal symptoms.
  • Overall agreement: 95% (A+ 68%, A 28%, A−5%).
  • GRADE B.
1.4 Dyspeptic Symptoms
  • Postprandial fullness, early satiation, epigastric pain, bloating, and belching often present.
  • Overall agreement: 95% (A+ 60%, A 35%, A−5%).
  • GRADE B.
1.5 Symptom Overlap
  • Symptoms overlap mainly w/ PDS, less w/ EPS symptoms of FD.
  • Overall agreement: 90% (A+ 45%, A 45%, A−8%).
  • GRADE B.
Additional Details
  • Delayed GE should be based on established reference range.
  • Asymptomatic delayed GE may be present, especially in diabetics.
  • Symptoms overlap w/ FD (Rome IV criteria).
Symptoms and GE
  • Association ≠ causation; symptoms may be related to other gastric sensorimotor dysfunctions.
  • Symptoms present for ≥3 months for diagnosis.
Symptom Prevalence
  • Nausea: >95%, associated w/ severity of delay.
  • Vomiting: Associated w/ nausea and more severe delay.
Overlap with FD
  • US study: 86% of idiopathic gastroparesis patients fulfilled ROME III criteria for FD.
  • PDS: 91%, EPS: 1.2%.
Pain and Bloating
  • Pain less prevalent in studies excluding opioid users.
  • Bloating common in functional digestive disorders and gastroparesis.
2. Epidemiology and Risk Factors
  • Epidemiology not established due to GE testing requirements.
  • Diabetes, gastric surgery, neurological disorders, connective tissue diseases, and certain drugs are risk factors.

  • Overall agreement: 93% (A+ 40%, A 53%, A−8%).

2.2 Diabetes

  • Some drugs (e.g., opioids) are associated w/ ↑ risk.
  • Overall agreement: 100% (A+ 63%, A 38%, A−0%).

Additional Details
  • Analyses suggest low prevalence of formally diagnosed gastroparesis.
  • Olmsted County: Incidence 6.3/100,000 person-years, prevalence 24.2/100,000.
  • UK CPRD: Prevalence 13.8/100,000, incidence 1.9/100,000.
Diabetes and Gastroparesis
  • Idiopathic subgroup largest, high prevalence among diabetics (25-38%).
  • More frequent in type 1 vs type 2 DM.
  • Cumulative proportions over 10 years: Type 1 DM 5.2%, Type 2 DM 1.0%, controls 0.2%.
Post-Infectious Gastroparesis
  • Limited evidence suggests viral GI infections may cause postinfectious gastroparesis.
  • NIDDK cohort: 19% reported history suggestive of infectious etiology.
Postsurgical and Other Associations
  • Postsurgical gastroparesis well established.
  • Hypothyroidism more frequent in gastroparesis (14% vs 8.2% in controls).
  • Several neurological and systemic disorders linked.
Opioids
  • Opioids have greatest impact on GE.
  • Patients on opioids should not be considered gastroparesis unless confirmed off opioids.

  • Associated w/ psychosocial comorbidities (anxiety, depression).

4. Pathophysiology of Gastroparesis
  • No consensus on specific mechanisms.
  • Delayed GE, impaired gastric accommodation, hypersensitivity to distention, duodenal alterations, loss of ICCs/enteric nerves/vagus nerve function, gastric smooth muscle changes, H. pylori, altered acid secretion/peptide hormones/duodenal sensitivity/microbiota, anxiety, depression, disordered signal processing, and genetic factors were not endorsed as primary mechanisms.

Additional Details
  • Symptoms are poor predictors of GE rate or response to prokinetics.
  • More severe symptom burden may be associated w/ more severely delayed emptying, but inconsistent.
Idiopathic Gastroparesis
  • Barostat studies: 43% impaired accommodation, 29% hypersensitivity to distension.
Diabetic Gastroparesis
  • ↓ gastric accommodation and sensory thresholds.
Histopathology
  • Animal model and biopsies showed ↓ density of ICCs.
Gastric Smooth Muscle
  • Conflicting data on fibrotic and inflammatory changes.
Vagal Dysfunction
  • Diabetic gastroparesis more frequently associated w/ vagal dysfunction.
Other Factors
  • No consistent correlation between H. pylori and GE rate.
  • No studies evaluated gastric acid secretion, duodenal sensitivity, or microbiota composition.
Psychological Factors
  • Anxiety and depression related to symptom severity and hospitalization rates, not etiology or GE rates.
Central Nervous System
  • Hypersensitivity to gastric distension present, likely involves altered processing of incoming signals.
  • fMRI study showed altered connectivity of the insula.
Genetic Factors
  • Long repeat polymorphism in heme oxygenase (HO1) associated w/ worse outcomes.
5. Diagnosis
  • Upper GI endoscopy and abnormal GE test are mandatory.
  • Scintigraphic GE assessment and breath test are valid.
  • Small bowel obstruction can be ruled out through imaging.

  • Overall agreement: 93% (A+ 75%, A 18%, A−8%).

Additional Details
  • EGD is prerequisite to exclude gastric outlet obstruction.
  • Food retention during EGD after overnight fast has been used as a sign, but poor sensitivity.
GE Scintigraphy
  • Gold standard: 99mTechnetium-labeled low-fat egg meal, imaging at 0, 1, 2, and 4 h.
  • Fairly reproducible.
GE Breath Test (GEBT)
  • FDA-approved, non-invasive, uses 13C.
  • High correlation w/ scintigraphy.
Wireless Motility Capsule (WMC)
  • FDA-approved.
  • Sensitivity 59-86%, specificity 64-81% compared to scintigraphy.
  • Pitfall: indigestible solid, empties w/ phase 3 migrating motor complexes.
Gastric Ultrasonography
  • Noninvasive, safe, cheap, widely available.
  • Unable to distinguish solid and liquid components.
Small Bowel Obstruction
  • Abdominal radiography (accuracy 50-86%).
  • CT scanning most accurate.

Additional Details
  • Small particle size diet reduces gastroparesis and reflux symptoms in diabetic gastroparesis.
  • Nutritional support and dietician engagement can correct calorie deficiencies.
PPIs
  • Efficacy not addressed in controlled trials.
  • High intake in gastroparesis patients (≥70%), >50% have overlapping GERD.
Antiemetics
  • Recommended as first-line, but no formal studies establishing efficacy.
  • Metoclopramide (dopamine-2 antagonist) approved in US, carries black box warning.
Metoclopramide Nasal Spray
  • Phase 2 RCT: 10/14 mg doses did not significantly reduce symptom scores vs placebo.
  • Significant effect in females only.
  • Adverse events: headache, fatigue, dysgeusia.
Domperidone
  • Peripherally acting dopamine-2 antagonist, ↓ nausea, ↑ GE.
  • Less likely to cause extrapyramidal side effects.
  • Associated w/ QTc prolongation.
5-HT3 Antagonists
  • Ondansetron, granisetron have antiemetic properties, but controlled studies lacking.
  • Open label study suggested efficacy of granisetron transdermal patches.
Aprepitant
  • Neurokinin-1 receptor antagonist, efficacious in some patients.
Prokinetic Agents
  • Enhance symptoms and improve GE rate.
  • Disparity: relationship between symptom improvement and GE rate is poor.
Itopride
  • Combined dopamine-2 antagonist and cholinesterase inhibitor.
  • Small study: no significant effect in diabetic gastroparesis.
5-HT4 Agonists
  • Cisapride withdrawn due to cardiologic side effects.
  • Tegaserod temporarily withdrawn.
  • Prucalopride: one study showed efficacy, another failed.
Motilin-Receptor Agonists
  • Camicinal, RaQualia, Mitemcinal studied.
  • Macrolide antibiotics (erythromycin) also act on motilin receptor, but risks and side effects exist.
Ghrelin-Receptor Agonists
  • TZP-101, TZP-102, RM-131 (relamorelin) studied.
  • Phase 3 program for relamorelin discontinued.
Neuromodulators
  • Nortriptyline showed no gain over placebo.
  • Amitriptyline appeared beneficial in ulcer-like FD.
Mirtazapine
  • Open label study: improvement in nausea, vomiting, and appetite.
Gastric Electrical Stimulation (GES)
  • Uncontrolled studies reported efficacy.
  • Recent sham-controlled study reported improvement on vomiting frequency, regardless of GE status.
Pyloric Botulinum Toxin Injection
  • Open-label studies reported short-term efficacy.
  • Subsequent controlled trials failed to demonstrate improvement.
Pyloric Myotomy
  • Open-label studies reported short and midterm efficacy.
  • No controlled validating studies.
Gastrectomy
  • Symptomatic improvement reported in 43-90%, morbidity up to 40%.
H. Pylori Eradication
  • Two studies suggested eradication enhanced GE rate, but used nonvalidated tests.
Herbal Therapies
  • Lack of data on effects in gastroparesis.
  • STW-5 (Iberogast) did not alter GE rate.
Psychological Therapies
  • Lack of specific reports in gastroparesis patients.
  • Acupuncture: Cochrane review found higher symptom improvement rates, but uncertain conclusion.
Nutritional Support
  • Enteral or parenteral nutrition for refractory gastroparesis w/ weight loss/nutritional deficiencies.
  • Jejunal tube feeding advocated for long-term support.
7. Prognosis of Gastroparesis
  • Prognosis depends on the cause.
  • No consensus on unfavorable long-term prognosis or increased mortality.
7.1 Long-Term Prognosis
  • The long-term prognosis is unfavorable.
  • STATEMENT NOT ENDORSED, overall agreement 35%: A+ 15%, A 20%, A−28%, D−23%, D 15%, D+ 0%.
  • GRADE B
7.2 Prognosis and Cause
  • The prognosis depends on the cause.
  • Overall agreement: 88% (A+ 33%, A+ 54%, A 10%, A−3%).
  • GRADE B
7.3 Life Expectancy
  • Life expectancy is shortened.
  • STATEMENT NOT ENDORSED, overall agreement 25%: A+ 5%, A 20%, A−35%, D−18%, D 23%, D+ 0%.
  • GRADE B
Additional Details
  • Symptoms and GE delay persist for several years in majority of patients.
  • NIDDK consortium: some symptom improvement at 48 weeks, but not from 48 to 192 weeks.
Factors Associated with Reduced Symptoms
  • Male sex, age >50, initial infectious prodrome, antidepressant use, 4-h gastric retention >20%.
Factors Associated with Lack of Improvement
  • Overweight/obesity, smoking, pain modulators, moderate-severe abdominal pain, severe GERD, moderate-severe depression.
Life Expectancy
  • Conflicting data.
  • One study: delayed GE not related to mortality after adjustment for comorbidities.
  • Another: 7% died, 22% needed long-term feeding.
Mortality Risk
  • Higher in diabetic vs idiopathic gastroparesis patients.
  • Population-based study: higher death rate due to cardiovascular comorbidity in diabetic patients.
Recommendations
  • Gastroparesis: Symptoms + delayed GE in absence of mechanical obstruction, as well as severely disturbed gastric motor function.
  • Nausea and vomiting are the cardinal symptoms.
  • Diabetes, upper abdominal surgeries, neurological and connective tissue diseases, as well as the use of certain drugs are risk factors.
  • Gastroparesis is a major source of healthcare costs and associated with a significant decrease in quality of life and with psychosocial co-morbidities.
  • Upper GI endoscopy and an abnormal gastric emptying test are mandatory for diagnosis.
  • Dietary adjustments, dopamine-2 antagonists or 5-HT4 agonists can be used to manage symptoms.
  • In case of severe weight loss, nutritional support may be needed.
  • The long-term prognosis depends on the underlying cause.
Key Consensus Points
  • Cardinal symptom pattern: nausea/vomiting, w/ or w/o overlapping PDS.
  • Areas of uncertainty: underlying pathophysiological factors, therapy.
Future Directions
  • Advances in understanding pathophysiology may allow refinement of definitions and identification of more specific phenotypes.
  • Need for more therapeutic trials.
