Medical Professional Liability in Gastroenterology:
Understanding the Claims Landscape and Proposed Mechanisms for Reform

Overview of Medical Professional Liability (MPL)
  • - The annual cost of the medical liability system is estimated at >$55 billion.
  • - This is equivalent to ~2.4% of total national health care spending.
  • - MPL insurance premiums are among the highest single expenses for practicing physicians.
  • - Being named in an MPL claim takes a significant psychological toll, regardless of the outcome.

Legal Elements of an MPL Claim
  • - MPL law is derived from English common law and is tort-based (a civil wrong causing harm).
  • - The goal of tort law is to promote "corrective justice" by compensating for negligently caused harms and deterring future poor medical treatment.
Plaintiff's Burden of Proof
  • - The plaintiff/patient must prove that it was "more likely than not" that four key elements existed.
1. Duty of Care
  • - A duty of care existed between the patient and the provider.

2. Breach of Duty
  • - The provider breached that duty of care.

3. Causation of Harm
  • - The provider's breach of duty directly caused the patient harm.

4. Quantifiable Damages
  • - The harm caused can be quantified as damages (economic, non-economic, etc.).


Statute of Limitations
  • - This is the maximum time a plaintiff has to file a claim after the alleged negligent act.
  • - It is typically 1–5 years, as codified by state law.
  • - This highlights the critical importance of detailed and accurate medical documentation.
Example: Michigan Law
  • - The statute of limitations is 2 years from the date of the alleged malpractice.
  • - A "discovery provision" extends the filing deadline to 6 months after the plaintiff discovers the alleged negligent act.
  • - This provision is valid only if the claim is filed within 6 years of the underlying malpractice event.



Prevalence & Characteristics of GI-Related MPL Claims
  • - Data is primarily sourced from the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB) and medical liability insurer databases.
NPDB Data (1992-2014)
  • - The GI-specific rate of paid claims declined from a high of 18.5 to a low of 12.1 claims per 1000 physician-years.
  • - The mean claim payment for gastroenterology had one of the largest increases, from $276,128 (1992-1996) to $390,538 (2009-2014).
Nature of Paid GI Claims
  • - Most involved major harms: 45.0% death and 44.5% major or significant physical injury.
  • - Most common alleged errors were:
  • - - Diagnostic error: 38.4%
  • - - Medication or treatment error: 34.5%
  • - - Surgical/procedural error: 18.5%


NPDB Study on High-Frequency Physicians (2005-2014)
  • - Most paid claims (97%) were settled, with only 3% decided by a judge/jury verdict.
  • - A small fraction of physicians, 1%, accounted for approximately one-third of all paid claims.
  • - Most physicians (84%) with any claim had only 1 paid claim over the 20-year study period.

Medical Professional Liability Association Database (1985-2005)
  • - Claims against gastroenterologists represented only ~1.8% of all reported claims.
  • - The most common reasons for claims were:
  • - - Errors in diagnosis: 28%
  • - - Improper performance of a procedure: 25%
Endoscopy-Related Claims
  • - Of ~1900 closed endoscopy-related claims, 41% involved colonoscopy.
  • - 25% of total claims resulted in a settlement or verdict for the patient.
  • - 70% were dropped or dismissed before trial.
  • - Colonoscopy-related claims had the highest total claim payments.
  • - ERCP-related claims had the highest mean claim payments.


Overall Claim Statistics
  • - Approximately 14% of gastroenterologists had an active medical liability claim annually.
  • - However, only 2% of these claims resulted in a payment.


Impact of Liability Fear on Practice Patterns
  • - Fear of medical liability is a prominent driver of medical overuse (defensive medicine).
  • - It is often the perceived risk, rather than the actual risk, that drives physician behavior.
Barrett’s Esophagus Screening & Surveillance
  • - A study found that gastroenterologists with a prior malpractice history were more likely to be aggressive in screening and surveillance for Barrett’s esophagus.
  • - This association had an Odds Ratio of 3.6 (95% CI, 1.1–12).

Invasive Diagnostic Testing
  • - Physicians reporting higher levels of malpractice concern were more likely to order invasive diagnostic testing for patients with chest pain, back pain, and headache.


Approaches to Medical Liability Reform
  • - MPL reforms can be divided into traditional and alternative approaches.
  • - These mechanisms vary widely by state (See Table 1).
Traditional Mechanisms
  • - Originated in the 1970s and 1980s in response to rising insurance premiums.
  • - Goal: Reduce the number of claims filed, limit payouts, and generally limit physician liability.
Damage Caps
  • - Limit the size of awards for noneconomic losses (e.g., pain and suffering) or punitive damages.

Limits on Attorney’s Fees
  • - Limit the percentage of a settlement or verdict that a plaintiff’s attorney may charge as a contingency fee.

Statutes of Limitation
  • - Set the maximum amount of time a plaintiff has to file a claim.

Affidavit/Certificate-of-Merit
  • - Requires an affidavit from a medical expert affirming that the claim has merit before it can be initiated.

Pretrial Screening Panels
  • - Expert panels review cases to ensure a baseline level of merit before they can proceed to trial.

Elimination of Joint and Several Liability
  • - Also known as the "fair share" rule.
  • - When multiple defendants exist, liability is limited to the percentage of fault allocated to each defendant.


Alternative (Innovative) Mechanisms
  • - These newer approaches seek to more effectively address the concept of "corrective justice".
Disclosure, Apology, and Offer Programs
  • - Involves proactive disclosure of adverse outcomes, investigation, apology, and an offer of compensation.
Program Outcomes
  • - In one large health system, this program led to:
  • - - A 36% decrease in the rate of claims.
  • - - A 30% reduction in time to claim resolution.
  • - - A 44% reduction in costs.


“Safe Harbor” Legislation
  • - Provides liability protection to physicians when they follow approved evidence-based guidelines or standards.
  • - May secondarily improve patient safety by encouraging guideline adherence.

Health Courts
  • - Specialized courts with administrative law judges experienced in medical liability.
  • - Use court-appointed, independent medical experts to avoid the "battle of the experts".

“No-Fault” Compensation Systems
  • - Replaces courts with an administrative body that grants awards without seeking to prove fault.
  • - Separates accountability from compensation.
  • - Has been used in New Zealand since 2005.



What to Do If Named in an MPL Claim
  • - Key recommendations for a provider who is named in a claim.
1. Notify Insurance Carrier
  • - Immediately notify your medical liability insurance carrier so that legal representation can be arranged.

2. Retain a Personal Attorney
  • - It is recommended to also retain a personal attorney to guard against any conflicts of interest that may arise with attorneys provided by your health system or practice.

3. Preserve All Records
  • - Preserve all records related to the claim.
  • - Do not alter any portion of the record under any circumstances.
  • - Careful documentation and risk communication efforts may be your savior.


Conclusions
  • - Even the most conscientious gastroenterologist may be named in an MPL claim.
  • - The overall rate of claims against gastroenterologists is low, and most claims are either dismissed or resolved in favor of the physician.
  • - Innovative alternative approaches to reform may hold great promise for enhancing fairness, transparency, and timely resolution of claims.
