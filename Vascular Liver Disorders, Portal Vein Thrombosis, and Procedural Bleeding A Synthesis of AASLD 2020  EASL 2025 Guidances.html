Vascular Liver Disorders, Portal Vein Thrombosis, and Procedural Bleeding: A Synthesis of AASLD 2020 & EASL 2025 Guidances
  • - Source 1 (AASLD 2020): Vascular Liver Disorders, Portal Vein Thrombosis, and Procedural Bleeding in Patients With Liver Disease: 2020 Practice Guidance by the American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases. Hepatology, Jan 2021.
  • - Source 2 (EASL 2025): EASL Clinical Practice Guidelines on vascular diseases of the liver. Journal of Hepatology, 2025.
  • - This mind map synthesizes and compares recommendations from both major guidelines, highlighting areas of consensus and unique contributions from each.
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the recommended AFP cutoff for suspecting HCC in a patient with Budd-Chiari Syndrome (BCS)?
  • - In children with Extrahepatic Portal Vein Obstruction (EHPVO), what is the preferred surgical correction?
  • - What is the only FDA-approved treatment for severe Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome (SOS)?
  • - What is the typical mortality rate from a ruptured Hepatic Artery Aneurysm (HAA)?
  • - In patients with recent PVT without cirrhosis, what percentage achieve complete recanalization with anticoagulation therapy according to a large prospective trial?

Hemostasis & Coagulation in Cirrhosis (AASLD Focus)
  • - Cirrhosis is no longer seen as a simple bleeding tendency, but a complex disturbance of both pro- and anti-hemostatic pathways.
  • - This leads to a 'rebalanced' but unstable hemostatic system.
Key Hemostatic Changes
  • - Promoting Bleeding:
  • - Thrombocytopenia & platelet function defects.
  • - Low levels of coagulation factors (II, V, VII, IX, X, XI).
  • - Hyperfibrinolysis (elevated tPA).
  • - Promoting Clotting:
  • - Elevated levels of von Willebrand factor (VWF) & Factor VIII.
  • - Decreased levels of natural anticoagulants (Protein C, S, Antithrombin).
  • - Decreased levels of plasminogen.
Net Effect
  • - The net effect is a fragile balance.
  • - Patients can have both bleeding and thrombotic risks simultaneously.
  • - Decompensation often favors hyperfibrinolysis, while ACLF/sepsis can severely inhibit it.


Laboratory Assessment
  • - Traditional tests like PT/INR and aPTT are inadequate and often misleading for assessing bleeding risk in cirrhosis.
  • - They only measure procoagulant factors and ignore the counterbalancing reduction in anticoagulant proteins.
Limitations of PT/INR
  • - AASLD: PT/INR has no predictive value for procedural or spontaneous bleeding in cirrhosis.
  • - It is a marker of liver disease severity (synthetic function), not hemostatic competency.

Global Hemostasis Tests
  • - Tests like Thromboelastography (TEG) / Rotational Thromboelastometry (ROTEM) and thrombin generation assays provide a better overall picture.
  • - They often show that patients with cirrhosis have normal to elevated thrombin-generating capacity and near-normal clot formation.
Clinical Utility & Limitations
  • - AASLD: The predictive value of these tests for procedural bleeding is unproven and contradictory.
  • - They may be useful in selecting patients who do not need preprocedural intervention (if results are normal).
  • - They are insensitive to VWF and Protein C, likely underestimating hemostatic competency.




Procedural Bleeding in Cirrhosis (AASLD Focus)
  • - Bleeding during procedures can be due to hemostatic failure, portal hypertension, or direct vessel injury.
  • - Interventions should target the specific cause.
Bleeding Risk Stratification
  • - Risk assessment is complex and depends on procedural, liver disease, and systemic factors.
Procedure Risk Categories (AASLD)
  • - Low Risk: Major bleeding expected in <1.5% of cases. Bleeding is easily controlled.
  • - Examples: Paracentesis, thoracentesis, diagnostic EGD, routine variceal banding.
  • - High Risk: Major bleeding risk >1.5% or bleeding is difficult to control or catastrophic.
  • - Examples: Liver biopsy, TIPS, polypectomy, dental extraction.

Liver Disease Factors
  • - Advanced portal hypertension is an independent risk factor for bleeding.
  • - ACLF and systemic inflammation directly alter hemostasis and increase bleeding risk.

Systemic Factors
  • - Acute and Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) are significant risk factors for bleeding.
  • - In one study of large-volume paracentesis, 89% (8 of 9) of patients with bleeding events had renal dysfunction.


Preprocedural Interventions
  • - AASLD strongly advises against the routine prophylactic correction of laboratory abnormalities before common procedures.
Platelet Interventions
  • - There is no data-driven specific platelet cutoff where bleeding risk is reliably increased.
  • - Prophylactic platelet transfusions do not substantially improve thrombin generation and carry risks (e.g., TRALI).
  • - AASLD Recommendation: It is reasonable to perform both low- and high-risk procedures without prophylactically correcting the platelet count.
Thrombopoietin (TPO) Receptor Agonists
  • - Avatrombopag and lusutrombopag are FDA-approved to increase platelet counts before a procedure.
  • - They are superior to placebo in achieving a target platelet count of ≥50,000/μL.
  • - However, studies showed no statistical difference in postprocedural bleeding events compared to placebo.
  • - An earlier agent, eltrombopag, was associated with excess thrombotic events (especially PVT).


Coagulation Interventions (INR Correction)
  • - The INR should not be used to gauge procedural bleeding risk.
  • - Prophylactic Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP) transfusion is associated with risks (volume overload, increased portal pressure, TRALI) and no proven benefits.
  • - FFP shows minimal efficacy in improving thrombin generation in vitro.
  • - AASLD Recommendation: Measures aimed at reducing the INR (e.g., FFP) are not recommended before procedures.

Fibrinogen Correction
  • - Plasma fibrinogen levels <100 mg/dL are associated with bleeding in critically ill patients, but a causal link is not established.
  • - AASLD Suggestion: Correction of plasma fibrinogen to levels >100 mg/dL using cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate could be considered before high-risk procedures, but supporting data is lacking.

Antifibrinolytic Therapy
  • - Agents like Epsilon-aminocaproic acid (EACA) and tranexamic acid are used for hyperfibrinolytic bleeding.
  • - A large trial of tranexamic acid in acute GI bleeding showed no reduction in death or bleeding and a higher risk of VTE in the cirrhosis subgroup.
  • - AASLD Recommendation: Prophylactic use of these agents to prevent procedural bleeding cannot be recommended.



Portal Vein Thrombosis (PVT)
  • - Formation of a thrombus in the portal vein or its branches. Can occur with or without cirrhosis.
Terminology & Classification
  • - Both guidelines emphasize the need for standardized terminology to allow for comparison across studies.
AASLD/EASL Recommended Nomenclature
  • - Time Course:
  • - Recent: Presumed to be <6 months old.
  • - Chronic: Persistent for >6 months.
  • - Occlusion:
  • - Minimally: <50% of lumen obstructed.
  • - Partially: >50% of lumen obstructed.
  • - Completely: No persistent lumen.
  • - Cavernous Transformation: Presence of gross portoportal collaterals. This term is preferred over 'chronic PVT' when present.

Classification Systems
  • - AASLD: Reviews multiple systems (Yerdel, Sarin, etc.), noting most were developed for liver transplant (LT) candidates and lack long-term outcome correlation in non-transplant patients.
  • - EASL: Also highlights the Yerdel classification's utility for LT surgical strategy but notes its limitations. Endorses the standardized nomenclature for broader use.


PVT in Patients with Cirrhosis
  • - PVT is a common complication in advanced liver disease.
Prevalence & Risk Factors
  • - Prevalence: Reported between 1.3% and 9.8% in patients with cirrhosis, reaching up to 9% in LT candidates.
  • - Prospective Incidence: 3.2% to 4.1% at 1 year after cirrhosis diagnosis.
Key Risk Factors
  • - Severity of Liver Disease: The most significant factor. PVT is a marker of advanced portal hypertension.
  • - Decreased PV Flow Velocity: A threshold of <15 cm/s is often cited as a risk factor.
  • - Etiology of Cirrhosis:
  • - AASLD & EASL: Both identify NASH/MASLD as an independent risk factor.
  • - EASL: Notes autoimmune liver diseases are also associated.
  • - Thrombophilia Workup:
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: An extensive thrombophilia workup is not generally recommended in patients with cirrhosis, as it rarely changes management.

Ruling out Malignancy
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: It is mandatory to rule out malignant venous obstruction due to HCC with contrast-enhanced imaging.


Impact on Mortality & Outcomes
  • - The role of PVT as a cause vs. a consequence of disease progression is debated.
Non-Transplant Setting
  • - AASLD: Literature is contradictory. Some studies suggest PVT is a consequence of decompensation and not an independent mortality risk. Others (e.g., enoxaparin prevention trial) imply a causal role by showing a survival benefit with PVT prevention.

Liver Transplant (LT) Setting
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: The presence of PVT at the time of transplant, especially occlusive PVT, is associated with increased technical difficulty and post-transplant mortality.
  • - AASLD: Notes that there are no RCTs to prove that pre-transplant treatment of PVT improves post-transplant outcomes.


Treatment: Anticoagulation
  • - The primary goals are to prevent thrombus extension, maintain portal vein patency for potential LT, and manage portal hypertension complications.
Indications for Treatment
  • - EASL & AASLD Consensus for LT Candidates: Anticoagulation should be used to improve feasibility and outcomes of LT, regardless of PVT degree.
  • - EASL & AASLD for Non-LT Candidates:
  • - Occlusive or >50% occlusive PVT: Anticoagulation should be considered/may be used.
  • - Minimally occlusive (<50%) PVT: Observation with serial imaging is reasonable. Treat if it progresses.

Efficacy
  • - Spontaneous recanalization occurs in up to 40% of cases.
  • - AASLD Meta-analysis: Complete PVR occurred in 42% of patients with anticoagulation vs. 13% without.
  • - EASL Meta-analysis: Anticoagulation reduced all-cause mortality (HR 0.6).
  • - Both guidelines note that therapy is most effective if started within 6 months of diagnosis.

Choice of Anticoagulant
  • - Traditional Agents (LMWH/VKAs): Historically the standard. Monitoring INR for VKAs is challenging in cirrhosis.
  • - Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs):
  • - AASLD (2020): Data is sparse and limited to well-compensated cirrhosis. Cautions use due to lack of large trials.
  • - EASL (2025): More current view. Recommends DOACs can be used in patients with Child-Pugh A or B cirrhosis. They are not recommended in Child-Pugh C.

Safety & Bleeding Risk
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Therapeutic anticoagulation appears to have similar non-portal hypertensive bleeding rates compared to the general population.
  • - Portal hypertension-related bleeding (e.g., variceal) does not appear to be increased by anticoagulants.
  • - Both guidelines state that EVL can likely be performed safely without stopping anticoagulation.


Treatment: TIPS
  • - TIPS can be used for PVT, especially with portal hypertension complications.
Indications
  • - EASL & AASLD Consensus: TIPS should be considered for patients with PVT and complications of portal hypertension (e.g., variceal bleeding, refractory ascites).
  • - EASL: Also suggests TIPS for PVT that progresses despite anticoagulation.

Anticoagulation Post-TIPS
  • - EASL: Cites an RCT showing no clear benefit of anticoagulation after covered TIPS for PVT. Recommends that routine anticoagulation is not needed to improve recanalization rates after TIPS.



PVT in Patients without Cirrhosis
  • - A rare disease where a thorough workup for underlying causes is crucial.
Risk Factors & Workup
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: A full investigation for myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPN) and other thrombophilic conditions is warranted, often with a hematologist.
  • - EASL: Notes abdominal cancer is present in up to 30% of cases, and inflammatory/infectious abdominal diseases in 20-30%.
  • - AASLD: Highlights that the G20210A prothrombin gene mutation is most prevalent among inherited thrombophilias in this setting.

Goals of Therapy & Treatment
  • - Aims are to prevent thrombus extension, avoid intestinal ischemia, and achieve recanalization to prevent portal hypertension.
Anticoagulation
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Anticoagulation should be initiated as soon as possible.
  • - Efficacy: A large prospective trial (AASLD) showed anticoagulation achieved complete recanalization in 38% of the cohort.
  • - Duration (EASL): For patients with a major and permanent risk factor, long-term anticoagulation is recommended. For others, it can be considered based on risk of recurrence.

DOACs vs. Traditional Agents
  • - AASLD (2020): Notes DOAC use is becoming commonplace despite exclusion from large trials. Cites a retrospective study showing superior efficacy and less bleeding for DOACs vs. warfarin.
  • - EASL (2025): Suggests DOACs as an alternative to LMWH/VKAs in the acute phase and that they may be preferred over VKAs for long-term therapy to improve adherence and safety.

Interventional Therapies
  • - AASLD: Local/systemic thrombolysis should only be considered in selected cases where intestinal ischemia persists despite anticoagulation.
  • - EASL: Agrees that thrombolysis should be reserved for severe disease after medical treatment fails.
  • - Both guidelines suggest portal vein recanalization (PVR) +/- TIPS for refractory complications of portal hypertension.




Hepatic Vein Thrombosis (Budd-Chiari Syndrome)
  • - Obstruction of hepatic venous outflow from small hepatic veins to the junction of the IVC and right atrium.
Etiology & Diagnosis
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: An underlying prothrombotic disorder is found in ~75% of patients. A full thrombophilia workup is recommended.
  • - Most Common Cause (Western): Myeloproliferative neoplasia (MPN), found in ~41% of cases.
  • - Diagnosis: Doppler ultrasound is the first-line imaging study. CT/MRI confirms the diagnosis and aids in planning.

Treatment Strategy
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: A progressive, stepwise therapeutic strategy is recommended, managed at expert centers.
Step 1: Medical Therapy
  • - AASLD & EASL: All patients should receive therapeutic anticoagulation, initiated immediately.
  • - EASL: Recommends LMWH followed by VKAs. Unfractionated heparin should be avoided due to high risk of heparin-induced thrombocytopenia. DOACs may be considered with preserved liver function.

Step 2: Interventional Procedures
  • - Angioplasty +/- Stenting: For patients with short, segmental stenosis.
  • - Thrombolysis: Limited role; only for selected cases of recent, incomplete thrombosis.

Step 3: Vascular Decompression (TIPS)
  • - AASLD & EASL: TIPS (or DIPS) is the treatment of choice when medical therapy or angioplasty fails.
  • - It is technically challenging and should be performed at expert centers.
  • - AASLD: 5-year transplant-free survival after TIPS is 72%.

Step 4: Liver Transplantation (LT)
  • - AASLD & EASL: Reserved for patients who fail less invasive approaches or present with acute liver failure.
  • - 5-year survival rates are >70%.


HCC Surveillance in Chronic BCS
  • - Patients with chronic BCS are at risk for developing HCC.
Recommendations
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Surveillance for HCC with ultrasound +/- alpha-fetoprotein every 6 months is recommended.
  • - AASLD: An AFP level of >15 ng/mL has been suggested as a predictive biomarker for HCC in BCS.
  • - EASL: Confirms that an AFP >15 ng/ml should raise suspicion.
  • - Diagnosis: Both guidelines state that due to altered vascularity, standard imaging criteria (e.g., LI-RADS) do not apply, and histological confirmation is required for suspicious lesions.



Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome (SOS)
  • - Also known as veno-occlusive disease (VOD), characterized by toxic/inflammatory damage to sinusoidal endothelial cells, leading to occlusion of small hepatic venules.
Etiology & Diagnosis
  • - Most Common Setting: Complication of myeloablative chemotherapy before hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT).
  • - Key Drugs: Alkylating agents (busulfan, cyclophosphamide), platinum complexes (oxaliplatin), thiopurines, gemtuzumab.
  • - Clinical Presentation: Acute onset (1-3 weeks post-exposure) of right upper quadrant pain, weight gain, hepatomegaly, and ascites.

Prophylaxis in HSCT
  • - Strategies to prevent SOS in high-risk patients undergoing HSCT.
Ursodeoxycholic Acid
  • - AASLD (2020): Notes mixed data from RCTs but a meta-analysis suggests overall benefit.
  • - EASL (2025): Cites a Cochrane meta-analysis showing reduction in mortality. Recommends prophylaxis with ursodeoxycholic acid in all patients.
  • - Dose: 12-15 mg/kg/day starting before conditioning and continuing for 3 months.

Defibrotide
  • - AASLD (2020): States benefit of prophylactic use is unproven and costs are high.
  • - EASL (2025): Cites the recent large HARMONY trial which showed no benefit for defibrotide prophylaxis. Generally does not recommend its use for prevention.


Treatment
  • - Management of established SOS.
Defibrotide
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Defibrotide is the only FDA/EU-approved treatment for SOS.
  • - Indication: Recommended for moderate-to-severe (AASLD) or severe (EASL) SOS.
  • - EASL: Suggests it can be considered for moderate SOS and that early initiation is preferable.
  • - Dose: 25 mg/kg/day for at least 21 days.

TIPS
  • - AASLD (2020): States TIPS is unproven and cannot be recommended.
  • - EASL (2025): Cites recent positive experiences and suggests TIPS may be considered in severe/very severe SOS with rapid deterioration despite medical therapy.


SOS outside HSCT (e.g., Oxaliplatin)
  • - EASL: Oxaliplatin is a major cause of SOS, often presenting with imaging changes (mosaic pattern) and splenomegaly without significant enzyme elevation.
  • - In the long term, it can lead to PSVD with portal hypertension.
  • - Treatment: Both guidelines suggest stopping the offending drug if possible. EASL notes that bevacizumab may protect against oxaliplatin-induced SOS.


Portosinusoidal Vascular Disorder (PSVD) / INCPH
  • - A group of entities characterized by portal hypertension in the absence of cirrhosis, PV obstruction, or BCS. EASL prefers the term PSVD, which encompasses AASLD's Idiopathic Noncirrhotic Portal Hypertension (INCPH).
Diagnosis
  • - Suspicion Raised By: Signs of portal hypertension (e.g., variceal bleeding) with discordant findings like mild/no liver dysfunction, low liver stiffness (<10 kPa), or low HVPG (<10 mmHg).
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: A liver biopsy is required to exclude cirrhosis and identify characteristic histological lesions (e.g., obliterative portal venopathy, nodular regenerative hyperplasia).

Associated Conditions & Workup
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: An extensive workup for associated conditions is recommended as they have prognostic significance.
Key Associated Conditions
  • - Immune Disorders: Common variable immunodeficiency, SLE, IBD. Found in up to 32% of patients (EASL).
  • - Prothrombotic Conditions: Antiphospholipid syndrome, Protein C/S deficiency.
  • - Hematological Disorders: Myeloproliferative disorders, aplastic anemia.
  • - Drug Exposure: Didanosine, azathioprine, oxaliplatin, thioguanine.
  • - Infections: HIV.


Natural History & Complications
  • - Primary Complication: Gastrointestinal bleeding from portal hypertension.
  • - Ascites and encephalopathy are less frequent and often triggered by bleeding.
Portal Vein Thrombosis (PVT)
  • - PVT is a very common complication.
  • - AASLD: Cumulative incidence in INCPH is higher (32%) than in cirrhosis (18%).
  • - EASL: Recommends surveillance for PVT every 6 months.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: HCC is extremely rare in this population.
  • - Routine surveillance for HCC is not recommended.


Treatment
  • - AASLD: No established independent therapy for INCPH itself. Management focuses on the complications of portal hypertension, generally following cirrhosis guidelines.
  • - EASL: Recommends TIPS or LT for severe or refractory portal hypertension-related complications.


Hereditary Hemorrhagic Telangiectasia (HHT) (AASLD Focus)
  • - Autosomal dominant genetic disease causing widespread arteriovenous malformations (AVMs).
  • - Liver vascular malformations (LVMs) are present in 40%-70% of patients.
Pathophysiology & Clinical Presentation
  • - Symptoms result from three main types of intrahepatic shunting:
  • - Hepatic Artery ---> Hepatic Vein: Leads to high-output heart failure (most common presentation).
  • - Hepatic Artery ---> Portal Vein: Leads to portal hypertension (ascites, varices).
  • - Portal Vein ---> Hepatic Vein: Leads to portosystemic encephalopathy.
Diagnostic Pitfalls
  • - Can be misdiagnosed as cirrhosis due to nodular liver, varices, and ascites.
  • - Liver synthetic function and platelet count are usually normal.


Diagnosis
  • - Suspect in patients with a liver bruit, enlarged hepatic artery, and heterogeneous liver enhancement on imaging.
  • - A family or personal history of recurrent nosebleeds is a key clue.
  • - Liver biopsy is not recommended due to high bleeding risk.

Management
  • - Should be managed at specialized centers with a multidisciplinary approach.
Standard Therapy
  • - Asymptomatic LVMs: No therapy or surveillance needed.
  • - Symptomatic LVMs: Treat the specific complication (e.g., diuretics for heart failure, standard management for portal hypertension).

Advanced Therapies (for non-responders)
  • - Bevacizumab: A monoclonal antibody against VEGF. Improves heart failure and ischemic cholangiopathy.
  • - Dose: 5 mg/kg intravenously every 2 weeks for six doses.
  • - Hepatic Artery Embolization/Ligation: Generally not recommended due to risk of hepatic/biliary necrosis.
  • - Liver Transplantation: For severe, refractory cases. Associated with high perioperative complications and potential for LVM recurrence in the graft.



Hepatic & Splenic Artery Aneurysms
  • - Uncommon vascular lesions, often found incidentally. It's critical to distinguish true aneurysms from pseudoaneurysms (higher rupture risk).
Diagnosis
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Multidetector CT angiography is the optimal tool for detection, characterization, and treatment planning.

Hepatic Artery Aneurysms (HAAs)
  • - Most are true aneurysms caused by atherosclerosis or trauma.
  • - Rupture Risk: Reported as high as 25% in some clinical series.
  • - Mortality from Rupture: >30%.

Splenic Artery Aneurysms (SAAs)
  • - Most common SAA. Associated with female gender, multiparity, and portal hypertension.
  • - Rupture Risk: Much lower than HAA, around 3% overall.
  • - Risk is negligible for true aneurysms <2 cm in diameter.
  • - Mortality from Rupture: 20%-36%.

Management & Intervention
  • - Decisions require a multidisciplinary approach (vascular surgery, interventional radiology).
Treatment Indications
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Urgent intervention for symptomatic or complicated aneurysms.
  • - Intervention is recommended for:
  • - All pseudoaneurysms, regardless of size.
  • - True aneurysms that are large (traditionally >2 cm) or show significant growth on serial imaging.
  • - Special populations: Women planning pregnancy and LT candidates.

Treatment Modality
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: If intervention is needed, endovascular repair should be considered first. Open surgery is reserved for cases where endovascular approach is not feasible.

Surveillance (Conservative Management)
  • - For asymptomatic true aneurysms <2 cm.
  • - AASLD: Recommends early follow-up imaging (e.g., at 3 and 12 months) to assess for growth.



Issues Specific to Children
  • - AASLD & EASL Consensus: Early referral to centers with expertise in pediatric liver disease, hepatobiliary surgery, and LT is recommended for all congenital or acquired vascular liver diseases.
Extrahepatic Portal Vein Obstruction (EHPVO)
  • - An acquired lesion, often following neonatal umbilical vein catheterization.
  • - Presents with variceal bleeding and hypersplenism, but preserved liver function.
  • - AASLD: Recommends evaluation for early intervention even in the presymptomatic stage.
Surgical Correction
  • - The preferred surgical procedure is the meso-Rex bypass (surgical extrahilar mesenterico/left portal shunt).
  • - This corrects physiological abnormalities like minimal hepatic encephalopathy.


Hepatic Hemangiomas
  • - Can be congenital or infantile. Many can be safely observed as they involute.
  • - AASLD: Glutathione expression occurs in 30%-50% of infantile hemangiomas.
  • - EASL & AASLD: These tumors respond well to nonselective beta-blockers like propranolol, which is the treatment of choice.

Congenital Portosystemic Shunts (Abernethy)
  • - Hallmark clinical feature is hyperammonemia.
  • - AASLD: Modern endovascular shunt closure techniques have made LT unnecessary in the vast majority of children.


Common Management Issues (EASL Focus)
  • - This section covers management principles applicable across various vascular liver diseases, primarily drawing from the more recent EASL 2025 guidelines.
Gastro-esophageal Varices
  • - Portal hypertension is a common final pathway for many of these disorders.
Surveillance
  • - Endoscopy is recommended when portal hypertension is suspected.
  • - Spleen stiffness measurement is a promising non-invasive tool. A value <40 kPa identifies patients with PSVD or portal cavernoma at very low risk of high-risk varices, potentially sparing endoscopy.
  • - Follow-up Interval: If no varices, repeat endoscopy in 2 years. If small varices, repeat in 1 year.

Prophylaxis of Bleeding
  • - Primary Prophylaxis: Non-selective beta-blockers (NSBBs) or endoscopic variceal ligation (EVL) are recommended for high-risk varices.
  • - Secondary Prophylaxis: Combination of NSBBs and EVL.
  • - Refractory Bleeding: TIPS is recommended.


Pregnancy
  • - Pregnancy should be planned when the liver disease and any prothrombotic condition are well-controlled.
Anticoagulation Management
  • - VKAs or DOACs must be switched to LMWH as soon as pregnancy is confirmed, and at least before the 4th week of gestation.
  • - LMWH is continued throughout the pregnancy.

Delivery
  • - Vaginal delivery is preferred over caesarean section.
  • - Platelet Count Thresholds:
  • - Vaginal Delivery: >20 x 10^9/L
  • - Caesarean Section: >50 x 10^9/L
  • - Epidural Anesthesia: >75 x 10^9/L

Post-partum & Breastfeeding
  • - Oestrogen-containing contraceptives should not be used.
  • - Breastfeeding is possible with beta-blockers and warfarin.
  • - Other VKAs and DOACs are contraindicated during breastfeeding.


Contraception
  • - Oestrogen-containing oral contraceptives are associated with an increased risk of thrombosis and should be avoided.
  • - Preferred methods: Intrauterine devices (IUDs), mechanical contraception (condoms), and microprogestatives (progestin-only pills).
