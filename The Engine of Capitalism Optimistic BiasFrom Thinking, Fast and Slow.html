The Engine of Capitalism: Optimistic Bias
From 'Thinking, Fast and Slow'
  • - This mind map explores the concepts of optimistic bias, overconfidence, and related cognitive biases as discussed in the book 'Thinking, Fast and Slow'.
  • - It focuses on how these biases shape decisions, especially in business and professional contexts.

üß† Optimistic Bias: An Overview
  • - A widespread cognitive bias where most people see the world as better than it is, their own qualities as more positive than they are, and their goals as more achievable than they are likely to be.
  • - It may be the most significant of the cognitive biases due to its impact on decisions.
A Double-Edged Sword
  • - Blessing: Fosters resilience, ambition, and action.
  • - Risk: Leads to underestimation of risks, poor planning, and failure.

Forecasting the Future
  • - People tend to exaggerate their ability to predict the future.
  • - This fosters optimistic overconfidence.


üòä The Optimist Profile
  • - An optimistic attitude is largely inherited and part of a general disposition for well-being.
Positive Traits
  • - Normally cheerful, happy, and popular.
  • - Resilient in adapting to failures.
  • - Reduced chance of clinical depression.
  • - Stronger immune system and better health habits, often leading to a longer life.

Role in Society
  • - Optimists play a disproportionate role in shaping our lives.
  • - They are the inventors, entrepreneurs, and leaders who seek challenges and take risks.
  • - Their success confirms their belief in their own judgment and ability to control events.

The Influencers
  • - Hypothesis: The people with the greatest influence on others are likely to be optimistic, overconfident, and take more risks than they realize.


üöÄ Entrepreneurial Delusions
  • - Optimistic bias is often the dominant factor when individuals or companies take on significant risks.
  • - Risk-takers often underestimate the odds they face.
Statistics vs. Belief
  • - Reality: The chance a small business in the US will survive for 5 years is about 35%.
  • - Entrepreneurs' Belief (General): They estimate the chance of success for 'any business like yours' is 60%.
  • - Entrepreneurs' Belief (Personal): 81% put their personal odds of success at 7/10 or higher, and 33% said their chance of failing was zero.

Persistence vs. Obstinance
  • - Optimism encourages persistence, but this can be costly.
  • - A study of inventors given objective feedback on their ideas showed this clearly.
Inventor Study Details
  • - Over 70% of inventions were predicted to fail (Grade D or E).
  • - These forecasts were highly accurate.
  • - After receiving a prediction of failure, about half the inventors quit.
  • - However, 47% continued their efforts, on average doubling their initial losses before giving up.


Financial Reality of Self-Employment
  • - Financial benefits are often mediocre.
  • - People with the same qualifications often achieve higher average returns by working for an employer.

The Hubris Hypothesis
  • - Explains why leaders of large businesses make huge, expensive bets on mergers and acquisitions.
  • - They mistakenly believe they can manage the other company's assets better.
  • - The stock market often reacts by downgrading the value of the acquiring firm, as these efforts fail more often than they succeed.
Overconfident CEOs
  • - Identified by the large amount of company stock they personally own.
  • - They take excessive risks, such as assuming debt instead of issuing equity.
  • - They are more likely to overpay for acquisitions and undertake 'value-destroying mergers'.
  • - This shows they are not just gambling with other people's money; they take bigger risks when their own money is at stake.



üôà Competition Neglect
  • - A key cognitive bias where we focus on our own goals, plans, and skills, while neglecting the plans and skills of others.
  • - This is a result of System 1's WYSIATI ('What You See Is All There Is').
How It Works
  • - We focus on what we can do, not what competitors will do.
  • - We focus on the role of skill and neglect the role of luck.
  • - We focus on what we know and neglect what we don't know.

Above-Average Effect
  • - The common finding that '90% of drivers believe they are better than average' is an example.
  • - This is a cognitive bias: people answer an easier question ('Am I a good driver?') instead of the harder one ('Am I better than the average driver?').

Consequences
  • - Excess Entry: More competitors enter a market than it can support.
  • - The average outcome for entrants is a loss.
  • - Example: Movie studios releasing big-budget films on the same holiday weekend, because each studio focuses only on its own film and marketing, not the competition. The reason given by a Disney chairman was Hubris.
Optimistic Martyrs
  • - Term for failed entrepreneurial firms.
  • - They are bad for their investors but can be good for the economy by signaling new market opportunities to more qualified competitors.



üòé Overconfidence
  • - Another manifestation of WYSIATI, where we construct a coherent story from the information available to us and ignore the information we don't have.
Case Study: CFO Forecasts
  • - A Duke University survey collected 11,600 forecasts of the S&P index from Chief Financial Officers (CFOs).
Findings
  • - Accuracy: The correlation between their estimates and the true value was slightly less than zero. They had no clue.
  • - Confidence: They set an '80% confidence interval' but outcomes fell outside this range 67% of the time (expected rate was 20%).
  • - Conclusion: CFOs were grossly overconfident and did not know their forecasts were worthless.


Case Study: Medicine
  • - A study compared antemortem (before death) diagnoses with autopsy results in an ICU.
  • - Result: Clinicians who were 'completely certain' of the diagnosis were wrong 40% of the time.

Social & Organizational Pressures
  • - Admitting ignorance or uncertainty is often socially unacceptable for experts.
  • - Confidence is valued over uncertainty.
  • - Organizations and clients reward providers of dangerously misleading (but confident) information more than truth-tellers.
  • - This can lead to a collective blindness to risk.


üõ†Ô∏è The Premortem: A Partial Remedy
  • - A technique proposed by psychologist Gary Klein to combat overconfidence and groupthink in organizations.
The Procedure
  • - When a team is close to a big decision, gather knowledgeable people for a brief session.
  • - The premise: 'Imagine we are a year into the future. We implemented the plan. The outcome was a disaster.'
  • - Each person then spends 5-10 minutes writing a brief history of that disaster.

Main Advantages
  • - Overcomes Groupthink: It legitimizes doubt and makes it safe to voice concerns, even after a decision seems to have been made.
  • - Unleashes Imagination: It directs knowledgeable people to look for potential threats and flaws in the plan that were previously overlooked.

Effectiveness
  • - It is not a perfect solution ('panacea').
  • - It helps reduce the damage from plans affected by WYSIATI and uncritical optimism.
