Writing the NIH Grant Proposal: A Step-by-Step Guide
Chapter 5 - Writing the Application, Part I
1. Scientific Content and Grant Overview
  • R01 is most common grant mechanism; other mechanisms noted when differences arise.
  • Training grants covered separately with specific details.
  • Specific Aims: One-page limit; write first to tell entire story with major points.
  • Research Strategy: 12 pages of text expanding Specific Aims with details, significance, preliminary work.
  • Approach section typically longest; most common reviewer complaint is insufficient detail.
  • Page limit creates level playing field—all applicants have only 12 pages.
1.1 Grant Structure Overview
  • Specific Aims page tells whole story; must include all major points.
  • 12-page text expands on Specific Aims with problem significance and preliminary data.
  • Innovation section describes novel contributions; Approach provides detailed methodology.
  • Reviewers expect more detail than space allows; must write efficiently to meet expectations.
2. General Principles of Scientific Writing
  • Prose must be outstanding; many underestimate importance of good scientific writing.
  • Grade school taught facts memorization, not engaging writing techniques—must overcome this.
  • Writing should flow naturally with transitions; avoid short choppy sentences.
  • Revise repeatedly until coherent; read aloud to others for feedback.
  • Methods must be understandable by non-specialists.
2.1 Writing Rules and Guidelines
  • Rule 1: Write from Reader's Perspective → Get into reader's head; doubt from unclear words colors perception.
  • Rule 2: Be Linear → Each point follows logically; veer off only when necessary, then return to main line.
  • Rule 3: Keep It Simple → Resist adding peripheral measures; primary measures must link to hypothesis, power analysis, statistical analysis.
  • Rule 4: Tell a Story → Like screenplay; each scene moves story forward logically.
  • Rule 5: Communicate Excitement → Sell importance of topic, innovative methods, public health implications.
  • Rule 6: Words Matter → Every word evaluated; substitution changes interpretation.
2.2 Practical Writing Strategies
  • Read work aloud to hear different rhythm; catch repetitions needing revision.
  • Get colleagues, non-field friends to read and provide impressions.
  • Cite work of anyone on study section who published in related field.
  • Use headings (worth the space); bold, italics, capitals for visual hierarchy.
  • Avoid jargon; use standard acronyms only to save space.
2.3 Space Management Techniques
  • White space breaks up text, makes reading easier for overburdened reviewers.
  • Use half line space between paragraphs rather than full space.
  • Reduce font to 4 points in "Format Paragraph" window if desperate for lines.
  • Never sacrifice readability elements that help reviewers process content.
3. Core Components of a Fundable Proposal
  • Three broad components required: great idea, feasibility demonstration, logical writing.
  • Great Idea: Must strike reviewers as potentially important with significant field impact.
  • Feasibility: Demonstrates ability to carry out proposed work; reviewers need confidence.
  • Logical Writing: Story emerges orderly—avoid peripheral ramifications that annoy reviewers.
3.1 Three Critical Components Explained
  • Great idea pushes science forward; must be immediately apparent to reviewers.
  • Feasibility is equally important as having great idea—can do what you promise.
  • Logical writing prevents boring reviewers with unnecessary material; saves space for essentials.
3.2 Feedback and Timeline Requirements
  • Begin proposal preparation minimum 8 weeks before deadline; 3 months better.
  • Ask colleagues and mentors for critical feedback—requires hours of their time.
  • Specific questions for readers: Were you persuaded? Did writing communicate excitement?
  • Story easy to follow? Assertions adequately supported? Project innovative? Methods appropriate?
3.3 Timeline and Preparation Strategy
  • Many investigators intend to start early but write up to deadline—hurts funding chances.
  • Adequate time allows ideas to coalesce into coherent story.
  • Sufficient time enables asking colleagues for suggestions without unreasonable deadlines.
4. NIH Review Criteria and Evaluation Framework
  • First-level review by Scientific Review Group (SRG): non-federal scientists with expertise.
  • Second-level review by I/C National Advisory Councils; both must recommend for funding.
4.1 Scoring System
  • Assigned reviewers score on five criteria using scale of 1 (best) to 9.
  • Five review criteria: Significance, Investigator(s), Innovation, Approach, Environment.
  • Preliminary overall impact score provided before meeting.
  • Streamlining: Only top half-scoring applications discussed; others "streamlined."
  • Final scores range 10-90 (multiplied by 10, averaged to one decimal place).
4.2 Five Core Review Criteria

1. Significance: Addresses important problem? Advances scientific knowledge or clinical practice?

2. Investigators: PIs, collaborators well-suited? ESIs/NIs have appropriate experience/training?

3. Innovation: Challenges current paradigms? Novel concepts, approaches, methodologies?

4. Approach: Overall strategy well-reasoned? Robust and unbiased design? Problems addressed?

5. Environment: Scientific environment contributes to success? Institutional support adequate?

4.3 Additional Review Considerations
  • Protections for Human Subjects: Risk justification, protections, potential benefits evaluated.
  • Inclusion of Women, Minorities, Children: Plans for appropriate representation reviewed.
  • Vertebrate Animals: Procedures justified, species appropriate, discomfort minimized.
  • Biohazards: Materials/procedures assessed for personnel and environmental safety.
4.4 Application Type Considerations
  • Resubmission Applications: Evaluated as now presented, with responses to previous comments.
  • Renewal Applications: Progress made in last funding period considered.
  • Revision Applications: Appropriateness of scope expansion evaluated.
5. New NIH Procedures: Rigor and Transparency Requirements
  • New procedures implemented for due dates on or after May 25, 2016.
  • Most important change: additional substantive material required for Significance/Approach sections.
  • NIH committed to promoting rigorous and transparent research in all supported science areas.
5.1 Scientific Rigor Definition
  • Scientific rigor: strict application of scientific method ensuring robust, unbiased experimental design.
  • Includes methodology, analysis, interpretation, reporting of results.
  • Full transparency in reporting experimental details enables reproduction and extension.
  • Investigators apply rigor elements appropriate for their science.
5.2 Four Key Areas of Updated Instructions

1. Scientific Premise: Research forming basis for proposed research question.

  • Applicants describe strengths/weaknesses of prior research cited as crucial support.
  • Consideration includes rigor of previous experimental designs, relevant biological variables, authentication.

2. Rigorous Experimental Design: For robust and unbiased results.

  • Strict application of scientific method ensures robust, unbiased methodology.
  • Full transparency enables others to reproduce findings.

3. Relevant Biological Variables: Sex, age, weight, underlying health conditions.

  • Sex frequently ignored in animal study designs—leads to incomplete understanding of sex-based differences.
  • NIH expects sex as biological variable (SABV) factored into research designs, analyses, reporting.
  • Strong justification required for applications proposing to study only one sex.

4. Authentication of Key Biological/Chemical Resources: Cell lines, specialty chemicals, antibodies.

  • May differ laboratory to laboratory or over time.
  • Quality/qualifications could influence research data.
  • Standard reagents (buffers, common biologicals) not expected to vary—don't need inclusion.
5.3 Addressing Scientific Rigor in Applications
  • Innovative research involves greater risk due to novelty but can still be rigorous.
  • Risk identified and managed by considering scientific premise, unknown factors, bias reduction strategies.
  • Strategies to mitigate risk increase commensurate with research type/stage.
  • Innovative projects expected to generate reproducible data for future studies.
5.4 Sample Size and Power Analysis Guidelines
  • Cannot present every detail but must succinctly state plans.
  • Example: "10 males and 10 females will be randomized to blinded treatment and control groups, giving 80% power to detect a treatment effect size of 65% compared to baseline response of 5% at significance level of 0.05."
  • NIH institutes/centers issue detailed guidelines for specific funding opportunities.
  • Examples: NINDS Guidance, NOT-MH-14-004, NOT-DA-14-007; PAR-13-023 (R21), RFA-NR-15-001 (R01).
5.5 Power Analysis Review
  • Updated review language directs peer reviewers to assess scientific rigor of experimental design.
  • Includes appropriateness of sample size justification under Approach criterion.
  • Formalizes NIH expectations that statistical power be addressed.
  • Program staff may recommend changes to experimental designs for conformity with best practices.
5.6 Key Biological Variables
  • Applicants consider biological variables relevant to experimental design.
  • Choice of animal model/human population varies with scientific topic.
  • Sex, age, weight, underlying health conditions may affect outcome—should be considered.
  • Strong justification from literature, preliminary data required for single-sex studies.
5.7 Key Resources Definition
  • Established resources used in proposed research.
  • May or may not be generated with NIH funds.
  • Quality critical to ability to reproduce results.
  • Each investigator determines which resources fit criteria and are key.
5.8 Institutional Training Grants Requirements
  • Required to include training program plan summary ensuring knowledge/skills for:
  • Rigorous, well-controlled experiments.
  • Consideration of relevant biological variables.
  • Use of authenticated biological/chemical resources.
  • Appropriate statistical tests for data analyses.
  • Separate attachment required describing instructional and curricular content in detail.
5.9 Institutional Career Development Applications (K12/KL2)
  • Required to include career development program plan summary ensuring scholars have:
  • Knowledge/skills for rigorous, well-controlled experiments.
  • Consideration of relevant biological variables.
  • Use of authenticated resources.
  • Appropriate statistical analyses.
  • Separate attachment required for detailed instructional content.
5.10 Individual Fellowship Applications
  • Required to summarize in research strategy section plans ensuring:
  • Rigorous, well-controlled experiments.
  • Consideration of relevant biological variables.
  • Use of authenticated resources.
  • Appropriate statistical tests.
  • Detailed description of instruction in rigorous experimental design required in Institutional Environment section.

6.2 Example #1: Animal Study Design
  • Aim 3: Male and female mice randomly allocated to experimental groups at age 3 months.
  • CUG repeat RNA, MBNL1 sequestration, splicing defects, myotonia fully developed at this age.
  • Compound administered at 3 doses (25%, 50%, 100% of MTD) for 4 weeks vs. vehicle controls.
  • IP administration unless biodistribution studies indicate preference for IV route.
  • Group size N=10 (5 males, 5 females) provides 90% power to detect 22% reduction of CUG repeat RNA by qRT-PCR (ANOVA, α=0.05).
  • Treatment assignment blinded to investigators participating in drug administration and endpoint analyses.
  • Laboratory has previous experience with randomized allocation and blinded analysis using this mouse model.
6.3 Example #2: High-Throughput Screening Assay
  • Aim 1: Primary screen combines SMN promoter with exons 1-6 and exon 7 splicing cassette in single construct.
  • Responds to compounds increasing SMN transcription, exon 7 inclusion, or stabilizing SMN RNA/protein.
  • Each point run in triplicate; compounds tested on three separate occasions, results averaged for EC50 with standard deviation.
  • Secondary screen: SMN protein levels analyzed by dose response in quantitative immunoblots with one-way ANOVA, post-hoc Dunnett or Bonferroni.
  • Aim 2: Each compound set includes blinded negative control (inactive, same solubilization as test compounds).
  • Mice randomly assigned within litter; data collected and submitted to PI.
  • SSD sample size power analysis ensures appropriately minimal mice used per experimental context.
6.4 Example #3: Longitudinal Study Design
  • Aim 2: Intensity signal data transformed to log values, modeled by longitudinal methods.
  • Composite difference in mean intensity signals over time between bi-specific T cells vs. control groups assumed 2.8 logs with composite standard deviation of 2.2 logs.
  • At least five repeated measurements per mouse after T cell infusion; within-mouse intra-correlation coefficient = 0.50.
  • Sample size of 10 mice per group provides at least 80% power to detect difference between treated vs. control with 5% significance level.
  • Log-rank test used to compare survival distribution between groups.
6.5 Example #4: Microbiome Study
  • Aim 1: Preliminary studies with N=100 DFUs using 16S rRNA sequencing detected dimensions significantly associated with outcomes.
  • Sample size provides sufficient power for metagenomic sequencing (more sensitive, less-biased assay).
  • Aim 3: Random Forests machine learning approach determines which metagenome features differentiate groups.
  • Bootstrap method assesses test error—ideal for small sample size (N=18).
  • Wilcoxon rank-sum tests assess significance between groups for diversity and load measures.

7. Writing Individual Sections of the Grant Text
7.1 The Title
  • See Chapter 7 for suggestions concerning project title.
  • Should be clear, concise, descriptive of research focus.
7.2 The Abstract
  • Write after completing 13 pages of main text—know exactly what you're doing at that point.
  • Read by all reviewers, not just assigned few; usually first thing read.
  • Must grab reviewers' interest immediately.
  • Provides context for proposed work; becomes public information if funded.
  • Guides assignment of proposal to particular study section.
7.3 Abstract Content Requirements
  • Describes nature of problem/research question.
  • States long-term objectives.
  • Explains innovative features of research.
  • Details specific aims and research design/methods.
  • Clarity crucial—write after fairly far along in writing process.
  • Ask colleagues/co-investigators to read and provide critical feedback.
7.4 Specific Aims Section
  • Along with abstract, often first thing reviewers read—influences perceptions of research and abilities.
  • Must accomplish great deal in short space (one page maximum).
  • Extremely carefully written—must tell entire story or enough context for details to follow.
  • Do not propose overly ambitious aims—reviewers worry scope exceeds budget/effort allocation.
7.5 Specific Aims Required Elements
  • Clear statement of problem/question to investigate.
  • Public health significance.
  • Background material providing context for proposal.
  • Why proposal is innovative and needs to be done.
  • What plan to do (basics of design, primary outcomes, study conditions).
  • Specific aims and, space permitting, hypotheses.
7.6 Example: Clinical Trial Specific Aims
  • Problem: Despite drug therapies, 50% of hypertensives on treatment have inadequately controlled BP.
  • Public health significance: Excess morbidity/mortality, huge economic burden.
  • Proposed solution: Guided breathing intervention as promising behavioral approach.
  • Small studies show positive short-term effects; long-term effects unknown.
  • High patient acceptability, no known side effects.
7.7 Guided Breathing Rationale
  • Slows breathing rate to 6-10 breaths/minute range.
  • Published studies show SBP/DBP reductions of 5.5/3.6–15.2/10.0 mmHg.
  • Effects surprisingly large from brief practice sessions (daily 15-minute for 8 weeks).
  • If persistent, represents effective, accessible, cost-efficient way to control BP.
7.8 Limitations of Published Studies

1. Sustained effects unknown: Only one small study (N=13) examined ambulatory BP effects.

2. Mechanism unclear: Is it special breathing rate or nonspecific relaxation effect?

3. Duration unknown: Only one 6-month duration study examined effects beyond 8 weeks.

  • Behavioral interventions need methods to sustain long-term effects including cost effectiveness.
7.9 Study Design Example
  • Blinded, randomized controlled trial (RCT) testing guided breathing in poorly controlled hypertensives.
  • Sample: N=400, 100 per arm.
  • Primary outcome: 24-hour ABP (superior predictor of target organ damage, cardiovascular events).
  • Control groups: Usual care and placebo (identical device not slowing breathing rate).
  • Two intervention arms: Standard Duration (8 weeks) and Extended Duration (12-month follow-up).
7.10 Specific Aims Listing

1. Recruit 440 hypertensive patients with goal of 100 in each of 4 arms.

2. Test effect of guided breathing on Ambulatory BP at 8 weeks (Brief) and 12 months (Extended).

3. Compare to usual care and placebo condition.

7.11 Primary Hypotheses

1. Participants in two intervention conditions will have lower ABP compared to UC and Placebo.

2. Participants in Placebo control condition will have lower ABP compared to UC.

3. Participants in both intervention conditions will have lower ABP at 12 months vs. Placebo and UC.

4. Participants in extended duration intervention will have lower ABP at 12 months vs. standard duration.

7.12 Additional Research Questions
  • Evaluate potential mechanisms mediating intervention effects on BP:
  • Baroreflex sensitivity.
  • Heart rate variability (HRV).
  • Changes in ambulatory respiration.
  • Self-reported changes in physical activity and anxiety.
7.13 Hypothesis Strategy Notes
  • Controversial whether to use aims, hypotheses, research questions—no clear guide.
  • Funded applications vary in approach—stick with focused Specific Aims, limit to 3-4.
  • Labeling as "Research Question" indicates not powered for analysis—strategic choice.
  • Primary hypotheses should be main points of study; secondary follow-up periods.
7.14 Hypothesis Wording Guidelines
  • Clearly specify relationship between independent and dependent variable.
  • Relate to proposed research (empirical test of that relationship).
  • Keep hypotheses simple—break compound predictions into separate hypotheses.
  • Example: Rather than "main effects and interaction," write separate Hypothesis 1, 2, 3.
7.15 Public Health Emphasis
  • Hit public health implications hard very early—often in first sentence.
  • Emphasize how study will address them whether basic or clinical researcher.
  • NIH leaders must show Congress they solve problems reducing morbidity/mortality and economic burden.
  • Basic researchers: connect findings to ultimate clinical/public health applications.
8. Research Strategy Section Examples
8.1 Research Strategy Structure
  • 12-page section with required headings:
  • A. Significance
  • B. Innovation
  • C. Approach
  • Preliminary Studies (new applications) or Progress Report (renewal/revision)
8.2 Significance Section Requirements
  • Explain importance of problem or critical barrier to progress in field.
  • Describe how proposed project improves scientific knowledge, technical capability, clinical practice.
  • Explain how concepts, methods, technologies driving field will change if aims achieved.
8.3 Significance Writing Strategy
  • If Specific Aims done well, much already included—avoid excessive redundancy.
  • Make sure reviewers never need to look elsewhere for clarification.
  • Keep Significance/Innovation sections to about 2-3 pages total—maximize methodology space.
8.4 Significance Example Overview
  • Drug treatment fallen short of getting most treated hypertensives to goal (BP <140/90 mmHg).
  • Proposed RCT powered to detect intervention effects vs. placebo and UC at 8 weeks and 12 months.
  • Will evaluate potential mediator of guided breathing effect on BP.
8.5 Pharmacologic Treatment Limitations
  • Only 53% of hypertensives on drug treatment, only 1/3 of all hypertensives have controlled BP.
  • Medications often expensive and may have side effects limiting adherence.
  • Treatment guidelines recommend adjunctive behavioral treatments.
8.6 Non-Pharmacologic Interventions
  • Relaxation-based interventions reported beneficial effects on BP.
  • Effect sizes: 9.0-9.7 mmHg (SBP) and 6.1-7.2 mmHg (DBP).
  • Interventions include autogenic training, progressive muscle relaxation, meditation, biofeedback.
  • Difficult to demonstrate replicable differences between specific treatment modality and active control.
8.7 Device-Guided Breathing Technology
  • Advances in technology, increased interest in HRV as cardiac health index.
  • Studies use HRV biofeedback coupled with devices guiding breathing to 6-10 breaths/minute (0.1 Hz) range.
  • Respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) has greatest amplitude in this range.
  • Appears safe—people compensate by breathing more deeply; pulse oximetry, end-tidal CO2 show little effect on blood gases.

8.9 Evidence Quality Assessment
  • Results overwhelmingly positive but limited by:
  • Small sample sizes in many studies.
  • Two studies lack control conditions.
  • Only one small trial (N=13) used ABP as outcome.
  • Consistency of effect sizes across studies suggests guided breathing useful intervention to test in adequately powered RCT.
8.10 Ambulatory Blood Pressure Rationale
  • ABP gold standard for intervention—not susceptible to white coat effect.
  • Superior predictor of target organ damage vs. office BP measurements.
  • Provides means to assess BP during sleep—critical for treatment evaluation.
  • Only one study used ABP with only 13 subjects—clearly requires additional work.
8.11 Mechanism Discussion Strategy
  • Main focus on clinical outcome (ABP)—measure for power estimates.
  • Sustained participant contact over 1 year at several visits enables mechanism data collection.
  • Low additional cost/burden to subjects.
  • Candidate mechanisms: alteration of baroreflex sensitivity, HRV, increased arterial compliance.
8.12 Significance Section Writing Principles
  • Narrative considers reader: terms defined, abbreviations explained, headers provided.
  • Large blocks broken up with white space.
  • New sections set up with preliminary statement explaining what comes next and why.
  • Issues that may be questionable/controversial explained immediately—not saved for end.
8.13 Innovation Section Requirements
  • Explain how application challenges and shifts current research/clinical practice paradigms.
  • Describe novel theoretical concepts, approaches, methodologies, instrumentation, interventions.
  • Explain refinements, improvements, new applications of existing concepts.
8.14 Innovation Section Content
  • What plan does differently from previous studies—definitive account of rationale.
  • Why plan is novel, cutting-edge, should excite reader—what are seriously cool aspects?
  • New approach.
  • New underlying theoretical model.
  • Improvements in study variables (more reliable instruments, objective measures, longer follow-up).
  • New technology.
  • Comparison of competing theories.
8.15 Innovation Example
  • ABP as primary outcome: Only one small study previously used ABP.
  • Not susceptible to white coat effect—superior predictor of target organ damage.
  • Large sample size: N=400 based on power analysis—previous studies largest was 79.
  • Longer follow-up: 12 months vs. previous longest of 12 weeks.
8.16 Writing Principles Summary
  • Story simple and clean, presented for easy following.
  • Logic explained—no part of story line allowed to sag.
  • When hypotheses strongly supported, made clear; when exploratory, evident.
  • Narrative makes reviewers aware of implications of having/omitting controversial hypotheses.
9. Approach Section
  • Must describe overall strategy, methodology, analyses to accomplish specific aims.
  • Include how data will be collected, analyzed, interpreted.
  • Resource-sharing plans as appropriate.
  • Discuss potential problems, alternative strategies, benchmarks for success.
  • If early stages of development, describe strategy to establish feasibility.
  • Address management of high-risk aspects.
  • Point out hazardous procedures, situations, materials with precautions.
  • Full discussion on use of select agents.
9.1 Approach Section Writing Strategy
  • Most important section—typically longest of the three (Significance, Innovation, Approach).
  • Must be extremely detailed yet concise within 12-page limit.
  • Most common reviewer complaint is insufficient detail in Approach section.
  • Reviewers want confidence you can execute proposed work.
  • Balance between ambitious scope and realistic feasibility.
9.2 Data Collection Methods
  • Describe specific procedures for collecting data related to each aim.
  • Include measurement instruments, timing of assessments, personnel responsibilities.
  • Address how data quality will be ensured and monitored.
  • For human subjects: recruitment procedures, inclusion/exclusion criteria, retention strategies.
  • For animal studies: species, strains, ages, sex, total number to be used.
9.3 Statistical Analysis Plan
  • Describe analytical approach for each hypothesis/aim.
  • Include power analysis justifying sample size.
  • Address multiple comparison corrections if applicable.
  • Describe handling of missing data, dropouts.
  • For complex designs: specify multilevel modeling, longitudinal analysis approaches.
  • Justify appropriateness of statistical tests for research questions.
9.4 Feasibility Considerations
  • Demonstrate ability to recruit required sample size.
  • Show expertise of team in proposed methodology.
  • Address potential barriers and contingency plans.
  • Include timeline for key milestones and deliverables.
  • For novel methods: pilot data supporting feasibility.
9.5 Risk Management
  • Identify high-risk aspects of proposed work.
  • Present alternative strategies if primary approach fails.
  • Define benchmarks for success in achieving aims.
  • Address potential problems before reviewers raise them.
10. Preliminary Studies and Progress Reports
10.1 Preliminary Studies for New Applications
  • Include information as part of Approach section (suggested for all applications when possible).
  • Discuss PD/PI's preliminary studies, data, experience pertinent to application.
  • Essential part of research grant application for most mechanisms.
  • Helps establish likelihood of success of proposed project.
  • Early Stage Investigators should include preliminary data.
  • For R01 applications, reviewers place less emphasis on ESI preliminary data vs. established investigators.
10.2 Preliminary Studies Content
  • Present relevant pilot data supporting feasibility of proposed approach.
  • Show track record of research team in related work.
  • Demonstrate preliminary findings that justify proposed hypotheses.
  • Include publications, manuscripts supporting foundation of proposal.
  • For Exploratory/Development Grants (R21), Small Research Grants (R03), AREA (R15): preliminary data less critical.
10.3 Progress Report for Renewal/Revision Applications
  • Provide as part of Approach section.
  • Include beginning and ending dates for period covered since last competitive review.
  • Summarize specific aims of previous project period.
  • Emphasize importance of findings and progress toward achievement.
  • Explain any significant changes to specific aims.
  • Describe new directions including changes from budget reductions.
10.4 Progress Report Required Elements
  • List of publications, manuscripts accepted for publication.
  • Patents and other printed materials.
  • Demonstrate productivity and progress toward goals.
  • Show return on NIH investment.
  • Address any delays or deviations from original plan with justifications.
11. Practical Writing Tips and Strategies
11.1 Interactive Writing Process
  • Not linear—regard as interactive process.
  • Some sections need to be written before others.
  • Modifying one section often influences previously written sections.
  • As research strategy develops, may find aims cannot be carried out.
  • Perhaps too expensive.
  • Power estimates require sample size without access.
  • May need to rewrite proposal parts relating to particular aim.
  • Budget must be written almost as you start writing proposal.
  • Research influences budget; budget influences research.
  • Can revise items later but don't want to significantly modify specific aims late.
11.2 Budget Integration Strategy
  • Write budget almost as you start writing proposal (Chapter 7 describes process).
  • Research will influence budget but budget influences research.
  • Avoid significantly modifying specific aims late—requires rethinking/rewriting whole proposal.
  • Request funding for resources needed to ensure successful study completion.
  • Rule of thumb: work out budget, then double it.
  • Don't try to be a bargain—request resources needed for success.
11.3 Study Section Analysis
  • Look on web to see precisely who will be on study section evaluating proposal.
  • http://www.csr.nih.gov/Roster_proto/sectionI.asp
  • Identify potential conflicts of interest (inform NIH).
  • Identify anyone who cannot provide unbiased assessment.
  • Cannot know exactly which panel members will review proposal but can make guesses.
  • Look at study panel early in writing process.
  • May find panel members not thought to cite—if reviewer, omission would sit poorly.
  • If panel members worked in proposal area, incorporate their particular points of view.
  • Don't have to agree but should address them.
11.4 Writing Timeline Recommendations
  • Begin minimum 8 weeks before deadline; 3 months better.
  • Adequate time allows ideas to coalesce into coherent story.
  • Sufficient time for colleague feedback without unreasonable deadlines.
  • Many investigators intend early start but write up to deadline—hurts funding chances.
  • Start now even if deadline seems far away.
11.5 Feedback Collection Strategy
  • Ask colleagues and mentors for critical feedback—requires hours of their time.
  • Co-investigators can be asked more than unofficial helpers.
  • Specific questions for readers:
  • Were you persuaded proposed study should be done?
  • Did writing communicate excitement about project?
  • Was story easy to follow? Any parts that sidetracked?
  • Were assertions adequately supported by literature/data?
  • Do you consider project innovative?
  • Were methods appropriate to study questions?
  • Was power analysis convincing?
  • What limitations should be addressed?
  • Was manuscript adequately proofread?
12. Additional Hints and Considerations
12.1 Ambition vs. Realism Balance
  • Proposal must be ambitious in scope but never appear unrealistic.
  • Must not exceed what proposed study can accomplish or ability to carry out.
  • Unlike paper, grant proposal must do more than replicate previous work.
  • Must move field forward—that's what reviewers want to fund.
  • Don't want reviewers labeling proposal "ambitious"—feeds concern about delivery.
12.2 Focus Strategy
  • Many inexperienced investigators show research bargain by collecting extra measures.
  • Resist temptation—appear inexperienced, not understanding work involved.
  • Just because measure not mentioned doesn't mean can't collect it during study.
  • Fight temptation to mention extra measures in proposal.
  • Ensure can find money if additional costs incurred.
12.3 Primary Measure Requirements
  • Every primary measure should be directly linked to:
  • Hypothesis.
  • Power analysis.
  • Statistical analysis.
  • Can mention other measures of interest because:
  • Want reviewers to know importance.
  • Might cost money requiring budget allocation.
  • Not necessarily want to power sample size to all measures.
  • Can list measures as "exploratory" but very limited extent.
  • Resisting outcome loading keeps proposal highly focused.
12.4 Reader-Centered Writing
  • Single most important point: write from audience perspective (assigned reviewers).
  • Must hear writing as though you were reviewer.
  • Larger audience: NIH organization and its agenda.
  • NIH leaders must convince Congress to allocate taxpayer money for research.
  • Must show solving problems for representatives and senators.
  • Address public health implications of funded research.
  • Basic researchers: connect work to reducing morbidity/mortality, economic burden.
  • Hit public health implications hard early—often in first sentence.
12.5 Jargon Avoidance
  • Aim for proposal understandable without asking/looking up expressions or acronyms.
  • Annoying to go back reminding what particular acronym means.
  • Use standard ones only, or long names costing serious space.
  • Reviewers from different fields won't understand field-specific jargon.
  • Jargon doesn't strengthen case with non-expert reviewers.
12.6 Visual Presentation
  • Use headings (sacrifice space—worth it).
  • Be creative making them stand out: bold-faced type, italics, all capitals.
  • Consistent hierarchy for headings.
  • Major headings: bold capitals.
  • Secondary headings: bold lowercase letters.
  • White space breaks up text, makes reading easier for overburdened reviewers.
  • Eyes tired, temper short by time reaching application.
  • Pictures and charts help break up page, give eyes rest.
  • Save space with half line space between paragraphs rather than full.
  • If desperate, use 4 points rather than 6 in "Format Paragraph" window.
12.7 Redundancy Balance
  • Story requires redundancy—reader not necessarily expert needs reminders.
  • Find balance between necessary prompts and over-redundancy.
  • Difficult for writer to judge—ask external readers for feedback.
  • Don't make reviewers look elsewhere in proposal for clarification.
  • Redundant enough concerning abstruse/difficult-to-remember points.
12.8 Technical Explanation Approach
  • Don't assume readers know technical matters considered commonplace.
  • Be diplomatic explaining—don't sound condescending.
  • Set up new sections with preliminary statement explaining what comes next and why.
  • When issues arise that may be questionable/controversial, explain decisions immediately.
  • Don't wait until end—damage may already done by then.
  • Anticipate reviewer questions and deal with them on the spot.
12.9 Table Usage Strategy
  • Tables take substantial room but may be necessary for clarity.
  • Example: Study results summary table provides evidence of intervention effectiveness.
  • Authors felt necessary—agreed with assessment.
  • Balance between space usage and information density.
12.10 Manuscript Proofreading
  • Must be adequately proofread before submission.
  • Errors create negative impression affecting overall perception.
  • Read aloud to catch awkward phrasing, grammatical errors.
  • Have multiple people proofread—fresh eyes catch different issues.
  • Consider professional editing services for critical proposals.
12.11 Citation Strategy
  • Cite work of anyone on study section who published in related field.
  • Senior investigators annoyed when groundbreaking work not cited.
  • Shows awareness of relevant literature and respect for field experts.
  • Don't overcite—focus on most relevant supporting work.
12.12 Acronym Management
  • Use standard acronyms only or for really long names costing serious space.
  • Define acronym on first use—subsequent uses can be abbreviated.
  • Avoid creating too many new acronyms.
  • Consider whether each acronym adds value or creates confusion.
12.13 Paragraph Structure
  • Every paragraph should make a point.
  • After writing, ask: What point am I trying to make?
  • Did I succeed in making the point?
  • Is language leading to reader's comprehension as intended?
  • Each paragraph should advance story toward goals.
12.14 Sentence-Level Clarity
  • Substitution of one word for another can color entire statement interpretation.
  • Think critically about every word choice.
  • Use precise, specific language rather than vague terms.
  • Avoid passive voice when active better communicates action.
  • Keep sentences focused but not choppy—maintain flow.
12.15 Transition Techniques
  • Transitions between sentences and paragraphs essential for flow.
  • Use transitional words/phrases: however, therefore, furthermore, in addition.
  • Ensure logical connection between ideas.
  • When veering off main line, carefully bring back to central argument.
12.16 Revision Process
  • Revise and revise until sick of it—that's the job.
  • Read work out loud to hear writing differently.
  • Find places requiring minor or extensive revision.
  • Even better: read to someone else and get impressions.
  • Get colleagues, friends not in field to provide feedback.
12.17 Story Structure
  • Think of writing a screenplay—each scene designed to move story forward.
  • Story must unfold so reader can follow and be engaged.
  • Should never violate reader's sense of logic.
  • Must make reader want to know what happens next.
  • Good science is interesting—interesting reading improves funding chances.
12.18 Excitement Communication
  • Grant proposal is selling something to specific customers.
  • Must communicate excitement about important research.
  • Want to excite readers about:
  • Importance of topic.
  • Innovative new methods devised for studying topic.
  • Broad implications and public health implications.
  • How it will move field forward.
12.19 Linear Writing Approach
  • Each point should follow logically from previous one.
  • Proceed along straight line as much as possible.
  • When must veer off, be careful to bring back.
  • Think proceeding up tree trunk—if go off on branch, return to main line.
  • Not place for ADD free rein—keep writing tight.
12.20 Simplicity Principle
  • As write idea, begin seeing other lines of thought to add.
  • May not pertain to main focus—resist temptation.
  • Particularly true for measures proposed:
  • May add peripheral measures easy/inexpensive to collect.
  • Might contribute depth but veers off main point.
  • Legitimate rationales for including them exist but may mean veering off.
  • Already doing study so easy to tack on extra measures.
  • Can show reviewers bargain—two or three studies for price of one.
  • Resist temptation.
12.21 Budget Justification
  • Don't underbudget—may be seen as sign of inexperience.
  • Reviewers may worry scope greater than budget/effort allocation.
  • Request funding for resources needed to ensure successful completion.
  • Work out budget, then double it.
  • Make sure requesting enough for success.
12.22 Public Health Framing
  • Basic researchers: connect findings to ultimate clinical applications.
  • Even pig artery inflammatory factor research connects to morbidity/mortality reduction.
  • Congress members excited about progress reducing economic burden.
  • Hit public health implications hard very early—often in first sentence.
  • Emphasize how study will address them regardless of research type.
12.23 Hypothesis Labeling Strategy
  • Primary hypotheses should be main points of study.
  • Question: What are implications of labeling one or other?
  • Reviewers look at power analyses—toward which outcome measures geared?
  • If hypothesis laid out, expect testing—have statistical power?
  • Primary vs. secondary distinction often follows follow-up period.
  • 8 weeks well-supported by previous studies.
  • 12 months no data on which to base prediction.
  • Don't power to analyses without basis.
12.24 Research Question Labeling
  • When to label aim as "Research Question"—controversial, no clear guide.
  • If goal relevant but not directly involved, label as Additional Research Question.
  • Subtext: probably not aiming to power this analysis—not strength of proposal.
  • May have sufficient sample size for testing if powered for main variables.
  • Wary of using "additional research questions."
  • Strategic reason: study meant to focus on clinical outcomes, not mechanisms.
  • Testing putative mechanisms important—absence raises reviewer questions.
  • Includes them even though story more complicated.
12.25 Mechanism Discussion Risk
  • Discussing mechanisms risks distracting from main point.
  • Needs details in relatively short space—may lose points rather than gain.
  • Judgment call, strategy decision.
  • Authors risked it and got funded—worth gamble but be careful.
12.26 Subtle Argument Techniques
  • Anticipate reviewer skepticism and address proactively.
  • For surprising effect sizes: "You are probably somewhat skeptical, but so were we."
  • Show awareness of larger picture—don't let reviewers think naive.
  • Talk to reviewers through language when issues need explaining.
  • Make excellent points that stimulate "hmmm, never thought of it" reaction.
12.27 Figure Usage
  • Figures not absolutely necessary but good to include if room allows.
  • Break up page, give eyes rest.
  • Authors added figure showing 2x2 study design and sample sizes per arm.
  • Good job explaining in text—anything to make easier for reviewers helps.
12.28 Controversial Point Handling
  • No clear guide on aims vs. hypotheses vs. research questions.
  • Talk to 10 experienced grant writers, get 10 opinions.
  • True even with several NIH program officers.
  • Look at 10 funded applications—done in 10 different ways.
  • Can do both if possible; otherwise stick with Specific Aims.
  • Keep focused, limit number (3 or 4).
12.29 Writing Rules Summary
  • Write from reader's perspective—get into their head.
  • Be linear—each point follows logically.
  • Keep it simple—don't veer off main point.
  • Tell a story—engage reader like screenplay.
  • Communicate excitement—sell the importance.
  • Words matter—evaluate every choice critically.
12.30 Final Submission Checklist
  • Address all five NIH review criteria explicitly.
  • Ensure story logical and easy to follow.
  • Check for jargon, undefined acronyms.
  • Verify all headings properly formatted.
  • Proofread multiple times by multiple people.
  • Confirm page limits met with proper formatting.
  • Check budget aligns with proposed work.
  • Ensure preliminary data supports feasibility if required.
  • Verify all required sections present and complete.
12.31 Success Factors
  • Great idea that excites reviewers immediately.
  • Demonstrated feasibility of proposed work.
  • Clear, logical writing that tells compelling story.
  • Appropriate scope—ambitious but realistic.
  • Strong preliminary data supporting approach.
  • Well-justified budget with adequate resources.
  • Addressed reviewer concerns proactively.
12.32 Common Pitfalls to Avoid
  • Insufficient detail in Approach section.
  • Overly ambitious aims exceeding budget/capability.
  • Jargon not defined for general audience.
  • Poor organization making story hard to follow.
  • Missing preliminary data when expected.
  • Underpowered sample size without justification.
  • Ignoring potential problems without mitigation plans.
12.33 Key Takeaways
  • Writing quality directly impacts funding success.
  • Reader perspective essential—reviewers are customers.
  • Story structure matters as much as content.
  • Prose must be clear, engaging, logically organized.
  • Every section should reinforce central narrative.
  • Revision is critical—don't submit first draft.
  • External feedback improves quality significantly.
