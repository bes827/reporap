Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy – Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol 103, No 2, 2026
  • Topic: Colonoscopic barotrauma and prevention
1. Barotrauma and Its Consequences
  • Least common cause of colonoscopic perforation (refs 1‑2).
  • Leading cause of malpractice claims in author’s experience (5/6 cases 2025).
  • Typical scenario: tight, angulated, narrowed sigmoid from severe diverticular disease.
  • Gas insufflation used → struggle to pass sigmoid; bowel prep often suboptimal.
Clinical Presentation & Immediate Risks
  • Large rupture perforations in rectosigmoid recognized during withdrawal.
  • Frequently accompanied by extensive peritoneal contamination, intraperitoneal gas → possible abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Rapid sepsis development; multiple mucosal breaks make endoscopic repair difficult.
Diagnosis & Acute Management
  • Recognized by astute endoscopist: diseased sigmoid + distended tympanitic abdomen on palpation/percussion.
  • Immediate actions: needle decompression of peritoneal cavity → ↓ intraperitoneal pressure, start antibiotics, emergent surgical consult.
  • Endoscopic repair often not feasible due to indistinct perforation site and poor prep.
2. Preventing Colonoscopic Barotrauma
  • Acute barotrauma occurs during procedure; delayed cases rare (proximal colon ischemia after release from recovery).
Risk Factors & Pathophysiology
  • Occurs almost exclusively in patients with narrowed, angulated sigmoid (severe diverticular disease).
  • Most patients with severe sigmoid disease do not develop barotrauma → but all barotrauma cases have diseased sigmoids.
  • Competent ileocecal valve + closed loop between sigmoid and valve prevents gas escape → ↑ intraluminal pressure.
Preventive Strategies
  • Stop gas insufflation upon entry into diseased sigmoid; switch to water‑filling (water does not cause barotrauma).
  • Indications for switching: need thinner instrument or difficulty negotiating angulated/narrow turns.
  • Carbon dioxide insufflation prevents delayed perforation but does not prevent acute barotrauma.
Additional Considerations
  • Brittle mucosa (e.g., collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) also raises risk; water‑filling recommended in such patients.
  • Expert witness perspective: fragile colon wall less predictive of malpractice than diseased sigmoid.
3. Conclusions and Recommendations (partial – continuation in subsequent runs)
  • Barotrauma perforations are rare but catastrophic; prevention outweighs treatment challenges.
  • “An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure” especially for barotrauma.
  • Colonoscopist should always consider risk when encountering angulated, narrowed, diseased sigmoid and promptly switch to water‑filling.
4. Conclusions and Recommendations
  • Barotrauma perforations rare but potentially disastrous; one event unacceptable.
  • Prevention is paramount: recognize diseased sigmoid every time colonoscope enters.
Core Preventive Principle
  • Switch to water‑filling immediately when:

1. Need thinner scope to traverse sigmoid.

2. Encounter struggle with angulated/narrowed turns.

Expected Outcomes of Water‑Filling
  • Eliminates gas pressure buildup → ↓ risk of closed‑loop formation.
  • No barotrauma despite severe diverticular disease or brittle mucosa.
Training & Dissemination
  • Educate trainees and partners on:
  • Identifying high‑risk sigmoid (tight, angulated, narrowed).
  • Promptly stopping gas insufflation → water infusion.
  • Immediate management steps if perforation occurs (needle decompression, antibiotics, surgery).
Institutional Policies Suggested
  • Protocol mandating gas‑to‑water switch checklist for diseased sigmoids.
  • Documentation of sigmoid assessment and insufflation method in procedure report.

7. Table 1 – Keys to Understanding & Preventing Colonoscopic Barotrauma (summarized)
  • Risk population: narrowed, angulated sigmoids—usually severe diverticular disease.
  • Incidence note: most severely diseased sigmoids do not develop barotrauma; however, all barotrauma cases have diseased sigmoids → maintain high suspicion.
  • CO₂ insufflation: prevents delayed perforation only; does not prevent acute intra‑procedure barotrauma.
  • Timing of injury: perforation typically occurs before scope reaches proximal colon → prevention essential.
  • Management challenge: perforation site often unidentifiable endoscopically; requires immediate needle decompression & surgical consult.
  • Prevention tactic: stop gas insufflation, switch to water‑filling only in severely diseased sigmoid segments.
8. Implications for Clinical Practice
  • Risk Assessment: Perform real‑time evaluation of sigmoid morphology (tightness, angulation, diverticular burden) before insufflation.
  • Insufflation Modality Decision Tree:
  • Start with CO₂ or air only if sigmoid appears normal.
  • Switch to water‑filling at first sign of resistance or when using a thinner scope.
  • Monitoring During Procedure:
  • Palpate abdomen intermittently for tympanitic tone.
  • Observe for sudden loss of insufflation pressure (possible leak).
Post‑Procedure Surveillance
  • In cases where gas was used in a diseased sigmoid, obtain immediate post‑procedure abdominal exam and consider plain radiograph to rule out occult perforation.
  • Early signs (tachycardia, abdominal pain, distension) → trigger emergent imaging and surgical consult.
9. Future Research & Quality Improvement
  • Prospective Registry: Capture all colonoscopies with documented sigmoid status, insufflation type, and outcomes to quantify barotrauma incidence accurately.
  • Device Innovation: Development of pressure‑sensing scopes that auto‑alert when intraluminal pressure exceeds safe thresholds (e.g., >15 mmHg).
  • Training Modules: Simulation‑based curricula focusing on water‑filling technique and rapid needle decompression skills.
Potential Study Designs
  • Randomized trial comparing standard gas insufflation vs. mandatory water‑filling in patients with known severe diverticular disease.
  • Cost‑effectiveness analysis of routine needle decompression kits availability in endoscopy suites.
10. Summary Checklist for Endoscopists

1. Identify high‑risk sigmoid (tight, angulated, diverticulosis).

2. Abort gas insufflation at first resistance; switch to water.

3. Use CO₂ only when confident sigmoid is low risk; remember it does not prevent acute barotrauma.

4. Continuously monitor abdomen for distension and tympany.

5. If perforation suspected:

  • Perform immediate needle decompression → ↓ intraperitoneal pressure.
  • Start broad‑spectrum antibiotics.
  • Call surgery emergently.
