Diagnosis of C. difficile Infection (CDI)
  • - Diagnosis is not always straightforward due to the high prevalence of asymptomatic colonization.
Diagnostic Challenges
  • - Differentiating active infection from colonization is a key challenge, especially in certain populations.
Colonization vs. Infection
  • - Asymptomatic colonization is common, especially in recently hospitalized patients or residents of long-term care facilities.

Post-Infection IBS
  • - Patients may have ongoing loose stools after CDI treatment due to post-infection IBS.
  • - C. difficile PCR may remain positive for a period after the infection has resolved, making it less reliable for confirming recurrence.

Chronic Diarrhea from Other Etiologies
  • - Patients with chronic diarrhea from other causes (e.g., IBD) might be colonized with C. difficile, complicating the diagnostic picture.


Testing Modalities
  • - No single test is perfect; each has distinct limitations and strengths.
Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests (NAAT/PCR)
  • - Detects the presence of a toxigenic (pathogenic) strain.
  • - Cannot differentiate whether toxin is actually being produced.
  • - High sensitivity led to its widespread adoption and a rise in 'CDI diagnoses' that may represent colonization.

Toxin Enzyme Immunoassays (EIA)
  • - Detects free toxin in the stool.
  • - More specific for actual infection but lacks sensitivity.
  • - Can miss up to 15% of true infections, especially early in the course.

Glutamate Dehydrogenase Assay (GDH)
  • - Used as a first-line screening assay as all C. difficile strains produce this enzyme.
  • - Cannot be a stand-alone test because non-toxigenic strains also produce GDH.
  • - Positive results require confirmatory testing.


Recommended Algorithmic Testing
  • - An algorithmic approach is recommended for the best diagnostic accuracy (See Figure 1).
Algorithm Steps
  • - Step 1: Start with a highly sensitive test (PCR or GDH).
  • - Step 2: If positive, reflex to the more specific EIA.

Interpretation of Results
  • - PCR/GDH Negative: No further testing or treatment is warranted.
  • - PCR/GDH Positive + EIA Positive: True, toxin-producing symptomatic CDI is likely.
  • - Discordant (PCR/GDH Positive + EIA Negative): The clinical picture is critical.
Managing Discordant Results
  • - One of the best clinical indicators of true infection is response to vancomycin.
  • - If symptoms persist without signs of severe disease (e.g., leukocytosis, megacolon), question the CDI diagnosis and explore other causes.
  • - Lower endoscopy with biopsies can be helpful; normal findings exclude CDI, and alternate diagnoses like microscopic colitis or IBD might be found.




Treatment of C. difficile Infection (CDI)
  • - Treatment must address two phases of the infection.
Phases of Infection
  • - Vegetative Phase: Releases toxins, causing symptoms. Best controlled with antimicrobials.
  • - Spore Phase: Associated with recurrence and transmission. Requires a healthy, diverse microbiota to be eradicated.

Antimicrobial Therapy
  • - Three main antimicrobials are used: metronidazole, vancomycin, and fidaxomicin.
Initial Infection
  • - Guidelines from ACG and IDSA/SHEA offer slightly different approaches.
ACG Guideline (Severity-Based)
  • - Triages patients based on severity criteria.
  • - Non-severe criteria: WBC count < 15,000/mL³ AND Creatinine < 1.5 mg/dL.
  • - Treatment Options for Non-Severe:
  • - Metronidazole 500 mg TID for 10 days
  • - Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID for 10 days
  • - Fidaxomicin 200 mg PO BID for 10 days

IDSA/SHEA Guideline (Cost-Agnostic)
  • - Preferred: Fidaxomicin 200 mg PO BID for 10 days.
  • - Acceptable Alternative: Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID for 10 days.
  • - Recommended only if others unavailable: Metronidazole 500 mg PO TID for 10 days.

Drug-Specific Considerations
  • - Metronidazole:
  • - Significant side effects (metallic taste, nausea) and high recurrence risk.
  • - Shown to be inferior in older patients, hospitalized patients, and those with multiple comorbidities.
  • - Has largely fallen out of favor for primary treatment.
  • - Vancomycin:
  • - Current 'go-to' treatment; inexpensive and effective.
  • - Initial treatment response is similar to fidaxomicin (85.8% in one trial).
  • - Associated with recurrence rates of ~25.3%.
  • - Fidaxomicin:
  • - Targeted spectrum of activity, preserving beneficial microbiota.
  • - Significantly lower recurrence rates (15.4% in pivotal trial).
  • - Main challenge has been cost, but a generic version was recently approved.


Fulminant Disease
  • - Definition: Hypotension, shock, ileus, and/or megacolon.
  • - Management: Requires ICU care and surgical consultation.
Antimicrobial Regimen
  • - Vancomycin 500 mg PO QID
  • - AND Metronidazole 500 mg IV TID
  • - If ileus is present: Add Vancomycin enema 500 mg QID.


Recurrent Infection (rCDI)
  • - Treatment strategies evolve with subsequent recurrences.
First Recurrence (2nd Episode)
  • - Fidaxomicin: Recommended if not used initially.
  • - Standard: 200 mg BID for 10 days.
  • - EXTEND regimen: 200 mg BID for 5 days, then one tablet every other day on days 7-25. This reduced 90-day recurrence by 11% over standard vancomycin.
  • - Vancomycin Taper-Pulse: An acceptable alternative.
  • - Regimen: 125 mg QID x 14 days ---> BID x 7 days ---> daily x 7 days ---> every 2nd day x 8 days ---> every 3rd day x 15 days (total duration 7 weeks).

Second or Further Recurrence (≥3rd Episode)
  • - Warrants adjunctive therapy targeting the spore phase.
  • - Treatment: Standard antimicrobial course (Vancomycin or Fidaxomicin) FOLLOWED BY microbiota restoration therapy.


Prophylaxis for Subsequent Antibiotic Needs
  • - For patients cured of rCDI who require another antimicrobial within the subsequent 6 months.
ACG Recommendation
  • - Conditionally recommends vancomycin prophylaxis with a low level of evidence.
  • - Dose: Oral vancomycin 125 mg once daily for the duration of the other antimicrobial, plus for 5 days thereafter.
  • - Rationale: Believed to be safe and potentially effective for patients at greatest risk for future recurrence (≥ 2 risk factors).



Risk Factors for Recurrence
  • - Age > 65
  • - Chronic proton pump inhibitor usage
  • - Immunocompromised status (e.g., CKD, Diabetes, Active chemo)
  • - Likely future concomitant antimicrobial usage
  • - Residence in a skilled nursing facility
  • - Severe underlying illness
  • - Significant inpatient hospital time

Microbiota Restoration Therapy
  • - Therapies designed to restore a healthy gut microbiota to prevent recurrence.
Conventional Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT)
  • - Highly effective for preventing rCDI but faces regulatory and access challenges.
Efficacy & Indications
  • - Efficacy: Meta-analysis showed rCDI prevention in 74.2% of FMT patients vs. 51.7% of controls (RR 1.59).
  • - NNT: The number needed to treat to prevent one infection was 3.
  • - Indication: Recommended after a 2nd recurrence (3rd episode) or earlier in high-risk patients.
  • - Timing: Administer after a standard 10-day anti-CDI course followed by a 48-72 hour washout period.

Administration Routes
  • - Colonoscopy: Allows for mucosal examination.
  • - Encapsulated (Oral): Preferred by most patients; cannot be used in those with dysphagia.
  • - Enema: Lower cost and avoids procedural risks but is less effective and may require repeat dosing.

Use in Special Populations
  • - Mild-to-Moderately Immunocompromised: Efficacy and safety appear comparable to immunocompetent patients.
  • - Severely Immunocompromised: High risk for life-threatening infection. Experts recommend delaying FMT in favor of extended antibiotic therapy until immune recovery.
  • - Severe/Fulminant CDI: Effective in hospitalized patients not responding to antibiotics. Meta-analysis of observational studies showed reduced mortality (RR 0.37), with an NNT of 4 to prevent one death.

Regulatory Status & Access Issues
  • - FDA regulates FMT as a biologic drug.
  • - A Nov 2022 FDA guidance stated that use of material from stool banks (like OpenBiome) is no longer permissible under 'enforcement discretion'.
  • - This has greatly impacted FMT use, especially for fulminant CDI.
  • - What is still permitted: Local/institutional stool banks and directed donor FMT.
Directed Donor FMT for Fulminant CDI
  • - Recommended if no response to standard antibiotics within 2-5 days.
  • - Donor Screening: Choose a healthy donor with no recent (90 days) antibiotic use and limited comorbidities. Conduct expeditious screening for viral/enteric infections (See Table 3).
  • - Protocol: Antibiotics are typically continued. Repeated FMTs (2 or 3) may be needed until clinical recovery (Fischer protocol).
  • - Contraindications: Bowel perforation, obstruction, or severe immunocompromise.



FDA-Approved Microbiota Therapies
  • - Commercially available, standardized products for preventing rCDI.
Fecal Microbiota, live-jslm (Rebyota®, RBL)
  • - Formulation: Broad consortium of spore and non-spore forming bacteria. Minimum of 1.5 x 10⁷ Bacteroides.
  • - Administration: Single 150 ml enema following antibiotic course and a 1-3 day washout period.
Efficacy & Safety
  • - PUNCH-CD3 Trial: 8-week response was 70.6% for RBL vs. 57.5% for placebo. Of responders, 92.1% remained responsive at 6 months.
  • - Open Label Study: Showed consistent safety and efficacy in patients with IBD, IBS, and mild-moderate immunocompromise.
  • - Indication: Patients at greatest risk for recurrence (≥ 2 risk factors with 1st recurrence, or anyone with ≥ 2nd recurrence).


Fecal Microbiota spores, live-brpk (Vowst®, VOS)
  • - Formulation: Defined consortium of Firmicutes spores isolated via an ethanol purification process.
  • - Administration: 4 capsules daily for 3 days following antibiotic course, a 2-4 day washout, and a bowel lavage.
Efficacy & Safety
  • - ECOSPOR III Trial: At 8 weeks, 88% of VOS patients remained responsive vs. 60% in the placebo group. 6-month sustained response was 79% vs 53%.
  • - Open Label Study: 8-week response was 91% overall, and 94% in those with a first recurrence.
  • - Indication: Patients at greatest risk for recurrence who are not severely immunocompromised and can swallow pills. Note: Patients with IBD, IBS, and immunocompromise were excluded from phase 3 trials.





Conclusion
  • - Diagnosis of CDI relies on testing algorithms (GDH/PCR screen with EIA confirmation) due to the lack of an ideal standalone test.
  • - Vancomycin and fidaxomicin are the mainstays of antibiotic therapy.
  • - Microbiota restoration therapy (RBL or VOS) is essential to prevent recurrence in high-risk patients.
  • - The future is promising, with a pipeline of new antibiotics and microbiota-based therapies in clinical trials.
