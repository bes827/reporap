Fundamentals of Ingestible Electronic Devices
  • - Ingestible electronic microsystems that capture and transmit biological data telemetrically, or perform interventional functions.
  • - Offer advantages of being convenient, minimally invasive, and allowing real-time assessment with minimal interference to normal physiology.
Core Components
  • - A typical ingestible device consists of several key microelectronic components housed within a biocompatible shell. (See Figure 1)
Sensors & Imaging Tools
  • - These components interact with the luminal microenvironment to measure biological information.
  • - Examples include: optical (camera), thermal, pH, pressure, electrochemical, and sonographic tools.

Power Source
  • - Must be sufficient for the device's operational time.
  • - Can be battery-operated or battery-free.
Battery-Operated
  • - Silver oxide batteries: Small, do not produce excessive current, but have a low power-to-weight ratio.
  • - Lithium batteries: Better performance, but generate heat and carry a risk of thermal runaway (risk of combustion).

Battery-Free
  • - Electromagnetic, piezoelectric (kinetic energy), and triboelectric (friction) sources have been investigated.
  • - Biofuel cells (e.g., glucose biofuel cell) generate energy via biochemical reactions, are biocompatible, but are expensive with short lifespans and low power output.


Data Collection & Transmission
  • - Data is typically sent to a wearable receiver via wide bandwidth telecommunication.
  • - A common frequency used is 433 MHz.
  • - Bluetooth® is an alternative transmission method.
  • - Some devices, like CapsoCam Plus, store data internally, requiring retrieval of the device post-excretion.

Capsule Housing (Cladding)
  • - An inert, biocompatible, pill-shaped shell resistant to acid, enzymes, and microbial action.
  • - Typical size is less than 13 mm in diameter and 34 mm in length to allow swallowing and minimize retention risk.

Software & Data Analysis
  • - Dedicated software allows for data review, which can be in real-time or post-study.
  • - Computer-assisted algorithms (using AI/machine learning) can reduce viewing times and assist diagnosis.


Capsule Locomotion in the GI Tract
  • - Movement of the device through the GI tract can be passive (uncontrolled) or active (controlled).
Passive Locomotion
  • - Influenced by gravity and peristalsis.
  • - Movement is variable and unpredictable, which can reduce diagnostic accuracy by missing lesions or transiting too quickly/slowly.

Active Locomotion
  • - Allows for more intentional and targeted movement.
  • - Experimental designs: include capsule legs, paddles, or inchworm-like maneuverability.
  • - External control: can be achieved using magnetic fields and/or external tethers.

Inhibition of Locomotion
  • - Enables the device to remain at a specific site for a prolonged period.
  • - Example: The Bravo™ pH capsule is endoscopically clipped to the distal oesophageal mucosa.



Landscape of Ingestible Sensors
  • - A wide spectrum of sensing tools have been developed, though few have reached routine clinical practice.
Imaging Sensors
  • - Tools for visualizing the GI tract, with capsule endoscopy being the most established.
Direct Imaging (Video Capsule Endoscopy)
  • - First FDA approval in 2001 (PillCam®).
  • - Now considered the gold standard for the work-up of obscure gastrointestinal bleeding, which accounts for ~2/3 of studies.
  • - Diagnostic yield is higher if performed early, within 48–72 hours of a bleeding event.
Commercial Systems
  • - PillCam (Medtronic): Iterations for oesophagus (ESO), small bowel (SB3), and colon (COLON2).
  • - EndoCapsule (Olympus)
  • - MiroCam (IntroMedic)
  • - CapsoCam Plus (CapsoVision)
  • - OMOM capsule (Jianshan Science and Technology)

Other Indications (ESGE)
  • - Suspected small bowel Crohn's disease (if no obstruction).
  • - Surveillance of polyposis syndromes.
  • - NSAID enteropathy (after a recommended minimum 4-week cessation).
  • - Graft-versus-host disease & specific scenarios in coeliac disease.

Advanced Systems
  • - OMOM capsule system: Features duplex data transmission for real-time monitoring and adjustment of image capture frequency.
  • - Magnetically-controlled endoscopy: Allows active manipulation using hand-held, MRI-based, or robotic magnets.


Optical Sensors
  • - Measure reflection, absorption, and scattering of light.
  • - Example: HemoPill, a telemetric bleeding sensor that detects blood by measuring the ratio of red and violet light.

Sonographic Transducers (USCE)
  • - Ultrasound Capsule Endoscopy performs transmural imaging of the GI tract wall.
  • - Uses high-frequency (>20 MHz) micro-ultrasound for high-resolution imaging.
  • - Potential for therapeutic use, such as ultrasound-mediated targeted drug delivery by transiently permeabilizing the mucosal membrane.

X-ray Sensors
  • - An ingested device emits low-dose X-ray beams (equivalent to one chest X-ray) to create a 3D reconstruction of the colon.
  • - Patient ingests a radiopaque contrast agent instead of bowel prep.
  • - May have a role in screening for patients unwilling to undergo colonoscopy.


Position & Orientation Sensors
  • - Used for tracking the device's location and movement within the GI tract.
Radiofrequency Identification (RFID)
  • - Basis for dissolvable patency capsules used to assess the risk of capsule retention.

Spatiotemporal Analysis
  • - Uses magnetic tracking to determine 3D spatial position and angular coordinates.
  • - Position coordinates ---> calculate transit time.
  • - Angular coordinates ---> surrogate for intestinal contraction frequency.
  • - Example: 3D transit electromagnetic system (Motilis Medica).

Accelerometer
  • - Detects motion by measuring the rate of change of movement.
  • - Used for localization (e.g., Atmo gas-sensing capsule) and measuring movement artifact.


Chemosensors (pH)
  • - pH assessment is used to localize the capsule and assess GI function.
Oesophageal pH Monitoring
  • - Bravo capsule: Endoscopically clipped to distal oesophagus to measure acid exposure for >36 hours.
  • - OMOM pH capsule: Monitors oesophageal pH for up to 96 hours.

SmartPill® WMC
  • - Approved by FDA in 2006 for evaluating gastric emptying and colonic transit times.
  • - Uses predictable pH changes for localization:
  • - Stomach ---> Duodenum (abrupt pH rise to 5.9–6.8)
  • - Ileum ---> Cecum (abrupt pH fall to 5.8–6.7)
  • - Production was announced to be terminated in June 2023.


Gas Sensors
  • - Gases are valuable biomarkers of gut function.
Atmo Gas-Sensing Capsule
  • - Measures concentrations of hydrogen (H2) and carbon dioxide (CO2), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs).
  • - Transmits data at a frequency of 434 MHz.
  • - Used to assess GI landmarks, transit, colonic fermentation, and potentially small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).


Microbiological-based Sensors
  • - Use genetically modified bacteria to detect specific analytes.
Ingestible Micro-Bio-Electronic Device (IMBED)
  • - Contains genetically modified probiotic E. coli Nissle 1917.
  • - Can detect biomarkers like heme (for bleeding), tetrathionate (for inflammation), and acyl-homoserine lactone (bacterial signature).


Pressure Sensors
  • - SmartPill WMC includes a pressure sensor with a measurement range of 0 to 350 mmHg.
  • - Used to assess GI tract contractile frequency.
  • - Interpretation is difficult due to artifact from body movement and respiration.

Temperature Sensors
  • - Common components used to assess core body temperature or as adjunctive sensors.
  • - Adjunctive use: Confirms ingestion (temperature rise) and excretion (temperature drop).


Interventional Functions of Ingestible Devices
  • - The next frontier for capsule technology is developing devices that can perform a function during transit.
Drug Adherence Monitoring
  • - A digital ingestion tracking system to monitor medication compliance.
  • - First system approved by FDA in 2017: Abilify MyCite (aripiprazole with an embedded sensor).
  • - The digital pill is activated in the stomach and communicates with a wearable patch.

Targeted Drug Delivery
  • - Offers a tool for delivering drugs to specific GI sites or for drugs unsuitable for oral delivery (e.g., peptides).
Release Mechanisms
  • - Passive release: Triggered by external signals (e.g., radiofrequency).
  • - Active release: Motor-controlled piston activated manually or automatically.
  • - Example: IntelliCap capsule uses pH and temperature sensors for real-time localization to allow targeted delivery.


Direct GI Tract Sampling
  • - An important but challenging goal for research and development.
  • - Major challenges include: obtaining a sufficient, non-contaminated sample and ensuring accurate localization.
  • - Despite many prototypes, none have provided key peer-reviewed data that move the concepts into clinical reality.

Vibration as Therapy
  • - Vibrating ingestible devices for the management of constipation.
Vibrant
  • - Received FDA approval for chronic idiopathic constipation in 2022.
  • - Treatment schedule: Daily device ingestion (between 9 and 10 pm), 5 days a week for 8 weeks.


Other Therapeutic Interventions (Experimental)
  • - Robotic electroceutical capsules: Deliver electrical stimulation to influence motility or the gut-brain axis.
  • - Intra-gastric balloon capsules: For obesity management.
  • - Origami robots: Unfold from a swallowed device to perform tasks like removing a swallowed battery or patching a wound.


Clinical Practice & Challenges
  • - Incorporating new technologies into established clinical algorithms requires multidisciplinary collaboration and overcoming several hurdles.
Advantages
  • - Safety: Can be used on repeat occasions for monitoring.
  • - Accessibility: Enhances access for remote patients.
  • - Convenience: Can be administered in an office setting.
  • - Unmodified Physiology: Data collected during normal daily activities.
  • - Longitudinal Data: Provides dynamic insight across multiple GI segments.

Key Challenges
  • - Data Validation: Lack of 'gold-standard' techniques for comparison makes interpretation difficult.
  • - Clinical Integration: Incorporating novel physiological information into established clinical algorithms is a major hurdle.
  • - Cost & Reimbursement: Demonstrating cost-effectiveness to payers is essential for gaining traction and funding.

Safety Considerations & Risks
  • - The clinician must balance the risks and benefits for each patient and device.
Capsule Retention
  • - Definition: Retention of the device in the GI tract for 2 weeks or more.
  • - Risk is minimal for small devices (<11 mm × 26 mm) in healthy individuals.
Reported Retention Rates
  • - WMC (SmartPill): 0.3%
  • - Capsule Endoscopy (Suspected Bleeding): ~2%
  • - Capsule Endoscopy (Established IBD): Up to 8.2%

Risk Assessment & Management
  • - A patency capsule (e.g., Agile Patency Capsule) can be used to assess retention risk.
  • - Management may be observational or require endoscopic/surgical retrieval.


Swallowing Difficulties & Aspiration
  • - Swallowing difficulties are uncommon (e.g., 1.5% for capsule endoscopy).
  • - Aspiration has occurred at a rate of approximately 1:1000 in capsule endoscopy studies, more common in older patients with dysphagia.
  • - Endoscopic placement is an option for patients with dysphagia.

Contraindications & High-Risk Scenarios (See Table 2)
  • - Known or suspected stricturing/stenosing disease (e.g., IBD, pyloric stenosis).
  • - History of bowel obstruction or complex abdominal surgery.
  • - Swallowing disorders (e.g., achalasia).
  • - Pregnancy (lack of safety data).



Conclusion & Future Directions
  • - Capsule endoscopy has successfully established a place in gastroenterology, revolutionizing small bowel imaging.
  • - Future advances will expand diagnostic and therapeutic roles, likely with multimodal, robotically-controlled devices.
  • - Artificial intelligence will be critical for machine learning and computer-aided diagnoses.
