AGA Clinical Practice Guideline on the Prevention and Treatment of Hepatitis B Virus Reactivation in At‑Risk Individuals
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2025
  • Topic: HBV reactivation risk management
  • Author(s): Faisal S. Ali et al.
Part 1 – Foundations & Methodology
1. Background & Aims
  • HBVr occurs after immune‑modulating exposures.
  • Antiviral prophylaxis can mitigate HBVr risk.
  • Monitoring alone may suffice for select low‑risk cases.
2. Guideline Development Process
  • Used GRADE framework to assess evidence.
  • Systematic review identified studies since 2014 version.
  • Evidence‑to‑Decision (EtD) considered benefits, harms, values, costs, equity.
3. Target Population & Scope
  • Frontline clinicians caring for at‑risk adults.
  • Includes patients on immunosuppressive drugs, biologics, and interventional therapies.
4. Evidence Review Highlights
  • Identified 12 RCTs comparing prophylaxis vs monitoring.
  • Pooled relative risk (RR) for HBVr with prophylaxis = 0.18 (95% CI 0.11–0.30).
  • Baseline HBVr risk stratified: low <1%, moderate 1‑10%, high >10%.
Part 2 – Risk Stratification & Recommendations
5. High‑Risk Exposures (baseline >10%)
  • Anthracycline derivatives in HBsAg‑positive patients → HIGH risk.
  • B‑cell depleting agents (rituximab, ofatumumab) → HIGH risk regardless serology.
  • Anti‑TNF agents in HBsAg‑positive individuals → HIGH risk.
6. Moderate‑Risk Exposures (1%–10%)
  • B‑cell depleting agents in HBsAg‑negative/anti‑HBc⁺ patients → MODERATE risk.
  • Anti‑IL6, cytokine/integrin inhibitors in HBsAg‑positive → HIGH; in anti‑HBc⁺ → MODERATE.
  • CAR‑T therapy in HBsAg‑negative/anti‑HBc⁺ patients → MODERATE risk.
7. Low‑Risk Exposures (<1%)
  • Methotrexate, azathioprine, 6‑MP monotherapy → baseline risk < 1%.
  • Intra‑articular steroid injections → low HBVr risk.
  • Anti‑TNF agents in HBsAg‑negative/anti‑HBc⁺ patients → LOW risk.
8. Evidence‑Based Recommendations Overview
  • Strong recommendation: antiviral prophylaxis for high‑risk individuals (moderate certainty).
  • Conditional recommendation: prophylaxis for moderate‑risk individuals (moderate certainty).
  • Conditional recommendation: monitoring alone for low‑risk individuals (moderate certainty).

Part 3 – Recommendations, Implementation & Clinical Implications
9. Recommendation 1 – High‑Risk Prophylaxis (Strong)
  • Antiviral prophylaxis favored over monitoring alone.
  • Initiate antivirals before risk‑imposing therapy start.
  • Continue ≥6 months after exposure cessation; B‑cell agents → at least 12 months.
10. Recommendation 2 – Moderate‑Risk Strategy (Conditional)
  • Conditional prophylaxis favored over monitoring alone.
  • Patients valuing avoiding long‑term antivirals may choose active monitoring.
  • Monitoring interval: 1‑to‑3 month visits, include HBV‑DNA & ALT assessments.
11. Recommendation 3 – Low‑Risk Management (Conditional)
  • Suggest monitoring alone for baseline risk < 1%.
  • Prophylaxis optional if patients prioritize avoiding reactivation over medication burden.
12. Recommendation 4 – Universal HBV Testing (Strong)
  • Test all at‑risk individuals per CDC guidance: HBsAg, anti‑HBs, total anti‑HBc.
  • No longer stratify screening by exposure magnitude.
  • Positive serology triggers baseline risk assessment & management algorithm.
