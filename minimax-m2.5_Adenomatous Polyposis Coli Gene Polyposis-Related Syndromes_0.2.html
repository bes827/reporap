Adenomatous Polyposis Coli Gene Polyposis-Related Syndromes
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology (AJG), 2026
  • Topic: APC Gene-Related Polyposis Syndromes (FAP, AFAP, GAPPS)
  • Author(s): Jennifer K. Maratt, MD, MS; Anneleise Frie, MD; Jennifer M. Weiss, MD, MS
1. Introduction & APC Gene Overview
  • APC (adenomatous polyposis coli) gene --> located on chromosome 5q21-22, functions as tumor suppressor
  • P/LP (pathogenic/likely pathogenic) germline variants --> loss of tumor suppressor function via B-catenin regulation impairment in Wnt signaling
  • Impairs cell migration, proliferation, and adhesion --> leads to GI polyposis syndromes (FAP/AFAP, GAPPS)
  • ~10-16% of colorectal cancers (CRC) and 1-3% of gastric cancers attributed to genetic predisposition
  • Up to 30% of APC variants arise de novo (during embryogenesis)
2. Diagnosis & Genetic Testing
  • Multigene panel testing recommended for individuals with ≥10 cumulative colorectal adenomas
  • Yield increases with polyp burden:
  • 10-19 adenomas: 5% APC variant prevalence (95% CI 3-5)
  • 20-99 adenomas: 10% (95% CI 9-11)
  • 100-999 adenomas: 56% (95% CI 54-59)
  • >1000 adenomas: 80% (95% CI 71-87)
  • Odds of APC variant increase with younger age at adenoma diagnosis:
  • Age 40-49: OR 2.7 (95% CI 2.2-3.4)
  • Age 30-39: OR 6.1 (95% CI 4.8-7.8)
  • Age <30: OR 15.4 (95% CI 12.2-19.5)
  • Positive family history warrants testing for first-degree relatives (FDR)
3. Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP): Epidemiology
  • Autosomal dominant inheritance pattern
  • Equal prevalence in men and women
  • Manifestations appear in adolescent/young adult years
  • Scandinavian birth prevalence: 1:10,000 to 1:31,260
  • Incidence rate: 0.62 to 2.38 per million
  • Phenotype variability due to germline variant differences, mosaicism, modifier genes, environmental factors
4. FAP: Diagnostic Criteria & Clinical Features
4.1 Diagnostic Thresholds
  • Classic FAP: ≥100 cumulative colorectal adenomas
  • AFAP (Attenuated FAP): <100 cumulative colorectal adenomas
  • Additional triggers: CRC at age <50 years, FDR with polyposis/CRC
4.2 Upper GI Manifestations
  • Fundic gland polyps (FGP): Most common gastric neoplasia, prevalence 20-88%
  • ~25% of FAP individuals have FGP with low-grade dysplasia
  • Duodenal adenomas: ~50% arise at or surrounding ampulla
  • Spigelman staging system guides management (number, size, histology, dysplasia)
4.3 Colorectal Cancer Risk
  • Classic FAP: ~100% lifetime CRC risk without intervention
  • AFAP: 70% lifetime CRC risk
  • Colonoscopy starting age 10-15 years, repeated every 1-2 years based on polyp burden
4.4 Genotype-Phenotype Correlations
  • Codons 157-1595: Classic FAP phenotype
  • 5' end (codons 1-233), exon 9 alternative spliced region, distal 3' end: AFAP phenotype
  • Codon 1309 variants: Higher polyp burden, early cancer development
  • 5' region of codon 160 and 3' region of codon 1450: Later onset, fewer polyps
5. FAP: Management of Upper GI Tract Polyposis
  • Goal: Early cancer detection and prevention via downstaging duodenal/papillary disease
5.1 Spigelman Staging System
  • Classifies duodenal polyps based on: number, size, histology, dysplasia
  • Translates to surveillance/management recommendations
  • Recent meta-analysis provides pooled duodenal cancer risk estimates per stage
  • Limitations: components don't uniformly predict risk, lacks papilla-specific variables
5.2 Duodenal Adenoma Management
  • Endoscopic surveillance intervals based on Spigelman stage
  • If endoscopic downstaging achieved --> continue monitoring based on highest previous stage
  • Surgical management indications:
  • Spigelman Stage III with high-grade dysplasia (unmanageable endoscopically)
  • Spigelman Stage IV
  • Duodenal or ampullary cancer
5.3 Gastric Polyposis Management
  • Most common: proximal polyposis with fundic gland polyps ± low-grade dysplasia
  • Rising gastric adenocarcinoma incidence in Western countries: 1.3-5% of FAP/AFAP
  • Precursor lesions: gastric adenomas, FGP with high-grade dysplasia
  • Endoscopic features associated with ↑ proximal gastric cancer risk:
  • "Carpeting" of gastric polyps
  • Solitary polyps >20mm
  • Well-circumscribed large white mucosal patch overlying polyposis
  • Polypoid mounds ≥2cm in width and depth
5.4 Gastrectomy Indications
  • Intramucosal or invasive cancer
  • Polypoid mounds with high-grade dysplasia
  • Endoscopically unresectable lesions with high-grade dysplasia
  • Multi-focal high-grade dysplasia
6. FAP: Management of Lower GI Tract Polyposis
6.1 Colonoscopic Surveillance
  • Start between ages 10-15 years
  • Continue every 1-2 years based on polyp burden (classic FAP vs AFAP)
  • Document estimated number of polyps (remaining and removed) and rectal polyp burden
  • Informs timing and type of surgery over time
6.2 Surgical Indications
  • Polyps no longer endoscopically manageable (dense polyposis or advanced features)
  • Advanced polyp features: size >10mm, villous components, high-grade dysplasia
  • Symptoms related to polyp burden (e.g., rectal bleeding)
  • Presence of CRC
6.3 Surgical Approaches
Total colectomy with ileal pouch anal anastomosis (IPAA)
  • Advantages: Bowel continuity maintained, reduced rectal cancer risk
  • Disadvantages: May require 2 stages with temporary ostomy, ↑ risk of bowel/bladder incontinence, sexual dysfunction, intraabdominal desmoid tumors, reduced fecundity/fertility
Total colectomy with ileorectal anastomosis (IRA)
  • Advantages: Bowel continuity maintained, reduced need for pelvic dissection, more predictable bowel function, less impact on fecundity/fertility
  • Disadvantages: Risk of rectal polyposis progression and rectal cancer, potential need for future proctectomy
Total proctocolectomy with end ileostomy
  • Advantages: Eliminates colon and rectal neoplasia risk, preferred for those with difficulty adhering to surveillance
  • Disadvantages: Lack of bowel continuity/stoma impacts QoL, risk of bowel/bladder dysfunction and sexual dysfunction, risk of infecundity/infertility
6.4 Post-Operative Surveillance
  • Anal transition zone/rectal cuff (IRA or IPAA): annual colonoscopy
  • Stoma/ileum (end ileostomy): every 3 years with visual inspection, or sooner based on polyp burden/features
7. Extraintestinal Manifestations
7.1 Congenital Hypertrophy of Retinal Pigment Epithelium (CHRPE)
  • Prevalence: 90% in FAP
  • Most common and earliest extraintestinal manifestation, may be present at birth
  • Benign, discrete flat pigmented or hypopigmented lesions on retina
  • Multifocal/bilateral more common in FAP
7.2 Desmoid Tumors
  • Prevalence: 10-20%
  • Histologically benign tumors from fibroblasts, potentially locally invasive
  • Third most common cause of mortality in FAP (after CRC and duodenal cancer)
  • Most commonly arise in abdominal wall, can occur in head, neck, extremities
  • High recurrence rate
  • Risk factors: APC P/LP variants in 3' region, family history of desmoid disease, abdominal surgery
  • Median time to development after surgery: 28.8-36 months
  • Higher risk after IPAA than IRA
7.3 Thyroid Cancer
  • Prevalence: 2-16%
  • Papillary thyroid cancer, cribriform-morular variant more common in FAP
  • NCCN recommendation: baseline thyroid ultrasound in late teenage years, repeat every 2-5 years if normal
7.4 Central Nervous System Tumors
  • Prevalence: <1%
  • Medulloblastoma most common, highest incidence before age 20
  • Rare: astrocytomas, ependymomas, pituitary adenomas
7.5 Dental Features
  • Prevalence: 30-75%
  • Supernumerary teeth, congenital absence, unerupted/impacted teeth, dentigerous cysts, odontomas
  • Present ~10 years prior to onset of colorectal polyposis
7.6 Osteomas
  • Prevalence: 60-85%
  • Benign bony growths, most commonly on frontal bones or mandible
  • Can affect maxilla or long bones
  • Present prior to onset of colorectal polyposis
7.7 Dermatologic Manifestations
  • Prevalence: 50-65%
  • Epidermoid cysts, fibromas, lipomas, pilomatricomas
  • Benign, can occur on any body part including face
7.8 Hepatoblastoma
  • Prevalence: 0.42-2.5%
  • Risk highest in first 5 years of life
  • Some guidelines recommend abdominal ultrasound with serum AFP every 3-6 months until age 5
7.9 Adrenal Neoplasms
  • Prevalence: ~15%
  • Adrenal adenoma most common, usually benign and incidental
7.10 Pancreatic Cancer Risk
  • Recent Danish Polyposis Register study: FAP individuals had significantly ↑ risk vs controls (HR 6.45; 95% CI 2.02-20.64)
  • AFAP vs controls: No significantly increased risk (HR 3.19; 95% CI 0.61-16.42)
  • Classic FAP: HR 7.66 (95% CI 1.67-35.26)
  • Most pancreatic cancers were adenocarcinomas
8. Chemoprevention
8.1 NSAIDs - Sulindac
  • Sulindac 150mg twice daily: Most convincing data for colorectal polyp regression, may allow delay in surgery
  • 4-month study: 6/10 patients had complete regression, 3 had almost complete regression
  • 9-month study: 44% decrease in polyp number from baseline (p=0.014), size decreased to 35% of baseline (p<0.001)
  • Does not replace surgical management
  • NCCN: "not known if decrease in colorectal polyp burden decreases cancer risk"
8.2 NSAIDs - Celecoxib
  • Celecoxib 400mg twice daily: 28% reduction in mean polyp number vs 4.5% placebo (p=0.003), 30.7% reduction in polyp burden vs 4.9% placebo (p=0.001)
9. Family Planning & Fertility Considerations
9.1 Impact on Fertility and Fecundity
  • Following colectomy, fertility (ability to get pregnant) and fecundity (time to achieving pregnancy) significantly affected
  • Three nationwide studies: fertility rate of 8% after IPAA or IRA vs 18.3% Japan national average
  • Infertility rate: 10-17% vs Netherlands estimated 10%
  • Meta-analyses: baseline infertility rates of 13-20% prior to IPAA rose to 48-63% after surgery
  • 2003 Scandinavian survey (230 women with FAP): fecundability ratios dropped from 1.05 before surgery to 0.46 after IPAA
  • Causes: anatomic changes from pelvic surgery → uterine retroflexion, pelvic adhesions, hydrosalpinx, fimbrial damage, tubal obstruction
9.2 Pregnancy Complications
  • 30% increased rate of C-section delivery
  • Indications: surgical scarring of perineum, prematurity, placenta previa, fear of destruction of anastomosis
9.3 Additional Considerations for CRC Diagnosis
  • Surgical planes below peritoneal reflection, chemotherapy (alkylating agents, platinum compounds like oxaliplatin, 5-FU with leucovorin), pelvic radiation --> deleterious effects on fertility
  • Family planning and infertility education should be considered early, whether for CRC treatment or risk-reducing surgery
9.4 Fertility Preservation Options
  • Prior to risk-reducing surgery: delay surgery (less favorable given cancer risk), oophoropexy, anti-adhesion products during surgery
  • In setting of CRC: GnRH analogs during chemotherapy to preserve primordial follicles, ovarian transposition away from radiation field
  • Assistive reproductive services: in vitro fertilization
  • Recommendations: fertility counseling prior to risk-reducing surgery or at time of CRC diagnosis, prior to treatment
9.5 Pre-implantation Genetic Testing (PGT)
  • 10-82% of couples cite fear of transmitting variant as reason to avoid having children
  • Prenatal testing options: chorionic villous sampling (10-12 weeks), amniocentesis (16-20 weeks)
  • Cell-free DNA testing from maternal serum (8-9 weeks) effective for hereditary colon cancer diagnosis
  • Ethical debates persist regarding pregnancy termination if fetus carries pathogenic variant
PGT for FAP
  • First attempt in 1998, multiple successful pregnancies reported since
  • European best practice guidelines exist; ACOG and ASRM outline use for monogenic disorders
  • Adverse outcomes: low birth weight (<5lbs 8oz), preterm delivery, hypertensive disorders of pregnancy
  • Congenital malformations, severely low birth weight (<2lbs 3oz), pre-preterm delivery occurred at low rates
  • Outcomes unclear if associated with PGT or confounding factors (underlying fertility status, patient characteristics)
  • Psychosocial outcomes: feelings of regret, difficulty making reproductive decisions observed
  • Survey studies: 43-90% would consider using PGT, 30% expressed positive attitudes regardless of family planning stage
10. GAPPS: Gastric Adenocarcinoma and Proximal Polyposis of the Stomach
10.1 Epidemiology
  • Rare autosomal dominant syndrome, first described by Worthley et al in 2012
  • True prevalence yet to be determined
  • Germline single nucleotide pathogenic variants affect APC promoter 1B region (distinct from large deletions in FAP)
10.2 Clinical Manifestations
  • Gastric, rather than duodenal or colorectal polyposis
  • No extraintestinal manifestations (unlike FAP)
  • Polyp distribution: gastric body and fundus, antrum spared
  • Within families: variable gastric polyp burden
  • Lifetime gastric adenocarcinoma risk: up to 25% (variable presentation)
10.3 Diagnostic Criteria (Worthley et al)

1. Gastric polyps restricted to body and fundus

2. No evidence of duodenal or colorectal polyposis

3. >100 polyps carpeting proximal stomach on index exam OR >30 polyps in FDR

4. Predominantly FGPs with some dysplasia (or family member with dysplastic FGPs or gastric adenocarcinoma)

10.4 Management
  • Evidence for precise timing/interval for gastric cancer screening unclear
  • Recent systematic review (12 studies, 113 patients): gastric cancer in 31 patients, median age 48 years (range 19-75)
  • Considerable phenotypic variation within and between families
  • Current guidance: annual upper endoscopies starting at age 15 years (limited evidence strength)
  • Colonoscopy starting at 25 years or earlier based on family history of colorectal neoplasia
  • Risk-reducing gastrectomy consideration: ~third decade, depends on high-risk features (dense polyposis limiting surveillance, dysplasia), patient health status, preferences, family history of gastric cancer
11. Additional Variations in APC Genotype and Phenotype
11.1 APC Mosaicism
  • Up to 80-90% of FAP cases arise from identifiable P/LP germline APC variant
  • Negative multigene panel testing should not exclude FAP possibility
  • Phenotype suggestive of FAP but negative molecular testing → may be due to mosaicism in up to 25% of individuals
  • Somatic mosaicism: new mutations during postzygotic phase → mosaic of wild-type and mutated cells
  • Disease phenotype based on organ where mutated cells cluster
Detection Methods
  • Testing leukocyte DNA from relatives of probands for same de novo APC variant
  • Protein truncation test
  • Denaturing high-performance liquid chromatography for variants with small allele frequencies
  • Next-generation and whole-exome sequencing increased detection likelihood, but also ↑ false positives
Testing Strategy
  • If no variants found at allele frequency threshold <5% in germline testing (suggesting variants not in hematopoietic cells), test tissue from affected organ (normal colorectal epithelium and/or adenomas)
11.2 APC I1307K Variant
  • Located in β-catenin binding domain
  • Unlike other APC PGVs: does not disrupt Wnt signaling, classified as low-penetrance pathogenic variant
  • Single nucleotide exchange (c.3920T>A): isoleucine substituted for lysine at codon 1307
  • Protein structure remains relatively intact but prone to DNA polymerase errors
Epidemiology
  • Highest frequency among individuals of Ashkenazi Jewish (AJ) descent
  • Prevalence: 6-10% in AJ population, 15-28% in AJ individuals with family history of CRC
Cancer Risk
  • Increased CRC risk, mean age of diagnosis ~60-70 years
  • Does not lead to polyposis classically seen with other APC PGVs
Screening Recommendations
  • Individuals with APC I1307K variant and AJ origin: start colonoscopy at age 40 or 10 years prior to FDR's CRC diagnosis (whichever earlier), repeat every 5 years
  • No evidence for increased CRC risk among non-AJ individuals with variant
  • Insufficient data to support increased extracolonic cancer risk in AJ or non-AJ carriers
  • Further studies needed to elucidate intestinal/extraintestinal manifestations among AJ and non-AJ individuals
12. Practical Considerations & Conclusions
12.1 Multidisciplinary Care
  • Optimal management requires longitudinal care to understand intestinal/extraintestinal disease burden over time
  • Ongoing relationship ensures benefits/limitations of screening/surveillance discussed, timing of interventions (including surgery) considered
  • Multidisciplinary team needed: pediatric and adult gastroenterologists, psychologists, genetic counselors, social workers, surgeons
12.2 Transitions of Care
  • Early onset of intestinal/extraintestinal manifestations in childhood/adolescent years → transitions from pediatric to adult providers inevitable
  • Transitions should avoid coinciding with major life events (e.g., surgery)
  • Children/adolescents should be involved in medical decision-making to ensure diagnosis competence
  • Assess readiness for transitions
