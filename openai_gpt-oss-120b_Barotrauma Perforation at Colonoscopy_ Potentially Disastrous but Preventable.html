Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol 103, No 2, 2026
  • Topic: Colonoscopic barotrauma and prevention strategies
  • Author: Douglas K. Rex, MD
1. Barotrauma Overview & Epidemiology
  • Least common cause of colonoscopic perforation in published series¹˒².
  • Leading cause of malpractice claims related to perforation (5/6 cases 2025).
  • Typical patient profile: tight, angulated, narrowed sigmoid from severe diverticular disease.
  • Risk amplified by gas insufflation vs water filling; poor bowel prep worsens lumen narrowing.
2. Clinical Presentation & Immediate Consequences
  • Rupture perforations in rectosigmoid often large → recognized on withdrawal → endoscopic repair or prompt surgery with limited contamination.
  • Barotrauma → extensive immediate peritoneal contamination, large intraperitoneal gas volumes → risk of abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Rapid sepsis development; multiple mucosal breaks obscure exact perforation site.
  • Diagnosis cues: diseased sigmoid + distended tympanitic abdomen on palpation/percussion.
3. Acute Management of Barotrauma Perforation
  • Immediate needle decompression of peritoneal cavity.
  • Prompt initiation of broad‑spectrum antibiotics.
  • Urgent surgical consultation; endoscopic repair usually not feasible.
  • Critical need for rapid recognition—many endoscopists lack prior experience with this scenario.
4. Prevention Strategies (Key Principles)
  • Acute barotrauma occurs during colonoscopy; delayed perforation rare, linked to retained gas post‑procedure.
  • Carbon dioxide insufflation prevents delayed perforation but does not prevent acute injury.
  • Core preventive concept: recognize diseased sigmoid → stop gas insufflation → switch to water filling.
  • Indications for switching: need thinner scope or difficulty navigating angulated/narrowed turns.
  • Additional risk groups: brittle mucosa (collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) → water filling advisable.
5. Risk Factors & Pathophysiological Mechanism
  • Diseased sigmoid colon → narrowed lumen, angulation, diverticular strictures.
  • Competent ileocecal valve prevents retrograde gas escape → closed‑loop between sigmoid and valve.
  • Closed loop → rapid intraluminal pressure rise → transmural rupture (barotrauma).
  • Additional risk modifiers:
  • Suboptimal bowel preparation → poor visualization, increased insufflation attempts.
  • Brittle mucosa (collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) → reduced wall tensile strength.
  • Use of standard air insufflation rather than CO₂ or water.
Closed‑Loop Dynamics
  • Gas insufflated cannot exit proximally (ileocecal valve competent).
  • Cannot exit distally (sigmoid obstruction).
  • Pressure ↑ sharply → ischemia → perforation site often multiple micro‑tears, hard to locate.
6. Evidence Base & Data Summary
  • Incidence: Barotrauma < 1% of all colonoscopic perforations (series 1,2).
  • Malpractice data: 5/6 negligence cases 2025 attributed to barotrauma (author’s expert witness experience).
  • Outcomes:
  • Immediate peritoneal contamination in > 80% of barotrauma cases.
  • Abdominal compartment syndrome reported when large gas volumes retained.
  • Mortality higher than therapeutic perforations due to delayed recognition and sepsis.

7. Clinical Recommendations & Practical Algorithm
  • Pre‑procedure assessment:
  • Review imaging/history for severe diverticular disease or known strictures.
  • Anticipate difficult sigmoid navigation → plan for water‑based insufflation.
  • During insertion:
  • If resistance encountered at sigmoid turn → consider thinner scope or immediate switch to water filling.
  • Monitor abdominal distension; palpate for tympany after each insufflation cycle.
  • If barotrauma suspected:
  • Stop procedure immediately.
  • Perform needle decompression of peritoneal cavity (large‑bore trocar).
  • Administer IV broad‑spectrum antibiotics (e.g., piperacillin‑tazobactam).
  • Call surgical team; prepare for emergent laparotomy or minimally invasive repair.
  • Post‑procedure:
  • Observe for signs of delayed perforation if patient reports inability to pass flatus after discharge.
  • Consider CO₂ insufflation in routine cases to reduce retained gas volume, though not protective against acute injury.
8. Conclusions & Take‑Home Messages
  • Barotrauma perforations are rare but carry disproportionate morbidity and legal risk.
  • Prevention hinges on early recognition of a diseased sigmoid and immediate transition to water filling.
  • Education of trainees and dissemination of the algorithm are essential to reduce incidence.
  • Even a single preventable barotrauma event justifies systematic adoption of these safeguards.
