Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol 103, No 2, 2026
  • Topic: Colonoscopic barotrauma and prevention strategies
  • Author: Douglas K. Rex, MD
1. Barotrauma Overview & Epidemiology
  • Least common cause of colonoscopic perforation in series¹˒².
  • Leading malpractice claim driver for perforations (5/6 cases 2025).
  • Typical scenario: tight, angulated, narrowed sigmoid from severe diverticular disease.
  • Gas insufflation used; water filling avoided → struggle to advance scope.
  • Poor bowel prep often co‑exists, worsening visualization and increasing risk.
2. Clinical Consequences of Barotrauma Perforation
  • Rupture in rectosigmoid usually large, recognized on withdrawal → endoscopic repair or prompt surgery.
  • Immediate extensive peritoneal contamination common, especially with poor prep.
  • Large intraperitoneal gas volumes can trigger abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Rapid sepsis development; multiple mucosal tears obscure exact perforation site.
  • Diagnosis hinges on: diseased sigmoid risk factor + palpation/percussion of tympanitic abdomen.
3. Acute Management of Barotrauma Perforation
  • Immediate needle decompression of peritoneal cavity required.
  • Prompt broad‑spectrum antibiotics initiation.
  • Urgent surgical consultation; endoscopic repair rarely feasible due to multiple breaks.
  • Endoscopist must act swiftly; lack of prior experience can delay critical interventions.
4. Prevention Strategies for Colonoscopic Barotrauma
  • Acute barotrauma occurs during the procedure; delayed perforation rare (proximal colon ischemia after release).
  • Carbon dioxide insufflation prevents delayed barotrauma but not acute events.
  • Core preventive principle: recognize diseased sigmoid → stop gas insufflation, switch to water filling.
  • Indications for water‑filling switch: need thinner instrument or difficulty navigating angulated/narrowed turns.
  • Additional risk groups: brittle mucosa (collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) – water filling advisable.
5. Risk Factors & Pathophysiology
  • Diseased sigmoid colon (severe diverticular disease) present in ≈100 % of barotrauma cases.
  • Most patients with severe diverticulosis do not develop barotrauma → high specificity, low sensitivity.
  • Competent ileocecal valve + closed loop between sigmoid and valve creates trapped gas → rapid pressure rise.
  • Brittle mucosa (collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) predisposes to wall rupture under pressure.
  • Suboptimal bowel preparation amplifies risk by limiting visual cues and increasing required insufflation force.
Closed‑Loop Mechanism
  • Gas cannot escape retrograde through anus AND cannot pass ileocecal valve → intraluminal pressure ↑ sharply.
  • Pressure gradient exceeds colonic wall tensile strength → transmural tear, often multiple mucosal breaks.
6. Preventive Measures & Procedural Recommendations
  • Immediate recognition of diseased sigmoid → stop air/CO₂ insufflation.
  • Switch to water filling of lumen; water does not generate barotrauma pressure.
  • Consider thinner endoscope when encountering angulation or narrowing.
Decision Algorithm for Switching to Water Filling

1. Encountered tight, angulated sigmoid AND difficulty advancing scope → stop gas, start water.

2. Need to change to a thinner instrument to traverse sigmoid → water fill before exchange.

3. Visible poor preparation compounding visualization → water fill to improve mucosal view and reduce insufflation pressure.

Insufflation Gas Choice
  • Carbon dioxide: rapid absorption, prevents delayed perforation; does not prevent acute barotrauma.
  • Air/room air: higher risk of retained gas volume → greater acute pressure spikes.
7. Clinical Recommendations & Education
  • Emphasize “ounce of prevention” principle in training curricula.
  • Teach trainees to palpate abdomen for tympany during insertion; early detection of distension.
  • Incorporate needle decompression drills into emergency response simulations.
  • Disseminate Table 1 key points (see below) to all endoscopy units.
Table 1 – Keys to Understanding & Preventing Colonoscopic Barotrauma
  • Occurs almost exclusively in narrowed, angulated sigmoids from severe diverticular disease.
  • Most severely diseased sigmoids do not develop barotrauma → maintain high index of suspicion each entry.
  • CO₂ insufflation does not prevent acute injury; only mitigates delayed perforation risk.
  • By the time perforation is evident, injury has already occurred → prevention paramount.
  • Perforation site often unidentifiable endoscopically; requires immediate needle decompression & surgical consult.
  • Prevention achieved by halting gas insufflation and switching to water filling in any severely diseased sigmoid.
8. Conclusions & Final Recommendations
  • Barotrauma perforations are rare but carry disproportionate morbidity (sepsis, compartment syndrome).
  • Preventable through vigilant recognition of at‑risk anatomy and immediate transition to water filling.
  • Institutional protocols should mandate stop‑gas → water rule for any angulated/narrowed sigmoid encounter.
  • Ongoing education of fellows, attending physicians, and nursing staff essential to reduce malpractice claims.

11. Summary of Practical Take‑Home Points
  • Identify diseased sigmoid early → stop gas insufflation.
  • Switch to water filling immediately when encountering angulation/narrowing.
  • Use CO₂ only for delayed perforation mitigation; does not replace water strategy for acute prevention.
  • Perform needle decompression & start antibiotics at first sign of peritoneal distension.
  • Educate all endoscopy personnel on closed‑loop pressure dynamics and emergency response.
