Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol 103, No 2, 2026
  • Topic: Colonoscopic barotrauma – incidence, risk factors, outcomes, prevention
1. Epidemiology & Legal Impact
  • Barotrauma = least common cause of colonoscopic perforation (refs 1‑2).
  • Leading cause of malpractice claims related to perforation (expert witness experience).
  • First 4 months 2025: 6 negligence cases reviewed → 5 due to barotrauma.
2. Pathophysiology & Clinical Presentation
2.1 Typical Patient Profile
  • Tight, angulated, narrowed sigmoid colon – usually severe diverticular disease.
  • Suboptimal bowel prep; poor visualization after sigmoid passage.
2.2 Mechanism of Injury
  • Gas insufflation → closed‑loop between diseased sigmoid and competent ileocecal valve.
  • Retrograde gas cannot escape → rapid intraluminal pressure rise → rupture.
  • Acute perforation occurs during scope insertion; delayed perforation rare (requires retained gas, proximal ischemia).
2.3 Immediate Clinical Signs
  • Large rectosigmoid rupture recognized on withdrawal.
  • Extensive peritoneal contamination, massive intraperitoneal gas → possible abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Rapid sepsis development.
  • Diagnosis by:
  • Palpation/percussion of distended, tympanitic abdomen.
  • Recognition of diseased sigmoid risk factor.
2.4 Management Challenges
  • Multiple mucosal tears → perforation site often unidentified.
  • Endoscopic repair usually infeasible.
  • Required actions:
  • Immediate needle decompression of peritoneal cavity.
  • Prompt IV antibiotics.
  • Emergent surgical consultation.
3. Prevention Strategies
3.1 General Principles
  • Barotrauma occurs almost exclusively in severely diseased sigmoids; most such patients do not develop it, but risk remains high.
  • Competent ileocecal valve + closed loop = highest risk scenario.
3.2 Insufflation Technique
  • Carbon‑dioxide insufflation → rapid absorption; prevents delayed barotrauma only, not acute injury.
  • Switch from gas to water filling immediately upon encountering:
  • Angulated, narrowed sigmoid.
  • Need for thinner instrument or difficulty negotiating turns.
3.3 Water‑Filling Protocol
  • Water does not generate pressure → eliminates barotrauma risk.
  • Indicated also in patients with brittle mucosa (e.g., collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis).
3.4 Operator Awareness & Training
  • Recognize diseased sigmoid entry as a “red flag.”
  • Educate trainees to stop gas insufflation and initiate water filling without delay.
4. Outcomes & Recommendations
  • Barotrauma perforations are rare but carry severe morbidity (large contamination, rapid sepsis, possible abdominal compartment syndrome).
  • Prevention is paramount: “an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure.”
  • Recommended practice:
  • Always assess sigmoid anatomy before insufflation.
  • Transition to water filling at first sign of angulation/narrowing.
  • Disseminate preventive protocol to all endoscopy staff and trainees.

Key Preventive Checklist (Table 1 Summary)

  • Occurs almost exclusively in narrowed, angulated sigmoids (severe diverticular disease).
  • Most patients with such sigmoids do not develop barotrauma → maintain high index of suspicion each time.
  • CO₂ insufflation ≠ protection against acute injury.
  • Perforation usually identified only after scope reaches proximal colon → prevention = everything.
  • Site often unidentifiable; immediate needle decompression + surgical consult required.
  • Stop gas, switch to water filling in every severely diseased sigmoid colon.

7. Conclusions
  • Barotrauma perforations are rare but carry high morbidity (large contamination, rapid sepsis, possible abdominal compartment syndrome).
  • Preventable when endoscopist recognizes diseased sigmoid and switches to water filling.
  • Even a single event is unacceptable given the severity and preventability.
8. Practical Recommendations for Endoscopy Units
8.1 Pre‑Procedure Assessment
  • Review prior imaging or reports for known severe diverticular disease or sigmoid angulation.
  • Ensure optimal bowel preparation; poor prep ↑ risk of missed recognition & contamination.
8.2 Intra‑Procedural Decision Algorithm

1. Enter sigmoid → assess lumen:

  • If tight/angulated/narrowed → stop gas insufflation.
  • Consider thinner scope or variable‑stiffness colonoscope.

2. Switch to water filling (saline or tap water).

3. Monitor intraluminal pressure visually; avoid overdistension.

8.3 Emergency Management Protocol
  • Immediate needle decompression of peritoneal cavity if perforation suspected.
  • Start broad‑spectrum IV antibiotics within minutes.
  • Call surgical team for urgent evaluation; prepare for possible laparotomy.
8.4 Training & Quality Assurance
  • Incorporate barotrauma risk recognition into fellowship curricula and continuing medical education.
  • Perform periodic audits of colonoscopy complications focusing on insufflation technique.
  • Share case reviews of barotrauma events to reinforce preventive steps.
9. Key Take‑Home Messages (Bullet Summary)
  • Risk factor: severely diseased, angulated sigmoid (usually diverticular).
  • Trigger: gas insufflation when lumen cannot vent → closed loop with ileocecal valve.
  • Prevention: stop gas, switch to water filling at first sign of difficulty.
  • CO₂ insufflation: prevents delayed perforation only; does not protect against acute barotrauma.
  • Recognition: distended tympanitic abdomen + known sigmoid disease → act fast.
  • Emergency steps: needle decompression → antibiotics → surgical consult.
