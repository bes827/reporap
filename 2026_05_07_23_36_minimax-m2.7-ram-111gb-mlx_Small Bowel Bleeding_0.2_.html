Small Bowel Bleeding
  • Source: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology 2025;23:24-27
  • Topic: Small bowel bleeding (SBB) diagnosis and management
  • Authors: Daniel Wild¹, Cynthia Ko² (¹Duke University Medical Center, ²University of Washington School of Medicine)
Clinical Problem: Why SBB Is Increasing
  • SBAEs = most common cause of SBB
  • Medical advances extended lives of patients with advanced comorbidities:
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Cirrhosis
  • Heart failure
  • Valvular heart disease
  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Vascular occlusive disease
  • Myelodysplasia
  • ↑ Use and potency of antithrombotic agents
  • ↓ Peptic ulcer disease and malignancy due to:
  • Anti-secretory therapy
  • H. pylori eradication efforts
  • Colorectal cancer screening increases
  • ↑ Diagnostic upper endoscopy
Unique Challenges of SBB
  • Typically occurs in elderly patients on antithrombotics with multiple comorbidities
  • Bleeding often intermittent
  • Small bowel --> difficult to access and treat
  • Even when treated endoscopically --> frequently recurs
  • Traditional "find and stop" approach often ineffective
  • Need multi-faceted, multi-disciplinary, longitudinal management paradigm
SBB Clinical Phenotypes Classification
  • 4 distinct clinical behaviors exist with overlap and transition between phenotypes
  • Proposed classification: SBB1, SBB2a, SBB2b, SBB3

SBB1 Phenotype
  • Mild iron deficiency anemia
  • Usually maintained on oral iron
  • Overt bleeding --> rare
  • Small bowel evaluation often normal or reveals non-bleeding SBAEs
SBB2a Phenotype
  • More problematic iron deficiency anemia
  • Often requires parenteral iron and periodic RBC transfusion
  • Intermittent overt bleeding, most often melena
  • Hospitalization can be required
  • Small bowel evaluation typically reveals vascular lesions, sometimes with active bleeding
SBB2b Phenotype
  • Frequently hospitalized for bleeding (typically melena)
  • Hemodynamic compromise --> rare
  • Small bowel evaluation frequently demonstrates active bleeding
SBB3 Phenotype
  • Typically younger with few or no comorbidities associated with SBB
  • Bleeding is most frequently acute onset
  • More robust bleeding typically due to a distinct, unifocal lesion
  • Quick recovery to asymptomatic baseline between episodes
  • Small bowel evaluation often reveals bleeding when performed close to event
  • Source may not be initially evident
Phenotype Transitions and Overlap
  • Patients can transition between first 3 phenotypes
  • SBB2-type patient may bleed from SBB3-type source (e.g., Dieulafoy lesion)
  • Otherwise healthy SBB3-type patient may present with SBB2-type bleeding (e.g., Osler-Weber-Rendu syndrome)
Management Options Overview
Supportive Care
  • Simplest management approach
  • Periodic hemoglobin monitoring
  • Iron supplementation
  • Cessation of potentially offending agents (e.g., NSAIDs)
  • Occasional RBC transfusions
Medical Therapy
  • Clear role, particularly for SBB2a and SBB2b
  • No FDA-approved medications specifically for SBB
  • Mounting data suggest several agents are beneficial
Somatostatin Analogs
  • Evidence of benefit in >75% of treated patients
  • Long-acting octreotide significantly ↓ number of infusions and need for endoscopic therapy vs standard care
Thalidomide
  • >50% reduction in bleeding episodes in >50% of patients vs 16% on placebo
  • Recent prospective randomized study demonstrated efficacy
Bevacizumab
  • Reduced RBC transfusions by >50% in more than 90% of patients
  • Benefit sustained for at least 1 year
  • Bowel perforation not described when used for SBB or associated with DAE
Other Agents (limited evidence)
  • Tranexamic acid: supported by small studies and case series
  • Tamoxifen: supported by small studies and case series
Medical Therapy Considerations
  • Side effects common but typically manageable
  • All remain off-label for SBB
  • Insurance approval often difficult to attain
Endoscopic Therapy
Push Enteroscopy
  • Preferred over standard upper endoscopy if recent investigations not performed
  • Visualizing proximal jejunum can be useful
Video Capsule Endoscopy (VCE)
  • Visualizes entire small bowel in >80% of exams
  • Diagnostic yield approximately 60% when performed for suspected SBB
  • Helps stratify patients:
  • Negative VCE --> less likely to have significant findings on DAE (28% vs 75% after positive VCE)
  • Negative VCE --> lower risk of recurrent bleeding
Device-Assisted Enteroscopy (DAE)
  • Available in US for >2 decades
  • Limited resource, often confined to academic referral centers
  • Enables direct interrogation of most small bowel lumen
  • Bleeding recurs in >40% even when SBAEs found and treated
  • Frail patients at ↑ risk for cardiopulmonary complications with general anesthesia
  • Perforation rare but can be devastating in this cohort
  • Role clearer when source suspected to be something other than SBAE
  • More likely to result in definitive therapy or change management
Interdisciplinary Interventions
Cardiology Role
  • Reduce or adjust antithrombotics
  • Consider left atrial appendage closure (Watchman procedure) for appropriate AFib patients to allow cessation of antithrombotics
Cardiac Intervention
  • Severe aortic stenosis (Heyde's syndrome):
  • Transcatheter aortic valve replacement
  • Leads to cessation of SBB in >80% of patients
Nephrology Role
  • Give colony-stimulating factors (e.g., epoetin)
  • Facilitate blood transfusions during dialysis for chronic kidney disease patients
Hematology Role
  • Exclude alternate/concomitant sources of anemia
  • Treat with parenteral iron when appropriate
  • Administer medical therapies (thalidomide, bevacizumab)
Interventional Radiology Role
  • May treat sources if detected on CT angiography
New SBB Paradigm: Phenotype-Specific Management
SBB1 Management Approach
  • Avoid unnecessary endoscopy unless:
  • Recent evaluations not performed, or
  • Symptoms suggestive of neoplasm
  • VCE useful to exclude significant lesion
  • Presence of SBAE should not reflexively prompt DAE
  • These patients typically safely managed with supportive care alone
SBB2a Management Approach
  • Challenging patients requiring all strategies discussed above
  • Best option depends on clinical trajectory:
  • Improvement
  • Progressive worsening
  • Fluctuation
  • Patient preferences and risk aversion must be carefully weighed, especially if near end of life
  • VCE findings factor heavily:
  • Active bleeding or reachable target --> more reasonable to consider at least one attempt at DAE in suitable candidates
  • If bleeding persists or recurs and supportive care insufficient --> initiate medical therapy
  • Modify as many clinical factors as possible with other specialists' assistance
SBB2b Management Approach
  • Similar considerations to SBB2a but more aggressive approach typically required
  • Source should ideally be identified with:
  • VCE
  • Push enteroscopy
  • CT angiography
  • Once identified --> further evaluation and treatment tailored to source and location within small bowel
  • Low threshold to initiate medical therapy when SBAEs are suspected source
  • Simultaneously modify clinical factors with other specialists' help
Medical Therapy Selection for SBB2b
  • Depends on:
  • Patient comorbidities
  • Insurance coverage
  • Availability of other specialists (hematology, cardiology)
  • General approach:
  • Start octreotide 50 mcg every 8 to 12 hours for initial therapy
  • If well-tolerated, effective, and insurance allows --> transition to depot injection starting at 20 mg monthly
  • Increase to 40 mg if needed
  • If octreotide fails --> consult hematology for:
  • Limited course of thalidomide (to avoid neuropathy risk with long-term use), or
  • Initiate bevacizumab
  • If these cannot be used --> offer tranexamic acid
SBB3 Management Approach
  • Most likely to stem from discrete source that can be definitively stopped
  • All efforts should identify source with VCE or cross-sectional imaging
  • Yield higher when performed closer to bleeding event
  • May need repeat if not initially diagnostic
  • Source may not be apparent unless actively bleeding
  • Once found --> reach and treat or remove the source
  • Typically requires:
  • DAE
  • Surgery with or without intraoperative enteroscopy
  • Angiography
Conclusion
  • SBB becoming increasingly common due to:
  • ↑ patient survival with comorbidities
  • ↑ antithrombotic use
  • Complexity and distinct challenges necessitate new management paradigm
  • Proposed classification of SBB phenotypes facilitates tailored management approach
  • Approach uses combination of:
  • Endoscopic treatment
  • Medical therapy
  • Multi-disciplinary risk factor modification
  • Despite optimal management --> definitive bleeding cessation for SBB1, SBB2a, and SBB2b unlikely
  • Long-term care required
Take Home Message
  • SBB increasingly encountered clinically with range of clinical phenotypes
  • Management requires dynamic, multifaceted approach tailored to these phenotypes
