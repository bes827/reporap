AGA Clinical Practice Update on the Management of Ascites, Volume Overload, and Hyponatremia in Cirrhosis: Expert Review
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2025;169:1547–1557
  • Topic: Management of Ascites, Volume Overload, and Hyponatremia in Cirrhosis
  • Author(s): Eric S. Orman, Brett E. Fortune, Binu V. John, Sumeet K. Asrani
1. Pathophysiology of Volume Overload in Cirrhosis
  • Mechanism: Portal hypertension → increased intrahepatic vascular resistance + portal blood flow → mesenteric and systemic vasodilation → neurohormonal activation (RAAS) → renal sodium/water reabsorption → intravascular/interstitial volume expansion
  • Ascites formation: Excess fluid leaks into peritoneal cavity when hepatic lymphatic system capacity exceeded; occurs with hypoalbuminemia (↓ oncotic pressure), ↑ hydrostatic pressure (portal hypertension), and increased capillary permeability
  • Hepatic hydrothorax (HH): Diaphragmatic defects allow ascitic fluid translocation into pleural space
  • Peripheral edema/anasarca: Interstitial fluid accumulation in peripheral tissues
  • Hyponatremia: Progressive circulatory dysfunction → effective intravascular hypovolemia → ↑ renal water retention → hypervolemic hyponatremia
  • Clinical impact: ↓ quality of life, repeated hospitalizations, predisposes to infections/hepatorenal syndrome, ↑ mortality
2. Best Practice Advice 1: Initial Management of Ascites, HH, and Volume Overload
  • Patients with cirrhosis + ascites/HH/volume overload → managed with dietary sodium restriction + diuretics at lowest effective dose
  • Dose escalation guided by: symptoms, weight, urine output, electrolyte/renal monitoring
  • Dietary sodium: Target <2000 mg/d; referral to dietitian for label reading, cooking techniques, patient/caregiver education
  • Dietary requirements: ≥35 kcal/kg/d (non-obese), protein 1.2–1.5 g/kg/d, micronutrient repletion; avoid overly restrictive diets causing caloric/protein deficiency
  • Fluid restriction: Not needed unless significant hyponatremia present
  • First-line diuretics: Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists + loop diuretics combined (furosemide:spironolactone 40:100 ratio)
  • Spironolactone: Start 100 mg/d (range 50–400 mg), once-daily morning dose; alternatives: amiloride, eplerenone for intolerance
  • Furosemide: Start 40 mg/d (range 20–160 mg), titrate by ascites severity, BP, electrolytes, renal function
  • Monitoring: Patient self-weighing daily; target weight loss 0.5 kg/d (without edema) or 1 kg/d (with edema)
  • Triggers of decompensation: Identify and address (alcohol, medications, hemodynamic changes, infection); may need to hold BP medications (especially renin-angiotensin system inhibitors)
3. Best Practice Advice 2: Diagnostic Paracentesis
  • Indications: New-onset ascites OR hospitalization for ascites-related symptoms/encephalopathy → receive diagnostic paracentesis ASAP
  • Testing required: Serum ascites albumin gradient (SAAG), cell count, Gram stain, culture
  • SAAG interpretation: ≥1.1 g/dL suggests portal hypertension; <1.1 g/dL warrants evaluation for other causes (heart failure, malignancy, tuberculosis)
  • SBP diagnosis: Neutrophil count >250/mm³ with/without culture positivity, no secondary peritonitis evidence
  • Culture method: Bedside inoculation into blood culture bottles
  • Timing: Paracentesis within 12–24 hours of admission → associated with lower hospital mortality
  • Additional testing if low SAAG or non-portal hypertension features: cytology, amylase, mycobacterial studies
4. Best Practice Advice 3: Hepatic Hydrothorax Management
  • Definition: Pleural effusion in portal hypertension setting without other causes (heart failure, lung disease, malignancy)
  • Prognosis: Poor prognostic sign; >4-fold ↑ mortality vs. refractory ascites alone
  • Diagnostic thoracentesis: Excludes other causes; evaluate for spontaneous bacterial empyema (SBE)
  • SBE diagnosis: Pleural fluid neutrophils >250/mm³ with/without culture positivity, no alternative infectious source
  • Therapeutic thoracentesis: Indicated for dyspnea and/or hypoxemia → symptom relief + lung expansion
  • First-line therapy: Sodium restriction + diuretics (same as ascites)
  • Symptom threshold: HH causes symptoms at much lower fluid volumes vs. ascites
5. Best Practice Advice 4: Liver Transplantation Evaluation
  • All patients with ascites and/or HH should be considered for liver transplantation evaluation, regardless of MELD score
  • Ascites significance: Most common initial decompensation event; marks critical step in disease natural history
  • Mortality impact: Once ascites develops, annual mortality rises from <5% to ~20%; 90-day mortality up to 50% for SBP/hepatorenal syndrome
  • Associated complications: Links to malnutrition, sarcopenia, frailty → ↑ mortality and potential transplantation preclusion
  • MELD considerations: Revised MELD includes sodium, sex, albumin to better reflect waitlist mortality and reduce disparities; ascites remains independent predictor of mortality beyond MELD
  • Transplant access for low-MELD ascites patients: living donors, extended criteria donors
  • HH-specific mortality: Independently associated with significantly higher mortality regardless of MELD; 1-year mortality 20%–50%
  • HH MELD exception: No standard OPTN exception; nonstandard exceptions may be granted for: ≥1 thoracentesis >1 L weekly × 4 weeks, transudative fluid, no heart failure, negative cultures/cytology, TIPS contraindications
5. Best Practice Advice 5: Management of Refractory Ascites and Hepatic Hydrothorax
  • Refractory ascites definition: Ascites that cannot be mobilized OR early recurrence cannot be satisfactorily prevented by medical therapy
  • Subcategories: Diuretic-resistant (nonresponse to sodium restriction + diuretics) vs. diuretic-intractable (complications preventing effective dose escalation)
  • Refractory HH definition: Unresponsive to salt restriction and diuretic therapy, requires therapeutic thoracentesis
  • Therapeutic paracentesis: Safe, effective method; faster symptom relief vs. intensive diuretic therapy with fewer complications (HE, electrolyte abnormalities, AKI)
  • Postparacentesis circulatory dysfunction: Marked by ↓ systemic vascular resistance + RAAS/sympathetic system activation → ↑ risks of kidney injury, electrolyte imbalances, HE, death
  • Bleeding risk: Rare even with coagulopathy/thrombocytopenia; prophylactic transfusions not advised
  • Refractory HH challenges: Lower fluid thresholds for symptoms vs. ascites; presents as dyspnea vs. distention
  • Thoracentesis risks: Higher than for other effusions (bleeding, infection, pneumothorax, re-expansion edema); chest wall portosystemic collaterals can cause catastrophic bleeding
  • Chest tube placement: High morbidity/mortality → should be avoided in HH
  • Indwelling tunneled pleural catheters: Promising option with relatively low complication rates; palliative for non-transplant candidates or bridge to transplantation with short wait times
6. Best Practice Advice 6: Albumin Administration Post-Paracentesis
  • Standard protocol: When ascites removed >5 L → administer 20%–25% IV albumin 6–8 g per every total liter removed
  • Rationale: Prevents postparacentesis circulatory dysfunction → reduces electrolyte derangements and mortality
  • 5 L cutoff: Somewhat arbitrary; recent data suggest smaller-volume paracentesis may also benefit from albumin
  • Dose-defining studies: Needed; different albumin doses have not shown outcome differences
  • Liberal albumin strategy considerations: For patients with hypotension, renal insufficiency, or electrolyte abnormalities → more severe postparacentesis circulatory dysfunction consequences
  • Case-by-case approach: Lower-volume thresholds and higher doses may be considered; avoid iatrogenic volume overload
7. Best Practice Advice 7: Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS) Referral
  • Indication: Well-selected patients with refractory ascites, HH, volume overload, or hyponatremia
  • Efficacy: Highly effective for portal hypertension; more effective than paracentesis for ascites control; associated with lower mortality
  • Patient-reported outcomes: Less clear; symptom relief may be offset by HE and TIPS dysfunction risk
  • Contemporary stents: Polytetrafluoroethylene-covered stents may reduce risks, although data limited
  • Absolute contraindications: Congestive heart failure, severe pulmonary hypertension, uncontrolled HE, sepsis
  • Relative contraindications: Ejection fraction <50%, severe diastolic/valvular dysfunction, right ventricular systolic pressure >45 mm Hg
  • HE risk: Occurs in up to 50% post-TIPS; risk factors include prior HE, impaired liver function, older age, renal dysfunction, hyponatremia, large gradient reduction, sarcopenia
  • Caution populations: Patients >70 years or MELD >18; indication increasingly expanded
  • Cardiac decompensation risk: Up to 20% within 1 year of TIPS
  • Preprocedural evaluation: Echocardiography for cardiac systolic/diastolic function and RVSP
  • Optimal timing: Undefined; most studies focus on refractory ascites/HH
  • Early TIPS strategy evidence: Patients with <6 paracenteses in 3 months showed better ascites control, fewer hospitalizations, improved survival without more HE
  • Cost-effectiveness: Early TIPS may be cost-effective but needs validation
  • Expert panel suggestion: TIPS after 3 large-volume paracenteses in 12 months, especially with other indications (e.g., prior variceal bleeding)
  • Non-TIPS candidates: Limited evidence supports indwelling peritoneal catheters; complication rates may be high
  • Alfapump system: Transfers ascites into bladder; improved quality of life in RCTs; infection risk; recently approved in US, widespread experience lacking
  • Peritoneovenous shunt: Consideration for select non-TIPS candidates; limited by shunt patency concerns, DIC, heart failure
8. Best Practice Advice 8: Diagnostic Workup for Hyponatremia in Cirrhosis
  • Hyponatremia definition: Serum sodium <130 mEq/L in cirrhosis
  • Pathophysiology: Hypervolemic hyponatremia from serum overdilution due to fluid retention from vasopressin secretion → ↑ water reabsorption → enhanced sodium reabsorption
  • Significance: Associated with significant morbidity and mortality; serum sodium incorporated into transplant allocation system within MELD
  • Components of workup:
  • Dietary and medication history (diuretics, bowel regimen)
  • Review of electrolyte and kidney function
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding assessment
  • Infectious workup including diagnostic paracentesis
  • Evaluation of secondary causes (thyroid or adrenal dysfunction)
  • Daily documentation: Sodium and fluid intake, alcohol use, medications
  • Additional assessments: Renal, thyroid, adrenal, cardiac function
  • Infection evaluation: Essential; hyponatremia may signal acute inflammation; include cultures and diagnostic paracentesis
  • Pseudohyponatremia: Laboratory artifact in hypertriglyceridemia or paraproteinemia; consider if serum osmolality not low (>280 mOsm/L)
  • Nonhepatic causes: If identified, targeted management follows standard protocols
9. Best Practice Advice 9: Outpatient Management of Asymptomatic Hypervolemic Hyponatremia
  • Management approach: Sodium + water restriction based on hyponatremia severity
  • Mild hyponatremia (serum sodium >120 mEq/L) + asymptomatic: Limit fluid intake to 1–1.5 L/d
  • Diuretic adjustment: Often need to reduce or discontinue diuretics
  • Sodium stabilization: Once sodium stabilizes >125 mEq/L, providers can resume or maintain diuretics
  • Lactulose/ laxative education: Important to avoid excessive stooling → volume depletion → worsening hyponatremia
  • Monitoring: Serial electrolyte and renal function monitoring is critical
  • Ambulatory albumin infusions: May help hyponatremia; data sparse; access limited by insurance and infusion site availability
10. Best Practice Advice 10: Inpatient Management of Severe/Symptomatic Hypervolemic Hyponatremia
  • Hospitalization criteria: Symptoms related to hyponatremia OR serum sodium <120 mEq/L
  • Symptoms: Cognitive changes, dizziness, seizures, cardiopulmonary alterations, nausea, vomiting
  • Precipitant evaluation: Include infections; assess for precipitating factors
  • Immediate actions:
  • Discontinue or adjust diuretics and laxatives
  • Assess renal and cardiac function
  • Correct hypokalemia especially with encephalopathy
  • Fluid intake restricted to less than urine output
  • Hypotension management (SBP <90 mm Hg): Hold nonselective beta-blockers for portal hypertension; endoscopic management of high-risk varices if unable to resume beta-blockers
  • Severe symptomatic cases: Immediate interventions—albumin infusion or vasoconstrictors (e.g., midodrine) → improve renal perfusion
  • Life-threatening hyponatremia or correction needed before transplant: Cautious 3% hypertonic saline use; avoid rapid correction to prevent osmotic demyelination syndrome
  • Vaptans (vasopressin antagonists): Can improve hyponatremia in cirrhosis; use declined due to adverse events (hepatic injury, mortality); now reserved mainly for short-term use in severe hyponatremia awaiting liver transplantation
  • Severe kidney injury: Hemodialysis may help correct hyponatremia
11. Best Practice Advice 11: Recurrent or Refractory Hyponatremia Management
  • Definition: Recurrent = redevelopment after prior episode; Refractory = persistence despite maximal therapy
  • Epidemiology: Hyponatremia recurs in >50% of cases; associated with poor survival
  • Mortality significance: Ongoing hyponatremia carries significant short-term mortality; severity increases transplantation need
  • Multidisciplinary approach required: Include transplant hepatology, nephrology, potentially anesthesiology
  • Evaluation: Rule out other causes (e.g., adrenal insufficiency)
  • Therapeutic options:
  • Vasoconstrictor therapy (e.g., terlipressin or norepinephrine): May improve sodium levels before transplantation
  • Hypertonic saline: For symptomatic or severe hyponatremia
  • Vaptans: Team-based decision for short-term use
  • Renal replacement therapy: If transplantation not an option, palliative care + hepatology for goals of care and symptom management
12. Best Practice Advice 12: Inpatient Management of Volume Overload
  • Clinical presentation: Anasarca with/without ascites or HH—common decompensation presentation in inpatient setting
  • Inpatient management indication: Patients progressing despite outpatient diuretic escalation OR persistent anasarca/volume overload signs
  • Evidence base: Focused study limited; guidance extrapolated from cardiology/nephrology literature
  • First steps:
  • Reverse aggravating factors (alcohol, NSAIDs)
  • Dietary sodium restriction
  • Medication review including anti-hypertensives (especially renin-angiotensin system inhibitors)
  • Diuresis
  • Goal: Promote urinary sodium excretion exceeding dietary sodium intake
  • Initial regimens: IV loop diuretics (furosemide or bumetanide) in bolus (2–3 times daily) or continuous fashion
  • Response monitoring: Daily symptom assessment, urine output, kidney function, weights
  • Escalation: If inadequate diuresis, escalate doses in bolus or continuous fashion every 2–3 days with monitoring
  • Bolus vs. continuous superiority: Unclear in cirrhosis
  • First-line agent comparison: Furosemide and bumetanide likely similar efficacy; torsemide may also play role (only oral in US)
  • Potency: IV furosemide ~2× potent as oral; bumetanide may have 1:1 oral-to-IV equivalence
  • Concurrent IV albumin role: Unclear; targeting serum albumin level in patients not admitted for volume overload does not reduce infection, kidney dysfunction, or death
13. Best Practice Advice 13: Advanced Strategies in Refractory Anasarca
  • Coordination: Partner with nephrology for inadequate diuretic response
  • Thiazide diuretics (e.g., metolazone): Consider in refractory edema unresponsive to loop diuretics
  • Adverse events limiting diuresis: Contraction alkalosis, electrolyte dysfunction
  • Serum creatinine elevation: Common but may not reflect worsening kidney function
  • Diuretic-resistant anasarca definition: No improvement despite maximal diuretics + persistently low urine sodium
  • Advanced strategies:
  • Pressors to augment diuresis in ICU setting
  • Ultrafiltration: Consider in select cases, especially liver transplantation candidates
  • Discharge planning: Transitional care with proper medication management, patient/caregiver education, timely follow-up to improve outcomes
14. Conclusions and Future Directions
  • Curative option: Liver transplantation remains the only curative option for cirrhosis with ascites, fluid overload, hyponatremia
  • Gap: Significant gap between donor organ supply and transplant demand → need for bridging strategies or quality of life improvement
  • Research needs: Clear needs to improve management of ascites, fluid overload, hyponatremia; ongoing studies warranted
  • Special population: Investigational therapeutic trials especially needed for advanced steatotic liver disease patients with significant comorbidities confounding older treatment paradigms
  • Disease definition refinement: Needed to enhance provider understanding, enable earlier recognition and appropriate management implementation
  • Care setting considerations: Many patients with complex fluid retention, ascites, hyponatremia managed at tertiary/transplantation centers; gastroenterologists and hospitalists must know when/how to provide optimal care and timely hepatology referral
  • Outcome impact: Without successful linkage, patients may fail to connect with liver centers → higher mortality, readmissions, poor outcomes
  • Future focus: Complement advances in treating underlying liver disease (e.g., metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease) with improved strategies for fluid overload to enhance survival, quality of life, and hope for patients/caregivers
