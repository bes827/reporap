How to Survive a Medical Malpractice Lawsuit – Key Guidance
  • Source: Ilene R. Brenner, *How to Survive a Medical Malpractice Lawsuit*, 2010 Blackwell Publishing.
  • Date of original publication: 2010; compiled for reference: 2025.
1. Emotional Impact & Immediate Response
  • The service of legal papers triggers intense shock, anxiety, and a cascade of self‑doubting thoughts about job security, finances, competence, and career future.
1.1 Shock and Psychological Reaction
  • Metaphor: feeling “hit on the head with a bat, stabbed in the back, and disemboweled” → extreme stress; common thoughts: “Will I lose my job?”, “Is my money protected?”, “Am I a bad doctor?”, “Is my career over?”.
1.2 First Coping Actions
  • Immediate outreach to trusted support (e.g., calling father or spouse) provides emotional grounding and initiates damage‑control mindset.
1.3 Time to Process & Decision‑Making
  • Allow minutes, hours, or days to assimilate the event before acting; ask “Now what?” and seek structured guidance.
2. Insurance Policy Review & Rights
  • Understanding your malpractice policy’s limits, attorney selection rights, and settlement clauses is essential for protecting personal assets and guiding strategic decisions.
2.1 Core Policy Clauses to Know
  • Three pivotal provisions: <b>Policy Limits</b>, <b>Attorney Selection</b>, and <b>Consent to Settle</b> dictate coverage scope, legal representation, and settlement authority.
2.1.1 Policy Limits
  • Typical per‑claim limit: <u>$1,000,000</u>.
  • States without caps on noneconomic damages (pain & suffering) require careful assessment of high‑value cases.
  • High economic‑damage scenarios (ICU stay, loss of wages, disability, future income) may exceed limits → consider settlement to protect assets.
2.1.2 Attorney Selection
  • Some policies grant the right to choose a defense attorney; others assign one from the insurer’s panel.
  • Conflict arises when employer‑assigned counsel also represents co‑defendants (e.g., hospital).
  • If conflict exists, you may request separate counsel per policy provisions.
2.1.3 Consent to Settle
  • Clause determines whether you can refuse a settlement; refusing low settlements helps avoid premium spikes and future employability issues.
  • Absence of this right leaves you at the insurer’s discretion, potentially increasing risk exposure.
2.2 Employer‑Purchased Policies & Options
  • When an employer (or hospital) buys your coverage, you often lack control over clauses; three strategic options exist.
2.2.1 Evaluate Employer Litigation Stance
  • Assess how aggressively the employer defends lawsuits; a reputation for taking cases to trial can deter plaintiffs.
2.2.2 Purchasing Personal Policy
  • **Pros:** full control over limits, settlement rights, and attorney choice.
  • **Cons:** higher individual premiums, possible loss of coverage if sued, lack of group rating discounts.
2.2.3 Leaving the Employer
  • If policy terms are unacceptable and cannot be negotiated, consider employment change to obtain independent coverage.
2.3 Claims‑Made vs Occurrence Policies
  • Policy type dictates reporting timing and coverage continuity after job changes.
2.3.1 Reporting Requirements for Claims‑Made
  • Coverage applies only to claims arising **during** the policy period; therefore, you must report a potential claim promptly (ideally before any lawsuit is filed) to preserve protection → <u>early reporting</u> essential.
2.3.2 Timing Insensitivity of Occurrence Policies
  • Covers incidents that occurred during the policy term regardless of when the claim is filed; no urgent reporting needed, though early notice remains prudent.
3. Legal Process: Claim Reporting & Attorney Interaction
  • Initiating contact with your insurer, managing evidence, and adhering to communication protocols are critical first steps in the defense timeline.
3.1 Initiating the Claim with Your Insurer
  • Call the insurance company immediately after being served; this activates legal‑defense coverage and expense reimbursement.
3.1.1 What to Expect from Insurer
  • Assigned defense attorney typically within <u>20 days</u> (state‑dependent).
  • Insurer will request details of the complaint, ask about alleged facts, and may direct you on record production.
3.2 Evidence Management & Documentation
  • Do **not** provide medical records until instructed; create exact copies of charts—including memos and billing—but exclude correspondence about legal matters.
3.2.1 Handling Claims‑Made Policy Evidence
  • Preserve all documentation to support future coverage, as claims‑made policies only protect incidents reported during the policy period.
3.2.2 Avoid Altering Records
  • Never modify, delete, or destroy original charts, diaries, or notes; tampering can lead to loss of case and sanctions for fraud.
3.3 Communication Restrictions
  • Limit discussions about the lawsuit to your attorney (and spouse for emotional support); avoid speaking with colleagues, patients, plaintiffs, or anyone who could become a witness.
3.3.1 Deposition Pitfalls
  • Refrain from consulting textbooks or journals before deposition; plaintiff’s counsel may pin you to “authoritative” sources → adopt mantra: **“Nothing is authoritative.”**
3.3.2 Post‑Attorney Research
  • Conduct any literature review only after attorney approval; such research may be protected from discovery depending on jurisdiction.
3.4 Managing Settlement Discussions
  • Insurers often push for low settlements to reduce premiums; if you possess a <b>Consent to Settle</b> clause, you can decline and pursue trial despite insurer pressure.
4. Litigation Conduct: Do’s and Don’ts
  • Behavioral guidelines help preserve credibility, protect evidence, and maintain professional integrity throughout the lawsuit.
4.1 What Not to Do
4.1.1 Avoid Independent Research
  • Do not look up books, journals, or online sources before attorney counsel; prevents being trapped by “authoritative” citations at deposition.
4.1.2 Refrain from Discussing Case Widely
  • Communicate only with your attorney and spouse; peer‑review settings are the sole exception for protected discussion.
4.1.3 Do Not Destroy or Alter Evidence
  • Preserve original records, charts, and any personal diaries; destruction can be construed as fraud and result in case loss.
4.1.4 Avoid the Blame Game
  • Do not self‑blame, blame patients, or view them as adversaries; maintain compassion to safeguard mental health and professional reputation.
4.2 What to Do
4.2.1 Preserve Compassion & Clinical Quality
  • Continue delivering evidence‑based care; document risk discussions (e.g., explain radiation risks of CT scans) → <b>risk disclosure</b> strengthens defense.
4.2.1 Seek Emotional Support
  • Lean on trusted family, consider professional counseling to mitigate stress and depression.
4.2.3 Follow Attorney Guidance Rigorously
  • Let your attorney handle all legal communications, evidence decisions, and strategic moves; avoid unilateral actions.
5. Foundations of a Medical Malpractice Claim
  • Legal theory requires proving duty, breach, proximate cause, and damages (or three elements in some states) to establish liability.
5.1 Definition of Medical Malpractice
  • AMA definition: “a doctor’s failure to exercise the degree of care and skill that a physician of the same specialty would use under similar circumstances.”
  • Distinguish between a bad outcome (statistical reality) and negligent conduct.
5.2 Four (or Three) Elements of Liability
5.2.1 Duty
  • Established by a physician‑patient relationship; exceptions include “drive‑by consults” when the physician is not on call.
5.2.2 Breach (Standard of Care)
  • Deviation from accepted practice for the specialty; expert testimony determines the applicable standard, which may evolve with new evidence.
5.2.3 Proximate Cause
  • Causation must be shown: either direct cause or a contributing factor in a continuous chain without intervening events.
  • Example: missed X‑ray that later revealed cancer **Δ** not causative if disease was already metastatic at the time of imaging.
5.2.4 Damages
  • Economic (lost wages, medical bills) and noneconomic (pain & suffering).
  • Punitive damages are rare and require proof of reckless or malicious conduct.
5.3 Statistical Context of Malpractice
  • NPDB data (1990‑2002): <u>5%</u> of physicians accounted for <u>54%</u> of malpractice payouts.
  • Discipline rates: only <u>8%</u> of doctors with ≥2 payouts and <u>17%</u> with ≥5 payouts were disciplined by state medical boards.
5.4 Common Misconceptions
  • Not every adverse outcome equals malpractice; examples of non‑malpractice include impaired physicians treating patients, performing unnecessary procedures for profit, or practicing outside one’s specialty without requisite expertise.
6. Prevention Strategies & Ancillary Issues
  • Proactive clinical and administrative practices can reduce liability risk; understanding EMTALA, board complaints, and ADR options further protects your career.
6.1 Clinical Practices that Reduce Liability
6.1.1 Phone Consults
  • Duty exists when you are on call; off‑call advice carries limited liability.
  • Document all telephone recommendations and follow‑up actions.
6.1.2 Missed Follow‑Up Appointments
  • Send certified letters warning of potential condition worsening → “You missed two appointments; your condition may deteriorate.”
  • Follow with phone calls, document each contact in the chart.
6.1.3 In‑Office Imaging
  • Self‑reading X‑rays increases liability; consider radiology overreads despite added cost to mitigate risk.
6.2 EMTALA Considerations
  • EMTALA mandates a medical screening exam before discharge and regulates inter‑hospital transfers.
  • Violations can lead to fines, Medicare exclusion, and civil suits (separate from malpractice).
  • EMTALA claims often bypass tort‑reform caps on noneconomic damages, making them attractive to plaintiffs.
6.3 Medical Board Complaints
  • Separate from lawsuits; focus on unprofessional behavior, patient endangerment, or impairment.
  • Boards may initiate investigations based on settlements or multiple payouts (e.g., five settlements in three years).
  • Representation is typically covered by malpractice insurance.
6.3.1 Potential Outcomes
  • Sanctions range from reprimand, fines, probation, to license suspension or revocation.
6.4 Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
  • **Negotiation:** Direct parties reach durable agreements; high control for both sides.
  • **Mediation:** Neutral facilitator assists negotiation; non‑binding but confidential.
  • **Arbitration:** Formal hearing by arbitrator; can be binding and faster than trial.
  • **Pretrial Screening Panels:** State‑run advisory bodies evaluate claim merit, often reducing frivolous lawsuits.
6.4.1 ADR Impact on Insurance & NPDB
  • Settlements reached via ADR are reported to the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB) → may increase future premiums and affect hospital privileges.
6.5 Choosing the Right Attorney (Reference)
  • Selecting a skilled defense lawyer is critical; see Chapter 3 for detailed criteria and steps to replace an unsatisfactory attorney.
