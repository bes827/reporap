Correlation Between Distensibility and Dysphagia is Dependent on Disease Activity in Pediatric Eosinophilic Esophagitis
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology
  • Topic: Pediatric Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE) - Distensibility and Dysphagia Correlation
  • Author(s): Not specified in provided text
1. Study Overview and Key Findings
  • Prospective pediatric cohort study
  • Clinically meaningful association identified between reduced DP and dysphagia severity
  • Relationship persists only in patients with active EoE when stratified by disease activity
  • Both impaired distensibility at the DP and inflammation contribute to burden of dysphagia in pediatric EoE
2. Distensibility Point (DP) Analysis
2.1. DP in Fibrostenotic EoE
  • DP is significantly reduced in patients with fibrostenotic EoE (consistent with prior reports)
  • Prior studies: references 3, 9, 11, 14, 19, 23
2.2. DP and Disease Activity
  • No differences observed between patients based on disease activity (prior studies showed mixed results: references 9, 22)
  • Prior studies found no correlation or weak correlation between distensibility and eosinophilia
  • Better correlation observed with endoscopic reference score (highly dependent on fibrostenotic features)
  • Significant reduction in DP observed for fibrostenotic features
2.3. DP Correlation with Dysphagia
  • Critical finding: correlation of dysphagia with DP
  • Strength: dysphagia assessed in week prior to FLIP using PEESS dysphagia domain (captures symptom frequency and severity)
  • Prior findings: DP predicted dysphagia as binary outcome (Kennedy et al.)
  • Reduced DP associated with:
  • Increased chewing time
  • Increased consumption time
  • Dysphagia in prior 30 days
  • History of food impaction (references 9, 11, 19)
2.4. DP and Inflammation Relationship
  • Correlation of DP with dysphagia restricted to active EoE → implies role for inflammation
  • Inflammation acts as contextual modifier rather than independent driver
3. Esophagogastric Junction (EGJ) Findings
3.1. EGJ Diameter in EoE
  • EGJ diameter reduced in EoE patients (consistent with prior report - reference 14)
  • Only a trend toward reduced EGJ diameter observed
  • No difference based on disease activity compared to control
3.2. EGJ in Fibrostenotic EoE
  • Significant reduction seen in EoE patients with fibrostenosis
  • EGJ outflow obstruction can occur in pediatric EoE (recent report suggests improvement with treatment - reference 24)
3.3. EGJ Correlation with Symptoms
  • EGJ diameter NOT correlated with symptoms assessed by PEESS
  • Only moderate correlation with DP
  • Further studies needed to understand clinical impact of reduced EGJ diameter in children with EoE
4. Dysphagia Assessment (PEESS)
4.1. PEESS Domain
  • Captures both symptom frequency and severity
  • Assessed in week prior to FLIP
4.2. Child vs Parent Reporting
  • When PEESS child and parent dysphagia assessed separately:
  • Correlation only seen with PEESS child dysphagia
  • Possible reasons:
  • Sample size limitation when stratifying by disease activity
  • Study powered to detect moderate but not weak correlations
  • Subtle differences in subjective symptom perception and reporting
  • Caregiver-reported symptoms may attenuate associations, particularly for younger children
5. Multivariable Analysis
  • DP independently predicts PEESS Dysphagia scores after adjusting for inflammatory burden
  • Supports primary role for impaired esophageal distensibility in symptom generation
  • Inflammation acts as contextual modifier rather than independent driver
6. Study Limitations
6.1. Patient Population
  • Patients who had undergone prior treatment for EoE included → could impact symptoms
6.2. Sample Size
  • Adequately powered to detect moderate correlations
  • Limited ability to detect weaker associations due to sample size
6.3. FLIP Procedure
  • Performed under general anesthesia (reference 25)
  • Previously shown to impact contractile response
  • DP unlikely impacted by anesthesia
6.4. Generalizability
  • Single-center cohort
  • Limited racial and ethnic diversity
  • Need for validation in larger, multicenter pediatric population
6.5. Future Study Recommendations
  • Studies limiting use of sevoflurane or paralytics better suited to assess relationship between contractile response and symptoms
