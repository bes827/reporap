Overlap of esophageal disorders of gut-brain interactions (E-DGBIs) and GERD is highly prevalent in patients with refractory reflux symptoms
  • Authors: Federico Caldart, Charlotte Gabriel, Blandine Vauquelin, Pauline Rivière, Arthur Berger, Frank Zerbib
  • Journal: American Journal of Gastroenterology
  • Publication Date: Publish Ahead of Print, DOI: 10.14309/ajg.0000000000003542
  • Objective: Determine the prevalence and characteristics of confirmed refractory GERD and E-DGBIs in consecutive patients with refractory reflux symptoms.
  • Conclusion: E-DGBIs are highly prevalent in patients with proven GERD and refractory reflux symptoms.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of patients with proven GERD and refractory symptoms have E-DGBI?
  • 2. What are the two main categories of E-DGBI based on ambulatory pH/pH-impedance monitoring?
  • 3. According to the Lyon 2.0 criteria, what AET threshold is considered pathological for GERD diagnosis off PPIs?
  • 4. What percentage of patients with PPI-refractory reflux-like symptoms had a final diagnosis of refractory GERD in this study?
  • 5. Name two non-esophageal DGBI conditions that are highly prevalent in patients with refractory reflux symptoms.
Key Definitions & Concepts
  • E-DGBIs: Esophageal disorders of gut-brain interaction.
  • GERD: Gastro-esophageal reflux disease.
  • PPIs: Proton pump inhibitors.
  • Refractory GERD: Persistent esophagitis/stricture or ongoing pathological reflux despite adequate PPI therapy.
E-DGBI Subtypes
  • Reflux Hypersensitivity (RH): Normal AET + positive symptom-reflux association.
  • Functional Heartburn (FH): Normal AET + no significant symptom-reflux correlation.
Lyon Consensus 2.0
  • GERD diagnosis requires troublesome symptoms + evidence of pathological reflux (endoscopy and/or ambulatory reflux monitoring).
  • AET >6% is pathological off PPIs (wireless monitoring: AET >6% for ≥2 days).
  • AET >4% or >80 reflux episodes on PPIs indicates refractory GERD.
Prevalence of E-DGBIs in Refractory GERD
  • Study found >70% of patients w/ proven GERD & refractory symptoms despite PPIs have E-DGBI.
  • A previously confirmed GERD diagnosis off PPIs does not rule out FH or RH in patients on PPIs.
Study Results
  • Out of 46 proven GERD patients, 28% had isolated refractory GERD, 72% had overlap of GERD and E-DGBIs.
  • In unproven GERD group, 31.5% had GERD, 68.5% had an E-DGBI.
Implications
  • E-DGBIs are the main cause of PPI failure in patients with proven GERD.
  • Highlights the limitation of symptom-based GERD diagnosis, especially when considering interventional treatment.
Patient Characteristics & Comparisons
  • E-DGBI patients w/ & w/o GERD overlap have similar symptom load, non-esophageal DGBI, anxiety, & depression scores.
  • No significant differences in gender or age between E-DGBI alone vs. E-DGBI-GERD overlap groups.
Visceral Sensitivity
  • Higher visceral sensitivity (VSI) observed in RH patients w/ GERD overlap (median 28.0) compared to RH-alone (median 18.0, p=0.033).
  • VSI score >37.5 indicates increased visceral sensitivity.
Refractory GERD vs. Overlap
  • Refractory GERD patients had higher AET (p=0.016), lower MNBI (p=0.006), & more reflux episodes (p<0.001) than GERD-E-DGBI overlap patients.
  • No significant differences in HADS & VSI scores, nor in IBS & FD prevalence between these groups.
Associated Conditions
  • High prevalence of functional dyspepsia (FD) (70.6%) and irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) (33.6%) reported.
  • IBS and FD have been reported in several studies to be associated with PPI refractoriness.
Treatment Implications
  • Only a minority of patients have actionable GERD & may benefit from interventional management.
  • pH-impedance monitoring on PPIs may help select patients who could benefit from laparoscopic fundoplication.
Future Research
  • Studies w/ outcome data needed to determine if patients w/ overlapping E-DGBIs & GERD should be identified w/ pH-impedance monitoring on PPI therapy before interventional treatment.
  • Future research should investigate long-term trajectories of GERD-E-DGBI overlap patients and evaluate responses to neuromodulators and dietary interventions.
