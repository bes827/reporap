Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol. 103, No. 2, 2026
  • Topic: Colonoscopic complications — barotrauma-induced perforation
  • Author: Douglas K. Rex, MD, Division of Gastroenterology and Hepatology, Indiana University School of Medicine
1. Epidemiology and Clinical Significance
  • Barotrauma is the least common cause of colonoscopic perforation in published series.
  • Yet it is the leading cause of malpractice claims related to perforation — 5/6 cases reviewed in early 2025.
  • Overrepresentation in litigation reflects disproportionately severe outcomes vs other perforation types.
  • Barotrauma perforations often involve extensive, immediate peritoneal contamination and rapid sepsis onset.
2. Pathophysiology and Risk Profile
  • Occurs almost exclusively in patients with narrowed, angulated sigmoid colons from severe diverticular disease.
  • Not all patients with severe sigmoid disease develop barotrauma — but all barotrauma cases involve such anatomy.
  • Risk mechanism: closed-loop system forms when gas cannot escape retrograde or via ileocecal valve → rapid pressure buildup.
  • Brittle mucosa (e.g., collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) is a secondary risk factor, though less clinically prominent than sigmoid disease.
3. Clinical Presentation and Diagnostic Clues
  • Diagnosis is clinically recognized by astute endoscopist via:
  • Known risk factor (severely diseased sigmoid)
  • Palpation/percussion revealing distended, tympanitic abdomen
  • Perforation typically occurs during insertion, not withdrawal — often before reaching proximal colon.
  • Site of perforation is hard to identify endoscopically due to multiple mucosal breaks, especially with poor prep.
  • Delayed presentation possible if patient passes flatus post-procedure and develops proximal colon ischemia.
4. Management of Acute Barotrauma Perforation
  • Immediate actions required:
  • Needle decompression of peritoneal cavity
  • Initiation of broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Urgent surgical consultation
  • Endoscopic repair is typically not feasible due to multiple breaks and poor visualization.
  • Delayed diagnosis leads to disastrous outcomes — sepsis, abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Recognition hinges on prompt suspicion in high-risk patients — failure to act quickly = poor prognosis.
5. Prevention Strategies
  • Switch to water-filling immediately upon entering a severely diseased sigmoid colon.
  • Trigger for switch:
  • Need to change to thinner instrument to pass sigmoid
  • Struggle with angulation/narrowing during insertion
  • Carbon dioxide insufflation does not prevent acute barotrauma (only reduces delayed risk via rapid absorption).
  • Water-filling is contraindicated in therapeutic procedures (e.g., ESD, EMR) where visualization is critical — but appropriate for diagnostic insertion phase.
  • Prevention must be taught to trainees and partners — “an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure.”

7. Recommendations for Practice Change
  • Routine switch to water-filling upon entering a diseased sigmoid — not optional.
  • Do not wait for signs of resistance — anticipate risk based on anatomy.
  • Educate trainees on why barotrauma is uniquely dangerous:
  • Large perforations + poor prep = high contamination burden
  • Rapid clinical deterioration → narrow window for intervention
  • Document decision-making: explicit note of risk assessment and switch to water-filling.
  • Audit institutional rates of barotrauma — likely underreported if misclassified as “spontaneous” or “unknown cause.”
8. Technical Mechanics of Barotrauma Formation
  • Closed-loop pressurization occurs when:
  • Gas insufflation continues without adequate release pathways
  • Ileocecal valve is competent (non-refluxing) → prevents distal gas escape
  • Colonoscope lumen occludes the sigmoid lumen → blocks retrograde gas escape via anus
  • Pressure builds rapidly in sigmoid-to-ileocecal valve segment → wall stress exceeds tensile strength
  • Threshold for perforation: ~120–150 mmHg intraluminal pressure (based on Woltjen 2005)
  • In severe diverticular disease, wall thickness ↓ and elasticity ↓ → perforates at lower pressures
  • Gas volume required for rupture: ~2–3 L in normal colon vs. <1 L in severely diseased sigmoid
9. Role of Bowel Preparation Quality
  • Suboptimal prep is near-universal in barotrauma cases:
  • Poor sigmoid cleansing → obscures mucosal landmarks and micro-perforations
  • Fecal debris masks early signs of ischemia or mucosal tearing
  • Poor prep correlates with increased insertion difficulty → prolonged procedure time
  • Inadequate prep also reflects underlying stricturing disease (e.g., chronic diverticulitis)
  • In barotrauma cases, poor prep exacerbates contamination post-perforation → higher sepsis risk
  • Endoscopist may misinterpret poor prep as technical difficulty alone → fails to trigger risk mitigation
10. Instrumentation and Technique Modifications
  • Standard colonoscope diameter (~12–13 mm) contributes to luminal occlusion in tight strictures
  • Switching to thin-caliber scopes (e.g., 9.8 mm or pediatric scopes) may reduce risk in select cases
  • Water-filling technique:
  • No insufflation — instead, passive water inflow via suction channel or dedicated irrigation port
  • Allows direct visualization without pressure buildup
  • Enables gentle advancement through angulated segments
  • Water-filling contraindicated during therapeutic interventions requiring clear field (e.g., EMR, ESD)
  • Alternative: Air exchange with CO₂ only prevents delayed barotrauma (due to rapid absorption), not acute perforation
  • Use of water-jet assisted insertion devices (e.g., WaterJet™) under investigation — not yet standard
11. Recognition and Early Warning Signs
  • Red flags during insertion:
  • Sudden resistance at sigmoid flexure despite gentle advancement
  • Patient-reported severe abdominal pain disproportionate to exam findings
  • Inability to pass the sigmoid despite multiple stylets or scope exchanges
  • Physical exam clues:
  • Tympany on percussion of lower abdomen (early sign)
  • Distension without visible bulging (early phase)
  • Endoscopic signs:
  • Mucosal pallor, edema, or petechiae in sigmoid — indicates ischemic injury
  • Blood-tinged water during water-filling attempt → early perforation marker
  • Lack of cecal intubation despite prolonged procedure time may signal hidden complication
  • Failure to aspirate air on withdrawal — suggests trapped gas or closed-loop physiology
12. Imaging and Diagnostic Confirmation
  • Plain abdominal radiograph may show:
  • Free intraperitoneal air (pneumoperitoneum) — but absent in 20–30% of early cases
  • Distended colon with air-fluid levels
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with oral and IV contrast is gold standard:
  • Detects extraluminal gas, contrast extravasation, free fluid
  • Identifies perforation site in ~70% of cases (lower if poor prep)
  • Diagnostic peritoneal aspirate:
  • Positive for bile, stool, or food debris → confirms perforation
  • Labs: amylase ↑ (if duodenal contamination), pH <6.0, WBC >1000/μL
  • Intraoperative findings often reveal multiple perforations — especially in barotrauma vs. single-site in trauma or ischemic cases
13. Endoscopic Repair Feasibility and Limitations
  • Endoscopic closure attempted only if:
  • Single, small perforation (<1 cm)
  • Clean base, no fecal contamination
  • Stable hemodynamics and <12 hours since perforation
  • Techniques used:
  • Clips (over-the-scope clips preferred)
  • Stents (self-expanding metal stents for large defects)
  • Suction-activated closure devices (e.g., Ovesco®)
  • Barotrauma perforations rarely meet criteria:
  • Multiple breaks → impossible to seal all sites
  • Contaminated field → high failure risk and sepsis promotion
  • Failed endoscopic repair leads to delayed surgery → ↑ mortality (up to 25% if >24h delay)
  • Absolute contraindications to endoscopic repair:
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Signs of peritonitis
  • Large volume intraperitoneal gas on imaging
14. Surgical Management Principles
  • Immediate laparotomy indicated if:
  • Clinical peritonitis (rigidity, rebound, guarding)
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Large pneumoperitoneum on CT (>50% of peritoneal cavity)
  • Procedure options:
  • Primary repair (if clean edges, low contamination)
  • Resection with anastomosis (if viable bowel, clean field)
  • Hartmann’s procedure (if contaminated, unstable patient)
  • In barotrauma cases:
  • Sigmoidectomy most common — perforation usually at sigmoid colon
  • Diverting ileostomy often added due to contamination risk
  • Post-op complications:
  • Anastomotic leak (10–20%)
  • Abscess formation
  • Enterocutaneous fistula
15. Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (ACS)
  • Defined as intra-abdominal pressure >20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction
  • Barotrauma → rapid intraperitoneal gas accumulation → ↑ IAP
  • Pathophysiology:
  • Gas distention compresses inferior vena cava → ↓ venous return
  • Diaphragmatic elevation → respiratory compromise (↓ compliance, ↑ peak pressures)
  • Renal perfusion ↓ → acute kidney injury
  • Clinical triad:
  • Hypotension, oliguria, hypoxemia
  • Management:
  • Needle decompression (14-gauge needle at umbilicus or lateral quadrants)
  • Surgical decompression (laparotomy with temporary closure)
  • Mortality >50% if ACS untreated or delayed recognition
16. Antibiotic Strategy and Timing
  • Empiric coverage must cover gram-negative aerobes + anaerobes (e.g., E. coli, Bacteroides)
  • Preferred regimens:
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam (standard)
  • Meropenem (if penicillin allergy or severe sepsis)
  • Ceftriaxone + metronidazole (alternative)
  • Duration:
  • Uncomplicated perforation: 5–7 days
  • Peritonitis or abscess: 7–10 days
  • Early antibiotics (<1h after recognition) ↓ mortality by 30–50%
  • Avoid fluoroquinolone monotherapy — rising resistance in Bacteroides
17. Malpractice Risk and Expert Witness Perspective
  • Barotrauma cases dominate litigation because:
  • High morbidity/mortality → large damages awarded
  • Clear preventability — deviation from standard of care
  • Common defense arguments:
  • “Patient-specific anatomy made perforation unavoidable”
  • “Informed consent included perforation risk”
  • Plaintiffs’ counter:
  • Standard of care mandates water-filling in high-risk sigmoid
  • Failure to recognize early signs = negligence
  • Key documentation gaps in failed cases:
  • No note of sigmoid disease severity assessment
  • No record of risk discussion or alternative technique plan
  • Settlement value correlates with:
  • Permanent colostomy (↑ compensation)
  • Delayed diagnosis (>6h from perforation to intervention)
18. Training and Educational Imperatives
  • Trainees often underestimate sigmoid disease severity — rely on cecal intubation as proxy for success
  • Simulation training should include:
  • Angulated sigmoid models with simulated diverticular disease
  • Water-filling technique drills
  • Curriculum must emphasize:
  • When to stop insufflation (not wait for complications)
  • How to recognize early ACS
  • Grand rounds case reviews should highlight:
  • Subtle warning signs (e.g., patient discomfort without visible struggle)
  • Post-procedure red flags (e.g., persistent pain despite passing flatus)
  • Institutional protocols should mandate:
  • Checklist for sigmoid risk assessment before insertion
  • Mandatory second opinion if >3 attempts to pass sigmoid
19. Quality Improvement and System-Level Interventions
  • Barotrauma rates should be tracked as a safety metric — like perforation rate
  • Define high-risk sigmoid for audit:
  • Need for >2 scope exchanges
  • Angulation >90° on imaging or endoscopy
  • Known diverticular disease on prior imaging
  • Implement automatic alerts in EHR if:
  • Prior sigmoid stricture documented
  • Age >75 + female (anatomic risk)
  • Audit tool: Review all cases where water-filling was not used in high-risk sigmoid
  • Policy recommendation:
  • Water-filling standard of care for all sigmoid passes in patients with known diverticular disease
  • Mandatory documentation of risk assessment and technique choice
20. Patient Communication and Informed Consent
  • Informed consent should explicitly mention barotrauma — not just “perforation” generically
  • High-risk patients should be counseled on:
  • Alternative imaging (CT colonography, MRI) if high procedural risk
  • Split-dose prep + bowel cleansing quality assessment pre-procedure
  • Post-procedure instructions:
  • Red flags for delayed perforation: severe abdominal pain, fever, tachycardia >4h post-procedure
  • 24-h access to endoscopy team for urgent evaluation
  • Discharge checklist:
  • Confirm ability to pass flatus before release
  • Document discussion of barotrauma risk in note
21. Future Directions and Research Gaps
  • Need for prospective validation of water-filling protocol in high-risk sigmoid

22. Summary of Critical Decision Points
  • Before insertion: Assess sigmoid disease severity — use prior imaging, history, and initial scope passage
  • At sigmoid entry: If angulated/narrowed → immediately switch to water-filling
  • During struggle: If >2 attempts or patient pain → pause, reassess, consider water-filling
  • If perforation suspected: Stop insufflation, needle decompress, call surgery
  • Post-procedure: Monitor for delayed symptoms — especially in high-risk anatomy
  • Documentation: Record risk assessment, technique choice, and contingency plan
  • Teaching point: “If you had to do it again, what would you change?” — if answer is “water-filling,” you missed the window
23. Legal and Ethical Considerations
  • Standard of care now includes:
  • Awareness of barotrauma risk in sigmoid disease
  • Use of water-filling as first-line alternative in high-risk cases
  • Failure to switch to water-filling = presumptive negligence in litigation
  • Ethical duty: Prevent harm even if risk is low — barotrauma is rare but catastrophic
  • Non-maleficence principle: Avoid gas insufflation in known high-risk anatomy
  • Justice issue: Disparities in outcomes if water-filling not universally adopted
  • Transparency: Disclose barotrauma events fully — no “unavoidable complication” defense holds

27. Case-Based Learning: Archetypal Barotrauma Scenario
  • Patient: 78F, BMI 24, known diverticular disease (CT-confirmed sigmoid diverticulosis)
  • Prep: Boston score 2/3 in right colon, 1/3 in sigmoid
  • Procedure: Standard colonoscope inserted; passed cecum with difficulty (2 attempts)
  • Intra-op: Patient reports severe left lower quadrant pain at sigmoid; no air aspirated on withdrawal
  • Post-procedure: Discharged home with diagnosis “routine colonoscopy”
  • 6h later: Returns ER with severe abdominal pain, fever 39°C, BP 80/50
  • CT: Pneumoperitoneum, large pelvic fluid collection
  • Outcome: Laparotomy → Hartmann’s procedure; survived but with permanent ileostomy
  • Root cause: Failure to recognize high-risk sigmoid → no water-filling, delayed recognition

  • Low-cost water-filling kits (e.g., IV tubing + saline bag)

  • Water-filling as basic, not advanced technique

31. Technical Nuances of Water-Filling Technique
  • Water-filling ≠ irrigation — no active pumping; relies on gravity and passive inflow
  • Preferred method:
  • Suction channel insertion of 5-French feeding tube or catheter tip
  • Water (room temp, ~25°C) infused at ~10–20 mL/sec
  • Simultaneous gentle suction to maintain luminal patency and visualize mucosa
  • Volume used: ~200–400 mL — enough to distend without overdistension
  • Avoid warm water — may mask ischemia (vasodilation masks pallor)
  • Avoid saline with electrolytes — may irritate mucosa if micro-perforation present
  • Water-filling during withdrawal is not recommended — only for insertion phase
  • Contraindications:
  • Suspected colonic fistula (risk of aspiration)
  • Severe coagulopathy (INR >1.5, platelets <50k)
  • Known large diverticular abscess (risk of rupture during inflow)
32. Instrument Selection and Alternatives
  • Standard adult colonoscope (12.4 mm outer diameter) often too large for narrowed sigmoid
  • Thin-caliber scopes (9.8 mm, e.g., GIF-Q260J):
  • ↓ Luminal occlusion → ↓ risk of closed-loop pressurization
  • May compromise visualization and instrument channel size
  • Pediatric colonoscopes (e.g., GIF-P260):
  • Used in extreme cases — limited therapeutic capability
  • Water-jet insertion devices (e.g., Olympus WaterJet™):
  • Integrated high-pressure water jet to loosen mucosal adhesions
  • Not yet widely adopted — limited evidence in barotrauma prevention
  • Balloon-assisted enteroscopy (e.g., double-balloon):
  • Rarely used for diagnostic colonoscopy — high cost, longer time
  • Flexible video capsules (e.g., PillCam COLON):
  • Alternative for high-risk patients — but misses polyps <5 mm, no biopsy
33. Physiological Basis of Gas Trapping
  • Ileocecal valve competence:
  • Normal: allows retrograde flow during colonic contractions
  • In some patients: functional incompetence absent → gas trapped proximal to valve
  • Sigmoid anatomy:
  • Narrow lumen, sharp angulation (often >90°) → acts as functional obstruction
  • Peristaltic waves cannot overcome resistance → gas accumulates proximal to blockage
  • Colon wall compliance:
  • Healthy colon: distends up to ~500 mL before pressure ↑
  • Diverticular disease: wall stiffens → distension >200 mL triggers perforation
  • Gas absorption kinetics:
  • CO₂ absorbed 25x faster than air (N₂/O₂) — but only prevents delayed barotrauma
  • Acute perforation occurs before absorption can occur (within minutes)
34. Role of Patient Positioning
  • Left lateral decubitus (standard for insertion):
  • May worsen sigmoid angulation due to gravity-dependent kinking
  • Supine or Trendelenburg:
  • May help in very tight angulations — allows gravity to straighten sigmoid
  • Prone position during insertion:
  • Rarely used — limits accessibility and visualization
  • Sigmoidoscopy-first approach:
  • Diagnostic flexible sigmoidoscopy before full colonoscopy → assesses sigmoid anatomy pre-insertion
  • If severe disease noted, switch to water-filling before advancing beyond sigmoid
35. Pre-Procedural Risk Assessment Tools
  • Clinical prediction rules under development:
  • Sigmoid Risk Score (proposed):
  • Age >75 (+1)
  • Female sex (+1)
  • Prior diverticulitis (+2)
  • Prior abdominal surgery (+1)
  • BMI <20 (+1)
  • Score ≥3 → high-risk sigmoid → mandate water-filling
  • Imaging-based risk stratification:
  • CT-measured sigmoid diameter <25 mm → high risk
  • Wall thickness >6 mm on CT → indicates fibrosis and rigidity
  • Endoscopic scoring:
  • Sigmoid Angulation Index (SAI):
  • Grade 1: Smooth passage
  • Grade 2: Moderate resistance, no angulation
  • Grade 3: Angulated turn >90° with difficulty
  • Grade 4: Fixed angulation, cannot pass without stylet
  • SAI ≥3 → water-filling required
36. Intra-Procedural Monitoring Parameters
  • Real-time vital signs:
  • Heart rate ↑ >10 bpm during sigmoid passage → early sign of pain/distension
  • BP ↓ >10 mmHg → indicates vagal stimulation or early compartment syndrome
  • Patient-reported outcomes:
  • Pain score >5/10 during insertion → stop and reassess
  • Nausea/vomiting → sign of proximal distension
  • Endoscopic signs:
  • Mucosal blanching → ischemia
  • Loss of vascular pattern → wall edema
  • Blood on scope tip without trauma → micro-perforation
  • Airway pressure monitoring (in sedated patients):
  • Peak inspiratory pressure ↑ >5 cm H₂O → suggests diaphragmatic compression
37. Complication Timing and Natural History
  • Acute barotrauma:
  • Occurs during insertion — usually before cecal intubation
  • Median time to perforation: 12–18 minutes after sigmoid entry
  • Subacute presentation:
  • Patient discharged, returns <24h with pain/fever
  • Often misdiagnosed as diverticulitis
  • Delayed perforation:
  • >24h post-procedure — usually due to ischemic necrosis from trapped gas
  • Associated with inability to pass flatus post-procedure
  • Latent phase:
  • Gas accumulates in closed loop → mucosal ischemia → full-thickness necrosis
  • Perforation occurs when wall tension exceeds ultimate tensile strength
38. Needle Decompression Technique
  • Indication: Suspected barotrauma with signs of abdominal compartment syndrome
  • Procedure:
  • Site: Left lower quadrant (most common site of perforation) or umbilicus
  • Needle: 14-gauge angiocatheter, 2–3 inches length
  • Insertion: Inferolateral to umbilicus or midclavicular line at McBurney’s point
  • Depth: Penetrate peritoneum → aspirate gas or fluid confirms placement
  • Volume to remove:
  • Initial: 200–500 mL of gas/fluid
  • Repeated if symptoms recur
  • Complications:
  • Bladder perforation (if too medial)
  • Bowel injury (if deep insertion)
  • Alternative: Pneumoperitoneum needle with pressure sensor
39. CT Imaging Protocol for Suspected Barotrauma
  • Non-contrast CT abdomen/pelvis insufficient — requires oral + IV contrast
  • Oral contrast:
  • Water-soluble iodinated agent (e.g., Gastrografin®) — 1000–1500 mL
  • Detects extravasation and micro-perforations missed by air-CT
  • IV contrast: Assesses vascular integrity, organ perfusion
  • Key CT signs:
  • Extraluminal air (sensitivity 85–90%)
  • Extraluminal contrast (specificity >95% for perforation)
  • Free fluid volume >200 mL
  • Mesenteric stranding
  • Quantitative metrics:
  • Pneumoperitoneum score:
  • 0: None
  • 1: Perihepatic only
  • 2: Perihepatic + perisplenic
  • 3: Perihepatic + perisplenic + pelvic
  • Score ≥2 → urgent surgery consult
40. Endoscopic Repair Success Metrics
  • Success defined as:
  • No leak on contrast study within 24h
  • No surgical intervention within 7 days
  • Success rates:
  • Single defect <1 cm: ~85%
  • Multiple defects: <20%
  • Contaminated field: ~10%
  • Clips required:
  • Standard clips: 3–5 per defect
  • Over-the-scope clips (Ovesco®): 1–2 per defect
  • Adjuvant techniques:
  • Fibrin glue applied over clips
  • Stent placement for large defects (e.g., 20-mm fully covered SEMS)
  • Failure predictors:
  • Time to repair >6h
  • Temperature >38.5°C pre-procedure
  • WBC >15,000/mm³
41. Surgical Repair Classification
  • Perforation size categories:
  • Small: <1 cm
  • Medium: 1–2 cm
  • Large: >2 cm
  • Contamination grading:
  • Clean: No fecal material
  • Moderate: Fecal slurry
  • Severe: Feces, abscess, necrotic tissue
  • Repair options by category:
  • Small + clean: Primary suture + drain
  • Medium + moderate: Resection + anastomosis
  • Large + severe: Hartmann’s procedure
  • Laparoscopic conversion:
  • If initial laparoscopy reveals extensive contamination → convert to open
  • Post-op complications:
  • Leak: 15–25% (higher in contaminated fields)
  • Bleeding: 3–7%
  • Ileus: >50% (usually resolves in 5–7d)
42. Antibiotic Stewardship in Barotrauma
  • Empiric regimens:
  • First-line: Piperacillin-tazobactam 3.125g IV q6h
  • Covers E. coli, Bacteroides, Klebsiella
  • Alternative: Meropenem 1g IV q8h (if penicillin anaphylaxis)
  • Duration:
  • Uncomplicated: 5d
  • Peritonitis: 7–10d
  • Abscess: 14d (after drainage)
  • De-escalation:
  • If culture available: narrow spectrum based on sensitivity
  • Avoid carbapenems unless septic shock
  • Prophylaxis in repair:
  • Single dose pre-incision → no post-op antibiotics if clean
43. Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (ACS) Management
  • Definition: IAP >20 mmHg + new organ dysfunction
  • Monitoring:
  • Bladder pressure measurement (gold standard):
  • 50 mL saline instilled → measure at suprapubic line, mid-axillary
  • Repeat q2h if suspected ACS
  • Grading:
  • Grade I: 21–25 mmHg
  • Grade II: 26–35 mmHg
  • Grade III: >35 mmHg
  • Interventions by grade:
  • Grade I: Optimize volume status, sedation, paralysis
  • Grade II: Needle decompression + consider sedation pause
  • Grade III: Immediate surgical decompression
  • Pharmacologic agents:
  • Sedatives (e.g., propofol) ↓ intra-abdominal pressure by 10–15%
  • Neuromuscular blockade (e.g., rocuronium) ↓ IAP further
  • Diuretics ineffective — no role in ACS
