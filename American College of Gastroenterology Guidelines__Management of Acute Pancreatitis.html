American College of Gastroenterology Guidelines:
Management of Acute Pancreatitis

  • 4. What is the definition of pancreatic necrosis in terms of size or percentage of the pancreas involved?
  • 5. What is the preferred route for delivering enteral feeding in patients with moderately severe or severe acute pancreatitis?
Diagnosis of Acute Pancreatitis
  • Diagnosis is established by identifying 2 of the following 3 criteria.
Diagnostic Criteria
Abdominal Pain
  • Typically epigastric or left upper quadrant, constant, with radiation to the back, chest, or flanks.
  • Pain described as dull, colicky, or in the lower abdomen is not consistent with AP.
  • Intensity and location of pain do not correlate with severity.
Serum Enzymes
  • Serum amylase and/or lipase greater than 3 times the upper limit of normal.
Imaging Findings
  • Characteristic findings from abdominal imaging (e.g., CT, MRI).
Laboratory Evaluation
  • Once the diagnosis is established, there is no reason to serially follow serum amylase or lipase levels.
Serum Lipase
  • Preferred over amylase for diagnosis.
  • More specific and remains elevated longer than amylase.
  • In some patient groups (e.g., diabetics), a cutoff of > 3-5 times the upper limit of normal may be needed.
Serum Amylase
  • Rises within hours of onset and returns to normal within 3–5 days.
  • May be within the normal range on admission in as many as one-fifth of patients.
  • Can be normal in alcohol-induced AP and hypertriglyceridemia.
Role of Imaging in Diagnosis
  • Routine early CT is not recommended for diagnosis or severity assessment.
Indications for Imaging
  • Reserved for patients where the diagnosis is unclear.
  • Recommended for patients who fail to improve clinically within the first 48–72 hours to assess for local complications.
Computed Tomography (CT)
  • Contrast-enhanced CT provides > 90% sensitivity and specificity for diagnosis.
  • Newer techniques like perfusion CT may detect necrosis earlier.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Comparable to CT for early assessment.
  • Advantages in patients with contrast allergy or renal insufficiency.
  • More accurate for detecting stones in the common bile duct (CBD) and pancreatic duct disruption.
Etiology of Acute Pancreatitis
  • The cause of AP can be established in most patients through initial evaluation.
Common Causes
Gallstones
  • Most common cause, accounting for 40%–70% of cases.
  • Transabdominal ultrasound should be performed on all patients with AP.
  • If the initial ultrasound is inconclusive, a repeat study is suggested.
Alcohol
  • Second most common cause, accounting for 25%–35% of cases.
  • Diagnosis requires a history of > 5 years of moderate or heavy alcohol consumption (> 50 g per day).
  • Only up to 5% of heavy drinkers develop clinically evident AP, suggesting other cofactors like genetics and tobacco use.
Other Etiologies
  • Caution must be exercised when attributing AP to rarer causes.
Hypertriglyceridemia
  • Accounts for up to 5% of all AP cases, and up to 56% of AP in pregnancy.
  • Serum triglyceride level should be > 1,000 mg/dL to be considered the cause.
  • The risk of AP increases by 4% for every 100 mg/dL of TG above the normal limit.
Pancreatic Tumors
  • Should be suspected in any patient > 40 years old with idiopathic pancreatitis.
  • Approximately 1% of AP cases are due to pancreatic cancer.
  • Requires evaluation with contrast-enhanced CT or MRI/MRCP.
Medications
  • A rare cause of AP.
  • Drugs with a clear association include 6-mercaptopurine, azathioprine, and didanosine.
Idiopathic and Recurrent AP
  • Recurrent AP occurs in approximately 20%–29% of patients after an initial attack.
Diagnostic Evaluation
  • For idiopathic AP (IAP), additional evaluation with repeat ultrasound, MRI, and/or endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) is suggested.
  • EUS can identify an etiology in almost a third of patients after an initial attack of IAP.
Role of Cholecystectomy in IAP
  • Gallstones or microlithiasis are often the underlying cause of IAP.
  • Following a second episode of AP with no identifiable cause, cholecystectomy is suggested to reduce recurrence.
  • The number needed to treat to prevent one attack is 5.
Initial Assessment & Risk Stratification
  • Early risk assessment is crucial to triage patients to the appropriate level of care.
Severity Classification (Revised Atlanta)
Mild AP
  • Absence of organ failure and local or systemic complications.
Moderately Severe AP
  • Characterized by transient organ failure (resolves within 48 hours) and/or the presence of local complications (e.g., fluid collections, necrosis).
Severe AP
  • Defined by the presence of persistent organ failure (fails to resolve within 48 hours).
Morphologic Classification
Interstitial Edematous Pancreatitis
  • Corresponds to mild disease, characterized by pancreatic edema.
Necrotizing Pancreatitis
  • Defined as diffuse or focal areas of nonviable pancreatic parenchyma > 3 cm in size or > 30% of the pancreas.
  • Includes peripancreatic necrosis (~45%), combined pancreatic and peripancreatic necrosis (~45%), and pure pancreatic necrosis (~5%).
Predicting Severe Disease
  • Scoring systems (Ranson, APACHE) are cumbersome and often inaccurate in the first 48 hours.
  • Expert clinician judgment and simple markers are often as effective.
Key Risk Factors for Severe Disease
  • Patient characteristics: Age > 55, Obesity (BMI > 30), altered mental status, comorbidities.
  • Laboratory findings: BUN > 20 mg/dL, rising BUN, HCT > 44%, rising HCT, elevated creatinine.
  • Radiology findings: Pleural effusions, pulmonary infiltrates.
Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS)
  • Defined by the presence of ≥2 of the following: Pulse > 90, Respirations > 20, Temp > 38°C or < 36°C, WBC > 12,000 or < 4,000.
  • Presence of SIRS at admission is highly predictive of developing organ failure.
  • SIRS has a sensitivity of 85% for predicting organ failure and 100% for mortality, but lacks specificity.
Triage and Monitoring
  • Patients with organ failure and/or SIRS should be admitted to a monitored bed setting or ICU.
  • Clinicians should remain vigilant for deterioration during the initial 48 hours, even in patients with seemingly mild disease.
Initial Management: Fluid Resuscitation
  • Intravenous hydration is the cornerstone of initial treatment for AP.
Fluid Type
  • We suggest using Lactated Ringer's solution over normal saline.
Benefits of Lactated Ringer's
  • Reduces systemic inflammation compared to saline.
  • More pH-balanced, avoiding the non-anion gap hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis associated with large volumes of normal saline.
Fluid Rate and Volume
  • We suggest moderately aggressive fluid resuscitation, especially within the first 24 hours.
Recommended Rates
  • In patients with no evidence of hypovolemia, an initial rate of no more than 1.5 mL/kg/hr should be administered.
  • In patients with hypovolemia, a bolus of 10 mL/kg should be given.
  • Most patients will likely benefit from 3–4 Liters in the first 24 hours.
Cautions
  • Aggressive hydration beyond 48 hours or in patients with established severe disease may be harmful.
  • Caution is needed in older patients and those with cardiovascular or renal comorbidities.
Monitoring Resuscitation
  • Fluid volumes need to be reassessed at frequent intervals, starting within 6 hours of presentation.
Goals of Therapy
  • The primary goal is to decrease the BUN.
  • A decrease in HCT (hemodilution) is also associated with better outcomes.
  • Monitor for signs of volume overload, such as pulmonary edema.
Role of ERCP in Acute Pancreatitis
  • The role of ERCP is limited and depends on the clinical context.
Acute Biliary Pancreatitis with Cholangitis
  • In patients with AP complicated by cholangitis, early ERCP (within the first 24 hours) is recommended.
  • This has been shown to decrease morbidity and mortality.
Acute Biliary Pancreatitis without Cholangitis
  • We suggest medical therapy over early ERCP (within the first 72 hours).
  • Most CBD stones pass spontaneously, and early ERCP in this setting does not reduce complications or mortality.
Screening for CBD Stones
  • In the absence of cholangitis or jaundice, if a CBD stone is suspected, MRCP or EUS should be used for screening.
  • Diagnostic ERCP should be avoided to prevent unnecessary risk.
Preventing Post-ERCP Pancreatitis (PEP)
  • AP is the most common complication of ERCP, with an incidence of 1%–30%.
Pharmacologic Prophylaxis
  • We recommend rectal indomethacin to prevent PEP in high-risk individuals.
  • The standard dose is a single 100 mg suppository given before or immediately after ERCP.
Mechanical Prophylaxis
  • We suggest placement of a pancreatic duct stent in patients at high risk for PEP who are also receiving rectal indomethacin.
  • Combination therapy is superior to indomethacin alone in high-risk patients at centers of expertise.
Periprocedural Hydration
  • Periprocedcedural hydration with Lactated Ringer's solution has been shown to prevent PEP.
  • One effective protocol is 3 mL/kg/hr during ERCP, a 20 mL/kg bolus after, followed by an 8-hour infusion at 3 mL/kg/hr.
  • Ongoing aggressive hydration post-ERCP may not be effective if rectal indomethacin is also used.
The Role of Antibiotics in AP
  • The use of antibiotics is controversial and depends on the clinical scenario.
Prophylactic Use in Sterile Necrosis
  • We suggest against prophylactic antibiotics in patients with severe AP and sterile necrosis.
  • Multiple well-designed, placebo-controlled trials have failed to show a benefit in reducing mortality or infection of necrosis.
Treatment of Infected Necrosis
  • Antibiotics are a key part of treatment for infected necrosis, along with eventual debridement/necrosectomy.
  • Antibiotics are often used to delay intervention beyond 4 weeks to allow the collection to mature.
  • Some patients may resolve the infection with antibiotics alone and avoid drainage.
Choice of Antibiotics
  • Antibiotics known to penetrate pancreatic necrosis should be used.
  • Examples include: carbapenems, quinolones, cephalosporins, and metronidazole.
Role of CT-Guided Fine-Needle Aspiration (FNA)
  • We suggest against routine FNA in patients with suspected infected pancreatic necrosis.
  • Management decisions are often not influenced by FNA results, as antibiotics are often started on clinical suspicion alone.
Antifungal Agents
  • Routine administration of antifungal agents along with antibiotics is not needed.
Nutrition in AP
  • Nutritional strategy has shifted from 'gut resting' to 'gut rousing' to maintain mucosal integrity and prevent bacterial translocation.
Nutrition in Mild AP
  • We suggest early oral feeding (within 24–48 hours) as tolerated.
  • This approach reduces hospital stay and costs.
Diet Type
  • We suggest initiating feeding with a low-fat solid diet.
  • A stepwise approach from clear liquids to solids is not necessary and provides fewer calories.
Nutrition in Moderately Severe & Severe AP
  • Enteral nutrition is preferred to prevent infectious complications by maintaining the gut barrier.
Enteral vs. Parenteral
  • Enteral nutrition is superior to Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN).
  • TPN is associated with more infectious and line-related complications and should be avoided unless the enteral route is not feasible.
Route of Enteral Feeding
  • A nasogastric route is preferred over a nasojejunal route.
  • Nasogastric feeding is comparable in safety and efficacy, and tube placement is easier and less expensive.
Timing of Enteral Feeding
  • The timing is controversial.
  • A large randomized trial found that early enteral feeding (within 24 hours) did not reduce infection rates or mortality compared to on-demand feeding.
The Role of Surgery in AP
  • Surgical management has evolved significantly, favoring delayed and minimally invasive approaches.
Cholecystectomy for Biliary Pancreatitis
  • In patients with mild gallstone pancreatitis, cholecystectomy should be performed early, preferably during the same hospital admission.
  • This reduces recurrent biliary events (readmission rate of 18% in delayed group vs 0% in early group) and is cost-effective.
  • In patients with necrosis, cholecystectomy is typically delayed.
Management of Pancreatic Necrosis
  • The approach to necrosis has shifted from early, aggressive surgery to a more conservative, delayed strategy.
Timing of Intervention
  • We suggest delaying any intervention (surgical, radiological, endoscopic) in stable patients with pancreatic necrosis.
  • The preferred delay is > 4 weeks to allow for the wall of the collection to mature (walled-off necrosis).
Infected Necrosis Management
  • Urgent surgery is no longer considered mandatory for all patients with infected necrosis.
  • Initial management for stable patients is a 2- to 4-week course of antibiotics.
  • Delaying necrosectomy until > 30 days after admission is associated with decreased mortality.
Minimally Invasive Approaches
  • Minimally invasive methods are preferred to open surgery for necrosectomy in stable patients.
  • These methods include percutaneous drainage, video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement (VARD), and endoscopic necrosectomy.
Step-Up Approach
  • A step-up approach (e.g., percutaneous catheter drainage followed by VARD) has shown lower morbidity and costs compared to open surgery.
  • Percutaneous drainage alone is successful in avoiding surgery in approximately 50% of cases.
Endoscopic Necrosectomy
  • Involves creating a transluminal opening (e.g., through the stomach) to remove necrotic tissue.
  • One randomized trial found endoscopic necrosectomy to be superior to surgical necrosectomy.
