üè• Indications for Hospitalization (BPA 1)
  • - Consider prompt hospitalization for patients with severe disease refractory to outpatient treatment.
  • - Also indicated for high suspicion of IBD-related complications or significant nutritional risk.
Key Presentations Warranting Admission
  • - Uncontrolled inflammation unresponsive to outpatient therapy.
  • - Suspicion of complications like intestinal obstruction, perforation, or intra-abdominal abscess.
  • - Severe symptoms/conditions requiring inpatient care:
  • - Dehydration and electrolyte derangements.
  • - Short bowel syndrome.
  • - Uncontrolled pain.
  • - Severe anemia requiring transfusions.
  • - Severe malnutrition requiring enteral or parenteral nutrition.

Acute Severe Ulcerative Colitis (ASUC)
  • - ASUC is a traditional indication for urgent hospitalization for IV corticosteroid therapy and exclusion of infection.
Truelove and Witts Criteria
  • - Defined as 6 or more bloody bowel movements daily AND at least one systemic sign.
Systemic Signs
  • - Tachycardia: Heart rate >90 beats/min
  • - Fever: Temperature >37.8 ¬∞C
  • - Anemia: Hemoglobin <10.5 g/dL
  • - Elevated ESR: >30 mm/h

Prognostic Value
  • - These criteria predict the risk of progression to colectomy.
  • - Colectomy rates rise from 8.5% to 48% based on the number of criteria met.


Role of C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • - CRP has a shorter half-life than ESR and is more responsive to acute inflammation.
  • - It has largely replaced ESR as a real-time inflammatory biomarker and predictor of colectomy.


Modern Hospitalization Trends
  • - Patients are now more likely hospitalized for IBD refractory to multiple steroid-sparing immune therapies rather than a classic fulminant ASUC presentation.


üìã Initial Inpatient Management (BPA 2 & 3)
  • - Initial management should include supportive care and evaluation for complications and infections.
Supportive Care (BPA 2)
  • - Perform a standard assessment with an IBD-tailored history, physical exam, and laboratory testing.
Core Supportive Measures
  • - IV hydration and electrolyte repletion.
  • - Treatment for acute anemias.
  • - Pain control as needed.

Nutritional Support
  • - Screen all patients for malnutrition using validated tools (e.g., Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool).
  • - Refer to a dietitian for a complete evaluation if indicated.
  • - Screen for vitamin deficiencies, especially B12 and D, and iron deficiency.
IV Iron Therapy
  • - Consider for patients with active inflammation, moderate to severe anemia (especially Hb <10 g/dL), or intolerance to oral iron.


Pain Management Strategy
  • - Data suggests 1 in 4 hospitalized IBD patients receive inadequate pain control.
  • - Address underlying factors: NG decompression for obstruction, abscess drainage, treating inflammation.
  • - Use a sequential stepped approach and shared decision-making.
  • - Avoid Opioids: Due to risks of narcotic bowel syndrome and addiction.
  • - Caution with NSAIDs: Potential to exacerbate intestinal ulceration.
  • - Consider Neuromodulators: Tricyclic antidepressants, SNRIs, and mirtazapine have shown efficacy for chronic GI pain.


Evaluation for Complications & Infections (BPA 3)
  • - A significant portion of IBD symptom flares are driven by concomitant gastrointestinal infections.
Infection Testing
  • - Stool Pathogens: Test all patients with diarrhea with stool culture and C. difficile toxin testing (using a 2-step approach).
  • - Cytomegalovirus (CMV): Evaluate for CMV in patients with worsening symptoms, systemic toxicity, or lack of response to corticosteroids.

Imaging for Complications
  • - Ulcerative Colitis:
  • - For suspected toxic megacolon (>6 cm transverse colon dilation), get a timely abdominal film and/or CT scan and consult colorectal surgery.
  • - Crohn's Disease:
  • - For suspected bowel obstruction or abscess, perform cross-sectional imaging (MR or CT) with IV contrast.
  • - Intestinal Ultrasound: Evolving evidence for its use as a diagnostic or monitoring tool for suspected bowel obstruction.



üìä Disease Activity Assessment (BPA 4)
  • - Assess disease activity with both biomarkers and endoscopic evaluation when indicated.
Biomarkers
  • - Objective serologic markers can assess disease activity at presentation.
Serologic Markers
  • - CRP: Valuable for predicting colectomy risk. A CRP ‚â•1.2 mg/dL (or ‚â•12 mg/L) has an 85% positive predictive value for severe disease.
  • - Other markers: Leukocyte count, platelet count, and albumin.
  • - CRP to Albumin Ratio: A high ratio is independently predictive of active disease.

Fecal Calprotectin
  • - Strongly correlates with endoscopic disease activity.
  • - Limitation: Processing can take up to 7 days, limiting its utility in the acute inpatient setting.
  • - A baseline measurement can be valuable for later trending.


Endoscopic Evaluation
  • - Early endoscopy is important for assessing inflammation severity and obtaining biopsies.
Procedure & Timing
  • - A flexible sigmoidoscopy with limited or no prep is often sufficient.
  • - Early endoscopy (within 48 hours of admission) is associated with decreased length of stay and hospital costs.

Ruling out CMV
  • - In cases of systemic toxicity, corticosteroid non-response, or ASUC, obtain mucosal biopsies to exclude CMV infection.
  • - Diagnosis requires histologic examination (H&E stain with immunohistochemistry).
  • - Treatment Criteria: Limit treatment to patients with clinically active IBD, high-density histologic CMV (5 or more inclusions), and non-response to corticosteroids.



üíâ VTE Prophylaxis (BPA 5)
  • - Hospitalized patients with IBD have a 2-fold increased risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE).
  • - Inflammation and corticosteroid use contribute to a hypercoagulable state.
Recommendation
  • - All hospitalized IBD patients without absolute contraindications should receive prophylactic anticoagulation.
  • - Options include subcutaneous low-molecular-weight heparin or fondaparinux.

Efficacy
  • - Prophylaxis is effective at reducing VTE events.
  • - Number Needed to Treat (NNT): 35
  • - Number Needed to Harm (NNH) for major bleeding: 114

Post-Discharge Prophylaxis
  • - Decision to continue after discharge should be individualized for high-risk patients (e.g., recent surgery, active malignancy, immobilization).


üî• Management of Severe Ulcerative Colitis (ASUC)
  • - A multi-faceted approach involving surgical consultation, medical therapy, and close monitoring is crucial.
Surgical Consultation & Counseling (BPA 6)
  • - Overall, 10% of hospitalized patients with UC will require surgery.
  • - Engage the surgical team early during hospitalization and counsel patients on the potential need for colectomy.
Toxic Megacolon
  • - A highly morbid complication presenting with acute colonic dilation (>6 cm) and systemic toxicity.
  • - Often requires emergent surgery.

Perioperative Management
  • - Consider transfer to a high-volume IBD center for non-responders.
  • - Recent data suggests no beneficial role for perioperative stress-dose corticosteroids.
  • - Follow an Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocol.


Initial Medical Therapy (BPA 7)
  • - First-line therapy for most hospitalized severe UC patients is intravenous corticosteroids.
Dosing
  • - Optimal dose is methylprednisolone 40‚Äì60 mg daily equivalent.
  • - A dose of 60 mg daily is common practice.
  • - Doses >60 mg/day confer no additional benefit and increase adverse effects.

Important Considerations
  • - Initiation should not be delayed while awaiting pathogen testing results.
  • - Should not be discontinued if C. difficile or CMV are detected, as long as pathogen-specific treatment is initiated concurrently.


Monitoring Treatment Response (BPA 8)
  • - Response to medical therapy should be assessed within 72 hours of initiation.
Oxford Criteria (Day 3 Assessment)
  • - Assess changes in stool frequency and CRP on day 3 of corticosteroid treatment.
  • - Predicts future colectomy risk.
High Risk for Colectomy
  • - 85% of patients will require colectomy if on day 3 they have either:
  • - 8 or more bowel movements daily, OR
  • - 3‚Äì8 bowel movements daily with a CRP >4.5 mg/dL.


Duration of Steroid Trial
  • - Continuing IV corticosteroid treatment beyond 7 days in non-responders provides no incremental benefit and increases risk of complications and mortality.

Intestinal Ultrasound
  • - An emerging tool for monitoring response.
  • - Significant changes in bowel wall thickness can be seen as early as 48 hours after starting corticosteroids in responders.


Rescue Therapy for Non-Responders (BPA 9)
  • - Preparations for rescue or salvage therapies should be initiated early in the admission for patients not responding to IV corticosteroids.
Medication Options
  • - Infliximab
  • - Cyclosporine
  • - Janus Kinase (JAK) inhibitors (e.g., tofacitinib, upadacitinib)

Infliximab Dosing
  • - The optimal dosing for ASUC is not yet known.
  • - A recent study showed no significant difference in response between intensified (10 mg/kg or accelerated induction) vs. standard (5 mg/kg) dosing.
  • - Consider accelerated dosing (e.g., 10 mg/kg) for patients at risk of treatment failure due to protein-losing colopathy (low albumin, high CRP).

JAK Inhibitors
  • - Optimal dose is unknown, but both standard and off-label high-intensity doses have been used effectively.
  • - Vaccination: Patients should receive a varicella zoster vaccine, but treatment initiation should not be delayed. The recombinant vaccine can be given after starting the JAK inhibitor.



üå™Ô∏è Management of Crohn's Disease Complications
  • - Inpatient management often focuses on complications like obstruction, abscesses, and perianal disease.
Intestinal Obstruction (BPA 10)
  • - Patients often present with abdominal distention, nausea, and vomiting.
Initial Management
  • - In absence of peritonitis or ischemia:
  • - Bowel rest
  • - Nasogastric decompression
  • - Intravenous fluids and electrolytes.

Diagnosis & Treatment
  • - Imaging: CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast (without oral contrast) is usually sufficient.
  • - Medical: If active inflammation is present, consider IV corticosteroids.
  • - Surgical: Indicated for predominantly fibrotic strictures or complications (perforation, abscess).
  • - Preoperatively, taper steroids to prednisone 20 mg daily if possible to reduce infection risk.

Post-Acute Management
  • - After resolution, consider endoscopic balloon-assisted dilation for short-segment (<5 cm) non-inflammatory strictures.


Intra-abdominal Abscess (BPA 11)
  • - Requires a multidisciplinary approach involving gastroenterology, colorectal surgery, and interventional radiology.
Initial Treatment
  • - Infection Control:
  • - IV antibiotics with GI flora coverage (e.g., ciprofloxacin and metronidazole).
  • - Percutaneous drainage should be pursued if the abscess is >2‚Äì3 cm.
  • - Urgent Surgery: Required for patients with peritonitis or uncontrolled sepsis.

Medical & Nutritional Therapy
  • - Once source control is achieved, treatment with steroid-sparing immune therapies (e.g., anti-TNF) is acceptable.
  • - Exclusive enteral nutrition can improve outcomes in select patients with undrainable abscesses.


Active Perianal Disease (BPA 12)
  • - Best managed with a combined medical and surgical approach.
Core Management
  • - Surgical: Examination under anesthesia, incision and drainage of abscesses, and seton placement are key immediate steps.
  • - Imaging: Contrast-enhanced CT or MR can delineate complex fistula tracts.
  • - Optimal Therapy: The combination of surgery, antibiotics, and anti-TNF therapy is considered the optimal approach.

Refractory Disease
  • - For severe refractory disease, a diverting colostomy or ileostomy can be a temporizing measure.



üîö Discharge Planning (BPA 13)
  • - A clear discharge transition plan is critical to prevent common and costly readmissions.
Discharge Criteria
  • - Discharge is appropriate after resolution or stabilization of the acute admission indication.
  • - Does not necessarily require normalization of inflammatory biomarkers (e.g., CRP) or imaging findings.

Transition Plan Components
  • - Communication: Identify and communicate with responsible outpatient clinicians.
  • - Patient Education: Provide clear instructions on corticosteroid tapering, outpatient infusion scheduling, and when to contact the medical team.
  • - Address Barriers: Proactively manage social determinants like prior authorizations, medication affordability, and transportation challenges.
