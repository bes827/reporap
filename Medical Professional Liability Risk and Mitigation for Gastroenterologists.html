Medical Professional Liability Risk and Mitigation for Gastroenterologists
  • Source: New Gastroenterol. 2021.
  • - A focused review for GI trainees, early-career, and seasoned gastroenterologists on Medical Professional Liability (MPL) concepts, trends, risk scenarios, and mitigation strategies.

Principles of Medical Professional Liability (MPL)
  • - MPL falls under tort law, which is a branch of civil law (as opposed to criminal law).
  • - A tort is a 'civil wrong that unfairly causes another to experience loss or harm resulting in legal liability'.
Basis of MPL Claims: Negligence
  • - MPL claims assert the tort of negligence.
  • - Aims to compensate the harmed patient and dissuade sub-optimal medical care.

Four Elements of a Successful Claim
  • - A successful MPL claim must prove all four elements.
1. Duty of Care
  • - The provider (tortfeasor) owed a duty of care to the injured party.

2. Breach of Duty
  • - The provider breached that duty of care.

3. Causation
  • - The breach of duty directly caused the damages.

4. Damages
  • - The patient experienced quantifiable harm or loss.


Burden of Proof
  • - The standard is not 'beyond a reasonable doubt' as in criminal law.
  • - The standard is 'to a reasonable medical probability'.


Trends in MPL Claims
  • - Gastroenterology (GI) is the 2nd-highest internal medicine subspecialty in both MPL claims made and paid.
Overall Claim Statistics (1985–2012)
  • - GI ranked 17th out of 20 specialties surveyed for total claims.
  • - GI-related claims constituted 1.8% of the 278,220 total MPL claims.

Claim Volume vs. Payout (1985-2012)
  • - The number of paid GI claims decreased by 34.6% (from 18.5 to 12.1 cases per 1,000 physician-years).
  • - Simultaneously, the average claim compensation (payout) increased by 23.3%.

Recent Costs (2009-2018)
  • - Average legal defense costs for paid GI claims: $97,392.
  • - Average paid amount per claim: $330,876.


GI-Specific MPL Risk Scenarios
  • - Many claims relate to medical errors or adverse events (AEs), both procedural and non-procedural.
Informed Consent
  • - Claims can arise from both inadequately informed consent and inadequately informed refusal.
  • - Most states apply the 'reasonable patient standard': providing enough information for an average patient to be an active participant in decision-making.
Key Components of Consent (Figure 1)
  • - 1. Nature of the proposed procedure/treatment.
  • - 2. Risks and benefits of undergoing vs. not undergoing it.
  • - 3. Presentation of reasonable alternatives.
  • - 4. Risks and benefits of those alternatives.
  • - 5. Assessment of the patient's comprehension of all points.

Best Practices
  • - Tailor consent to each patient and procedure; avoid a one-size-fits-all approach.
  • - Document the patient's understanding of the specific, tailored information provided to improve consent quality and decrease MPL risk.


Endoscopic Procedures
  • - Procedure-related claims represent approximately 25% of all GI-related MPL claims.
Claim Breakdown by Procedure
  • - Colonoscopy: 52% of procedure-related claims.
  • - ERCP: 16%.
  • - EGD: 11%.

Post-Procedure AE Management
  • - If signs/symptoms of a potential AE develop (e.g., tachycardia, fever, pain, hypotension):
  • - ---> Initiate stabilizing measures and evaluation (e.g., bloodwork, imaging).
  • - ---> Consider hospital admission until discharge is deemed safe.

ERCP-Specific Risks
  • - ERCP has historically had the highest average compensation of any GI procedure.
  • - A majority of claims involve an allegation that the procedure was not indicated or was performed for diagnostic purposes instead of using non-invasive imaging.

PEG Placement Risks
  • - Carries a high potential for MPL claims despite a low AE rate (0.1–1%).
  • - High morbidity/mortality from AEs is often due to severe underlying patient comorbidities.
  • - Timing of a patient's demise may coincide with PEG placement, prompting unfounded perceptions of causality.
Safeguards for PEG in Patients Lacking Capacity
  • - Ask the family to select one surrogate decision-maker.
  • - Discuss risks, benefits, and goals in the context of the patient's overall clinical trajectory and life expectancy.
  • - Base consent or refusal on what the patient would have wished.
  • - Consider having a medical professional witness this discussion.



Peri-procedural Anti-thrombotic Agents
  • - Management of anti-coagulants and anti-platelets can be complex and pose MPL risk.
Key Considerations
  • - Procedural bleeding risk.
  • - Indication for anti-thrombotic therapy and risk of a thrombotic event.
  • - Duration of action of the agent and available bridging options.

Optimal Management Strategy (Figure 1)
  • - Engage in shared decision-making.
  • - Discuss with the provider managing the anti-thrombotic therapy to conduct a risk-benefit assessment.
  • - Involve the patient in the final decision.



Provider Defensive Mechanisms
  • - Behaviors intended to mitigate MPL risk can, paradoxically, increase it.
Assurance Behaviors
  • - The practice of recommending or performing additional services (e.g., imaging, procedures, referrals) that are not clearly indicated.
  • - Driven by fear of MPL risk or missing a diagnosis.
Prevalence & Consequences
  • - > 50% of gastroenterologists worldwide have performed procedures without clear indications.
  • - Nearly one-third of annual endoscopic procedures have questionable indications.
  • - This can inadvertently increase AE risk, MPL risk, and healthcare costs.


Avoidance Behaviors
  • - Providers avoiding participation in potentially high-risk clinical interventions, even those for which they are credentialed.
  • - Driven by fear of MPL risk.
Examples
  • - An advanced endoscopist referring a 'high-risk' elderly patient with cholangitis for ERCP to another provider.
  • - A gastroenterologist referring a large polyp resection they are proficient in performing.

Negative Consequences
  • - Patients may not receive indicated interventions or experience delays in care.
  • - Increased potential for loss to follow-up.
  • - Can be viewed as non-compliance with the standard of care, thus increasing MPL risk.



Documentation Tenets
  • - Thorough documentation can significantly decrease MPL risk as it is often used as legal evidence.
  • - Remember the adage: 'If it’s not documented, it did not occur.'
Key Areas to Document (Figure 1)
  • - Pre-procedure discussion of potential AEs (e.g., bleeding, perforation), risk of procedural failure (e.g., missed lesions), and medication/sedation risks.
  • - Steps taken during a procedure and anatomic landmarks encountered to prove competency.
  • - Use of language services and provision of translated materials.

'Curbside' vs. Formal Consults
  • - Curbside: A general query where information is provided. Does not require documentation.
  • - Consultation: Providing medical advice for a specific patient (e.g., identifiers shared, EHR accessed). Should be documented, even if seemingly trivial.

Language & Cultural Factors (Figure 1)
  • - Use 24/7 telephone interpreter services for non-English speakers.
  • - Provide native/preferred language written materials (e.g., consent forms) when available.
  • - Document all such efforts to best protect against MPL claims.


Challenges Posed by Telemedicine
  • - Increased utilization does not lessen the clinician’s duty to the patient and may expose them to greater MPL risk.
Principle 1: Standard of Care
  • - The same standard of care applies to both virtual and in-person encounters.
  • - Do not overlook necessary pre-procedure workups (e.g., anesthesia consult, labs) for patients seen via telemedicine.

Principle 2: Privacy & HIPAA
  • - Patient privacy and HIPAA regulations are not waived during telemedicine encounters.
  • - Breaches of privacy may result in an MPL claim.

Procedure Triage
  • - During periods of decreased procedural capacity, triaging can be challenging.
  • - It is advisable to use standardized institutional criteria to prioritize procedures, while allowing for justifiable exceptions.


Vicarious Liability
  • - Liability can extend to individuals who did not commit a wrong but on whose behalf the wrongdoer acted.
  • - Gastroenterologists may be liable not only for their own actions but also for those of personnel they supervise (e.g., fellow trainees, non-physician practitioners).
Purpose & Model
  • - Aims to ensure systemic checks and balances are in place to mitigate or avoid harm.
  • - Illustrated by Reason’s 'Swiss Cheese Model' of system failure.


Conclusion & Key Mitigation Strategies
  • - Being informed about MPL tenets and high-risk situations can assuage anxiety and highlight proactive risk mitigation strategies.
General Mitigation Practices (Figure 1)
  • - Effective communication with patients.
  • - Adequate informed consent/refusal processes.
  • - Thorough documentation of counseling, peri-procedure events, and post-procedure recommendations.
  • - Maintenance of proper certification and privileging.
