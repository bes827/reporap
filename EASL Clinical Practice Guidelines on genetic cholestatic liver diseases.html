General Recommendations
  • - These guidelines focus on monogenic disorders causing defects in bile formation and/or flow.
  • - While common in young age, genetic variants can manifest later in life.
  • - Heterozygous carriers may have milder symptoms but carry predispositions for liver issues like gallstones, ICP, DILI, and malignancies.
Genetic Testing
  • - Recommended early in infants/children with cholestasis of unclear cause.
  • - In adults, recommended after excluding more frequent causes, or if unresponsive to treatment.
  • - Genetic counselling must precede genetic testing.
Recommended Techniques
  • - Next-generation sequencing (NGS) techniques like panel sequencing and whole-exome sequencing (WES) are recommended.
  • - Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) can be considered if WES is inconclusive.
  • - Sanger sequencing of individual genes is now discouraged, with a possible exception for ABCB4 gene testing in young patients with small duct primary sclerosing cholangitis.

Data Re-analysis
  • - Re-analyse WES/WGS data at least every 3 years for patients without a diagnosis to check for newly discovered variants.

Variant Classification
  • - Mutations are classified from Class 1 (benign) to Class 5 (pathogenic).
  • - Class 3 denotes Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS), which are common and challenging for patient counselling.

Disease Onset & Phenotype
  • - Early onset: Genetic variants are often the major determinant.
  • - Late onset: Phenotype often results from interaction of variants with environmental factors (drugs, pregnancy, infections).


Pruritus Management
  • - A comprehensive, stepwise approach to the challenging and debilitating symptom of cholestatic pruritus.
  • - Many recommendations are based on expert opinion due to a lack of high-quality studies.
  • - See Fig. 2 for flowchart.
First-Line Therapy
  • - General measures like emollients and cool baths.

Second-Line Therapy
  • - IBAT inhibitors (Maralixibat, Odevixibat): Interrupt the enterohepatic circulation (EHC) of bile acids. Efficacy varies from none to high depending on PFIC type and genotype.
  • - Rifampicin: Mechanism unknown, possibly via PXR activation. Moderate efficacy.
  • - Bile acid sequestrants (Cholestyramine): Marginal efficacy.
  • - UDCA: Not generally first-line due to lack of evidence, but often tried early due to its low-risk profile.

Third-Line Therapy
  • - Naltrexone (Opioid antagonist): Marginal efficacy.
  • - Sertraline (SSRI): Modulates CNS pruritoception. Marginal efficacy.
  • - Bezafibrate (Fibrate): Mechanism not fully understood. Intermediate efficacy in adults.

Refractory Pruritus / Fourth-Line
  • - Naso-biliary drainage (temporary).
  • - Surgical interruption of EHC (Partial External/Internal Biliary Diversion).
  • - MARS (Molecular Adsorbent Recirculating System).
  • - Liver Transplantation as the ultimate option.



Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency (AATD)
  • - Caused by mutations in the SERPINA1 gene.
  • - Leads to defective protein accumulation in hepatocytes, causing liver disease, and lack of lung protection, causing emphysema.
Diagnosis & Testing
  • - Recommended first step: Measure serum AAT levels.
  • - Measurement should be done in situations without inflammation, as AAT is an acute phase protein.
Diagnostic Cut-offs
  • - A cut-off of <50 mg/dL (9.2 lmol/L) raises suspicion of severe AATD.
  • - Normal range is 90-175 mg/dL.

Confirmatory Testing
  • - Phenotyping (isoelectric focusing) can be used for a quick decision.
  • - Genotyping should be used for a definitive diagnosis.

Key Genotype: Pi*ZZ
  • - Accounts for 95% of severe AATD cases.
  • - Results in an 85% decrease in plasma AAT levels.
  • - Greatly increases the risk of cirrhosis (Fig. 5).


Treatment
  • - UDCA: Insufficient data to advise for or against treatment in children.
  • - Fazirsiran (small-interfering RNA): Showed promise in a phase II study by decreasing AAT production and improving liver tests in adults.

Monitoring & Prognosis
  • - Severe Disease Markers: Impaired liver synthetic function (prolonged INR, decreased albumin) or decompensation of cirrhosis (ascites, varices) indicate poor outcomes.
  • - Liver Stiffness Measurement (LSM): Can be used in adults with Pi*ZZ to estimate histological fibrosis.
  • - Liver Biopsy: Should be considered when careful non-invasive evaluation remains inconclusive.

Lifestyle Counselling
  • - Strongly recommended as smoking, obesity, and alcohol consumption have negative health effects in patients with AATD.

AATD in Liver Transplantation
  • - Pi*MZ donor organs: Should only be considered when other suitable organs are lacking and no signs of liver injury are present.
  • - Pi*ZZ donor organs: Should not be used.


Alagille Syndrome (ALGS)
  • - Autosomal dominant multi-system disease caused by mutations in JAG1 or, less commonly, NOTCH2.
  • - Characterized by bile duct paucity.
Liver Biopsy
  • - The routine use of liver biopsy for determining prognosis or management of liver disease in ALGS is not recommended.

Pruritus Treatment
  • - IBAT inhibitors (Maralixibat, Odevixibat) should be offered to patients with ALGS and cholestatic pruritus.
  • - RCTs (ICONIC, ASSERT) have shown significant reductions in serum bile acids (sBA) and pruritus scores.

Hypercholesterolemia & Xanthomas
  • - Statins are not recommended for hyperlipidemia or xanthomas in ALGS.
  • - Hyperlipidemia is largely due to Lipoprotein-X, which is not thought to be atherogenic.


FIC1 Deficiency (PFIC1)
  • - Caused by mutations in the ATP8B1 gene, encoding FIC1.
  • - Leads to both hepatic and extrahepatic manifestations (e.g., pancreatitis, diarrhoea).
Enterohepatic Circulation (EHC) Interruption
  • - Surgical interruption of the EHC is not recommended as a routine treatment to prolong native liver survival.

Post-Liver Transplant Management
  • - For post-transplant diarrhoea and steatohepatitis, medical or surgical interruption of the EHC could be used.


BSEP Deficiency (PFIC2)
  • - Caused by mutations in the ABCB11 gene, encoding the Bile Salt Export Pump (BSEP).
  • - Associated with a high risk of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
Genotype-Directed Management
  • - Management, including follow-up, screening, and treatment, should be based on the specific genotype.
Responsive Genotypes (e.g., p.D482G, p.E297G)
  • - Associated with a less severe phenotype and residual BSEP function.
  • - Median native liver survival is >18 years.
  • - Show sustained response to surgical interruption of the EHC.

Severe Genotypes (e.g., two PPTMs)
  • - PPTM = Predicted Protein-Truncating Mutation.
  • - Associated with poor native liver survival and poor response to EHC interruption.
  • - High incidence of HCC at a young age.


Enterohepatic Circulation (EHC) Interruption
  • - Surgical EHC interruption: Should be considered in patients with a responsive genotype to prolong native liver survival. A post-op sBA level <102 lmol/L is associated with long-term success.
  • - Medical EHC interruption (IBAT inhibitors): Should be considered to reduce pruritus in patients with missense mutations.

HCC Screening
  • - For patients with missense mutations: An HCC screening interval of 3 to 6 months is recommended.
  • - For patients with biallelic PPTMs: A more frequent HCC screening interval of 3 months is recommended.

BSEP Alloimmunisation (Post-Transplant)
  • - Occurs in patients with no BSEP expression who develop an immune response to the donor BSEP.
  • - Diagnosis: Detection of anti-BSEP antibodies in plasma or anti-canalicular antibodies on liver tissue staining.
  • - Treatment: Intensify immunosuppression; consider a combination of rituximab and plasmapheresis. Re-transplantation may be necessary.


MDR3 Deficiency (PFIC3)
  • - Caused by mutations in the ABCB4 gene.
  • - Characterized by high GGT levels, unlike FIC1 and BSEP deficiencies.
  • - Heterozygous mutations can cause a spectrum of diseases including ICP, DILI, and LPAC syndrome.
Genotype & Management
  • - In patients with biallelic ABCB4 variants, genetics should be used to predict phenotype and response to UDCA therapy.
UDCA Response
  • - Patients with missense variants are more likely to respond to UDCA therapy.
  • - Patients with two PPTMs (predicted protein-truncating mutations) usually do not respond or only partially.


Follow-up & Screening
  • - Patients with known pathogenic ABCB4 variants should be offered follow-up for liver-related complications, including malignancy (HCC/CCA).
  • - Genetic testing should be offered to first-degree relatives.
Follow-up Protocol (See Table 4)
  • - Adjust follow-up to clinical presentation and genotype.
  • - Visits every 3-12 months depending on severity.
  • - Perform medical history, lab analysis, and non-invasive fibrosis screening (e.g., FIB-4, elastography).
  • - Liver ultrasound and/or MRI with MRCP are recommended at least yearly.


UDCA Treatment
  • - Strongly recommended for those with at least one ABCB4 missense variant and a clinical phenotype.
  • - May be offered to patients with biallelic PPTM variants, though response is limited.

MDR3 in Liver Transplantation
  • - Long-term UDCA therapy is recommended for recipients of a graft from a donor with a monoallelic pathogenic MDR3 variant.


Bile Acid Synthesis Defects (BASD)
  • - Ultra-rare autosomal recessive disorders from mutations in genes of the bile acid synthesis pathway.
  • - Most frequent are 3b-HSD deficiency (HSD3B7 gene) and D4-3-oxoR deficiency (AKR1D1 gene).
  • - Key features: cholestasis, normal GGT, absence of pruritus, and low total serum primary bile acids.
Treatment
  • - Patients should be treated with an oral primary bile acid.
  • - This is a life-saving replacement therapy that restores bile flow and downregulates the production of toxic atypical bile acids.
Drug of Choice
  • - Cholic acid (CA) is the bile acid of choice for treatment.
  • - It is safe, effective, and not hepatotoxic or teratogenic.



Other Types of PFIC
  • - The spectrum of Progressive Familial Intrahepatic Cholestasis (PFIC) has expanded due to NGS.
  • - Includes very rare deficiencies like TJP2, FXR, MYO5B, USP53, and others (See Table 5).
  • - Due to rarity, data is limited and patients should be managed in tertiary care centers.

Future Perspectives
  • - Recent advances include a better understanding of the natural history of these diseases through international consortia (GALA, NAPPED).
  • - The introduction of IBAT inhibitors has opened new therapeutic avenues, but long-term data on disease modification is needed.
  • - A clear unmet need exists for treatments for patients who do not respond to IBAT inhibition.
