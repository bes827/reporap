AGA Clinical Practice Update on Risk Stratification and Emerging Surveillance Strategies for Hepatocellular Carcinoma
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2026; Vol. ■, Iss. ■
  • Topic: HCC surveillance, risk stratification, biomarkers, and clinical guidance
  • Author(s): Nicole E. Rich, Augusto Villanueva, Jorge A. Marrero, Fasiha Kanwal
HCC Background and Epidemiology
  • HCC --> 3rd leading cause of cancer-related death worldwide
  • Leading cause of mortality in patients with cirrhosis
  • Survival rates improving due to advancements in:
  • Surgical therapies
  • Locoregional therapies
  • Systemic therapies
  • Only 30%–40% of HCC cases diagnosed at early stage
  • Surveillance underuse --> fewer than 1 in 4 patients with cirrhosis receiving surveillance
  • Epidemiology shifting from viral hepatitis C (HCV) --> non-viral liver diseases
  • MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease)
  • Alcohol-associated liver disease
  • Non-viral etiologies have lower annual HCC incidence but represent rapidly growing populations
Best Strategy for Reducing HCC Morbidity and Mortality
1.1 Prevention of Cirrhosis (BPA 1)
  • Primary prevention --> treat underlying liver disease
  • Key strategies include:
  • Vaccination for hepatitis B virus (HBV)
  • Treatment of viral hepatitis (HCV and HBV)
  • Recognizing and treating alcohol use disorder
  • Managing metabolic syndrome
  • Addressing liver diseases at early stages
1.2 Viral Hepatitis Management
  • HBV lifetime HCC risk: 10%–25%
  • Universal vaccination programs --> ↓ HBV-related HCC in US
  • Nucleos(t)ide analogs (tenofovir, entecavir):
  • Suppress viral replication
  • ↓ liver inflammation
  • Prevent progression to cirrhosis
  • Screen for coinfection with HCV, hepatitis D virus, HIV --> ↑ HCC risk vs HBV monoinfection
  • Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) for HCV:
  • Effective, well-tolerated oral medications
  • Achieve sustained virologic response (SVR)
  • Halt liver disease progression
  • Can reverse some cirrhosis damage --> ↓ HCC risk
1.3 MASLD Prevention and Management
  • MASLD --> most rapidly increasing etiology of HCC in US
  • Closely linked to:
  • Metabolic syndrome
  • Obesity
  • Type 2 diabetes (all independent HCC risk factors)
  • Lifestyle modifications:
  • Weight loss
  • Balanced diet
  • Regular exercise
  • Emerging pharmacotherapy:
  • Resmetirom (oral liver-directed thyroid hormone receptor β-agonist)
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists
  • Other drugs in MASLD pipeline with various mechanisms
  • These therapies may ↓ HCC risk by reducing MASH-associated fibrosis
1.4 Alcohol-Associated Liver Disease Prevention
  • Chronic heavy alcohol consumption:
  • Induces liver inflammation
  • Causes steatosis and fibrosis
  • Leads to cirrhosis --> ↑ HCC risk
  • Prevention efforts:
  • Behavioral interventions
  • Pharmacologic treatments (e.g., naltrexone)
  • Public health policies (alcohol taxes, awareness campaigns)
Current Surveillance Strategy (BPA 2)
2.1 Recommended Modality
  • Preferred surveillance: semiannual ultrasound (US) + α-fetoprotein (AFP)
  • Benefits of surveillance:
  • ↑ detection of early-stage HCC
  • Access to curative therapy
  • Improved survival
2.2 Surveillance Populations
  • Surveillance recommended for:
  • All patients with cirrhosis (any etiology)
  • Selected patients with chronic HBV infection
  • Child-Turcotte-Pugh (CTP) class A or B cirrhosis --> surveillance beneficial
  • CTP class C cirrhosis (not transplant candidates) --> little benefit due to competing mortality
  • CTP class C on transplant waitlist --> surveillance still important to:
  • Identify early-stage tumors (may ↑ transplant priority)
  • Detect larger tumors that may preclude transplantation
  • Not advised for patients with life expectancy <1–2 years
2.3 Evidence for Surveillance
  • Randomized trial in chronic HBV patients:
  • Surveillance --> ↓ HCC mortality
  • Hazard ratio (HR) 0.63 [95% CI, 0.41–0.98]
  • No similar RCT for cirrhosis patients
  • Meta-analyses of observational studies demonstrate:
  • Improved early detection
  • Access to curative treatment
  • Survival benefits
  • Benefits persist after adjusting for lead-time and length-time biases
2.4 Ultrasound Performance
  • Cornerstone of surveillance but has limitations:
  • Operator dependent
  • Worse performance in:
  • Patients with obesity
  • Severe steatosis
  • Hepatic decompensation
2.5 AFP Addition Benefits
  • Sensitivity comparison:
  • US + AFP: 63%
  • US alone: 51%
  • Small decrease in specificity with combination
  • Diagnostic odds ratio higher for combination than US alone
  • Cost-effectiveness analyses support US + AFP combination
2.6 CT and MRI Considerations
  • CT and MRI may offer higher sensitivity/specificity than US
  • Not recommended for routine surveillance due to:
  • Radiation and contrast exposure concerns
  • High cost
  • Low availability (limited radiology capacity)
  • Decision models suggest MRI may be cost-effective in select populations
  • Incremental cost-effectiveness ratio varies based on:
  • HCC incidence
  • Cirrhosis etiology
  • Test costs
2.7 Surveillance Frequency and Implementation
  • Semiannual surveillance recommended:
  • Detects tumors at earlier stage vs annual
  • Improves survival compared with annual surveillance
  • Uptake remains low (~24%) even among subspecialist-managed patients
  • Strategies to ↑ uptake:
  • Outreach: mailed invitations
  • In-reach: EMR reminders, provider education
  • These approaches significantly ↑ participation in surveillance programs
Surveillance in Patients Without Cirrhosis (BPA 3)
3.1 General Principle
  • Only subset of chronic HBV patients should undergo surveillance without cirrhosis
  • Surveillance not advised for non-cirrhotic patients from other etiologies due to low annual incidence
3.2 Post-SVR HCV Patients
  • Cost-effectiveness threshold: ~1.0% annual HCC incidence
  • HCV cirrhosis post-SVR:
  • ↑ HCC risk persists up to 10 years after achieving SVR
  • Surveillance should continue indefinitely
  • HCV patients without cirrhosis post-SVR:
  • HCC incidence significantly below cost-effectiveness threshold
  • Routine surveillance likely not warranted
3.3 MASLD Patients Without Cirrhosis
  • Up to 20% of MASLD-HCC occurs in individuals without cirrhosis
  • Overall HCC incidence in non-cirrhotic MASLD: ~0.008 per 100 person-years
  • More accurate tools needed to risk stratify:
  • Patients with cured HCV without cirrhosis
  • MASLD patients without cirrhosis
3.4 F3 Fibrosis Patients
  • Routine surveillance not advised for patients with stage F3 fibrosis or lower
  • Clinicians should recognize potential misclassification of fibrosis using noninvasive testing
  • Individualized discussion may be appropriate for:
  • Patients with uncertain fibrosis staging
  • Additional risk factors (e.g., family history of HCC)
  • Shared decision making recommended
Potential Harms of Surveillance (BPA 4)
4.1 Physical Harms
  • Current modalities are noninvasive but not without risks
  • False-positive results on US:
  • Lead to unnecessary contrast-enhanced imaging or biopsy
  • Liver biopsy complications (rare but serious):
  • Bleeding
  • Infection
  • Tumor seeding
  • Study of 614 patients in HCC surveillance:
  • 19.2% (n=118) had abnormal screening results
  • Only 26 developed HCC
  • Physical harms observed in 10% of patients (mostly mild/moderate)
4.2 Psychological Harms
  • Often overlooked in surveillance discussions
  • Indeterminate lesions or incidental findings cause:
  • Anxiety
  • Stress
  • Diminished quality of life
  • Even benign findings may prompt months of testing and patient worry
4.3 Financial Harms
  • US is relatively low-cost, but expenses accumulate:
  • Repeat testing
  • Follow-up diagnostic imaging
  • Trial of HCC screening outreach:
  • 21.8% of patients reported moderate to severe financial burden
  • Additional costs:
  • Missed work
  • Travel costs
4.4 Clinical Implications
  • Individualize surveillance decisions
  • For patients with limited life expectancy or non-transplant candidates:
  • Surveillance offers little benefit
  • May only expose to harm
  • Shared decision making is key:
  • Patients should understand limitations
  • Potential for false-positive and false-negative results
  • Emotional distress and financial implications
Novel Blood-Based Biomarkers (BPA 5)
5.1 Current Limitations
  • US + AFP has suboptimal sensitivity
  • Need for improved surveillance strategies
5.2 Individual Biomarkers Studied
  • Lectin-reactive AFP fraction
  • Des-γ-carboxyprothrombin (DCP)
  • These do not outperform AFP individually
  • Combination strategies have led to composite scores
5.3 GALAD Score
  • Incorporates:
  • Age
  • Sex
  • AFP
  • Lectin-reactive AFP fraction
  • DCP
  • Case-control studies: 72%–82% sensitivity for early-stage HCC
  • Phase 3 validation (n=1558 patients with cirrhosis):
  • Cutoff of –1.36
  • Assessed 6 months before HCC diagnosis
  • 65% sensitivity, 82% specificity
  • Cutoffs benchmarked at ~80% specificity (corresponds to ~20% false-positive rate)
  • Performance more modest in longitudinal cohorts
5.4 HES Algorithm Version 2.0
  • Incorporates:
  • AFP trends
  • Lectin-reactive AFP
  • DCP
  • ALT
  • Platelet count
  • Age
  • Cirrhosis etiology
  • Phase 3 evaluation: improved overall performance vs other scores (GALAD, ASAP)
  • Limitation: 47% sensitivity for early-stage HCC
  • Optimal cutoff values require further validation
5.5 Blood-Based Biomarkers as Adjunct
  • Few observational studies suggest adjunct to imaging may improve sensitivity
  • Limitations:
  • ↑ cost
  • Does not address patient-level barriers to imaging-based screening
  • No prospective studies directly comparing adjunct biomarker + imaging vs either modality alone
  • Current evidence insufficient to recommend blood-based biomarkers as adjunct (not replacement) to imaging
5.6 Liquid Biopsy Approaches
  • Emerging technologies:
  • Cell-free DNA methylation assays
  • Circulating tumor cells
  • Extracellular vesicle profiling
  • Early-phase results promising:
  • Some studies report >80% sensitivity and specificity
  • Limitations:
  • Most data from phase 2 studies
  • Few with FDA breakthrough device designation
  • Not yet recommended for clinical practice
5.7 Abbreviated MRI as Radiologic Approach
  • Abbreviated MRI:
  • Reduces number of sequences
  • Shortens examination time
  • ↓ costs
  • Prospective study (208 patients):
  • Annual abbreviated MRI vs semiannual US
  • 71% sensitivity (abbreviated MRI) vs 45% (US)
  • For early-stage HCC
  • Small cohort studies suggest performance comparable to conventional MRI
  • PREMIUM trial evaluating abbreviated MRI in large US-based study
5.8 Ongoing Trials
  • TRACER trial: will provide data on biomarker-based surveillance effectiveness and harms
  • PREMIUM trial (Preventing Liver Cancer Mortality Through Imaging With Ultrasound vs MRI):
  • Will provide definitive data on abbreviated MRI utility for HCC surveillance
Multicancer Blood Panels (BPA 6)
6.1 Evidence Summary
  • Early retrospective study: 62% of localized tumors detected using targeted error-correction sequencing of cell-free DNA
  • CancerSEEK panels (cfDNA mutations + protein biomarkers):
  • Improved sensitivity for ovary, liver, stomach, pancreas, esophagus cancers
  • Prospective case-control study (2800 participants):
  • Whole-genome DNA methylation achieved:
  • 98% specificity
  • 34%–39% sensitivity
  • These panels studied in general populations with few cirrhosis/HCC patients
  • Should not be used as standard tools for HCC surveillance
HCC Risk Stratification Scores in Cirrhosis (BPA 7)
7.1 Overview
  • Annual HCC incidence in cirrhosis: 1%–8%
  • Risk stratification could:
  • Tailor surveillance intensity and modality to individual risk
  • Direct resources to highest-risk patients
  • ↓ screening intensity and harms for lower-risk patients
7.2 Current Model Limitations
  • Most models derived from cohorts with:
  • Viral etiologies
  • Limited diversity
  • Few women and racial/ethnic minority groups
  • Simple models (demographic + clinical factors):
  • Practical advantage: uses routinely collected data
  • Modest performance: C-indices ~0.70–0.80
  • Precludes widespread clinical use
7.3 Multidimensional Models
  • Incorporate factors from multiple domains:
  • Genetic markers
  • Metabolic markers
  • Radiomic markers
  • May better distinguish low-risk from high-risk patients
7.4 Genetic Risk Score Integration
  • Study using prospective cohorts:
  • 7-SNP genetic risk score associated with HCC risk
  • Adding genetic score to clinical model:
  • C-indices: 0.786 (with GRS) vs 0.783 (clinical model alone)
  • Small but measurable improvement
7.5 Biomarker Panel Models
  • El-Serag et al derived and internally validated:
  • 9-biomarker signature
  • Predicts HCC risk in cirrhosis
  • Compared with clinical model (demographic, clinical, lifestyle variables):
  • Small but statistically significant improvement in predictive value
7.6 Cost Considerations
  • Adding factors from multiple domains ↑ cost of additional testing
  • Must weigh costs against incremental improvements in performance
7.7 Validation Gaps
  • Most models not extensively externally validated
  • No consensus on acceptable false-negative or false-positive thresholds
  • Widespread use will remain limited until clinically relevant thresholds are established
7.8 Key Risk Stratification Models
aMAP Score (Johnson, 2022)
  • Variables: age, gender, platelet count, ALBI score
  • C-index: 0.78 (95% CI, 0.76–0.80) overall
  • Incidence rates by risk group:
  • Low-risk: 0.98 per 1000-person-years
  • Moderate-risk: 7.05 per 1000-person-years
  • High-risk: 29.1 per 1000-person-years
  • Externally validated: Yes
Ioannou Model (2019)
  • Etiology: 100% alcohol-related or MASLD-related cirrhosis
  • Variables: age, gender, diabetes, BMI, platelet count, albumin, AST/ALT ratio
  • AUROC: 0.75 (MASLD), 0.76 (ALD)
  • Risk groups by annual HCC incidence:
  • High-risk: >3%
  • Medium-risk: 1%–3%
  • Low-risk: <1%
Nahon Model (2023)
  • Etiology: Alcohol-related and/or HCV-cured cirrhosis
  • Variables:
  • Genetic: PNPLA3, TM6SF2, HSD17B13, APOE, MBOAT7, WNT3A-WNT9A rs708113
  • With and without clinical factors (age, sex, diabetes, platelet count, GGT, albumin)
  • C-index of clinical model: 0.769 for 5-year HCC risk
  • Adding genetic score → 0.786
  • Risk groups by 5-year HCC incidence:
  • Group 1 (n=627): 5.4%
  • Group 2 (n=276): 10.7%
  • Group 3 (n=242): 15.3%
Kanwal Model (2023)
  • Etiology: Multi-etiology
  • Variables: age, sex, smoking, alcohol use, BMI, etiology, AFP, albumin, ALT, platelet
  • AUROC: 0.77 (95% CI, 0.71–0.83) at 2 years
  • 3-year HCC risk: lowest decile 0.4% to highest decile 18.7%
  • Externally validated: Yes
El-Serag Model (2024)
  • Etiology: Multi-etiology
  • Variables: clinical factors + 9-marker panel
  • AUROC:
  • At 1 year: 0.78 (95% CI, 0.77–0.78)
  • At unspecified time: 0.748 (95% CI, 0.74–0.75)
HBV Risk Scores Without Cirrhosis (BPA 8)
8.1 Overview
  • Several risk prediction models exist for patients with chronic HBV
  • Enable risk-stratified surveillance and individualized clinical decisions
8.2 Model Comparison
  • Recent comparative study:
  • 7 of 10 models showed AUC ≥0.75
  • Models with highest AUC (>0.80): APA-B, RWS-HCC, REAL-B (all incorporate AFP)
  • Three models identified low-risk groups with minimal misclassification
  • PAGE-B, mPAGE-B, AASL-HCC, REAL-B low-risk groups had zero HCC cases during study
  • REAL-B and AASL-HCC demonstrated better discrimination than PAGE-B
8.3 Validated HBV Risk Models
PAGE-B Score
  • Components: age, sex, platelet count
  • Risk groups and cutoffs:
  • Low-risk: <10
  • Intermediate risk: 10–17
  • High-risk: ≥18
REAL-B Score
  • Components: age, male, alcohol use, cirrhosis, diabetes, platelet, AFP
  • Risk groups and cutoffs:
  • Low-risk: <4
  • Intermediate risk: 4–7
  • High-risk: 8–13
AASL-HCC Score
  • Components: age, male, albumin, cirrhosis
  • Risk groups and cutoffs:
  • Low-risk: ≤5
  • Intermediate risk: 6–19
  • High-risk: ≥20
8.4 Other HBV Risk Models
  • REACH-B: age, sex, ALT, HBeAg, HBV DNA
  • mPAGE-B: age, sex, platelet, albumin
  • CU-HCC: age, cirrhosis, albumin, bilirubin, HBV DNA
  • HCC-RESCUE: age, sex, cirrhosis
  • CAMD: cirrhosis, age, sex, diabetes mellitus
  • APA-B: age, platelet, AFP
8.5 Additional Considerations for HBV
  • Other factors should inform surveillance decisions:
  • Endemic region origin
  • Age
  • Family history of HCC
8.6 Emerging Models and Approaches
  • PAGED-B model: incorporates HBV DNA viral load into PAGE-B
  • Performs well in patients transitioning from chronic hepatitis B to HBeAg-positive HBV
  • Artificial intelligence/machine learning approaches:
  • SMART-HCC score using liver stiffness measurement + 8 parameters
  • Includes: cirrhosis etiology, age, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, creatinine, platelet count, sex, hypertension
  • These models require further external validation before widespread recommendation
Surveillance Populations Summary (Table 1)
Sufficient Risk – Surveillance Advised
  • CTP class A or B cirrhosis (any etiology): >1.0% annual incidence
  • Hepatitis C (viremic and post-SVR)
  • Hepatitis B
  • Alcohol-associated liver disease
  • MASLD
  • Other etiologies (e.g., primary biliary cholangitis, autoimmune liver disease)
  • CTP class C cirrhosis (if transplant candidate): >1.0% annual incidence
  • Chronic HBV without cirrhosis: >0.2% annual incidence
  • Men aged >40 years from endemic countries
  • Women aged >50 years from endemic countries
  • Patients from Africa at earlier age
  • Family history of HCC
  • PAGE-B score ≥10
Insufficient Risk – Surveillance Not Routinely Advised
  • Patients with stage F3 fibrosis or lower (including MASLD and HCV):
  • <0.2% annual incidence
  • Awaiting new biomarkers and/or better risk stratification
Key Conclusions
10.1 Surveillance Recommendations
  • Preferred strategy: semiannual US + AFP
  • Benefits: early detection, curative therapy access, improved survival
  • Semiannual frequency superior to annual
10.2 Risk Stratification Needs
  • Improved biomarkers and risk tools needed for:
  • Higher-risk patients → more accurate identification
  • Lower-risk patients → less intensive monitoring/surveillance
  • Current models show modest performance (C-indices ~0.70–0.80)
10.3 Novel Biomarkers Status
  • GALAD and other composite scores show promise but:
  • Insufficient evidence for routine clinical use
  • Should not replace guideline-recommended tests
  • Abbreviated MRI shows improved sensitivity vs US (71% vs 45%)
  • Ongoing trials will guide integration into practice:
  • TRACER trial (biomarker-based surveillance)
  • PREMIUM trial (abbreviated MRI)
10.4 Clinical Implementation Gaps
  • Multicancer blood panels not recommended for HCC surveillance
  • Risk stratification models require:
  • External validation in diverse populations
  • Consensus on acceptable thresholds
  • Evidence of impact on clinician/patient behavior and outcomes
10.5 Future Directions
  • Precision screening strategies needed for clinical practice
  • Collaborative efforts to enable earlier adoption of validated tools
  • Goal: improve HCC early detection and outcomes through better risk stratification and surveillance
