Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC): Diagnosis and Management
  • - Source 1 (EASL): EASL Clinical Practice Guidelines: The diagnosis and management of patients with primary biliary cholangitis. Journal of Hepatology 2017 vol. 67 j 145–172.
  • - Source 2 (UpToDate): Overview of the management of primary biliary cholangitis. Literature review current through Nov 2025, topic last updated Feb 14, 2025.
Knowledge Test

  • - What percentage of UDCA-treated patients have an inadequate biochemical response after one year?
  • - What is the recommended screening interval for hepatocellular carcinoma in patients with PBC and cirrhosis?
  • - Obeticholic acid is contraindicated in which specific patient populations due to reports of liver failure?
  • - According to the Paris-I criteria for assessing UDCA response, what are the three biochemical thresholds that define treatment failure?

Introduction & Epidemiology
  • - PBC is a chronic, progressive autoimmune cholestatic liver disease targeting small intralobular bile ducts.
  • - Untreated, it can lead to ductopenia, fibrosis, biliary cirrhosis, and end-stage liver disease.
  • - Predominantly affects women, with an estimated global prevalence of 1 in 1,000 women over age 40.
Epidemiology
  • - Incidence (Europe): 1–2 per 100,000 population per year.
  • - Prevalence: Commonly cited range is 1.9–40.2 per 100,000.
  • - Female Predominance: Female-to-male ratio is approximately 10:1, though recent data suggest increasing male prevalence.

Pathogenesis
  • - Involves interaction of environmental triggers with immunogenetic and epigenetic risk.
  • - Leads to chronic immune-mediated biliary epithelial cell (BEC) injury, cholestasis, and fibrosis.
  • - Key pathways include IL-12 and JAK-STAT signaling.
Biliary Injury Cycle
  • - Immune injury and cholestasis are interdependent.
  • - Downregulation of the Cl-/HCO3- exchanger (AE2) weakens the protective biliary 'bicarbonate umbrella'.
  • - This sensitizes cholangiocytes to apoptosis from hydrophobic bile acids, perpetuating inflammation and fibrosis.


Natural History (Untreated)
  • - Historic UK cohorts showed average survival of 9–10 years from presentation.
  • - A Yale study showed median survival up to 16 years in asymptomatic patients.
  • - Median time to develop extensive fibrosis is 2 years without effective therapy.


Diagnosis
  • - PBC should be suspected in patients with persistent cholestatic liver tests (especially elevated ALP) or symptoms like pruritus and fatigue.
Diagnostic Criteria
  • - EASL Guideline: Diagnosis can be made in adults with elevated ALP if AMA is present at a titre ≥1:40.
  • - Liver biopsy is not required for diagnosis if these criteria are met.
AMA-Negative PBC
  • - Accounts for 5-10% of cases.
  • - Diagnosis can be made in patients with cholestasis and specific ANA patterns.
  • - Key ANA Patterns: Nuclear dots (anti-sp100) or perinuclear rims (anti-gp210).

Important Caveat
  • - AMA reactivity alone is NOT sufficient to diagnose PBC.
  • - Patients with positive AMA but normal liver tests should have annual biochemical reassessment.


Initial Workup
  • - A structured approach is recommended for any patient with chronic cholestasis.
1. History & Physical Exam
  • - Inquire about associated autoimmune diseases: Sjögren's, Hashimoto's thyroiditis, celiac disease, systemic sclerosis.
  • - Thorough drug history is crucial to rule out DILI (Drug-Induced Liver Injury).
  • - Physical signs may include hepatosplenomegaly, jaundice, xanthelasma, and scratch marks.

2. Abdominal Ultrasound
  • - First-line imaging to rule out extrahepatic biliary obstruction, mass lesions, or gallbladder abnormalities.
  • - Hilar lymphadenopathy is a frequent but non-specific finding in PBC.

3. Serological Tests
  • - Antimitochondrial Antibodies (AMA): The diagnostic hallmark. Targets PDC-E2. Found in >90% of patients. Highly specific (98%) in the context of chronic cholestasis.
  • - Antinuclear Antibodies (ANA): Present in ~30% of patients. Specific patterns (anti-sp100, anti-gp210) are diagnostic in AMA-negative cases.

4. Extended Imaging (if needed)
  • - MRCP is recommended in patients with unexplained cholestasis to visualize the biliary tree and rule out PSC.
  • - EUS is an alternative for evaluating distal biliary disease.


Biochemical Abnormalities
  • - Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP): Typically elevated; level is associated with disease progression.
  • - GGT: Also elevated, often before ALP.
  • - Immunoglobulins: Elevated IgM is a characteristic feature.
  • - Transaminases (AST/ALT): May be elevated. An AST/ALT ratio >1 can suggest fibrosis. Prominent elevation may suggest AIH overlap.
  • - Bilirubin: Elevation is a late sign, typical of advanced disease.

Role of Liver Biopsy
  • - Not necessary for routine diagnosis when serology is typical.
  • - Recommended when:
  • - PBC-specific antibodies (AMA, sp100, gp210) are absent.
  • - Co-existent AIH or NASH is suspected.
  • - Other systemic co-morbidities are present.
Histological Findings
  • - Hallmark Lesion: Chronic non-suppurative destructive cholangitis, known as the "florid duct lesion".
  • - Inflammatory infiltrate consists of lymphocytes, macrophages, and eosinophils; epithelioid granulomas may be seen.
  • - Progressive bile duct damage leads to ductopenia.
Staging Systems
  • - Ludwig & Scheuer: 4 stages, with Stage 4 indicating cirrhosis.
  • - Nakanuma (New System): Scores fibrosis, bile duct loss, and orcein-positive granules. More accurately predicts 10-year outcomes.




Risk Stratification & Prognosis
  • - All patients should be evaluated for their risk of progression to identify those needing additional therapy.
  • - Patients at greatest risk are those with inadequate biochemical response to therapy and those with cirrhosis.
Static Risk Factors (at presentation)
  • - Factors identifiable at diagnosis or during follow-up, independent of treatment response.
Demographics
  • - Younger age at diagnosis (<45 years) is a strong risk factor for inadequate response.
  • - Male sex is associated with more advanced disease at presentation, poorer UDCA response, and higher HCC risk.

Biochemistry & Serology
  • - Baseline Bilirubin & Albumin: Can stratify patients into low, medium, and high risk.
  • - PBC-specific ANA: Presence of anti-gp210 or anti-sp100 may predict an unfavorable course.
  • - Anti-centromere antibodies: Associated with a portal hypertensive phenotype.

Non-Invasive Fibrosis Assessment
  • - Liver Stiffness Measurement (LSM) via VCTE (FibroScan) is a key tool.
  • - LSM >9.6 kPa is associated with a 5-fold increased risk of liver decompensation.
  • - Serum Markers: APRI, ELF score, and WFA+-M2BP have shown prognostic ability.

Histology
  • - Advanced histological stage is a poor prognostic factor.
  • - Interface hepatitis and ductopenia are independent predictors of progression.


Dynamic Risk Assessment: Response to UDCA
  • - Biochemical response to UDCA after 1 year is the most validated and critical tool for risk stratification.
  • - Inadequate response is seen in 25% to 50% of patients, depending on the criteria used (UpToDate: ~35%).
  • - Evaluation at 6 months may have equivalent predictive performance.
Qualitative (Binary) Criteria
  • - Various criteria exist to define 'responder' vs. 'non-responder'.
Paris-I Criteria
  • - Most widely validated criteria.
  • - Inadequate response if, after 1 year of UDCA, any of the following are present:
  • - ALP ≥3 x ULN
  • - AST ≥2 x ULN
  • - Bilirubin >1 mg/dL (17 µmol/L)

Paris-II Criteria
  • - Designed for early-stage patients.
  • - Inadequate response if, after 1 year of UDCA, any of the following are present:
  • - ALP ≥1.5 x ULN
  • - AST ≥1.5 x ULN
  • - Bilirubin >1 mg/dL (17 µmol/L)

Other Criteria
  • - Toronto: ALP >1.67 x ULN after 24 months (predicts histological progression).
  • - Barcelona: Decrease in ALP <40% OR ALP remains >1 x ULN after 12 months.


Quantitative (Continuous) Scoring Systems
  • - These models provide a more nuanced risk score and have better predictive performance than binary criteria.
GLOBE Score
  • - Incorporates age, bilirubin, albumin, ALP, and platelet count at 1 year.
  • - Improves patient risk classification by ~10% compared to Paris-I.
  • - Predicts that ~40% of UDCA-treated patients have shorter transplant-free survival than matched controls.

UK-PBC Score
  • - Incorporates bilirubin, ALP, AST/ALT at 1 year, plus baseline albumin and platelet count.


Prognosis for Responders
  • - Early-stage patients with ALP <1.5 x ULN and normal bilirubin after one year of UDCA have a transplant-free survival not significantly different from a healthy control population.



Treatment: Therapies to Slow Disease Progression
  • - The goals of therapy are to prevent end-stage complications and manage symptoms.
First-Line Therapy: Ursodeoxycholic Acid (UDCA)
  • - Recommendation: Recommended as first-line pharmacotherapy for ALL patients with PBC.
  • - Dose: 13–15 mg/kg/day, given orally, either once daily or in divided doses. Therapy is lifelong.
  • - Safety: Very safe. Side effects are minimal (modest weight gain of ~3kg, hair thinning, rarely diarrhea). Considered safe in pregnancy.
Mechanism of Action
  • - A hydrophilic bile acid that becomes predominant in the bile acid pool.
  • - Stimulates hepatobiliary secretion (post-transcriptional secretagogue).
  • - Exerts cytoprotective and anti-apoptotic effects on hepatocytes and cholangiocytes.

Efficacy
  • - Biochemical: Improvement in liver tests is typically seen within 3 months.
  • - Histological: Slows progression from early-stage disease to fibrosis/cirrhosis. 4-year probability of remaining early-stage was 76% with UDCA vs. 29% with placebo.
  • - Clinical Outcomes: Reduces risk of developing esophageal varices (16% vs. 58% over 4 years). Global PBC Study Group meta-analysis showed significantly improved liver transplant-free survival.


Second-Line Therapy (for UDCA non-responders/intolerant)
  • - For patients with an inadequate biochemical response after one year of UDCA.
  • - UpToDate defines this as ALP remaining above ULN.
Obeticholic Acid (OCA)
  • - Class: Farnesoid X receptor (FXR) agonist.
  • - Indication: Approved for use in combination with UDCA in inadequate responders, or as monotherapy in UDCA-intolerant patients.
  • - Dose: Start at 5 mg daily, titrate to 10 mg after 6 months based on tolerability and response.
Efficacy (POISE Trial)
  • - Recruited patients with ALP ≥1.67 x ULN or elevated bilirubin.
  • - Primary Endpoint Met: Biochemical response was 46-47% in OCA groups vs. 10% in placebo group.
  • - Mean ALP reduction was 33-39% with OCA vs. 5% with placebo.

Adverse Effects & Contraindications
  • - Pruritus: Dose-dependent, leading to discontinuation in 1-10% of patients.
  • - Lipids: Can cause a reversible decrease in HDL and total cholesterol.
  • - CONTRAINDICATION (CRITICAL): Decompensated cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B or C), a prior decompensation event, or compensated cirrhosis with portal hypertension. Associated with reports of hepatic decompensation and liver failure.


PPAR Agonists (Unique to UpToDate 2025)
  • - A newer class of agents for second-line therapy. Not mentioned in 2017 EASL guidelines.
  • - Can be combined with UDCA or used as monotherapy. Not for use in decompensated cirrhosis.
Elafibranor
  • - Class: Dual PPAR-alpha and PPAR-delta agonist.
  • - Dose: 80 mg once daily.
  • - Efficacy: Achieved biochemical response in 51% of patients vs. 4% for placebo after 52 weeks. ALP normalization in 15% vs. 0%.

Seladelpar
  • - Class: PPAR-delta agonist.
  • - Dose: 10 mg once daily.
  • - Efficacy: Achieved biochemical response in 62% of patients vs. 20% for placebo after 12 months. ALP normalization in 25% vs. 0%.
  • - Also showed a reduction in pruritus severity.


Fibrates (Bezafibrate, Fenofibrate)
  • - Class: Pan-PPAR agonists (Bezafibrate) or PPAR-alpha agonists (Fenofibrate).
  • - Status: Considered off-label in many regions. Bezafibrate is not available in the US.
  • - Dose (Bezafibrate): 400 mg/day.
Efficacy & Benefits
  • - Improves liver biochemistries in UDCA non-responders. A trial showed complete biochemical response in 31% vs. 0% for placebo.
  • - May improve symptoms of pruritus and fatigue.

Adverse Effects
  • - Can cause elevations in ALT/AST (transcriptional effect).
  • - May cause creatinine elevation (requires monitoring).
  • - Musculoskeletal pain reported in 5-10% of patients.



Other Investigational Therapies
  • - Therapies with limited or uncertain roles.
Budesonide
  • - Role: Reserved for non-cirrhotic patients with marked inflammatory changes (interface hepatitis) who have failed other therapies.
  • - Mechanism: Glucocorticoid with high first-pass metabolism.
  • - Efficacy: Data is mixed. Some trials show improved biochemistry and histology, while others show minimal benefit and increased risk of bone density loss.
  • - Caution: Can have deleterious outcomes in patients with cirrhosis and portal hypertension.



Management of Symptoms & Extrahepatic Manifestations
  • - Symptoms often do not correlate with disease severity and do not improve with UDCA or OCA.
  • - A structured approach to symptom evaluation and management is essential for patient QoL.
Pruritus (Itch)
  • - A characteristic cholestatic symptom. A stepwise management approach is recommended.
Step 1: Bile Acid Sequestrants
  • - Agent: Cholestyramine.
  • - Dosing Caution: Must be taken 2-4 hours before or after other medications (including UDCA/OCA) to avoid impairing their absorption.

Step 2: Pregnane X Receptor (PXR) Agonist
  • - Agent: Rifampicin.
  • - Dose: 150-300 mg daily.
  • - Monitoring: Requires monitoring of liver tests due to risk of hepatotoxicity. Should be stopped if toxicity occurs.

Step 3: Opiate Antagonists
  • - Agent: Naltrexone.
  • - Dosing: Start at a low dose to avoid opiate withdrawal-like reactions.

Other & Refractory Itch
  • - Empirical: SSRIs (e.g., Sertraline) are sometimes used.
  • - Severe/Intractable Pruritus: May be a feature of an aggressive ductopenic variant with a poor prognosis. These patients should be referred to an expert center and may be candidates for liver transplantation.


Fatigue
  • - The most common symptom, reported by >50% of patients; severe in 20%.
  • - No specific licensed therapy exists. Does not improve with liver-directed therapy or transplantation.
  • - Management Focus:
  • - Seek and treat alternate causes: anemia, hypothyroidism, sleep disturbance.
  • - Advise on coping strategies and avoidance of social isolation.

Sicca Complex (Dry Eyes/Mouth)
  • - Very common in PBC.
  • - Management:
  • - First-line: Artificial tears and saliva substitutes.
  • - Refractory: Pilocarpine or cevimeline (muscarinic agonists).
  • - Advise on oral hygiene to prevent dental caries.


Management of Liver-Related Complications
  • - Focuses on complications of chronic cholestasis and cirrhosis.
Metabolic Bone Disease (Osteoporosis)
  • - A common complication.
  • - Screening: Bone mineral densitometry (DEXA) at presentation and every 2-5 years depending on risk.
  • - Management:
  • - Calcium and Vitamin D supplementation (per local practice).
  • - Bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate) are effective but should be used with caution in patients with esophageal varices.

Fat-Soluble Vitamin Deficiency
  • - Uncommon except in patients with prolonged jaundice.
  • - Consider measuring levels of vitamins A, D, E, K in icteric patients.
  • - Prophylactic Vitamin K should be given prior to invasive procedures in severe cholestasis.

Hyperlipidemia
  • - Elevated lipids (especially HDL) are a feature of cholestasis, driven by abnormal lipoprotein X.
  • - There is no substantial evidence of increased cardiovascular risk in PBC.
  • - Routine treatment with statins is not recommended unless concomitant metabolic syndrome or other cardiovascular risk factors are present.

Portal Hypertension & Varices
  • - Can rarely develop in the pre-cirrhotic stage but is mostly acomplication of cirrhosis.
  • - Patients with varices often have other markers of high-risk disease (low albumin, high bilirubin).
Screening
  • - EASL & UpToDate recommend following Baveno-VI guidelines.
  • - Screening endoscopy is recommended for patients with cirrhosis, typically every 2-3 years.
  • - Non-invasive markers can identify high-risk patients: LSM ≥20 kPa or platelet count <150 x 10^9/L.

Management
  • - Primary Prophylaxis: Non-selective beta-blockers are indicated for large esophageal varices.
  • - Endoscopic Variceal Ligation (EVL) is recommended for varices at high risk of bleeding.
  • - Patients with fatigue may find beta-blockade challenging symptomatically.


Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
  • - A serious complication, primarily in patients with cirrhosis.
Risk Factors
  • - Cirrhosis is the main risk factor.
  • - Other key risk factors include:
  • - Male sex
  • - Inadequate response to UDCA
  • - Advanced histological stage at diagnosis.
Incidence
  • - A large international cohort showed an incidence rate of 3.4 cases per 1,000 patient-years.


Surveillance
  • - Indication: All patients with PBC and suspected or confirmed cirrhosis.
  • - Method: Abdominal ultrasound, with or without alpha-fetoprotein (AFP).
  • - Frequency: Every 6 months.



Special Populations & Variants
  • - Management considerations for specific patient groups and disease presentations.
PBC with Features of Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)
  • - Occurs in ~8-10% of patients, sometimes called 'overlap syndrome'.
  • - Can present simultaneously with PBC or develop sequentially years later.
  • - Prognosis is generally worse than for PBC alone.
Diagnosis
  • - Liver biopsy is considered mandatory to confirm features of AIH.
  • - Suspect in patients with disproportionately high ALT and/or IgG levels.
  • - The Paris criteria are most commonly used for definition.
Paris Criteria
  • - Requires meeting criteria for both PBC and AIH.
  • - PBC (≥2 of 3): ALP ≥2xULN or GGT ≥5xULN; AMA ≥1:40; Florid duct lesion.
  • - AIH (≥2 of 3): ALT ≥5xULN; IgG ≥2xULN or SMA positive; Moderate/severe interface hepatitis.


Treatment
  • - Dual therapy is required.
  • - UDCA at standard dose (13-15 mg/kg/day) for the PBC component.
  • - Immunosuppression for the AIH component, typically with corticosteroids (prednisone/prednisolone) +/- azathioprine.
  • - EASL suggests immunosuppression for severe interface hepatitis and consideration for moderate cases.


Pregnancy
  • - Pregnancy is typically well-tolerated in non-cirrhotic patients.
  • - Patients with cirrhosis and portal hypertension have a higher risk of maternal and fetal complications and require specialist pre-conception counseling and monitoring.
Medication Management
  • - UDCA: Should be continued. Considered safe during conception, pregnancy, and lactation.
  • - Cholestyramine: Considered safe for pruritus.
  • - Rifampicin: Can be used for severe pruritus, with expert opinion suggesting use from the third trimester onwards.

Clinical Considerations
  • - Pruritus may worsen during pregnancy.
  • - Post-partum cholestatic flares have been described, requiring follow-up.
  • - Cirrhotic patients should undergo endoscopy to screen for varices, preferably in the second trimester.



Liver Transplantation
  • - The ultimate therapy for end-stage disease. PBC as an indication for transplant has declined due to effective medical therapies.
Indications for Assessment
  • - Decompensated Cirrhosis: Ascites, variceal hemorrhage, encephalopathy.
  • - Biochemical Markers of Severity:
  • - MELD score ≥15
  • - Persistently rising bilirubin >50-85 µmol/L (3-5 mg/dL)
  • - Refractory Symptoms: Severe, intractable pruritus unresponsive to all medical therapy.

Outcomes & Post-Transplant Course
  • - Survival: Excellent, with 5-year survival rates of 80-85%, often better than for other liver diseases.
  • - Fatigue: Often persists after transplantation and is NOT an indication for transplant.
  • - PBC Recurrence: Occurs in ~20% of patients. Diagnosis requires histology. Rarely leads to graft loss. UDCA may be used to treat biochemical abnormalities in recurrent PBC.


Long-Term Monitoring & Care Organization
  • - All patients require structured, lifelong follow-up to monitor disease activity, assess treatment response, and screen for complications.
Monitoring Schedule
  • - Liver Tests & Platelets: Every 3 to 6 months. (UpToDate specifies every 3 months for patients on OCA).
  • - Thyroid Function: TSH annually to screen for hypothyroidism.
  • - Bone Density: DEXA scan every 2 years (UpToDate) or as indicated by risk.
  • - Fat-Soluble Vitamins: Annually check Vitamin A, D, and prothrombin time (for Vitamin K).

Cirrhosis Surveillance
  • - HCC: Liver ultrasound every 6 months.
  • - Varices: Upper endoscopy every 2 to 3 years.

General Measures
  • - Immunizations: Vaccinate for Hepatitis A and B if non-immune. Provide pneumococcal and annual influenza vaccines.
  • - Alcohol: Advise patients to abstain from alcohol, especially heavy use.

Care Standards & Patient Support (EASL)
  • - Recommends development of local Care Pathways to standardize management.
  • - Suggests using Clinical Audit Standards to improve quality of care.
  • - Patients should be informed of Patient Support Groups for education and coping strategies, particularly for managing fatigue and social isolation.
