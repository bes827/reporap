Study Guide: Designing Observational Studies in Epidemiology
  • This guide provides a comprehensive overview of observational study designs in epidemiology.
  • It focuses on core concepts, design principles, strengths, weaknesses, and bias mitigation strategies.
  • Target audience: physicians seeking to design, interpret, and critique epidemiological research.
Knowledge Test
  • Test your understanding of key epidemiological concepts:
  • 1. What is the primary metric generated by a cross-sectional study, and how does it fundamentally differ from a cohort study's primary metric?
  • 2. In what specific scenario is a case-crossover study considered the ideal design?
  • 3. What is 'immortal time bias' and why is it problematic in cohort studies of treatments?
  • 4. Name two primary strategies to mitigate recall bias in case-control studies.
  • 5. What is the key advantage of a nested case-cohort study over a standard nested case-control design regarding multiple outcomes?
Core Epidemiological Concepts
  • A firm grasp of core terminology is essential for designing, interpreting, and critiquing epidemiological research.
  • These concepts are fundamental building blocks for understanding study strengths and limitations.
  • Mastering these terms enables evaluation of study validity and selection of appropriate designs.
Cross-Sectional Study
  • All measurements (predictors + outcomes) made on a single occasion / short period.
  • No follow-up involved.
  • Provides a 'snapshot' of health/habits @ a specific time.
Examples
  • NHANES: info on US population health/habits @ specific time.
  • Study: childhood obesity ↔ hours watching television.
  • Study: movie smoking exposure ↔ smoking initiation in children; prevalence of ever trying smoking was 22% (highest quartile movie smoking) vs. 2% (lowest).
Cohort Study
  • Participants (cohort) assembled @ outset, followed longitudinally over time.
  • Estimates outcome incidence among exposed / unexposed groups.
  • Forward-looking approach: observes how exposures relate to future disease development.
Examples
  • Nurses' Health Studies (NHS): examining diseases in women since 1976.
  • Study: menstrual cycle regularity ↔ risk of death (NHS-II).
  • Study: 'door-to-needle time' for stroke treatment ↔ 1-year mortality in Medicare patients.
  • Study: postmenopausal hormone therapy ↔ heart disease.
Case-Control Study
  • Investigator starts w/ sample of people w/ outcome (cases) + sample w/o outcome (controls).
  • Looks backward to measure + compare previous exposures in both groups.
  • Efficient for rare diseases / long latent periods.
Examples
  • Comparing previous exposure to arc welding in ocular melanoma cases vs. healthy controls.
  • Study: Hepatitis A outbreak in Australia; comparing frozen pomegranate arils consumption between cases + controls.
  • Study: maternal diethylstilbestrol use ↔ vaginal cancer in daughters.
  • Study: prone sleeping position ↔ SIDS.
Prevalence
  • Proportion of people w/ disease / condition @ one point in time.
  • Calculated as: # people w/ disease / # people @ risk.
  • Primary metric for cross-sectional studies.
Examples
  • Prevalence of smoking in demographic groups (NHANES).
  • Prevalence of ever trying smoking as outcome in children + movie smoking exposure study.
Incidence (Cumulative Incidence & Incidence Rate)
  • Proportion of people who get a disease / condition over a given period.
  • Cumulative incidence: # who get disease / # @ risk.
  • Incidence rate: # who get disease / person-time @ risk.
Examples
  • Incidence of death before age 70 in Nurses’ Health Study II.
  • Incidence of 1-year mortality after stroke (Man et al. study).
Odds Ratio (OR)
  • Measure of association: compares odds of exposure in cases to odds of exposure in controls.
  • Approximates risk ratio when outcome is rare (≤ 10%).
  • Primary measure of association in case-control studies.
Examples
  • Hepatitis A outbreak study: OR for consumption of frozen pomegranate arils was 45.0.
  • Opiate overdose deaths study: opiate agonist treatment associated w/ OR of 0.09.
  • Case-crossover study: mobile phone use + car crashes, OR was 4.5 (27 discordant pairs phone use before crash vs. 6 w/o).
Risk Ratio (RR)
  • Quotient of risks in two groups: risk in exposed group / risk in unexposed group.
  • Also known as relative risk.
  • Primary measure of association in cohort studies.
Examples
  • Nurses' Health Study: RR of major coronary heart disease for women taking estrogen w/ progestin was 0.39.
Selection Bias
  • Bias arising from separate sampling of cases + controls in case-control studies.
  • Occurs when selected cases ≠ representative of all cases (wrt exposure).
  • Or when selected controls ≠ representative of population that produced cases.
Examples
  • Vasectomy + prostate cancer study: cases may over-represent men w/ more medical care (e.g., vasectomy), leading to bias.
  • Hepatitis A study: using controls w/ other infectious diseases problematic if exposure related to general food/water safety.
Recall Bias (Differential Misclassification)
  • Type of measurement bias.
  • Being diagnosed w/ disease → cases remember/report past exposures differently from controls.
  • Cases may be sensitized to potential causes.
Examples
  • Sun exposure + melanoma study: cases might recall sun exposure history differently than controls.
  • Twin study: twin w/ melanoma asked which twin sunbathed more as child, OR 2.2; healthy co-twin asked same, OR 0.8.
Nested Case-Control Study
  • Case-control study 'nested' within a defined cohort.
  • Cases are incident cases occurring within the cohort.
  • Controls sampled from remaining members of the same cohort.
Examples
  • Malignant melanoma study nested within a cohort that collected sun exposure data years earlier.
  • Used to directly test for recall bias.
Nested Case-Cohort Study
  • Similar to nested case-control, but 'control' group is random sample of all cohort members @ baseline.
  • Includes individuals who may later develop the outcome.
  • Single random 'subcohort' serves as control group for multiple outcomes.
Examples
  • Plasma ACE2 study: used PURE study cohort to see if ACE2 levels predicted death / cardiometabolic diseases.
  • Single random 'subcohort' served as control for multiple outcomes (death, MI, stroke, etc.).
Incidence-Density Design
  • Nested case-control design where controls sampled from 'risk sets' for each case as it occurs.
  • Risk set: cohort members followed for same length of time as case, but not yet a case.
  • Accounts for changes over time.
Examples
  • Italian study of opiate overdose deaths: sampled controls still alive on date of each case's death.
  • Matched on follow-up time to account for variations in treatment status over time.
Case-Crossover Study
  • Each case serves as their own control.
  • Exposures @ or just before outcome compared w/ exposures of same cases @ other points in time.
  • Used for studying short-term effects of intermittent exposures.
Examples
  • Study on mobile phone use + car crashes: compared phone usage 10 min before crash to usage @ other times (e.g., 24 hrs / 7 days before) for same individual.
Immortal Time Bias
  • Bias in cohort studies of treatments due to misclassified exposure time.
  • Study design makes it impossible for exposed participant to have outcome during certain period.
  • Falsely inflates event rate in non-user period, making treatment appear protective.
Examples
  • Postmenopausal hormone therapy studies: woman starting hormones in 1981, reported in 1982, counted as 'non-user' in 1981.
  • If heart attack in 1981, wrongly counted as non-user event.
  • Biased results, making hormone therapy appear safer.
Core Study Designs: Deep Dive
  • This section systematically builds understanding of primary observational study designs.
  • Covers definitions, practical applications, inherent strengths, and critical vulnerabilities to bias.
  • Uses a question-and-answer format for clarity.
Cross-Sectional Studies
  • All measurements (predictors + outcomes) made @ single point in time / very short period.
  • Investigator draws sample, measures simultaneously; no follow-up.
  • Provides a 'snapshot' of the population.
Defining Characteristic
  • All measurements made @ single point in time / very short period.
  • No follow-up period.
  • Example: NHANES provides 'snapshot' of US health/habits.
Primary Metric: Prevalence
  • Prevalence: proportion of people w/ disease / condition @ single point in time.
  • Differs from cohort studies, which measure incidence (new disease over time).
  • Cross-sectional: current condition; Cohort: rate of new cases.
Strengths
  • Efficiency + practicality are primary strengths.
  • Speed + Low Cost: no follow-up, relatively fast + inexpensive.
  • No Loss to Follow-up: one-time data collection eliminates participant dropout.
  • Utility as First Step: provides baseline data for cohort study / clinical trial @ low cost.
Weakness: Establishing Causality
  • Most significant weakness: difficulty establishing sequence of events.
  • Predictor + outcome measured simultaneously → impossible to determine if predictor preceded outcome.
  • Example: childhood obesity ↔ TV watching; cannot clarify cause/effect direction.
Cohort Studies
  • Group of participants (cohort) assembled @ outset, followed longitudinally over time.
  • Predictor variables measured @ beginning.
  • Cohort monitored to determine outcome incidence.
Fundamental Principle
  • Cohort assembled @ outset, followed longitudinally over time.
  • Predictor variables measured @ beginning.
  • Monitored for outcome incidence; forward-looking approach.
Prospective vs. Retrospective
  • Key difference: timing of data collection relative to study start.
  • Prospective: investigator assembles cohort, measures predictors, follows forward for outcomes.
  • Retrospective: investigator identifies past cohort, collects past predictor data (records), ascertains outcomes that already happened.
Prospective Examples
  • Nurses' Health Study II: participants enrolled 1989, menstrual cycles asked 1993, followed for mortality.
Retrospective Examples
  • Stroke patients study: used existing hospital data to examine 'door-to-needle time' (predictor) ↔ 1-year mortality (outcome), both already occurred.
Strategic Advantages of Prospective Design
  • Increases study validity.
  • Greater Control Over Measurements: rigorous protocols for predictors + outcomes, maximizing accuracy, minimizing missing data.
  • Avoids Bias in Measuring Predictors: exposures measured before outcomes → prevents recall bias, ensures correct temporal sequence.
  • Clearer Participant Selection: direct control over enrollment criteria, ensuring well-defined cohort.
Multiple-Cohort Study
  • Compares two+ distinct cohorts, often defined by different exposure levels.
  • Useful for studying rare exposures (e.g., occupational/environmental hazards).
  • Inefficient to follow large general population for rare exposures.
Example
  • Jaundice and Infant Feeding (JIFee) study: compared two exposed cohorts (newborns w/ high bilirubin, newborns readmitted for dehydration) to unexposed cohort (randomly selected newborns).
Immortal Time Bias
  • Flaw: study design gives exposure group credit for time outcome could not occur.
  • Period misclassified as unexposed time.
  • Can make treatment appear safer / more effective than it is.
Mechanism + Impact
  • Example: early postmenopausal hormone therapy studies, exposure updated biennially.
  • Woman starting hormones in 1981, reported in 1982, classified 'non-user' for 1981.
  • If heart attack in 1981, wrongly attributed to non-user period.
  • 'Immortal' non-user time (when on drug) biased results, making therapy appear protective.
Problem w/ 'Prevalent Users'
  • Studying individuals already taking medication @ study start is problematic.
  • Four key reasons for concern.
  • Can lead to biased results, making treatments seem more beneficial.
Reasons for Problem
  • 1. Overrepresentation of Adherers (Healthy User Effect): prevalent users often adhere better, have better health outcomes regardless of drug effect.
  • 2. Missing Early Adverse Effects: misses those who stopped treatment early due to adverse effects, especially problematic for early harms.
  • 3. Complicates Confounding Control: treatment may alter confounder levels (e.g., statin ↓ cholesterol); difficult to adjust for original effect.
  • 4. Inability to Emulate a Trial: cannot map to clear clinical decision (starting vs. not starting, or continuing vs. stopping).
Case-Control Studies
  • Investigator starts w/ outcome: identifies cases (w/ disease) + controls (w/o disease).
  • Looks backward in time to measure + compare past exposures.
  • Fundamentally differs from cohort studies (forward-looking).
Basic Design
  • Starts w/ outcome: cases (w/ disease) + controls (w/o disease).
  • Looks backward to measure + compare past exposures.
  • Cohort studies are forward-looking: start w/ exposure, follow for outcome.
Primary Strength: Efficiency for Rare Diseases
  • Most efficient + often only feasible option for studying rare diseases / long latent periods.
  • Starts w/ individuals already having disease.
  • Bypasses need to follow massive healthy cohort for years/decades for few outcome events.
Example
  • Circumcision + penile cancer: cohort study needs >6,000 men for decades.
  • Case-control: same statistical power w/ 16 cases + 16 controls.
Susceptibility to Selection Bias
  • Highly susceptible to selection bias, arising from separate sampling of cases + controls.
  • Two main sources of bias.
  • Mitigation strategies are crucial.
Main Sources of Bias
  • 1. Finding a Representative Sample of Cases: cases available (e.g., @ hospital) may not represent all cases (undiagnosed, misdiagnosed, died).
  • Bias if factors making case available link to exposure.
  • Example: vasectomy + prostate cancer study biased if cases drawn from men w/ more medical screening (vasectomy linked to screening).
  • 2. Sampling Controls: most challenging task; goal is to sample controls from same population that produced cases.
  • Controls should be people who would have been selected as a case if they had developed the disease.
Mitigation Strategies for Sampling Controls
  • 1. Clinic/Hospital-Based Controls: convenient, from same facility as cases.
  • May partially account for care-seeking, but problematic if control's condition relates to exposure.
  • 2. Population-Based Controls: use disease registries / health plan data for representative 'non-cases' from same population.
  • Often most desirable design.
  • 3. Using Two+ Control Groups: select multiple control groups w/ different methods to check consistency.
  • Example: oral cholera vaccine study in Haiti compared cases to 'test-negative' controls + 'community' controls; found similar results.
Differential Measurement Bias (Recall Bias)
  • Cases remember/report past exposures differently than controls.
  • Diagnosis of disease sensitizes them to potential causes.
  • Challenging bias to combat.
Mechanism + Impact
  • Example: twin study (one w/ melanoma).
  • Melanoma twin asked about childhood sunbathing: OR 2.2.
  • Healthy co-twin asked same: OR 0.8.
  • Suggests case's memory biased by diagnosis.
Strategies to Avoid
  • 1. Use data recorded before outcome occurred: best approach when possible (e.g., vaccination records in cholera study).
  • 2. Blinding: reduces bias.
  • Blinding participants: embed exposure questions among 'dummy' questions about other plausible risk factors.
  • Blinding observers (interviewers/lab techs): prevents differential probing / biased interpretation of measurements.
Hybrid and Advanced Case-Control Designs
  • Traditional case-control design is powerful but vulnerable to significant biases.
  • These hybrid/advanced designs developed to solve these problems.
  • Offer greater validity by leveraging strengths of a cohort structure.
Nested Case-Control Study
  • Improves upon traditional design by drawing cases + controls from single, well-defined cohort.
  • Avoids selection biases inherent in traditional designs (different populations).
  • If exposure data collected prospectively, also avoids recall bias.
Advantages
  • Avoids selection bias: cases + controls from same underlying population.
  • Avoids recall bias: if exposure data collected prospectively (banked specimens, baseline questionnaires) before outcome.
Incidence-Density Nested Case-Control Study
  • Controls sampled from 'risk sets' @ time each case identified.
  • Risk set: cohort members followed for same time as case, but not yet developed outcome.
  • Primary advantage: accounts for changes in risk factors over time + variable follow-up periods.
Example
  • Italian study on opiate overdose deaths: assessed protective effect of agonist treatment.
  • For each death (case), sampled controls still alive on that date.
  • Compared treatment status in 2 months prior to event, capturing risk changes.
Nested Case-Cohort Design
  • 'Control' group is random sample of entire cohort @ baseline.
  • Includes individuals who may later become cases.
  • Subcohort compared to all cases arising during follow-up.
Key Advantages over Nested Case-Control
  • 1. Single control group for multiple outcomes: subcohort represents source population, serves as comparison for several case sets (e.g., MI, stroke, diabetes).
  • 2. Provides info on risk factor prevalence in whole cohort: random subcohort is representative sample, allows direct measurement.
Example
  • Plasma ACE2 study: single random 'subcohort' used to assess link between ACE2 levels + five different outcomes (death, MI, stroke, heart failure, diabetes).
Case-Crossover Study
  • Ideal design for studying short-term effects of intermittent exposures on an outcome.
  • Each case serves as their own control.
  • Exposure status immediately before event compared to same individual's exposure status @ other, non-event times.
Mechanism + Advantage
  • Self-comparison powerfully controls for stable confounding factors (genetics, chronic conditions) that don't change over short periods.
  • Example: mobile phone usage + car crashes study.
  • Compared driver's phone use 10 min before crash to phone use while driving @ same time of day on previous days.
Choosing the Right Observational Design
  • Choosing the right design balances the ideal (often prospective cohort for causality) w/ practical constraints.
  • Consider time, cost, and nature of the disease.
  • Weigh criteria to select appropriate design for research question.
Research Goal
  • Cross-Sectional: best for describing prevalence of disease/risk factor @ single point in time (e.g., % US adults w/ hypertension?). Weak for causality (unclear sequence).
  • Cohort: strongest observational design for determining incidence + investigating potential causes. Clearly establishes exposure precedes outcome, critical for causal inference.
  • Case-Control: primarily identifies risk factors by estimating association strength (via OR). Cannot determine prevalence or incidence directly.
Rarity of the Outcome
  • Case-Control: most efficient + often only feasible option for rare outcomes / long latency periods.
  • Cohort & Cross-Sectional: inefficient + often impractical for rare outcomes; require enormous sample sizes for enough cases.
Time and Cost Constraints
  • Cross-Sectional: fastest + least expensive (no follow-up).
  • Case-Control: relatively fast + inexpensive (no long follow-up).
  • Cohort (Prospective): most expensive + time-consuming (years/decades of follow-up).
  • Cohort (Retrospective): cheaper + faster if high-quality historical data exists.
Susceptibility to Major Biases
  • Cohort (Prospective): generally least susceptible to bias. Avoids recall bias (exposures measured before outcome), minimizes selection bias (well-defined cohort). Loss to follow-up is primary concern.
  • Case-Control: highly susceptible to selection bias (sampling cases/controls separately) + recall bias (retrospective exposure measurement). These are biggest weaknesses.
  • Cross-Sectional: less prone to recall/selection bias than case-control. Limited by inability to establish temporal sequence of events.
