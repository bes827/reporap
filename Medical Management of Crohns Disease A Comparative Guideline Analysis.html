Medical Management of Crohn's Disease: A Comparative Guideline Analysis
  • - Source 1 (ECCO): ECCO Guidelines on Therapeutics in Crohn’s Disease: Medical Treatment. Journal of Crohn's and Colitis, 2024.
  • - Source 2 (AGA): AGA Living Clinical Practice Guideline on the Pharmacologic Management of Moderate-to-Severe Crohn’s Disease. Gastroenterology, 2025.
knowledge test
  • - What is the recommended maintenance dose of parenteral methotrexate according to ECCO? 15 mg weekly.
  • - The AGA classifies which two advanced therapies as 'lower efficacy' for biologic-naïve patients? Certolizumab pegol and upadacitinib.
  • - The ECCO guidelines state that loss-of-function variants of the NUDT15 genotype, common in Asian populations, predispose to myelosuppression with thiopurines.
  • - According to the AGA, what is the clinically meaningful difference (CMD) threshold for comparisons between two active therapies? 5%.
  • - In the ECCO guidelines, what was the relative risk for clinical remission at Week 26 for infliximab + azathioprine vs infliximab monotherapy in the SONIC trial? RR: 1.64.

Guideline Comparison: Key Differences in Scope & Approach
  • - The two guidelines offer complementary perspectives on managing Crohn's Disease.
Scope & Focus
  • - ECCO (2024): Takes a broader approach.
  • - Covers mild-to-severe disease.
  • - Includes non-pharmacologic topics like Nutritional Therapy, the role of the Multidisciplinary Team (MDT), and Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM).
  • - AGA (2025): Tightly focused on pharmacologic management of moderate-to-severe CD.
  • - Does not cover mild disease, nutrition, or care delivery models.

Positioning & Sequencing of Therapies
  • - This is the most significant point of divergence.
ECCO Approach
  • - Adopts a drug-by-drug evaluation.
  • - Concludes there is insufficient evidence to direct a specific sequence of advanced therapies.
  • - Recommends decisions be based on efficacy, safety, patient/disease characteristics, and cost, viewing drugs on individual merit rather than a fixed sequence.

AGA Approach
  • - Provides explicit, hierarchical positioning of advanced therapies.
  • - Based on a comprehensive Network Meta-Analysis (NMA).
  • - Stratifies recommendations for biologic-naïve vs. biologic-exposed patients.
  • - Classifies drugs into efficacy tiers (e.g., Higher, Intermediate, Lower efficacy) to guide selection.


Recommendation Strength & Evidence Interpretation
  • - While often agreeing on a drug's general efficacy, the guidelines sometimes assign different strengths to recommendations or interpret trial data differently.
Example: Adalimumab Combination Therapy
  • - ECCO: Suggests monotherapy over combination in naïve patients, based on the DIAMOND trial.
  • - AGA: Makes NO RECOMMENDATION, citing the same trial but concluding it represents a knowledge gap.

Example: Upadacitinib
  • - ECCO: Gives a strong recommendation for moderate-to-severe CD.
  • - AGA: Gives a strong recommendation but notes FDA reserves it for TNF-failure. More importantly, their NMA classifies it as lower efficacy in naïve patients but higher efficacy in exposed patients.



General Treatment Principles
  • - Both guidelines emphasize a shift towards more proactive and objective-driven management.
Early & Effective Treatment
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines strongly advocate for early and effective treatment to prevent long-term complications.
ECCO (Practice Point 6)
  • - Emphasizes avoidance of diagnostic and treatment initiation delays.
  • - Cites the PROFILE trial: 'Top-down' (early infliximab + immunomodulator) was superior to 'accelerated step-up' care.
  • - Sustained steroid/surgery-free remission at Week 48: 79% (top-down) vs 15% (step-up).

AGA (Recommendation 15)
  • - Suggests upfront use of advanced therapy compared with step-up therapy (initial corticosteroids/immunomodulators).
  • - Based on trials like PROFILE and REACT-1 showing reduced disease-related complications.


Treat-to-Target (T2T)
  • - Both guidelines support the T2T concept but differ on the strength of evidence for specific targets.
ECCO (Practice Point 6)
  • - Recommends a tight control and T2T approach.
  • - States that targeting objective measures (e.g., fecal calprotectin, CRP) improves outcomes over targeting symptoms alone.

AGA (Recommendation 16)
  • - Makes NO RECOMMENDATION for treating to a target of endoscopic remission vs. symptomatic remission.
  • - Cites this as a knowledge gap due to conflicting trial data (STARDUST, REACT-2).


Role of Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) & Shared Decision Making (SDM)
  • - ECCO Exclusive (Practice Point 5): This topic is only covered in detail by ECCO.
MDT Involvement
  • - Recommends MDT involvement in clinical management and joint decision-making.
  • - MDT includes: gastroenterologists, IBD nurses, surgeons, psychologists, dietitians, radiologists, pathologists, pharmacists.
  • - Associated with reduced hospital admissions, surgery, and costs.

Shared Decision Making (SDM)
  • - SDM is fundamental to patient-centered care.
  • - Patients in SDM groups are more likely to report adherence to treatment (p = 0.001).



Positioning of Advanced Therapies
  • - This section highlights the primary difference between the two guidelines: AGA's structured, evidence-based positioning versus ECCO's more individualized approach.
ECCO Stance: Individualized Choice
  • - Practice Point 3: There is insufficient evidence to direct how advanced therapies should be positioned in a therapeutic algorithm.
  • - Decisions should consider: Efficacy, Safety, Patient preferences/characteristics, Disease characteristics, Cost/access.
  • - Drugs should be considered by merit, not sequenced as conventional to advanced.

AGA Stance: NMA-Based Tiering
  • - Uses a Network Meta-Analysis (NMA) to provide comparative efficacy estimates and create efficacy tiers.
  • - Recommendations are stratified by prior treatment exposure.
For Biologic-Naïve Patients (Recommendation 3)
  • - Suggests using a HIGHER efficacy medication over a LOWER efficacy medication.
HIGHER Efficacy Medications
  • - Infliximab
  • - Adalimumab
  • - Vedolizumab
  • - Ustekinumab
  • - Risankizumab
  • - Mirikizumab
  • - Guselkumab

LOWER Efficacy Medications
  • - Certolizumab pegol
  • - Upadacitinib


For Patients with Prior Advanced Therapy Exposure (Recommendation 4)
  • - Suggests using a HIGHER or INTERMEDIATE efficacy medication over a LOWER efficacy medication.
HIGHER Efficacy Medications
  • - Adalimumab
  • - Risankizumab
  • - Guselkumab
  • - Upadacitinib

INTERMEDIATE Efficacy Medications
  • - Ustekinumab
  • - Mirikizumab

LOWER Efficacy Medications
  • - Vedolizumab
  • - Certolizumab pegol




5-Aminosalicylates (5-ASA)
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines strongly recommend against the use of 5-ASA for Crohn's Disease.
ECCO (Statements 1.1 & 1.2)
  • - Strong recommendation against use for induction of remission.
  • - Meta-analysis of 7 RCTs showed clinical remission rates similar to placebo (RR: 1.28; 95% CI: 0.97–1.69).
  • - Strong recommendation against use for maintenance of remission.
  • - Meta-analysis of 11 trials showed no benefit (Risk Ratio for relapse 0.98).

AGA
  • - The AGA guideline focuses on moderate-to-severe disease and does not specifically issue a recommendation on 5-ASA, as it is not considered a therapy for this disease severity.


Corticosteroids
  • - Consensus: Effective for induction but should NOT be used for maintenance due to significant side effects.
Locally Acting Steroids (Budesonide)
  • - ECCO (Statement 2.1): Strongly recommends budesonide for induction in active, mild-to-moderate CD limited to the ileum and/or ascending colon.
  • - Dose: 9 mg daily.
  • - Superior to placebo for inducing clinical remission at Week 8 (RR: 1.93).
  • - Has a more favorable side-effect profile than systemic steroids.

Systemic Corticosteroids (Prednisone, Methylprednisolone)
  • - ECCO (Statement 2.2): Suggests use as induction therapy in active, moderate-to-severe CD.
  • - Recommendation is 'weak' due to the availability of agents with better risk-benefit profiles.
  • - Should only be used when no alternative is available for timely administration.
  • - Associated with increased risk of infection and death.


Immunomodulators (Thiopurines & Methotrexate)
  • - General consensus on roles, but AGA provides more granular recommendations.
Thiopurines (Azathioprine, Mercaptopurine)
  • - Consensus: Not for induction, can be used for maintenance.
Induction of Remission
  • - ECCO (Statement 3.1): Strong recommendation against thiopurine monotherapy for induction.
  • - AGA (Recommendation 5): Suggests AGAINST thiopurine monotherapy for induction.

Maintenance of Remission
  • - ECCO (Statement 3.2): Suggests thiopurine monotherapy can be used for maintenance (weak recommendation).
  • - Meta-analysis showed superiority to placebo for maintaining remission (RR: 1.19).
  • - AGA (Recommendation 6): SUGGESTS using thiopurine monotherapy for maintaining remission (typically corticosteroid-induced).

Safety & Monitoring
  • - ECCO: Highlights risk of pancreatitis, leukopenia, infections, lymphoproliferative disorders, and non-melanoma skin cancer (NMSC).
  • - Recommends pre-treatment analysis of TPMT and NUDT15 (especially in Asian populations) to reduce myelosuppression risk.


Methotrexate (MTX)
  • - Consensus: Parenteral form is effective; oral form is not.
Parenteral MTX (Subcutaneous/Intramuscular)
  • - ECCO (Statements 4.1 & 4.2): Suggests parenteral MTX for both induction and maintenance in moderate-to-severe CD.
  • - Induction dose: 25 mg/week IM was superior to placebo (RR: 2.06).
  • - Maintenance dose: 15 mg/week IM was superior to placebo (RR: 1.67).
  • - AGA (Recommendation 7): SUGGESTS using subcutaneous or intramuscular MTX monotherapy.

Oral MTX
  • - ECCO: Notes that studies of oral MTX at lower doses (12.5-15 mg weekly) failed to show efficacy.
  • - AGA (Recommendation 8): Suggests AGAINST using oral MTX monotherapy.

Safety & Considerations
  • - Both: MTX is teratogenic and contraindicated in pregnancy or those planning pregnancy.
  • - AGA: Recommends daily folic acid supplementation.



Anti-TNFα Agents
  • - Includes Infliximab, Adalimumab, and Certolizumab. Generally effective, but nuances exist regarding combination therapy and positioning.
Infliximab (IFX)
  • - Consensus: Strong recommendations for use in moderate-to-severe CD.
Monotherapy Efficacy
  • - ECCO (Statements 5.1 & 5.2): Recommends IFX for both induction and maintenance.
  • - AGA (Recommendation 1): Recommends IFX over no treatment.

Combination Therapy (with Thiopurines)
  • - Consensus: Combination therapy is superior to monotherapy, especially for induction.
ECCO (Statements 5.3 & 5.4)
  • - Recommends combination therapy with a thiopurine when starting IFX for induction.
  • - Cites SONIC trial: Higher clinical remission at Week 26 (RR: 1.64) and mucosal healing (RR: 1.82) vs IFX mono.
  • - Recommends continuing combination for a minimum of 6-12 months.

AGA (Recommendation 9)
  • - SUGGESTS using IFX in combination with thiopurines over IFX monotherapy, particularly in thiopurine-naïve patients.


De-escalation from Combination Therapy
  • - Consensus: In long-term remission, withdraw the immunomodulator, not the anti-TNF.
ECCO (Statement 5.5)
  • - Suggests de-escalation to anti-TNF monotherapy by withdrawing thiopurines.
  • - Cites SPARE trial: Relapse over 2 years was much lower when continuing IFX mono (9%) vs immunomodulator mono (35%).

AGA (Recommendations 13 & 14)
  • - SUGGESTS withdrawing the IMMUNOMODULATOR in patients in remission for at least 6 months.
  • - Suggests AGAINST withdrawal of the TNF ANTAGONIST.


Formulations
  • - ECCO: Notes availability of subcutaneous (SC) maintenance formulation after IV induction.
  • - AGA: Notes SC formulation has comparable efficacy to IV maintenance.


Adalimumab (ADA)
  • - Consensus: Effective for induction and maintenance. Key difference in recommendation for combination therapy.
Monotherapy Efficacy
  • - ECCO (Statements 6.1 & 6.2): Recommends ADA for both induction and maintenance.
  • - AGA (Recommendation 1): Recommends ADA over no treatment.

Combination Therapy
  • - Divergence: Guidelines interpret the evidence differently.
ECCO (Statements 6.3 & 6.4)
  • - Suggests ADA monotherapy should be used over combination therapy in biologic-naïve patients.
  • - Based on DIAMOND trial, which showed no superiority for combination therapy in clinical remission at Week 26 (RR: 0.95).

AGA (Recommendation 11)
  • - Makes NO RECOMMENDATION on using ADA in combination with thiopurines or methotrexate.
  • - Cites this as a knowledge gap, also referencing the DIAMOND trial but deeming the evidence insufficient for a firm recommendation.



Certolizumab Pegol
  • - Both guidelines view it as an option, but with weaker recommendations compared to other anti-TNFs.
ECCO (Statements 7.1 & 7.2)
  • - Suggests certolizumab can be used for induction and maintenance (weak recommendation).
  • - Notes it is not approved by the European Medicines Agency (EMA) for CD.

AGA (Recommendation 2)
  • - Suggests the use of certolizumab pegol over no treatment (conditional recommendation).
  • - In their positioning model, it is classified as a LOWER efficacy medication for both naïve and exposed patients.


Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM) for Anti-TNFs
  • - ECCO Exclusive: Only ECCO provides specific guidance on TDM.
Proactive TDM (Statement 8.1)
  • - Insufficient evidence to recommend proactive TDM over reactive TDM or standard of care (weak recommendation).
  • - Pooled RCT data showed no significant difference in clinical or endoscopic remission.

Reactive TDM & General Use (Practice Point 1)
  • - TDM may be used when optimising dose in patients treated with anti-TNF therapy.
  • - Useful for investigating primary non-response (PNR) or loss of response (LOR).



Anti-Integrin Therapy (Vedolizumab)
  • - Effective gut-selective therapy, but AGA positioning depends on prior biologic exposure.
ECCO (Statements 12.1 & 12.2)
  • - Recommends vedolizumab as induction and maintenance therapy in moderate-to-severe CD (strong recommendation).
  • - Superior to placebo for induction of clinical remission (RR: 2.00) and maintenance (RR: 1.55).

AGA (Recommendation 2 & Positioning)
  • - Suggests the use of vedolizumab over no treatment (conditional recommendation).
  • - Positioning Nuance:
  • - Classified as HIGHER efficacy in biologic-naïve patients.
  • - Classified as LOWER efficacy in patients with prior advanced therapy exposure.


IL-12/23 & IL-23 Inhibitors
  • - Includes Ustekinumab and Risankizumab. Both are strongly recommended by both guidelines.
Ustekinumab (IL-12/23)
  • - Consensus: Strong recommendations for use.
ECCO (Statements 9.1 & 9.2)
  • - Recommends ustekinumab for induction and maintenance (strong recommendation).
  • - Induction: Superior to placebo for clinical remission (RR: 1.76).
  • - Maintenance: Superior to placebo for clinical remission (RR: 1.42).

AGA (Recommendation 1 & Positioning)
  • - Recommends ustekinumab over no treatment (strong recommendation).
  • - Positioning Nuance:
  • - Classified as HIGHER efficacy in biologic-naïve patients.
  • - Classified as INTERMEDIATE efficacy in patients with prior advanced therapy exposure.

Head-to-Head vs. Adalimumab (SEAVUE Trial)
  • - ECCO (Statements 10.1 & 10.2): Suggests adalimumab and ustekinumab are equally as effective for induction and maintenance in biologic-naïve patients (weak recommendation).
  • - AGA: Incorporates this trial into its NMA, leading to both drugs being in the 'Higher Efficacy' tier for naïve patients.
SEAVUE Trial Details
  • - Phase 3b active comparator trial in biologic-naïve patients.
  • - Primary endpoint: Clinical remission (CDAI < 150) at Week 52.
  • - Results at Week 52: Ustekinumab 64.9% vs Adalimumab 61.0% (not statistically significant).



Risankizumab (IL-23)
  • - Consensus: Strong recommendations for use.
ECCO (Statements 11.1 & 11.2)
  • - Recommends risankizumab for induction and maintenance (strong recommendation, high-quality evidence).
  • - Induction: Superior to placebo for clinical remission (RR: 1.95) and endoscopic remission (RR: 3.22).
  • - Maintenance: Superior to placebo for clinical remission (RR: 1.31) and endoscopic response (RR: 2.13).

AGA (Recommendation 1 & Positioning)
  • - Recommends risankizumab over no treatment (strong recommendation).
  • - Positioning: Classified as HIGHER efficacy in both biologic-naïve and prior advanced therapy exposed patients.


Other IL-23 Inhibitors (Mirikizumab, Guselkumab)
  • - AGA Exclusive: These newer agents are covered by the AGA guideline but not ECCO.
Mirikizumab
  • - AGA (Recommendation 1 & Positioning): Recommends over no treatment.
  • - Positioning Nuance:
  • - Classified as HIGHER efficacy in biologic-naïve patients.
  • - Classified as INTERMEDIATE efficacy in patients with prior advanced therapy exposure.

Guselkumab
  • - AGA (Recommendation 1 & Positioning): Recommends over no treatment.
  • - Positioning: Classified as HIGHER efficacy in both biologic-naïve and prior advanced therapy exposed patients.



JAK Inhibitors (Upadacitinib)
  • - Consensus: Effective for moderate-to-severe CD, but AGA highlights a nuanced positioning.
ECCO (Statements 13.1 & 13.2)
  • - Recommends upadacitinib for induction and maintenance (strong recommendation).
  • - Induction (45mg): Superior to placebo for clinical remission (44.4% vs 25.1%) and endoscopic response (40.2% vs 8.4%).
  • - Maintenance (15mg & 30mg): Both doses superior to placebo for clinical remission and endoscopic response at Week 52.

AGA (Recommendation 1 & Positioning)
  • - Recommends upadacitinib over no treatment, but notes FDA reserves it for TNF-failure.
  • - Positioning Nuance (Critical Difference):
  • - Classified as LOWER efficacy in biologic-naïve patients.
  • - Classified as HIGHER efficacy in patients with prior advanced therapy exposure.

Safety Considerations
  • - ECCO: Notes higher rates of Herpes Zoster infection with upadacitinib (4.0% for 15mg, 7.2% for 30mg vs 4.7% placebo).
  • - AGA: Highlights potential increased risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), VTE, and cancer, especially in older adults with cardiovascular risk factors.


Nutritional Therapy
  • - ECCO Exclusive: This topic is only covered in detail by ECCO.
Exclusive Enteral Nutrition (EEN) for Induction
  • - ECCO (Statement 14.1): Suggests EEN for induction in mild-to-moderate CD as an alternative to corticosteroids (weak recommendation).
  • - Best for motivated patients with dietetic support.
  • - In adults, less effective than steroids in intention-to-treat analysis (RR: 0.65) due to compliance issues.
  • - Per-protocol analysis shows similar remission rates.

Other Dietary Therapies
  • - ECCO (Practice Points 2A & 2B): Discusses emerging evidence for food-based diets.
Crohn’s Disease Exclusion Diet (CDED)
  • - Most studied food-based diet for CD.
  • - Pilot RCT in adults with mild-to-moderate disease showed a 62% remission rate at Week 6.

Partial Enteral Nutrition (PEN) for Maintenance
  • - Might be considered for maintaining remission.
  • - Meta-analysis showed adding PEN to infliximab led to 74.5% remission at 1 year vs 49.2% with infliximab alone.

Other Diets
  • - Specific Carbohydrate Diet (SCD): Showed similar symptomatic remission to Mediterranean diet (~40%), but Mediterranean diet is easier to adhere to.
  • - Low FODMAP Diet: Recommended for functional symptoms in quiescent CD, not for active inflammation.



Advanced Combination Therapy (ACT)
  • - ECCO Exclusive (Practice Point 4): Refers to combining biologic agents and/or small molecules.
Indications for ACT
  • - Uncontrolled extraintestinal manifestations.
  • - Concomitant immune-mediated diseases.
  • - Refractory CD where surgery is not feasible.

Current Evidence
  • - No evidence to support ACT upfront in patients naïve to advanced therapies.
  • - Evidence is mostly retrospective and of limited quality.
  • - EXPLORER trial (single-arm): Investigated vedolizumab + adalimumab + methotrexate in newly diagnosed, high-risk CD.
