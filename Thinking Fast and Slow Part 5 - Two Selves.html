Thinking Fast and Slow: Part 5 - Two Selves
  • - A mind map of the key concepts from Part 5 of Daniel Kahneman's book, focusing on the distinction between the 'experiencing self' and the 'remembering self'.

  • - In the cold-hand experiment, what percentage of participants chose to repeat the longer, more painful episode?
  • - What is the term for the bias where the length of an experience has little to no effect on our memory of it?

  • - What is the name of the policy approach that aims to help people make better decisions without curtailing their freedom?

Core Concept: The Two Selves
  • - A fundamental conflict exists between two different ways we experience and think about our lives.
The Experiencing Self
  • - This self lives in the present and answers the question: “Does it hurt now?”
  • - It processes life as a series of moments.
  • - Its 'voice' is often lost or ignored in favor of memory.

The Remembering Self
  • - This self is the storyteller and scorekeeper, answering the question: “How was it, on the whole?”
  • - It makes decisions based on memories, which can be flawed.
  • - It is the perspective we adopt when thinking about our lives.

Conflict & Tyranny
  • - The remembering self governs what we learn from living and makes decisions for the future.
  • - This can lead to choices that are not in the best interest of the experiencing self.
  • - This is the tyranny of the remembering self.
Example: Ruined Symphony
  • - A long, beautiful symphony ending with a screeching sound is remembered as 'ruined'.
  • - The remembering self gives a failing grade to the entire episode because of the bad ending.
  • - The experiencing self had a mostly good experience that cannot be undone.



Two Meanings of 'Utility'
  • - The term 'utility' has been used in two distinct ways, leading to confusion between experience and choice.
Experienced Utility (Bentham)
  • - Refers to the actual experience of pleasure and pain.
  • - The 'hedonimeter' concept: a measure of the level of pleasure or pain experienced at any moment.
  • - The total experienced utility is the 'area under the curve' over time.

Decision Utility (Economists)
  • - Refers to 'wantability' – what we choose or prefer.
  • - Expected utility theory is about rational rules governing these choices, not about the actual feelings (hedonic experiences).

Discrepancy Example: Painful Injections
  • - People would pay more to reduce injections from 6 to 4 than from 20 to 18.
  • - The decision utility is different, even though the experienced utility (avoiding 2 injections) is the same.
  • - This is an error, as it assigns different values to the same amount of pain reduction.


Memory vs. Experience: Key Biases
  • - The remembering self does not create an accurate summary of experiences. It uses mental shortcuts that lead to systematic errors.
The Peak-End Rule
  • - Retrospective ratings are predicted by the average of the most intense moment (peak) and the final moment (end) of an experience.

Duration Neglect
  • - The duration of an episode has no effect on the memory or global rating of it.
  • - This contradicts our preference for short pains and long pleasures.

Evidence: Colonoscopy Study
  • - Patients rated their pain every 60 seconds during a colonoscopy and gave a global rating afterward.
Patient A vs. Patient B
  • - Patient A: Shorter procedure (8 min), but pain was high at the end (Peak 8, End 7).
  • - Patient B: Longer procedure (24 min), but pain was low at the end (Peak 8, End 1).
  • - Result: Patient A had a much worse memory (Peak-End average 7.5) than Patient B (Peak-End average 4.5), despite Patient B suffering for a longer duration.

Clinical Implications
  • - To reduce memory of pain: Lower the peak intensity and ensure gradual relief.
  • - To reduce actual pain experienced: Conduct the procedure swiftly, even if it increases peak pain.


Evidence: Cold-Hand Experiment
  • - A direct test of conflict between the experiencing and remembering selves.
Experimental Design
  • - Short Episode: 60 seconds in painfully cold water (14°C).
  • - Long Episode: 60 seconds in cold water + 30 seconds in slightly warmer water.
  • - Participants were then asked to choose which episode to repeat.

Results & Conclusion
  • - The long trial was objectively worse (more total pain).
  • - However, its memory was less aversive due to a better 'end'.
  • - Fully 80% of participants chose to repeat the longer episode.
  • - This is a clear mistake where decision utility (based on flawed memory) overrides experienced utility.



Life as a Story
  • - The remembering self composes stories, not summaries. In stories, duration is irrelevant, but key moments and the ending are critical.
Narrative Dominance
  • - We care about significant events and memorable moments, not about time passing.
  • - The ending often defines the character of the entire story (e.g., La Traviata opera).

Evidence: 'Jen's Life' Study
  • - Participants evaluated the total happiness of a fictitious person's life.
Findings
  • - Duration Neglect: Doubling Jen's happy life from 30 to 60 years had no effect on the evaluation of her total happiness.
  • - Less-is-More Effect: Adding 5 'slightly happy' years to a 'very happy' life caused a substantial drop in the evaluation of total happiness.
  • - This shows life is evaluated as a prototype or average, not a sum.


Amnesic Vacations
  • - A thought experiment reveals our priorities.
The Scenario
  • - Imagine a vacation where at the end, all photos will be destroyed and a potion will wipe your memory of it.
  • - How much is this vacation worth to you?

Implication
  • - Most people say the value is greatly reduced, some wouldn't go at all.
  • - This reveals we often prioritize the remembering self (constructing memories) over the experiencing self (enjoying the moment).



Measuring Experienced Well-Being
  • - Moving beyond simple life satisfaction surveys to measure the well-being of the experiencing self.
Measurement Methods
  • - Experience Sampling: Pinging people at random times during the day to ask about their current feelings.
  • - Day Reconstruction Method (DRM): A more practical method where people relive the previous day, breaking it into episodes and rating their feelings.

The U-Index
  • - A duration-weighted measure of well-being.
  • - Definition: The percentage of time an individual spends in an unpleasant state.
  • - Findings: American women 19%, French women 16%, Danish women 14%.
  • - Activities with high U-Index for American women: Morning commute (29%), Work (27%), Child care (24%).

Key Determinants of Well-Being
  • - Attention is key: Our emotional state is largely determined by what we attend to.
  • - Situational factors (e.g., socializing with coworkers, time pressure) are more important than general factors (e.g., benefits, status).
  • - Happiness is largely the experience of spending time with people you love and who love you.

Can Money Buy Happiness?
  • - The answer depends on which self you ask.
Experienced Well-Being
  • - Being poor makes one miserable.
  • - Beyond a household income of about $75,000 in high-cost areas, the average increase in experienced well-being is zero.
  • - Higher income may reduce the ability to enjoy small pleasures.

Life Satisfaction
  • - Higher income brings higher life satisfaction, well beyond the $75,000 point.
  • - This shows that life satisfaction and experienced well-being are two different concepts.



The Focusing Illusion
  • - A cognitive bias that occurs when people place too much importance on one aspect of an event, causing an error in accurately predicting the utility of a future outcome.
Core Idea
  • - "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is when you are thinking about it."

Mechanism
  • - When you are asked to evaluate something, you substitute the answer to an easier question: "How do I feel about this thing when I'm thinking about it?"
  • - This is a form of WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is) and duration neglect.

Examples
California Climate
  • - People in the Midwest and California both believe Californians are happier due to the climate.
  • - In reality, there is no difference in life satisfaction.
  • - People focus on the climate and ignore the countless other factors that determine well-being.

Paraplegics
  • - People vastly overestimate the proportion of time paraplegics spend in a bad mood.
  • - They focus on the condition and fail to account for adaptation, where attention is withdrawn from the new situation over time.

Miswanting
  • - The focusing illusion is a rich source of miswanting (bad choices from errors of affective forecasting).
  • - It creates a bias for goods that are initially exciting (a new car) over experiences that retain attention value (a weekly social club).



Conclusions & Implications
  • - A summary of the three major distinctions discussed in the book: Two Selves, Econs vs. Humans, and Two Systems.
Two Selves Revisited
  • - The remembering self's rules (peak-end, duration neglect) are poor guides for decision-making because time is the ultimate finite resource.
  • - A hybrid view is needed: a theory of well-being must consider both the experiencing self and the remembering self.

Econs vs. Humans
  • - Econs: Fictional agents who are rational (logically coherent). The basis for traditional economics.
  • - Humans: Not irrational, but not Econs. They are susceptible to biases and often need help making better decisions.

Libertarian Paternalism (Nudge)
  • - The policy approach of helping Humans make better choices without restricting their freedom.
  • - It involves designing the 'choice architecture' to favor better outcomes.
  • - Example: Save More Tomorrow program, which makes saving the default option and ties increases to pay raises.

Two Systems Revisited
  • - System 1 (fast, intuitive) is the origin of most of what we do right, but also the source of predictable biases.
  • - System 2 (slow, effortful) is who we think we are, but it is often lazy and endorses System 1's suggestions.
  • - How to improve: Recognize the signs of a 'cognitive minefield', slow down, and ask for reinforcement from System 2.
