Writing the Methods Section
  • A guide for researchers on structuring and writing the Methods section for various types of medical studies.
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the recommended verb tense for describing methods?
  • - What does the acronym CONSORT stand for?
  • - In which article format does the Results section immediately follow the Introduction?
  • - What is the term for reusing your own previously published text in a new manuscript?
  • - What is the typical P value at which differences are considered statistically significant?

Core Principles of the Methods Section
  • - Goal: Report the study with sufficient detail to permit an experienced investigator to repeat it.
  • - Emphasizes thoroughness and transparency in describing study design, performance, and data analysis.
Key Reporting Guidelines & Initiatives
  • - Several organizations provide guidelines to enhance rigor and reproducibility.
NIH Guidelines
  • - Published for preclinical research.

CONSORT (Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials)
  • - Provides guidance for reporting randomized controlled trials.
  • - Features the CONSORT Statement: a 25-item checklist and flow diagram for transparency.

EQUATOR Network
  • - Enhancing the QUAlity and Transparency Of health Research.
  • - Maintains a comprehensive database of reporting guidelines.

ARRIVE Guidelines 2.0
  • - Animal Research: Reporting In Vivo Experiments.
  • - Aims to improve the reporting of research using animals.

STAR Methods
  • - Structured, Transparent, Accessible Reporting format from Cell Press.
  • - Guides authors in organizing the Methods section to facilitate replication.

Research Resource Identifiers (RRIDs)
  • - Unique alphanumeric identifiers for materials like cell lines, antibodies, and software.
  • - Promoted by the Resource Identification Initiative.



Structure for Animal Studies
  • - Based on the ARRIVE guidelines 2.0.
Animal Provenance & Status
  • - Detail the source of animals.
  • - Report on health/immune status, genetic modification, and genotype.
  • - List any previous procedures performed.

Number of Animals
  • - State the total number of animals used.
  • - Provide a justification for the sample size (e.g., power calculations).

Procedures
  • - Describe what was done to the animals.
  • - If animals were euthanized, specify the method.

Experiments
  • - Describe what experiments were performed, why, and how.
  • - Chronological order is often effective.
  • - Each experiment can be a separate paragraph, starting with its objective.

Statistical Tests
  • - See 'Common Statistical Reporting' section for details.


Structure for Retrospective Clinical Studies
  • - A common structure for reporting studies based on existing data (e.g., chart reviews, databases).
Subjects and Selection Criteria
  • - Specify the source of study subjects (e.g., chart review, database).
  • - Detail inclusion and exclusion criteria, including date ranges.

Ethics
  • - State whether the study was approved by an institutional review board (IRB).
  • - Mention the use of informed consent or if it was waived by the IRB.

Data Reviewed
  • - List the specific types of data extracted from patient records.

Subgroups
  • - Explain how study subgroups were defined.

Number of Patients
  • - Report the final number of patients included in the study and in each subgroup.
  • - This can be in Methods or at the beginning of Results.

Evaluations and Interventions
  • - Provide general information on pretreatment evaluations, treatments, and follow-up.
  • - If treatment variations are the focus, report numbers per treatment type in the Results section alongside outcomes.

Statistical Tests
  • - See 'Common Statistical Reporting' section for details.


Structure for Prospective Clinical Studies (CONSORT)
  • - Follows recommendations from the CONSORT statement for randomized clinical trials.
Trial Design
  • - Describe the trial design (e.g., parallel, factorial).
  • - Include the allocation ratio.
  • - Report any important changes to methods after commencement, with reasons.

Participants
  • - State the eligibility criteria for participants.
  • - Describe the settings and locations where data were collected.

Interventions
  • - Detail the interventions for each group sufficiently to allow replication.
  • - Include how and when they were administered.

Outcomes
  • - Completely define prespecified primary and secondary outcome measures.
  • - Include how and when they were assessed.
  • - Report any changes to outcomes after the trial started, with reasons.

Sample Size
  • - Explain how sample size was determined.
  • - If applicable, describe any interim analyses and stopping guidelines.

Randomization
  • - Details on the randomization process.
Sequence Generation
  • - Method used to generate the random allocation sequence.
  • - Type of randomization (e.g., blocking, block size).

Allocation Concealment
  • - Mechanism used to implement the sequence (e.g., sequentially numbered containers).
  • - Describe steps taken to conceal the sequence until assignment.

Implementation
  • - State who generated the sequence, who enrolled participants, and who assigned them to interventions.


Blinding
  • - If done, state who was blinded (e.g., participants, care providers, outcome assessors) and how.
  • - If relevant, describe the similarity of interventions.

Statistical Methods
  • - See 'Common Statistical Reporting' section for details.
  • - Also include methods for additional analyses (e.g., subgroup, adjusted analyses).

Ethical Conduct
  • - Describe procedures to ensure ethical conduct.
  • - e.g., IRB approval, informed consent process.


Common Statistical Reporting (All Study Types)
  • - Key elements for the statistical tests subsection.
Outcome Measures
  • - Define outcome measures.
  • - Specify the minimum differences considered clinically important.

Tests Performed
  • - List all statistical tests performed.
  • - Provide references for all but well-known tests (e.g., Student t-test, χ² test).

Software
  • - Name any software packages used for analysis.

Purpose of Tests
  • - Explain what each test was used to evaluate.

Significance Level
  • - State the P value at which differences were considered statistically significant (e.g., P < 0.05).


General Writing Style & Conventions
  • - Guidelines for formatting, tone, and reusing text.
Journal-Specific Tailoring
  • - Always check the target journal's instructions for authors.
Location of Methods
  • - Usually after Introduction, before Results.
  • - Some journals place it last, after the Discussion.
  • - Some place details in figure legends or online supplements.

Level of Detail
  • - For broad readership journals ---> more detail to be understandable to other fields.
  • - For specialized journals ---> less detail for methods well-known to the audience.

Citing Previous Methods
  • - For a previously described technique, name it and cite the original article.
  • - It is helpful to add a brief description (e.g., 'Briefly, ...').
  • - Always describe any modifications made to the published technique.


Verb Tense
  • - The correct tense is crucial for clarity.
Describing Methods
  • - Use the past tense.
  • - Example: 'We performed PCR analysis...'

Describing Prior Events
  • - Use the past perfect tense for events before the study.
  • - Example: '...previous therapy had failed.'
  • - Simple past tense is often sufficient if the sequence is clear.

Common Pitfall
  • - Avoid using present tense, even for routine procedures.
  • - The report is about what was done for this specific study, not what is usually done.


Text Recycling ('Self-Plagiarism')
  • - The reuse of one's own previously published text, called text recycling, is common but requires care.
General Guideline (COPE, BioMed Central)
  • - Use of identical phrases in methods is often unavoidable and acceptable.
  • - Editors use discretion based on the field.

Author Responsibilities
  • - Disclose the text recycling in the cover letter to the journal.
  • - Provide a citation in the text indicating the original source.

TRRP Principles
  • - The Text Recycling Research Project (TRRP) provides key principles:
  • - Methods descriptions may be recycled but must be reviewed for accuracy.
  • - If corresponding authors differ, permission should be obtained.

Publisher Policies
  • - Policies vary; always check the target journal's instructions.
  • - Example: Anesthesiology permits limited recycling in Methods with proper citation.


Article Formats: IMRAD vs. IRDAM
  • - The placement of the Methods section can depend on the journal's format.
IMRAD Format
  • - Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion.
  • - The most common format in clinical journals.

IRDAM Format
  • - Introduction, Results, Discussion, And Methods.
  • - Common in high-impact basic science journals.
  • - Rationale: Experiments are often based on results of prior experiments, so results drive the narrative.

Writing for IRDAM
  • - The Results section is composed of subsections for each experiment.
  • - Each subsection includes the goal, basic methods, results, and a brief comment.
  • - The main Methods section at the end should contain only technical details, as the 'why' is explained in Results.
