Overlap of Esophageal Disorders and GERD
  • Article: Overlap of esophageal disorders of gut-brain interactions (EDGBIs) and GERD is highly prevalent in patients with refractory reflux symptoms.
  • Authors: Federico Caldart, Charlotte Gabriel, Blandine Vauquelin, Pauline Rivière, Arthur Berger, Frank Zerbib.
  • Journal: American Journal of Gastroenterology.
  • DOI: 10.14309/ajg.0000000000003542
Key Concepts
  • Focus: Prevalence & characteristics of refractory GERD & E-DGBIs in patients w/ refractory reflux symptoms.
  • Key Findings:
  • - E-DGBIs are highly prevalent in proven GERD patients w/ refractory symptoms.
  • - Prior GERD diagnosis ≠ rule out FH/RH on PPIs.
E-DGBIs
  • Esophageal Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction.
  • Includes: Reflux Hypersensitivity (RH) & Functional Heartburn (FH).
Reflux Hypersensitivity (RH)
  • Normal AET + Positive symptom-reflux association (SAP >95% and/or SI >50%).
Symptom Association
  • Defined by: Symptom Index (SI) & Symptom Association Probability (SAP).
  • Positive if: SAP > 95% and/or SI > 50%.
Symptom Index (SI)
  • Measure of symptom correlation with reflux events.
Symptom Association Probability (SAP)
  • Statistical probability of symptom correlation with reflux events.
Functional Heartburn (FH)
  • Normal AET + Negative symptom association (SAP ≤95% and SI ≤50%).
GERD
  • Gastro-Esophageal Reflux Disease.
  • Requires: Troublesome symptoms + Evidence of pathological reflux (endoscopy/ambulatory reflux monitoring).
Refractory GERD
  • Persistent esophagitis/stricture despite therapy OR Ongoing pathological reflux on pH-impedance monitoring on PPIs.
Actionable GERD
  • Ongoing GERD despite PPI therapy.
  • Candidates for: Laparoscopic antireflux surgery or magnetic sphincter augmentation.
Diagnosis of GERD
  • Requires compatible troublesome symptoms and evidence of pathological reflux based on endoscopy and/or ambulatory reflux monitoring.
Overlap
  • Coexistence of GERD and E-DGBIs.
  • Patients may have persistent symptoms related to E-DGBIs despite GERD treatment.
Study Design & Methods
  • Prospective data collection from consecutive adult patients w/ typical refractory reflux symptoms.
  • Exclusion: Atypical symptoms, prior GI surgery, endoscopically proven refractory GERD.
  • Assessment: Endoscopy, manometry, pH/pH-impedance monitoring, questionnaires.
Study Population
  • Patients w/ typical symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation, NCCP) referred to Bordeaux University Hospital (Jan 2022 - May 2024).
  • Exclusion criteria: Previous GI surgery, primary atypical symptoms, probe misplacement, non-adherence, inability to stop PPIs.
Data Collection
  • Age, gender, primary symptoms, pH/pH-impedance data, esophageal motor disorders, endoscopy findings.
  • Questionnaires: RDQ, Rome IV (IBS/FD), HADS, VSI.
Reflux Disease Questionnaire (RDQ)
  • Assesses reflux symptom burden.
Rome IV Questionnaire
  • Identifies IBS & FD.
Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS)
  • Measures anxiety & depression levels.
Visceral Sensitivity Index (VSI)
  • Assesses visceral sensitivity.
  • Increased VSI: Value > 37.5.
Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
  • Unproven GERD: pH study off PPIs (standard pH catheter/wireless pH capsule).
  • Proven GERD: pH-impedance study on twice-daily PPIs.
Lyon 2.0 Criteria
  • Used to determine patients’ phenotypes.
  • Off PPIs: AET >6% pathological, 4-6% inconclusive, <4% normal.
  • Proven GERD: AET >4% or >80 reflux episodes on PPI conclusive for refractory GERD.
MNBI Evaluation
  • Evaluated during entire supine period.
Results
  • 119 patients included (67% female, mean age 49.2 years).
  • Unproven GERD (n=73): 23 GERD, 50 E-DGBI (28 RH, 22 FH).
  • Proven GERD (n=46): 13 refractory GERD, 33 GERD/E-DGBI overlap (22 FH, 11 RH).
Unproven GERD Group (n=73)
  • 9 had grade A esophagitis, 64 normal endoscopy.
  • After pH study off PPI: 23 GERD, 50 E-DGBI (28 RH, 22 FH).
  • 14 patients w/ inconclusive AET (4-6%) included in E-DGBI group based on SAP & SI.
  • 26 & 8 patients had anxiety & depression scores above 8.
  • 16 (21.9%) patients had VSI > 37.5.
Proven GERD Group (n=46)
  • 25 had normal endoscopy, 4 grade A, 16 grade B/C esophagitis, 1 Barrett’s.
  • 13 (28%) confirmed refractory GERD (7 ↑AET, 6 >80 reflux episodes).
  • 33 (72%) no evidence of pathological GERD on PPIs -> GERD/E-DGBI overlap (22 FH, 11 RH).
  • 13 & 6 patients had anxiety & depression scores above 8.
  • 10 (21.7%) patients had VSI > 37.5.
Prevalence of FD & IBS
  • High prevalence of FD (70.6%) & IBS (33.6%) symptoms reported.
E-DGBI Patients Comparison
  • Compared E-DGBI alone (n=50) vs. GERD overlap (n=33).
  • No significant differences: gender, age, MNBI, RDQ, HADS, VSI, IBS, FD.
RH Patients Comparison
  • Compared RH alone (n=28) vs. GERD overlap (n=11).
  • No significant differences: gender, age, HADS, RDQ, IBS, FD.
  • ↑ Visceral sensitivity (VSI) in GERD overlap group (p=0.033).
FH Patients Comparison
  • Compared FH alone (n=22) vs. GERD overlap (n=22).
  • No significant differences: MNBI, RDQ, HADS, VSI, IBS, FD.
Refractory GERD vs. Overlap Comparison
  • Compared refractory GERD (n=13) vs. GERD-E-DGBI overlap (n=33).
  • Refractory GERD: ↑ AET (p=0.016), ↓ MNBI (p=0.006), ↑ reflux episodes (p<0.001).
  • RDQ higher in refractory GERD (p=0.085, not significant).
  • No significant differences: HADS, VSI, IBS, FD.
Discussion
  • E-DGBIs are main cause of PPI failure in proven GERD (72%).
  • Prior GERD diagnosis ≠ rule out FH/RH on PPIs.
  • Only 10.9% had refractory GERD after investigations.
  • High prevalence of non-esophageal DGBI conditions (IBS, FD).
E-DGBI Prevalence
  • E-DGBIs present in up to 40% of patients w/ refractory reflux-like symptoms treated empirically w/ PPIs.
  • Present study: E-DGBIs are main cause of PPI failure in proven GERD (72%).
Actionable GERD
  • In present study, among 46 patients with proven GERD studied with pH-impedance monitoring on PPIs, only 13 (28.2%) had pathological reflux, 11 (23.9%) had reflux hypersensitivity, and 22 (47.8%) had functional heartburn.
Overlap Similarity
  • Patients w/ overlapping GERD & E-DGBIs had similar symptom scores & psychometric profiles to patients w/ E-DGBIs alone.
  • Both groups likely share similar underlying mechanisms (altered autonomic activity, ↑ nociceptor sensitivity, altered central processing).
Psychological Factors
  • Psychological factors significantly impact symptom severity more than physiological parameters in refractory reflux symptoms.
Limitations
  • Retrospective design, no treatment outcome data, adherence to PPI therapy not checked for all patients with unproven GERD, tertiary care center, low number of patients with truly refractory GERD.
Conclusion
  • E-DGBIs are highly prevalent in proven GERD patients w/ refractory symptoms despite PPIs.
  • Patients w/ E-DGBIs & proven GERD have similar clinical & psychometric profiles to patients w/ E-DGBI w/o GERD.
  • Outcome data needed to determine if these patients should be identified using pH-impedance monitoring on PPIs before interventional treatment.
Future Research
  • Should investigate long-term trajectories of GERD-EDGBI overlap patients and evaluate responses to neuromodulators and dietary interventions.
