Thinking Fast and Slow
Appendix A: Judgment Under Uncertainty - Heuristics and Biases
  • - Authors: Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman.
  • - This appendix explains how people make judgments and decisions when faced with uncertainty.
  • - People use simple mental shortcuts, called heuristics, which are generally useful but can lead to predictable, systematic errors (biases).

1. Representativeness Heuristic
  • - Definition: Judging the probability of something (A) by how much it resembles, or is representative of, something else (B).
  • - Key Idea: We judge based on stereotypes or similarity, not on actual probability.
  • - Example: People think Steve, a 'meek and tidy soul', is more likely to be a librarian than a farmer because his description matches the stereotype of a librarian.
Insensitivity to Prior Probability (Base Rates)
  • - Problem: People ignore the base-rate frequency (the actual statistical prevalence) of an outcome when they have a descriptive story, even if the story is useless.
  • - This is a major violation of Bayes' rule of probability.
Engineer vs. Lawyer Experiment
  • - Subjects read personality descriptions from a group of 100 people.
  • - Condition 1: Group has 70 engineers and 30 lawyers.
  • - Condition 2: Group has 30 engineers and 70 lawyers.
  • - Result: Subjects gave the same probability judgments in both conditions, ignoring the base rates. They focused only on how much the description matched the stereotype of an engineer or lawyer.
Worthless Evidence
  • - When given a vague, uninformative description (e.g., "Dick is a 30-year-old man..."), people also ignored the base rates.
  • - They judged the probability of Dick being an engineer as 50%, regardless of whether the group had 70% or 30% engineers.
  • - Key Takeaway: People use base rates correctly only when they have no other information. Worthless information is treated as if it's valuable.



Insensitivity to Sample Size
  • - Problem: People don't understand that larger samples are less likely to deviate from the average.
  • - We expect small samples to be just as representative of the population as large samples.
Hospital Babies Example
  • - A town has two hospitals:
  • - - Large hospital: 45 babies born per day.
  • - - Small hospital: 15 babies born per day.
  • - Question: Which hospital recorded more days with >60% boys born?
  • - Most people answered: About the same (53 students).
  • - Correct Answer: The smaller hospital, because its daily results will have much more variability.

Urn of Balls Example
  • - An urn has 2/3 red balls and 1/3 white balls.
  • - - Sample 1: 4 red, 1 white (80% red).
  • - - Sample 2: 12 red, 8 white (60% red).
  • - Question: Which sample gives more confidence the urn is mostly red?
  • - Most people chose Sample 1 because the proportion is higher.
  • - Correct Answer: Sample 2 provides much stronger evidence because of its larger size. The correct posterior odds are 16 to 1 for the 12:8 sample vs. 8 to 1 for the 4:1 sample.


Misconceptions of Chance
  • - Problem: We expect random sequences to look 'random' even when they are short (Belief in the Law of Small Numbers).
  • - We think chance is a self-correcting process.
Gambler's Fallacy
  • - After a long run of 'red' on a roulette wheel, people believe 'black' is now due.
  • - Reality: Chance is not self-correcting. Deviations are not corrected; they are merely diluted as more events occur.

Impact on Researchers
  • - Even experienced research psychologists believe small samples are highly representative of the population.
  • - This leads to:
  • - - Using samples of inadequate size.
  • - - Over-interpreting findings from small studies.
  • - - Grossly overestimating the chance that results can be replicated.


Insensitivity to Predictability
  • - Problem: When making predictions, people rely on how well a description fits an outcome, ignoring the actual predictability of the evidence.
  • - If a description is very favorable, people predict a very positive outcome, even if the information is unreliable.
Student Teacher Study
  • - Subjects read a description of a student teacher's practice lesson.
  • - - Group 1: Evaluated the quality of the lesson.
  • - - Group 2: Predicted the teacher's standing 5 years later.
  • - Result: The predictions were identical to the evaluations. People made extreme predictions based on limited data, ignoring the low predictability of future success from a single lesson.


The Illusion of Validity
  • - Definition: Unwarranted confidence in predictions produced by a good 'fit' between the predicted outcome and the input information.
  • - This illusion persists even when we know the information is unreliable or outdated.
  • - Example: Psychologists often feel very confident in their interview-based predictions, despite literature showing interviews are highly fallible.
Confidence from Consistency
  • - Confidence increases with the internal consistency of input data.
  • - Example: More confidence in predicting the GPA of a student with all B's than one with many A's and C's.
  • - Statistical Fact: Redundant/correlated inputs (which create consistency) actually decrease predictive accuracy compared to independent inputs.


Misconceptions of Regression to the Mean
  • - Definition: In any series of events with a random element, an extreme outcome is likely to be followed by a less extreme one (closer to the average).
  • - Problem: People don't expect regression to occur and often invent causal explanations for it.
Flight Instructor Example
  • - Instructors observed:
  • - - Praise for a smooth landing ---> poorer next landing.
  • - - Criticism for a rough landing ---> better next landing.
  • - Erroneous Conclusion: They concluded that punishment is more effective than reward.
  • - Correct Explanation: It was simply regression toward the mean. An exceptionally good or bad performance is likely to be followed by a more average one, regardless of feedback.

The Human Condition
  • - Because of regression, we are often statistically punished for rewarding others and rewarded for punishing them.
  • - This happens by chance alone, but we misinterpret it as a causal relationship.



2. Availability Heuristic
  • - Definition: Judging the frequency or probability of an event by the ease with which instances come to mind.
  • - Key Idea: If it's easy to recall, we think it's common or likely.
  • - This is often useful, but ease of recall is affected by factors other than frequency.
Biases Due to Retrievability of Instances
  • - A class whose instances are more easily retrieved will seem more numerous.
Famous Names Experiment
  • - Subjects heard a list of names of men and women.
  • - In some lists, the men were more famous; in others, the women were.
  • - Result: In every case, subjects judged that the sex that had the more famous personalities was more numerous in the list, even when it wasn't.

Other Factors
  • - Salience: Seeing a house burn has a greater impact on your perceived risk of fire than reading about it.
  • - Recency: Seeing a car accident temporarily increases your subjective probability of being in one.


Biases Due to Effectiveness of a Search Set
  • - We judge frequency by how easily we can search for examples in our mind.
Words Starting with 'r' vs. 'r' in Third Position
  • - Question: Is it more likely a word starts with 'r' or has 'r' as the third letter?
  • - Most people say starts with 'r' because it's much easier to search for words that way (e.g., road, run).
  • - Reality: Consonants like 'r' or 'k' are actually more frequent in the third position (e.g., car, park).


Biases of Imaginability
  • - Problem: We assess frequency by how easily we can construct or imagine instances, but this ease does not reflect actual frequency.
Committee Size Example
  • - Question: From a group of 10 people, how many different committees of size 'k' can be formed?
  • - People estimate by trying to imagine the committees.
  • - It's easier to imagine small committees (e.g., size 2) than large ones (e.g., size 8).
  • - Result: People's estimates decreased as committee size increased. For k=2, median estimate was 70; for k=8, it was 20.
  • - Correct Answer: The number of committees of size k is the same as size (10-k). The number for k=2 and k=8 is both 45.

Real-Life Risk Assessment
  • - The perceived risk of an expedition is judged by how easily we can imagine potential disasters.
  • - Conversely, risk can be underestimated if dangers are hard to conceive or don't come to mind.


Illusory Correlation
  • - Definition: Seeing a relationship between two events that are not actually connected, simply because they are strongly associated in our minds.
  • - Example: In a study, subjects were shown patient drawings and clinical diagnoses. They 'rediscovered' common but unfounded clinical beliefs, like a link between suspiciousness and peculiar eyes in drawings.
  • - This effect persisted even when the actual data showed a negative correlation.


3. Adjustment and Anchoring Heuristic
  • - Definition: Making estimates by starting from an initial value (an 'anchor') and adjusting it to get the final answer.
  • - Problem: Adjustments are typically insufficient, so the final answer is biased toward the initial anchor.
Insufficient Adjustment
  • - The anchor can be suggested by the problem or be the result of a partial calculation.
Wheel of Fortune Experiment
  • - Subjects were asked to estimate the percentage of African countries in the UN.
  • - First, a random number (the anchor) was spun on a wheel.
  • - - Group 1 Anchor: 10 ---> Median Estimate: 25%
  • - - Group 2 Anchor: 65 ---> Median Estimate: 45%
  • - The arbitrary anchor had a marked effect on the estimates.

Biases in Evaluating Conjunctive & Disjunctive Events
  • - The probability of an elementary event acts as an anchor.
Conjunctive Events (Chain-like)
  • - A series of events that must all occur for a successful outcome (e.g., completing a project).
  • - People anchor on the high probability of each individual event and fail to adjust enough for the cumulative effect.
  • - Result: We tend to overestimate the probability of conjunctive events, leading to unwarranted optimism.

Disjunctive Events (Funnel-like)
  • - A series of events where only one needs to occur for an outcome (e.g., failure of a complex system).
  • - People anchor on the low probability of any single component failing.
  • - Result: We tend to underestimate the probability of disjunctive events, leading to underestimation of risks in complex systems.


Anchoring in Subjective Probability Distributions
  • - Problem: When asked to provide a confidence interval (e.g., a range where they are 98% sure the true answer lies), people anchor on their best estimate and don't adjust far enough outwards.
  • - Result: People state overly narrow confidence intervals, reflecting more certainty than is justified.
Calibration Study
  • - In most studies, the true values of quantities fell outside the subjects' stated 98% confidence interval about 30% of the time.
  • - This bias affects both naive and sophisticated subjects and is not fixed by rewarding accuracy.



Appendix B: Choices, Values, and Frames
  • - This section discusses the cognitive and psychophysical factors that determine our choices.
  • - It introduces Prospect Theory as an alternative to rational choice theory.
Prospect Theory & Value Function
  • - People don't think in terms of total wealth, but in terms of gains and losses from a reference point (the status quo).
  • - The subjective value function is S-shaped.
Key Properties of the Value Function
  • - 1. Reference Dependence: Defined on gains and losses, not total wealth.
  • - 2. Diminishing Sensitivity: Concave for gains (risk aversion) and convex for losses (risk seeking).
  • - - The difference between a $100 and $200 gain feels bigger than between a $1100 and $1200 gain.
  • - 3. Loss Aversion: The function is steeper for losses than for gains. A loss of $X is more painful than a gain of $X is pleasurable.

Risk Attitudes
  • - In the domain of gains: People are generally risk averse.
  • - - Most prefer a sure $800 over an 85% chance to win $1000.
  • - In the domain of losses: People are generally risk seeking.
  • - - Most prefer an 85% chance to lose $1000 over a sure loss of $800.


Framing of Outcomes & Violation of Invariance
  • - Invariance Principle: A rational person's preferences should not change based on how a problem is described or 'framed'.
  • - Reality: Framing has a massive effect on choices, making invariance psychologically unfeasible.
Asian Disease Problem
  • - An unusual disease is expected to kill 600 people.
Frame 1: 'Lives Saved' (Gains)
  • - Program A: 200 people will be saved. (Chosen by 72%)
  • - Program B: 1/3 probability 600 are saved, 2/3 probability no one is saved.
  • - Result: People are risk averse, preferring the certain gain.

Frame 2: 'Lives Lost' (Losses)
  • - Program C: 400 people will die.
  • - Program D: 1/3 probability nobody dies, 2/3 probability 600 die. (Chosen by 78%)
  • - Result: People become risk seeking, preferring the gamble to avoid the certain loss.


Violation of Dominance
  • - Framing can lead people to choose an option that is objectively worse (dominated).
  • - In a problem with two concurrent decisions (one a gain, one a loss), 73% of subjects chose a combination of options (A and D) that was demonstrably worse than the combination they rejected (B and C).


The Psychophysics of Chances (Decision Weights)
  • - People don't weight probabilities linearly. We use 'decision weights' instead.
Weighting Function
  • - Certainty Effect: We give special weight to certainty. A change from 95% to 100% has a much bigger impact than from 30% to 35%.
  • - Overweighting of Low Probabilities: We overweight small chances, which explains the appeal of both lottery tickets (improbable gains) and insurance (improbable losses).
  • - Underweighting of Moderate/High Probabilities: We underweight most other probabilities.

Pseudo-certainty Effect
  • - An event that is actually uncertain is weighted as if it were certain because of framing.
  • - In a two-stage game, people ignored the first stage (a 25% chance to proceed) and treated the second stage choice as if it were between a sure thing and a gamble.
  • - This reversed their preferences compared to an equivalent single-stage problem.


Mental Accounting
  • - We organize outcomes in our minds using separate 'mental accounts'.
  • - An option is acceptable if the value of its advantages exceeds the value of its disadvantages within a specific account.
Topical Accounts
  • - We frame decisions based on the immediate context or 'topic'.
Calculator & Jacket Problem
  • - Would you drive 20 minutes to save $5?
  • - - On a $15 calculator: 68% of people said YES.
  • - - On a $125 calculator: Only 29% of people said YES.
  • - Reason: The $5 saving is framed relative to the price of the item in the topical account. It feels like a huge discount on the $15 item, but a tiny one on the $125 item.


Lost Ticket vs. Lost Cash
  • - You are going to a play with a $10 ticket.
Problem 1: You lose the $10 ticket.
  • - Would you buy another one?
  • - Result: 54% said NO.
  • - Reason: The cost of seeing the play is now posted to the 'theater account' as $20, which feels too expensive.

Problem 2: You lose a $10 bill.
  • - Would you still buy a ticket?
  • - Result: 88% said YES.
  • - Reason: The lost cash is not posted to the 'theater account'. It's a general loss, and you just feel slightly less wealthy.



Losses vs. Costs
  • - A disadvantage can be framed as either a 'cost' or a 'loss', which changes our choices.
Endowment Effect
  • - Definition: We value things we own more highly than things we don't.
  • - It is more painful to give up an asset than it is pleasurable to obtain it.
  • - Result: Selling prices are consistently higher than buying prices for the same item.
  • - This effect favors stability over change.

Cost-Loss Discrepancy
  • - Losses are more aversive than costs.
  • - Example: More people will refuse a gamble with a 90% chance to lose $5 than will refuse to pay $5 for a lottery ticket with the same odds.
  • - Thinking of the $5 as a payment (cost) is more acceptable than thinking of it as a potential loss.

Dead-Loss Effect
  • - A person with tennis elbow continues to play at a club they paid a membership for, just to avoid wasting the investment.
  • - Reason: Continuing to play frames the fee as a 'cost' for an activity. Stopping would force them to recognize the fee as a 'dead loss', which is psychologically more painful.
