Drug Insight: Gastrointestinal and Hepatic Adverse Effects of Molecular-Targeted Agents in Cancer Therapy
  • Journal: Nat Clin Pract Oncol CME
  • Posted: 3/17/2008
  • Updated: 3/18/2008
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of patients on erlotinib in the BR.21 trial experienced any grade of diarrhea?
  • 2. What is the proposed mechanism for gefitinib-induced diarrhea that can be mitigated by low-dose aspirin?
  • 3. Gemtuzumab ozogamicin is associated with what specific type of liver injury, and what is the likely mechanism?
  • 4. Sunitinib and sorafenib can cause asymptomatic elevation of which pancreatic enzymes?
  • 5. In the BRiTE registry, what was the incidence of gastrointestinal perforation in patients with metastatic colorectal cancer treated with bevacizumab?
Introduction
  • Targeted therapies (mAbs, TKIs) block specific cancer pathways.
  • These agents also affect normal cells expressing the target → adverse effects (AEs).
  • GI & hepatic AEs are common, can → treatment discontinuation & ↓ cancer control.
Importance of Understanding GI/Hepatic AEs
  • 1. Unacceptable side effects → discontinuation of therapy.
  • 2. Need for oral treatments for AEs → alters patient compliance.
  • 3. Iatrogenic GI/liver changes → alter pharmacokinetics of oral agents.
  • 4. AEs might be surrogate markers of treatment efficacy (e.g., EGFR inhibitor rash).
Diarrhea
  • A major cause of treatment discontinuation & dose-limiting toxicity.
  • Can ↓ drug efficacy.
Incidence
  • Varies significantly by drug and dose.
Erlotinib (BR.21 trial, NSCLC)
  • Diarrhea incidence: 55% w/ erlotinib vs 19% w/ placebo.
  • Grade 3-5 diarrhea: 6% w/ erlotinib vs <1% w/ placebo.
  • Consequences: Dose reduction in 5%; treatment discontinuation in 6%.
Gefitinib (IDEAL 1 trial, NSCLC)
  • Diarrhea incidence:
  • - 24% @ 250 mg/day.
  • - 43% @ 500 mg/day.
Correlation with Efficacy
  • Diarrhea may be a marker of clinical benefit for some agents.
  • - Gefitinib: Diarrhea is an independent predictor of response.
  • - Sorafenib: Patients w/ diarrhea → significantly ↑ time to progression.
  • - EGFR mutations: No correlation found w/ diarrhea severity or frequency.
Clinical Pattern
  • Oral compounds typically induce chronic diarrhea starting days after initiation.
  • Persists throughout treatment.
Agent-Specific Patterns
  • - Sorafenib: Onset within 1st week; mostly Grade 1-2; dose-dependent; resolves w/ cessation.
  • - Anti-EGFR TKIs: Median onset ≈14 days; usually moderate; controlled w/ dose reduction & loperamide.
  • - Imatinib: Dose-related; risk factors → high dose, female sex, primary GI tumor site.
  • - Flavopiridol: Watery, secretory diarrhea (↑ Na+, Cl-); peak on day 3; no mucosal damage.
  • - Bortezomib: Watery diarrhea, often w/ abdominal pain/cramps; starts 12-18h post-infusion, lasts 1-2 days.
  • - Farnesyl transferase inhibitors: Secretory diarrhea on day 3.
Histopathology & Combination Therapy
  • - HKI-272 (pan-EGFR TKI): Mild duodenal mucosal changes (gland dilatation, edema, villus atrophy).
  • - Combination w/ cytotoxic agents: Can lead to additive toxicity → ↑ dose reductions/discontinuations.
Management
  • First, exclude other causes: laxatives, infection, obstruction, radiation toxicity.
  • Loperamide is a useful treatment to ↓ intestinal motility.
Specific Strategies
  • - EGFR inhibitors: Dose reduction is effective. For severe cases (e.g., HKI-272), start loperamide immediately (2x2mg tablets, then 1 tablet q2h).
  • - Flavopiridol: Loperamide + colestyramine is effective but poorly tolerated.
  • - Octreotide: Efficacious for chemotherapy-induced diarrhea, but data lacking for targeted agents.
Pathophysiology
  • Mechanisms are not fully understood and are likely multifactorial.
Secretory Diarrhea
  • Results from excessive chloride (Cl-) secretion & deficient sodium (Na+) absorption.
  • - EGFR role: Normally a negative regulator of Cl- secretion in the colon.
  • - EGFR inhibitors: Block this regulation → ↑ Cl- secretion → secretory diarrhea.
  • - Flavopiridol: Potentiates natural secretagogs (e.g., bile acids) → ↑ Cl- secretion.
Direct Mucosal Damage
  • Hypothesis: Direct toxic effect on intestinal mucosa → malabsorption.
Supporting Evidence
  • 1. More common w/ oral drugs vs. mAbs.
  • 2. Dose-dependent for some oral TKIs.
  • 3. mTOR inhibitors (preclinical): Cause intestinal atrophy & ↓ fatty acid uptake.
  • 4. Flavopiridol: Poor glucuronidation → ↑ intestinal exposure → 91% diarrhea risk.
Other Mechanisms
  • - Altered Microflora: Temsirolimus (mTOR inhibitor) may alter bowel flora → colitis.
  • - Dysmotility: Loperamide efficacy suggests a motility component. May target KIT+ Cajal cells.
  • - Autonomic Neuropathy: Bortezomib-induced diarrhea may be due to intestinal autonomic nerve dysfunction.
  • - Inflammatory Mediators: Gefitinib may ↑ Thromboxane A2 (a Cl- secretagog); low-dose aspirin reduced diarrhea in one study.
Hepatotoxicity
  • Second most common reason for imatinib discontinuation.
  • Often presents as asymptomatic elevation of transaminases.
Mechanisms of Mild Hepatotoxicity
  • Often involves altered bilirubin metabolism or transport.
Enzyme Induction & Transporter Interaction
  • - Flavopiridol: Inhibits MRP2 transporter → ↓ excretion of bilirubin glucuronide → conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (15% of cases).
  • - Sorafenib/Erlotinib: Inhibit UGT1A1 enzyme → ↓ bilirubin conjugation → unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
  • - EGFR pathway inhibition: May reduce trafficking of Bile Salt Export Pump (BSEP) → cholestasis.
Severe Hepatitis
  • Can manifest as cytolytic hepatitis, portal hypertension, or acute liver failure.
Agent-Specific Severe Toxicity
  • - Imatinib: Grade 3 ↑ AST/ALT in 4%; onset usually within 3 months. Histology shows cytolytic hepatitis w/ necrosis.
  • - Gefitinib: Severe cytolytic hepatitis reported.
  • - Gemtuzumab ozogamicin: Induces portal hypertension (0.9% incidence, higher w/ HSCT). Histology shows sinusoidal fibrosis & hepatocyte necrosis.
Mechanisms of Severe Hepatotoxicity
  • Mechanisms are complex and not well understood.
Proposed Mechanisms
  • - Direct Toxic Effect: Suggested by link to high serum drug concentrations (imatinib) & interactions w/ CYP3A4 inhibitors.
  • - Hypersensitivity: Imatinib reported to induce autoimmune hepatitis.
  • - Endothelial Damage: Imatinib may damage endothelial cells, causing fibrin thrombi in hepatic veins.
  • - Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome (SOS): Gemtuzumab targets CD33+ cells in liver sinusoids → endothelial damage & ischemic necrosis.
Management
  • Management is often empiric due to unknown mechanisms.
  • Exclude other causes of liver disease (viral, etc.) with imaging and labs.
Monitoring & Dose Adjustment
  • - Monitoring: Baseline LFTs. For imatinib, weekly for 1st month, then monthly.
  • - Grade 3/4 Toxicity (>5x ULN): Interrupt therapy. Reintroduce at reduced dose once resolved to Grade ≤1.
  • - Recurrent Grade 3: Discontinue therapy.
  • - Grade 2 Toxicity: Continue at a lower dose.
Special Considerations
  • - Bilirubin Rise (Erlotinib/Gefitinib): May reflect drug metabolism, not pathology. Can continue safely unless Grade 3/4.
  • - Drug Interactions: Avoid co-administration with strong CYP3A4/CYP3A5 inducers/inhibitors.
  • - Concomitant Hepatotoxins: Avoid alcohol, high-dose paracetamol.
  • - Hepatic Impairment: Limited data; patients w/ severe (Child-Pugh C) impairment are excluded from trials.
Treatment of Hepatotoxicity
  • No standard prevention method exists.
  • - Steroids: Failed to prevent gefitinib toxicity but resolved imatinib-induced toxicity in a few cases.
Elevation of Pancreatic Enzymes
  • Hyperlipasemia & hyperamylasemia seen with Sorafenib, Sunitinib, Nilotinib.
  • Not observed with bevacizumab or anti-EGFR therapies.
Incidence & Clinical Pattern
  • - Sunitinib: Grade 3-4 enzyme elevation in 21% of patients.
  • - Nilotinib: Grade 3-4 hyperlipasemia in 28%.
  • - Characteristics: Not dose-dependent; occurs in first weeks; usually asymptomatic; resolves within 2 weeks of withdrawal or even with continued treatment.
Pathophysiology & Management
  • Pathophysiology is unknown.
  • Diagnosis of pancreatitis requires abdominal pain + lipase >3x ULN; isolated elevation is insufficient.
Recommendations
  • - Grade 1-2 w/o symptoms: Continue treatment.
  • - Grade 3-4 (lipase >2x ULN): Discontinue until enzymes return to Grade ≤1.
Intestinal Bleeding and Perforation
  • Associated with bevacizumab in combination with chemotherapy.
Incidence & Risk Factors
  • - Incidence (BRiTE registry): GI perforation in 1.7% of patients w/ metastatic colorectal cancer.
  • - Onset: Most occur within first 3 months of treatment.
  • - High-Risk Factors: Intact primary tumor; recent sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy; prior pelvic radiation.
Mechanism & Management
  • - Mechanism: Rapid tumor response → necrosis → perforation.
  • - Management: Monitor carefully for symptoms. Discontinue bevacizumab permanently if perforation occurs.
Conclusions & Key Points
  • GI and hepatic AEs are common with molecular-targeted therapies.
  • Understanding these AEs is crucial for maintaining treatment and cancer control.
Summary
  • - Pathophysiology of toxicities is largely unknown.
  • - Diarrhea mechanisms: Primarily secretory and direct mucosal damage.
  • - Hepatic toxicity mechanisms: Often involve pharmacokinetic interactions.
  • - AEs may provide insights into drug mechanisms and normal GI/liver physiology.
