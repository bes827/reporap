1. Introduction: AI Transformation in Gastroenterology

AI has expanded exponentially in clinical medicine, deployed for behavior change, data-driven diagnosis, image-based detection, clinical decision support, and treatment guidance. In gastroenterology specifically, AI assists in computer-aided early detection (CADe) of GI cancers during endoscopy. In inflammatory bowel disease, AI analyzes genomic datasets, constructs risk prediction models, enhances diagnostic precision, and analyzes endoscopic images for disease severity grading. AI also predicts disease outcomes and assists in managing chronic liver conditions.

Integration of AI into routine GI practice can transform healthcare delivery by improving clinical decision-making, enhancing efficiency, and optimizing outcomes. However, realizing these benefits requires reconciling ethical, legal, and regulatory concerns to ensure responsible use.

2. Equity and Fairness: Ensuring Unbiased AI

Clinical AI applications have tremendous promise in enhancing clinical efficiency, tailoring resources to high-need populations, and addressing persistent health inequities. However, they also risk perpetuating or introducing new inequities when applied to clinical settings.

2.1 Algorithmic Bias

Algorithmic bias occurs when an AI algorithm demonstrates significantly different performance across population subgroups—patients with different demographic characteristics, socioeconomic groups, comorbidity levels, geographic areas (rural vs. urban), or healthcare settings (academic health center vs. community hospital). Experts recommend considering equity at each stage of the AI lifecycle.

2.2 Problem Conception Stage

If an AI algorithm is programmed to answer the wrong question, the resulting clinical tool addresses the wrong issue. Consider "no show" or "predictive overbooking" algorithms deployed in GI endoscopy units—these primarily solve financial problems by ensuring slots are filled but do not address why certain patients miss appointments (structural and socioeconomic barriers). This illustrates the need to carefully consider the problem, its drivers, and patient subgroups most affected. Considering how AI output will be used in real-world settings is critical—if predictions link high-risk patients to supportive resources (appointment reminders, transportation assistance) rather than overbooking alone, it would more appropriately target the underlying problem and advance health equity. Diversifying AI development teams further reduces inequitable framing.

2.3 Data Collection and Preparation

Data-related bias can be instilled in two ways: use of unrepresentative training data and bias within training data. A recent study showed AI models trained on US patient data were disproportionately trained on cohorts from just 3 states, mostly using data from large academic health centers and hospital systems—potentially instilling bias through unrepresentative training data. The completeness of data for at-risk populations must also be assessed; poor populations have more scattered, fragmented care, so an algorithm built using one hospital's data has more complete datasets for affluent patients. When training data does not reflect real-world diversity, the resulting AI may perform poorly on unrepresented subpopulations in real-world clinical settings.

Consider an AI model predicting colorectal cancer risk based on patient symptoms and medical history. If trained primarily on non-Hispanic white populations without accounting for known differences in CRC incidence, presentation, and outcomes among Black, Hispanic, or Asian patients, it may underestimate risk for these groups—resulting in missed or delayed diagnoses and perpetuating existing health disparities.

Recommended approaches to eliminating bias include: (1) creating curated, open databases with deidentified or aggregate patient information to enhance accessibility of representative data; (2) pre-training models with large, non-specific datasets or synthetic data to boost ability to recognize diverse cases before training with original data; (3) using collaborative training techniques; and (4) promoting data transparency by documenting distributions of patient characteristics and explaining reasoning for implementing (or not) techniques to improve model performance with respect to race or other characteristics.

2.4 Model Development and Validation

To understand whether a model is equitable, one must understand how it was developed. Bias can be inadvertently built into models by their design if they neglect to consider race and other relevant factors where existing disparities impact the AI's use case. Recognizing and mitigating bias during model development through "bias accounting" is vital—this process measures important accuracy metrics across patient groups and care settings to uncover potential inequities (e.g., ensuring no difference in sensitivity/specificity or positive outcome rates across all groups). Validating AI algorithms prospectively in real-world healthcare settings is key, as retrospective data alone are not rigorous enough to account for biases emerging in real-world settings.

Stakeholders must also understand how algorithms work—they must be "explainable" or "interpretable." Imagine a busy GI clinic uses AI to triage patients for colonoscopy after positive FOBT. The AI analyzes patient histories, lab results, prior imaging, and symptom severity. The gastroenterologist notices the AI recommends urgent colonoscopy for a patient with mild anemia and intermittent abdominal pain, but routine scheduling for an elderly patient with similar symptoms. Requesting an explanation, the AI explains it flagged the first patient as high-risk due to family history of colon cancer and recent unexplained weight loss, while the elderly patient had negative previous colonoscopies and stable hemoglobin. This clear reasoning reassures the care team and helps justify prioritization decisions—explainability ensures clinicians can understand and trust how AI weighs risk factors.

2.5 Model Implementation and Monitoring

Clinical deployment represents a beginning, not an end. To recognize and account for human factors manifesting during real-world implementation, continuously monitoring and refining AI models is critical to ensure they perform as intended (accuracy of predictions, clinical outcomes across patient subgroups) and avoid model drift (degradation of predictive accuracy over time because real-world data has changed from initial training data). Talking to stakeholders (providers, patients, administrators) to identify potential fairness problems and assessing why they might choose to use or ignore model recommendations is helpful. If inequities are identified, the model must be adjusted accordingly, or end-users informed that the AI tool should only be used in certain populations.

Trust and governance are critical. Many AI models and resulting tools are proprietary, developed with commercialization in mind—transparency necessary to assess bias and understand data inputs/outputs/outcomes can be challenging. Regulation and algorithmic oversight can help with monitoring and transparency, instilling greater trust. Robust patient engagement is necessary to bolster datasets and ensure unbiased data. Communicating how data is used, safeguarding privacy, and investing in education around good data helps build trust.

2.6 Other Equity Considerations

While AI can improve access by assisting clinicians and "automating" certain aspects of GI care, using it as a substitute or replacement for clinical encounters in certain contexts risks creating a tiered system of access. Imagine in a remote community without gastroenterologists, AI is deployed to help screen and manage patients with suspected inflammatory bowel disease, providing essential care where none existed before. Yet if this AI is used in place of direct specialist evaluation in a well-resourced hospital, patients may receive less comprehensive care, reinforcing a two-tiered system and widening disparities. Increasing use of patient-facing AI technologies for self-monitoring may also exacerbate existing barriers around access to technology (the digital divide). Experts recommend considering the existing state of healthcare resources in each context to ensure just implementation.

3. Preserving Patient Autonomy

Development and use of informed consent in healthcare is a foundational principle. The seminal case of Canterbury v. Spence held that every patient of sound mind has the right to determine what shall be done with their own body and to be informed of risks, alternatives, and consequences that a reasonable person would consider significant—the "reasonable patient standard." The Belmont Report on medical research ethics also emphasizes respect for persons and autonomy.

3.1 AI and Informed Consent Challenges

Introduction of AI raises both new and traditional concerns regarding informed consent and patient autonomy. Whether use of AI needs to be disclosed at all, only in specified circumstances, or must always be disclosed remains controversial. Concerns exist about how AI may be clearly explained when some "black box" functions lack clarity by definition. Further attempts to explain AI may sometimes provide limited value and even detract from time needed for typical medical decision-making. Nevertheless, surveys have shown patient interest in understanding whether AI has influenced their healthcare.

The initial requirement for informed consent after Canterbury may have fueled similar complaints about difficulty imposed on healthcare providers with uncertain benefits to patients. Yet detailed understanding of AI operation would not necessarily be required to explain its benefits and risks, especially as routine use of AI in non-medical contexts becomes more familiar.

3.2 Adapting Consent Processes

Discussions regarding improving informed consent by shifting from typical in-person discussions toward more digital, interactive formats, or using shared decision-making with a certified tool as an alternative to informed consent, pre-existed expanded AI use. Adapting current practice to include explanation of AI use in advance, and reserving doctor time to answer patient questions that might include AI-related concerns, may accomplish the goal of adequately informing patients with limited need for further discussion during visits. There are already articles providing example consent templates for AI technologies, including use of AI scribes for clinical visits.

Discussions regarding whether notification or consent is ethically required, and development of institutional protocols to ensure consistent application, should go hand-in-hand with organizational decisions to deploy an AI tool and not be left to providers to navigate independently. This is critical to ethical practice in ensuring patients retain full control over their healthcare decisions and for liability reasons. The ethical and legal obligations and best practices regarding preserving patient autonomy with increasing integration of AI are in early stages and will continue to evolve.

3.3 AI Streamlining Consent Processes

Potential use of AI to streamline the informed consent process itself and optimize clinical workflow, particularly in large-volume endoscopy units, also deserves discussion. Imagine a busy GI practice deploys an AI virtual assistant that guides patients through informed consent for endoscopic procedures. This tool could explain risks, benefits, and alternatives in plain language, customize discussions based on each patient's medical history and language preference, and respond to patient questions in real time.

While such technology offers notable advantages in terms of personalization, accessibility, and consistency, its use presents challenges: risk of oversimplifying or overwhelming patients with information; potential trust issues when patients interact with a virtual assistant instead of a clinician; and technical concerns where system errors could compromise patient understanding or documentation. Ultimately, while AI has potential to make informed consent clearer and more patient-centered, human oversight remains essential. Content must be carefully vetted for completeness and clinical appropriateness; safeguards should confirm patients view and acknowledge all critical information; every patient must be able to easily access a provider for further questions. It is also wise to consult a healthcare attorney to assess legal risks associated with an AI-driven informed consent process in GI practice.

4. Ensuring Data Privacy and Security

AI has exciting potential to improve healthcare access, enhance patient health literacy, and increase both healthcare efficiency and quality. However, the unquenchable thirst for study data and need for large representative data sets to train AI and refine models raise significant privacy concerns. How is data accessed by AI companies? How is it stored? Who owns the HIPAA-protected data? How can patients consent? In general, for large research studies, a carefully constituted institutional review board would need to be convinced that the study is in the public interest, low risk to patients, and uses de-identified data. That does not appear to be the case with all AI data-gathering, which may be much more contractual. There is also concern that what was previously felt to be de-identified data can now be re-identified. What is the risk of personal health data being acquired by an employer or by a marketing company? A GI-specific discussion suggests adoption of patient-provider data use agreements, provider-vendor contracts, data sharing and use review boards, and robust data security measures to ensure data privacy.

4.1 Practical Implications for GI Practice

Imagine a GI clinic deploys an AI tool to predict flare-ups for patients with Crohn's disease by analyzing medical records, lab results, and real-time data from wearable devices. Sensitive health information flows through the AI system to deliver tailored alerts and care plans. This tool is expected to enable proactive, personalized care, potentially reducing hospitalizations and improving outcomes. However, several potential data privacy and security pitfalls must be addressed.

4.2 Key Privacy Risks

First, large amounts of sensitive GI health data, if not properly encrypted, could be vulnerable to hacking or unauthorized access, exposing patients' conditions and histories. Second, it is critical that patients be informed about what data is collected, how it is used, and who will have access to that data. Third, strict adherence to HIPAA and institutional policies to protect patient privacy is critical. While AI can revolutionize GI care, robust data security measures and transparent privacy practices are essential to safeguard sensitive patient information and maintain trust.

5. Human Agency: The Role of Human Judgment and Expertise

An important but underrecognized risk of increased clinical reliance on AI is potential erosion of the physician-patient relationship. Ethical medical decision-making is informed not only by clinical guidelines and medical knowledge, but also by a patient's goals, values, and priorities. While AI tools can be useful in supplementing clinical judgment or in clinical decision support in grey areas, they cannot replace human clinical judgment—in other words, they should not be deployed to make the decisions themselves.

5.1 Ethical Dangers of AI Deployment

There are clear ethical dangers in deploying AI for tasks that require moral agency—the capacity of an individual or entity to make choices based on a notion of right and wrong and take responsibility for the consequences of those choices. For example, while in one study patients preferred a chatbot's responses over physician responses and rated them higher-quality and more empathetic, it does not follow that these tasks should be fully delegated to AI. Likewise, we cannot expect AI to be able to contextualize care by understanding the social factors influencing a patient's health or health choices.

5.2 Clinical Judgment in Practice

Imagine a hospital uses an AI tool to recommend treatment plans for patients newly diagnosed with ulcerative colitis. The AI analyzes symptoms, lab results, and prior responses to medication to suggest therapy—often preferring biologics in severe cases. During rounds, the AI recommends starting a patient on a specific biologic. However, the gastroenterologist notes the patient's complex psychosocial situation, including unreliable follow-up and limited insurance coverage for the chosen biologic. Drawing on her expertise, she discusses alternative therapies and arranges additional social support—tailoring care beyond what the AI can assess. While the AI tool in this case can streamline data-driven recommendations and flag key clinical concerns, it cannot fully account for nuances such as patient preferences, social factors, and insurance logistics. It is critical, then, for clinicians to use judgment, empathy, and experience to interpret AI output, adapt care, and ensure treatments are practical, ethical, and aligned with the patient's best interests. In short, human judgment remains essential in GI care; while AI provides valuable insights and guidance, clinicians must synthesize these with holistic expertise for optimal patient outcomes.

5.3 Risk of Over-Reliance and Deskilling

Furthermore, there is concern that use of AI will create reliance on AI in healthcare, including an assumption that AI is always accurate. In a study of residents deliberately fed an ECG misdiagnosis, residents more often chose the AI misdiagnosis over their own correct interpretation. In gastroenterology, a recent multicenter observational study raised concerns of "de-skilling" with clinician over-reliance on AI-guided polyp detection during colonoscopy. Here, the AI assistance which appeared destined to improve ADR did not live up to that promise. These studies suggest that the promise of AI in improving healthcare should be critically evaluated before accepting the theoretical premise.

6. Accountability for AI-Related Harm

Responsible use of AI also requires reenvisioning established frameworks governing legal liability—who should be held accountable for AI-related patient harm, and under what circumstances? Determining who is liable for patient harm resulting from care jointly delivered by physicians and AI—the clinician, healthcare institution, AI developer, or a combination—is legally complex and ambiguous, particularly as AI technology becomes more autonomous and less transparent. AI-related injuries do not always fit neatly into traditional tort-based liability frameworks, which (in medical malpractice) assume a human decisionmaker or (in products liability) a "product" rather than an AI algorithm. Introduction of an AI "third actor" blurs the lines between clinical judgment, AI performance, and systemic oversight in a way that current legal liability frameworks are not designed to handle. The evolving nature of AI algorithms, which may be updated or changed after deployment, further complicates the issue. Clarifying these legal uncertainties is critical, as unresolved liability concerns could chill adoption of promising AI applications that could improve patient outcomes and enhance overall care quality.

6.1 Evolving Legal Landscape

Because integration of AI into clinical settings is relatively recent, the field of tort liability for AI-related medical harm is still evolving and legal precedent is scarce. In this void, legal scholars have identified two likely scenarios in which a physician might face liability in following an autonomous AI's recommendation, leading to patient harm: (1) situations in which the AI correctly recommends management consistent with the current standard of care and the physician disregards this recommendation, resulting in patient harm; and (2) situations in which the AI erroneously recommends management with non-standard care, and the physician follows this recommendation, resulting in patient harm. Clarity on these liability questions will only come as these factual scenarios are specifically litigated and legal precedent is established.

6.2 Medical Malpractice Cases

However, in the few reported medical malpractice cases filed against physicians who followed the erroneous recommendation of a software application in making care decisions, alleging the provider should have scrutinized the software more closely and relied on their own medical knowledge and experience rather than following the software's recommendation, motions for summary judgment were denied, meaning that the cases could proceed to trial. In these cases, physicians are evaluated against what other specialists would have done (i.e., the standard of care), as discussed below.

6.3 Standard of Care Determination

To determine legal liability under traditional tort-based frameworks, a patient must prove that they were owed a duty of care, and that this duty was breached by failure to adhere to the applicable standard of care, leading to patient harm. With AI tools still emerging, determining how use of a specific AI tool impacts the professionally recognized standard of care can be challenging. Furthermore, healthcare-related standards of care are often fluid, evolving over time in response to updated evidence, practice patterns, public perception, and other factors. As clinical applications of AI in gastroenterology expand and evolve, professional society guidance regarding its proper use in clinical decision-making can serve as helpful (though not definitive) evidence of the standard of care for liability determination.

6.4 Professional Society Guidance

Several major GI societies have published limited guidance relevant to GI endoscopy. A 2025 AGA living clinical practice guideline declined to recommend for or against use of CADe-assisted colonoscopy in light of the very low certainty of evidence for critical outcomes, while acknowledging its potential to continually improve as an iterative AI application. An ESGE position statement suggests that AI-assisted detection rates for suspicious lesions or adenomas should match those of expert endoscopists, and that AI-based photo-documentation of anatomical landmarks should be achieved in ≥90% of procedures. However, guidelines are currently silent on the appropriate role of numerous other emerging AI tools. Determination of the standard of care in these cases would thus rely primarily on medical expert testimony.

6.5 Algorithmic Transparency Challenges

Lack of AI algorithmic transparency also can make it difficult to prove that a physician's decision to accept or depart from an AI's recommendation was unreasonable in a malpractice claim. Likewise, it may be difficult to prove that the wrong model output for a particular patient was foreseeable by the physician. In claims against healthcare facilities, model opacity may deem it challenging for a plaintiff/patient to prove that the facility negligently selected, maintained, or monitored the AI. In a product liability claim against the AI developer, it may be challenging to show that a reasonable alternative safer design exists. From a legal perspective, there is a need for ongoing validation, safety monitoring, and updates of AI tools to ensure product safety. There are also numerous data privacy concerns with AI that must be addressed both for medicolegal and ethical reasons.

6.6 Risk Management Considerations

There are several important considerations to guide risk management as the liability landscape and standards of care continue to evolve. First, liability risk assessment must be incorporated into the earliest stages of AI development and clinical integration—this involves assessing for each tool how severe the harm to patients would be if algorithmic error is not caught and implementing reasonable safeguards, as AI is a heterogeneous group of technologies with varying liability risks. Second, for both ethical and medicolegal reasons, it is important to be transparent with clinicians (end-users) about model inputs/outputs, error rates, differential performance, and information that can assist clinicians in interpreting AI-enabled predictive models. Third, the possibility of using AI in clinical decision-making should be integrated into informed consent processes.

6.7 Legislative and Regulatory Gaps

The lack of specific legislative and regulatory guidance on allocation of liability among various stakeholders, including clinicians, AI developers, and healthcare institutions, further complicates this space, highlighting the need for updated legal frameworks and clearer definitions of responsibility in the context of AI-enabled clinical decision support systems. This remains a work-in-progress and will continue to evolve as novel legal theories are tested in the courts creating legal precedent that can be applied in subsequent cases.

6.8 Practical Liability Scenario

Imagine a hospital implements an AI-powered CADe system to assist gastroenterologists during colonoscopies by automatically flagging potential polyps. One day, the gastroenterologist performs a screening colonoscopy with the help of the AI, which fails to highlight a subtle, flat lesion. Months later, the patient develops advanced colorectal cancer from the missed lesion. When the patient's family files a complaint, questions arise: is the gastroenterologist solely responsible for the missed lesion, or does some liability rest with the hospital for deploying the AI system, or even with the AI developers? With AI involved, responsibility for errors can be diffuse (i.e., shared among the physician, hospital, and AI developer) and existing medical malpractice frameworks do not always clearly address AI-driven decisions or omissions. Therefore, hospitals must define who oversees and verifies AI decisions, ensure physicians understand AI limitations, and clarify liability in case of AI-related harm. As AI use grows in GI care, liability for errors or missed findings becomes complex, emphasizing the need for clear clinical, legal, and ethical frameworks to safeguard patients and clinicians. Shared liability contracts, in which the AI developer and end user accept a negotiated level of responsibility for harms or unexpected outcomes caused by an AI tool in its clinical application, are one option to manage liability risk.

6.9 Types of AI-Related Liability

Medical malpractice involves deviating from the standard of care set by the profession by failing to critically evaluate AI recommendations—typical defendant is the physician.

Ordinary negligence involves deviating from the norms set by an industry and courts by (1) deciding to implement an improper AI system, or (2) for an employee's negligent decisions related to AI systems ("vicarious liability")—typical defendant is the health system/institution.

Products liability involves designing a product that caused an injury—typical defendant is AI developers/manufacturers.

7. The Role of Regulatory Oversight

Governmental regulation per se has become more controversial in recent years, with some arguing that regulation of AI prevents harm to an otherwise unprotected population unable to understand the intricacies of AI technologies, and others arguing that regulation impedes innovation, thus depriving that population the benefit of valuable products that have survived marketplace competition. The opaque, "black box" character of AI, the general lack of understanding by a broad population, and the economic clout needed to develop AI and the power of the industries that will control it suggest that a middle ground approach of expertly crafted regulation, designed to protect but not impede, may accomplish the goals of both protection and encouraging innovation on a level playing field.

7.1 FDA and International Regulatory Framework

Thus far, the FDA integrates some devices using AI into its approval process and has cleared them for approved use, as has the Conformité Européenne marking. Indeed, several computer-assisted endoscopic detection devices are now FDA-approved and in commercial use. There are attempts by the FDA to cooperate more globally, including with the UK's Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency and Health Canada, in achieving international consensus.

7.2 Professional Society Guidance

Various non-governmental organizations also have stepped into this space, such as the Coalition for Health AI (CHAI), a community of academic health systems, organizations, and experts in AI and data science whose goal is to advance responsible development, deployment, and oversight of healthcare AI. Professional societies also have weighed in with white papers and guidelines. However, these are suggestions without the force of law.

7.3 EU AI Act

The European Union has recognized the importance of a comprehensive legal framework for AI and introduced the first governmental comprehensive legal framework, the EU AI Act. However, the US has yet to establish a similar broad legal framework for AI.

7.4 Need for Effective Oversight

We believe effective regulatory oversight is critical in ensuring safe, reliable, and equitable AI. Such oversight can ensure that AI tools, such as computer-aided endoscopic detection and diagnosis devices, undergo rigorous testing before and after clinical deployment, reducing risk of harm and fostering trust.

8. Peering Through the Crystal Ball: The Future of AI in Clinical Gastroenterology

Attorneys are loathe to speculate about future outcomes, as these projections are so often wrong, and new developments render futuristic predictions rapidly obsolete. Nevertheless, in the spirit of deliberate speculation, we offer our thoughts on the future legal and ethical impact of AI in our specialty, recognizing that the future of AI is likely to evolve in unexpected ways.

8.1 Future Legal Risks and Accountability

From a legal perspective, AI introduces new pathways for risk and accountability in clinical care, including gastroenterology. The legal mandate to practice within the standard of care could be complicated: gastroenterologists could face allegations of breaching the standard of care by either adopting or avoiding use of AI, failing to challenge AI-generated recommendations or hallucinations that appear clinically unsound, or deploying unsupervised or inadequately validated AI in patient care. In GI, this could extend to decisions surrounding use of AI-driven diagnostic image analysis, pathology interpretation, or risk stratification for CRC—where both decisions to rely on and disregard AI recommendations may be scrutinized in the setting of adverse patient outcomes.

8.2 Lessons from Early Technology Adoption

Even more troubling are the potential ethical failures, some very hard to predict, in the deployment and use of AI in the clinical environment. The authors think of the early days of the internet, with its great promise in democratizing information, enhancing diagnostics, streamlining clinical workflows such as documentation, and improving patient engagement. Few in the medical community foresaw the emergence of issues such as privacy breaches affecting EHRs, clinician burnout related to increased accessibility/blurring of work-life boundaries, or the unchecked influence of proprietary algorithms shaped primarily by the interests of large corporations. These experiences serve as cautionary tales for the adoption of AI in gastroenterology and other areas of medicine.

8.3 Predicted Benefits and Concerns

We believe AI will become a ubiquitous and indispensable part of clinical gastroenterology, enhancing both practice efficiency and patient outcomes in myriad ways. But we worry about the significant risks that will accompany these benefits: conflict of interest may distort AI design and deployment, and corporate priorities might eclipse patient welfare. Lax regulation could open the door to misuse of sensitive digestive health data, threatening privacy, exacerbating disparities in access to GI care, and perhaps diminishing opportunities for human connection and weakening the patient-doctor relationship.

8.4 Workforce and Economic Concerns

Unfettered use of AI also could contribute to a deskilled and burnt-out GI workforce, with AI errors concealed by opaque algorithms and our most vulnerable patients relegated to cheerful chatbots in lieu of genuine clinical encounters. Financial pressures could also intensify. Consider, for example, the specter of dynamic, AI-driven pricing models that calculate patient-specific charges for endoscopy or advanced therapies, or targeted advertising that exploits personal health anxieties—both potentially fueling medical debt at the expense of broader patient needs.

8.5 Broader Societal Implications

Finally, we cannot ignore the larger societal conversation about AI's existential risks, as highlighted by Geoffrey Hinton (the "godfather of AI") and other thought leaders—though such concerns reside outside the current scope of our review.

9. Conclusion

The coming years will bring unprecedented opportunities for gastroenterologists to leverage AI for diagnosis, risk stratification, workflow optimization, and patient engagement. However, we must diligently balance these advances with ethical stewardship, resisting pressures that would deskill clinicians, undermine patient autonomy, erode privacy, or widen health disparities. By ensuring AI's responsible use in the clinical environment, we can fully realize its great promise in improving GI care while avoiding its potential ethical and legal pitfalls.
