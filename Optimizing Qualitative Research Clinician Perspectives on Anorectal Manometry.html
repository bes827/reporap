Project Critique & Optimization
  • Feedback and suggestions for a research outline, grounded in Hsieh & Shannon (2005) and illustrated by Lagrotteria et al. (2021).
  • Aims to refine methodology for exploring clinician perspectives on anorectal manometry (ARM).
Theory vs. Guiding Assumptions
  • Distinction between a formal, established theory and a researcher's hypothesis/guiding assumptions.
  • Researcher's statement ('clinicians are hesitant...') is a hypothesis, not a formal theory.
Implication for Method Choice
  • Using assumptions to direct coding from the start → strong bias; potential to miss unexpected findings.
  • Assumptions are useful for literature review/discussion, but shouldn't dictate initial analysis.
Choosing Content Analysis Approach
  • Commitment to one primary content analysis approach is crucial for research alignment.
  • Three types: Conventional, Directed, Summative.
Conventional Content Analysis: Best Fit
  • Designed for studies describing phenomena w/ limited existing research.
  • Aims to understand raw, unfiltered clinician perspectives.
How it Works
  • Immerse in transcripts → allow codes/categories to emerge directly from data.
  • Inductive process: avoids preconceived categories.
Example in Practice
  • Lagrotteria et al. (2021) study: understood clinicians' experiences w/ new program.
  • Used conventional content analysis to analyze interview transcripts; themes emerged from data.
Process
  • Open coding → developing code list via consensus → organizing into categories.
  • Direct application of conventional method.
Why Not Directed?
  • Directed approach used to validate/extend existing, published theory.
  • Not suitable here ∵ no formal theory to test.
Why Not Summative?
  • Summative approach too narrow: focuses on counting/comparing keywords (e.g., 'discomfort' vs. 'pain').
  • Interprets underlying context *after* word usage analysis; research question is broader/exploratory.
Structuring Interview Guide
  • Conventional approach directly influences interview question design.
  • Focus on eliciting unique participant perspectives.
Open-Ended Questions
  • Start broad to encourage sharing unique perspectives.
  • Goal: participant guides conversation; Hsieh & Shannon: questions should be open-ended ('Can you tell me more about that?').
Example: Question 1
  • Proposed 'Question 1' is perfect: 'Can you tell me about your experience with anorectal manometry?'
Scenarios as Prompts
  • Case scenarios are useful as springboards for discussion, not quizzes.
  • Frame to explore thought processes.
Example Scenario
  • 'Let's walk through a common scenario, like a patient with chronic constipation. Can you tell me about your thought process for evaluation and where, if at all, anorectal manometry fits in?'
Avoid Leading Questions
  • Leading questions (e.g., 'Do you think the test interpretation is confusing?') → participants may agree to please researcher.
  • Rephrase to be open-ended.
Revised Question Example
  • Instead of 'Do you think the test interpretation is confusing?', ask: 'What has your experience been with interpreting the results of this test?'
Clarifying Analysis Plan
  • Avoid blending different methods; stick to Conventional Content Analysis language.
  • Framework Analysis is a different qualitative method, though steps are similar.
Content Analysis Process
  • Specific steps for conventional content analysis.
  • Ensures clarity and consistency in methodology.
Immersion
  • Reading all transcripts repeatedly → gain sense of the whole.
Coding
  • Word-by-word reading → highlighting text capturing key concepts.
  • Initial codes often use participants' own words, written in margins.
Categorizing
  • Grouping/clustering codes into broader categories/themes.
Defining
  • Developing clear definitions for final codes and categories.

Research Question

Study Design

Methodology

Sampling & Recruitment
