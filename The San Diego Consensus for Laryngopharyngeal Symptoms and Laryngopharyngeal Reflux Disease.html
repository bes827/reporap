The San Diego Consensus for Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease

  • - https://doi.org/10.14309/ajg.0000000000003482
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the recommended duration for a trial of twice-daily PPI therapy for LPS to determine a symptomatic response?
  • - What percentage of patients diagnosed with LPR have normal objective ambulatory reflux monitoring studies?
  • - What distal esophageal acid exposure time (AET) on ambulatory reflux monitoring is considered definitively abnormal and meets the criteria for proven GERD?
  • - What validated patient-reported outcome instrument can help identify individuals with elevated levels of hypervigilance and anxiety related to their LPS?
  • - What is the diagnostic yield of GERD in patients referred for LPS when using prolonged wireless pH monitoring versus 24-hour pH impedance monitoring?

Core Concepts: Definition & Terminology
  • - A key distinction is made between Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS) and the objective diagnosis of Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD).
  • - The presence of LPS does NOT equate to LPRD.
Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS)
  • - Chronic and frequent throat/upper airway symptoms with the potential to be induced by reflux.
  • - The term LPS itself does not implicate reflux as the etiology.
Criteria
  • - Frequent: Occurring at least twice weekly.
  • - Chronic: Present for more than 8 weeks.

Included Symptoms
  • - Cough (89% agreement)
  • - Voice hoarseness/change (80% agreement)
  • - Throat clearing (86% agreement)
  • - Excess throat phlegm (82% agreement)
  • - Throat pain/sore throat (89% agreement)

Globus
  • - An important symptom warranting evaluation.
  • - Often a negative predictor of reflux physiology.

Symptom Presentation
  • - Can be isolated.
  • - Can be concurrent with typical esophageal reflux symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain).


Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD)
  • - True LPRD is much less common than LPS.
  • - As many as 60% of patients diagnosed with LPR have normal objective ambulatory reflux monitoring studies.
  • - The proportion of individuals with LPS found to have objective LPRD is only ~35%.
Diagnostic Requirements
  • - Requires BOTH:
  • - 1. Chronic, troublesome LPS.
  • - 2. Objective evidence supporting a relationship between symptoms and gastroesophageal reflux.

Forms of Objective Evidence
  • - LPS response to anti-reflux therapy (only if esophageal symptoms coexist).
  • - OR Diagnostic evidence on upper GI endoscopy or ambulatory reflux monitoring.


Impact & Burden
  • - Accounts for up to 10% of otolaryngology and gastroenterology visits.
  • - Healthcare costs are 5.4 times higher than for GERD, partly from diagnostic uncertainty.
  • - Significant negative impact on quality of life (anxiety, depression).


Diagnostic Evaluation of LPS
  • - A structured approach is needed, starting with laryngoscopy and symptom characterization based on the presence or absence of esophageal symptoms.
Role of Laryngoscopy
  • - Primary Role: A necessary step to assess for non-reflux causes, especially malignancy.
  • - LPRD cannot be diagnosed based on laryngoscopy findings alone.
Findings
  • - Findings like infraglottic edema, mucosal hypertrophy, and erythema are nonspecific for LPRD.
  • - These findings may be present in healthy, asymptomatic individuals.

Scoring Instruments
  • - Instruments like the Reflux Finding Score (RFS) and Reflux Sign Assessment are not diagnostic of LPRD due to suboptimal specificity.

Videostroboscopy
  • - Should be performed when white-light laryngoscopy is unrevealing in patients with hoarseness to evaluate for other etiologies.


Symptom Characterization & Medical Trials
  • - The first step is to determine if LPS is isolated or occurs with concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms.
LPS with Esophageal Symptoms
  • - A trial of medical anti-reflux management is appropriate.
  • - Should be accompanied by dietary and lifestyle modifications.
PPI Trial
  • - Twice daily (BID) PPI (e.g., omeprazole 20mg BID).
  • - A 3-month trial is sufficient to determine symptomatic response.

Alginate Therapy
  • - Can be used in conjunction with a PPI trial.
  • - Taken 4 times daily (after meals and before bedtime).


Isolated LPS
  • - Empiric medical trials are not recommended as a first-line therapy.
  • - Reflux testing can personalize management better than empiric trials.


Esophageal Evaluation
  • - Performed for LPS unresponsive to empiric trials (if applicable) or for isolated LPS.
  • - Includes Upper GI Endoscopy and/or Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring.
Upper GI Endoscopy
  • - Performed off acid suppression to establish GERD presence.
  • - Up to 47% of patients with LPS may show erosive findings or hiatal hernia.
Conclusive GERD Evidence
  • - Los Angeles (LA) grades B, C, or D esophagitis.
  • - Biopsy-proven Barrett’s esophagus.

Supportive Evidence
  • - Disrupted esophagogastric junction (e.g., hiatal hernia).

Cervical Inlet Patch
  • - Area of heterotopic gastric mucosa in the proximal esophagus.
  • - Association with LPS is unclear; value of ablation is not established.


Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
  • - The reference standard for diagnosing LPRD.
  • - Should be performed off acid suppression in patients with unproven GERD.
Indications
  • - Required prior to any invasive anti-reflux management (endoscopic or surgical).
  • - For isolated LPS to personalize management.
  • - Should be considered if long-term medical management is planned.

Modalities: pH-Impedance vs. Wireless pH
  • - These two methods are not mutually exclusive and have distinct roles. (See Table 3)
24-hour pH-Impedance
  • - Best for: Patients with isolated LPS to understand mechanisms.
  • - Detects proximal, weakly-acidic, and non-acidic reflux events.
  • - HEMII-pH catheters can identify pharyngeal reflux in 81% of patients.

96-hour Wireless pH
  • - Best for: Patients with concomitant esophageal symptoms and before escalating management.
  • - Accounts for day-to-day variability, which is a major advantage.
  • - Higher diagnostic yield for GERD in LPS patients (50%) compared to 24-hr pH-impedance (27%).


Interpretation of Findings
  • - Focuses on distal esophageal Acid Exposure Time (AET).
Proven GERD
  • - Distal esophageal AET > 6.0%.

Physiologic Reflux
  • - Distal esophageal AET < 4.0%.
  • - If AET is <4.0% on all 4 days of wireless monitoring, it predicts a low likelihood of response to antisecretory therapy.


Oropharyngeal pH Monitoring (e.g., Restech)
  • - Not recommended as a stand-alone tool.
  • - Has poor specificity for LPRD and poor concordance with other methods.




Laryngeal Hyperresponse & Hypervigilance
  • - Addresses non-reflux mechanisms and brain-larynx interactions that contribute to LPS.
  • - These factors can be the primary etiology of LPS, with or without pathologic reflux.
Behavioral Factors
  • - A predisposition to manifest emotional distress through sensory changes in the larynx or voice.
Laryngeal Hyperresponse
  • - Repetitive laryngeal behaviors ---> throat clearing, coughing, breath-holding.
  • - Biomechanical strain at the vocal folds ---> altered voice quality or globus sensation.

Laryngeal Hypervigilance
  • - Increased threat-induced attention to the larynx.
  • - Often coexists with symptom-specific anxiety (fear/worry about LPS and its consequences).

Laryngeal Cognitive-Affective Tool (LCAT)
  • - A validated patient-reported outcome (PRO) instrument.
  • - Helps identify individuals with elevated levels of hypervigilance and symptom-specific anxiety related to their LPS.
  • - Useful for selecting candidates who may benefit from behavioral intervention.


Laryngeal Behavioral Therapy
  • - Aims to modify dysregulated laryngeal patterns and cognitive perspectives associated with LPS.
  • - Can be used alone or in combination with other modalities like neuromodulators.
Laryngeal Recalibration Therapy (LRT)
  • - Provided by a voice-specialized speech-language pathologist (SLP).
  • - A structured approach combining:
  • - - Traditional voice therapy techniques.
  • - - Autonomic regulation (heart rate variability biofeedback).
  • - - Cognitive reframing (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy).

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • - Considered if psychological processes persist after LRT or neuromodulation.
  • - Targets specific cognitive, emotional, and behavioral processes exacerbating symptoms.
  • - Delivered by a mental health professional with specialized health psychology training.


Pharmacologic Neuromodulation
  • - May improve laryngeal hypersensitivity.
  • - Considered in LPRD with poor response to acid suppression or in isolated LPS with negative reflux testing.
Gabapentin & Pregabalin
  • - May be an effective adjunct to LRT for LPS, especially cough.

Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
  • - May help LPS symptoms.
  • - Caution: Anticholinergic side effects like xerostomia (dry mouth) should be monitored.

SSRIs & SNRIs
  • - Not specifically studied in LPS.
  • - May benefit individuals with LPS and concomitant clinical anxiety and/or depression.



Patient-Reported Outcome (PRO) Instruments & Risk Tools
  • - PROs are used to assess symptoms and treatment response, but are not diagnostic tools for LPRD.
LPRD-Specific PROs
  • - Instruments like LPR-HRQL, Reflux Symptom Index (RSI), and Reflux Symptom Score (RSS) are not specific enough to diagnose LPRD.
Reflux Symptom Index (RSI)
  • - Nonspecific for LPRD but can track laryngological complaints over time.
  • - A cutoff of ≥13 is not considered clinically significant or reliable for diagnosis.


GERD-Specific PROs
  • - Instruments like GERDQ and GERD-HRQL are useful for assessing esophageal symptoms but are not specific for LPS.

Risk Stratification Tools
  • - Tools like HAS-BEER and COuGH RefluX are promising but require more data before they can be adopted into the consensus algorithm.


San Diego Consensus: Clinical Paradigm Summary
  • - This consensus provides a modern, evidence-based, interdisciplinary paradigm for LPS and LPRD.
Core Principle: Distinguish LPS from LPRD
  • - LPS: The symptomatic presentation (cough, throat clearing, etc.).
  • - LPRD: The objective diagnosis, requiring both LPS and objective evidence of reflux.
  • - This distinction is critical to separate patients needing reflux therapy from those needing symptomatic/behavioral interventions.

Underlying Mechanisms
  • - Mechanisms are varied and not limited to simple reflux, explaining why fewer than 50% of LPS patients respond to antisecretory therapy.
  • - Mechanisms include:
  • - - Direct Injury: From acid, pepsin, bile salts.
  • - - Indirect Injury: Repetitive trauma from vagally-mediated cough/clearing.
  • - - Reflex Pathway: Vagally-mediated airway stimulation.
  • - - Brain-Larynx Interaction: Heightened cognitive-behavioral responses.

Proposed Evaluation & Management Algorithm
  • - See Figure 1 for the full algorithm.
Initial Evaluation
  • - 1. Laryngoscopy: To rule out non-reflux causes (e.g., malignancy).
  • - 2. Symptom Assessment: Differentiate between isolated LPS and LPS with concurrent esophageal symptoms.

Management Pathway: LPS + Esophageal Symptoms
  • - 1. Empiric Trial: Twice-daily PPI for 3 months ± alginates, plus lifestyle changes.
  • - 2. Assess Response:
  • - - Full Response: Likely LPRD, continue management.
  • - - Partial/No Response: Proceed to objective esophageal testing.

Management Pathway: Isolated LPS
  • - 1. Objective Testing First: Proceed with objective esophageal testing (endoscopy, reflux monitoring) before anti-reflux management to personalize care.
  • - 2. Consider Brain-Larynx Interaction: Disordered laryngeal behaviors should be considered at the outset.

Role of Objective Testing
  • - Required before any invasive anti-reflux therapy (endoscopic or surgical).
  • - Absence of GERD (AET <4.0%) makes it unlikely that anti-reflux therapies will improve LPS.

Consideration for Non-Responders
  • - In isolated LPS or when LPS fails to respond to anti-reflux therapies (even in confirmed LPRD), consider overlap with laryngeal hyperresponsiveness and hypervigilance.
  • - Recommend behavioral therapy (LRT, CBT) and/or neuromodulation.
