The San Diego Consensus for Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease
  • This article presents the San Diego Consensus for Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS) and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD).
  • It aims to establish a modern, interdisciplinary care paradigm for evaluating and managing these conditions.
  • The consensus was developed by an international panel of experts from gastroenterology, otolaryngology, speech-language pathology, and clinical psychology.
Background
  • Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR) is a term often used for aerodigestive symptoms without objective reflux evidence.
  • This leads to diagnostic uncertainty and prolonged, often unsuccessful, treatments.
  • The initiative aims to create evidence-based recommendations for LPS evaluation and management.
Methods
  • A 28-member international, inter-disciplinary working group was formed.
  • The group included gastroenterologists, laryngologists, speech-language pathologists, and a clinical psychologist.
  • The RAND Appropriateness Method (RAM) was used to determine the appropriateness of statements, with ≥80% agreement required for recommendations.
Working Group Meetings
  • The initiative spanned one year (October 2023 to October 2024).
  • Comprised seven working group meetings: five virtual and two hybrid with in-person meetings in Hong Kong (May 2024) and Austria (October 2024).
  • Several rounds of independent voting and sub-group meetings were conducted.
Sub-groups
  • 14 members were assigned to sub-groups.
  • These sub-groups focused on: Definition & Terminology, Initial Diagnostic Evaluation, Reflux Monitoring, Therapeutic Trial, Behavioral Factors & Therapy, and Risk Stratification.
  • Each sub-group was led by two interdisciplinary co-chairs.
RAND Appropriateness Method (RAM)
  • A validated methodology was utilized for determining the appropriateness of statements.
  • This method included independent electronic voting to determine the appropriateness of each statement.
  • Statements ranked as appropriate with ≥80% agreement were included as final recommendations.
Definition & Terminology
  • Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS) are defined as chronic and frequent throat and upper airway symptoms potentially induced by retrograde flow of gastric content.
  • LPS includes cough, voice hoarseness/change, throat clearing, excess phlegm, and throat pain/sore throat.
  • Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD) requires chronic troublesome LPS AND objective evidence supporting the relationship between symptoms and gastroesophageal reflux.
Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS)
  • Symptoms must be frequent, occurring at least twice weekly, and present for more than 8 weeks.
  • Commonly cited symptoms include cough (89%), regurgitation (89%), throat pain/sore throat (89%), throat clearing (86%), excess phlegm (82%), and hoarseness/voice change (80%).
  • Globus is an important symptom warranting further evaluation but did not reach agreement for inclusion as an LPS.
Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD)
  • The proportion of individuals found to have objective LPRD on diagnostic evaluation is low, approximately 35%.
  • Both LPS and objective evidence of gastro-esophageal reflux are needed for a diagnosis of LPRD.
  • Diagnostic evidence supporting the relationship between LPS and gastroesophageal reflux is required prior to invasive anti-reflux management.
Diagnostic Evaluation of LPS
  • Laryngoscopy is a necessary part of evaluation of LPS to assess for other non-reflux related otolaryngologic processes including malignancy.
  • LPRD cannot be diagnosed based on laryngoscopy findings alone.
  • The presence of concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms versus absence, referred to as isolated LPS, should be determined.
Role of Laryngoscopy
  • Laryngoscopy is necessary to rule out alternate laryngeal pathologies including neoplasia.
  • Findings on laryngoscopy alone cannot diagnose LPRD.
  • Laryngoscopic findings such as infraglottic edema, interarytenoid mucosal hypertrophy, and post-cricoid mucosal edema are non-specific for LPRD.
Symptom Characterization & Role of Medical Trials
  • The presence of concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms versus absence, referred to as isolated LPS, should be determined.
  • In patients with LPS & esophageal reflux symptoms, a trial of medical anti-reflux management, along with dietary and lifestyle modification, is appropriate.
  • In patients with isolated LPS, esophageal evaluation is recommended instead of empiric medical trials.
Medical Anti-Reflux Trial
  • Includes twice daily PPI for 3 months with or without alginate therapy.
  • Alginate therapy taken four times daily (after meals and before bedtime) can decrease LPS.
  • In patients with isolated LPS, acid-reducing medications are not recommended as first-line therapy without diagnostic evidence of reflux.
Esophageal Evaluation
  • Esophageal evaluation, including upper GI endoscopy and/or ambulatory reflux monitoring, is the next step in the investigation of LPS with typical esophageal symptoms when unresponsive to empiric treatment trials, and in isolated LPS.
Upper GI Endoscopy
  • Conclusive evidence of GERD on upper GI endoscopy includes Los Angeles grades B, C, D esophagitis and/or biopsy proven Barrett’s esophagus.
  • The presence of disrupted esophagogastric junction on endoscopy, such as hiatal hernia, may serve as adjunctive evidence for LPRD.
  • Office-based transnasal esophagoscopy (TNE) has been shown to be similarly diagnostic for Barrett’s esophagus and certain other esophageal pathologies compared to standard upper endoscopy.
Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
  • Ambulatory reflux monitoring is the reference standard for diagnosing LPRD.
  • LPRD diagnosis is required prior to invasive anti-reflux management.
  • In patients with isolated LPS, reflux testing can personalize management better than empiric treatment trials.
Indications
  • Diagnostic evaluation for LPRD should be considered if long-term medical anti-reflux management is recommended.
  • Evidence of abnormal reflux burden is essential prior to endoscopic or surgical antireflux interventions.
  • In the absence of endoscopic evidence of GERD, ambulatory reflux monitoring is the reference standard for diagnosing LPRD.
Modality & Interpretation of Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
  • Ambulatory reflux monitoring for evaluation of LPRD in patients with unproven GERD should be performed off acid suppression therapy.
  • pH-impedance and wireless pH monitoring measure different properties of LPRD and are not mutually exclusive.
  • Distal esophageal acid exposure time (AET) greater than 6.0% on ambulatory reflux monitoring meets criteria for proven GERD.
pH-Impedance Monitoring
  • A diagnostic option for LPRD, particularly in patients with isolated laryngopharyngeal symptoms.
  • Offers the ability to detect proximal reflux events with greater detail.
  • Hypopharyngeal reflux events were demonstrated using dual channel or hypopharyngeal-esophageal multichannel intraluminal impedance with dual pH (HEMII-pH) catheters.
Prolonged Wireless pH Testing
  • A diagnostic option for LPRD, particularly in patients with concomitant esophageal reflux symptoms and prior to escalation of reflux management.
  • AET less than 4.0% on all four days of 96-hour wireless pH monitoring predicts low likelihood of symptom response with anti-secretory therapy.
  • Prolonged pH monitoring also provides a higher diagnostic yield of pathologic GERD in patients with LPS.
Oropharyngeal pH Testing
  • Oropharyngeal pH monitoring alone has poor specificity for LPRD in patients with laryngopharyngeal symptoms.
  • This technique is not specific, cannot detect weakly-acidic or non-acidic reflux, has poor concordance with traditional pH-impedance monitoring, and does not correlate with clinical outcomes.
Laryngeal Hyperresponse and Hypervigilance
  • Hyperresponsive laryngeal behaviors, with or without true LPRD, may contribute to or be the primary etiology of LPS.
  • Laryngeal symptom-specific anxiety and hypervigilance may contribute to or be the primary etiology of LPS.
  • The Laryngeal Cognitive Affective Tool can help identify individuals with symptom-specific anxiety and hypervigilance.
Laryngeal Behavioral Factors
  • The larynx is a critical intersection of breathing, communication and swallowing.
  • Laryngeal hyperresponse can manifest as repetitive laryngeal behaviors such as throat clearing or coughing, breath-holding, or as biomechanical strain at the vocal folds.
  • Laryngeal hypervigilance, defined as increased threat-induced attention to the larynx, and symptom-specific anxiety, defined as fear/worry about LPS and perceived consequences, are psychological processes that can influence LPS.
Laryngeal Behavioral Therapy
  • Individuals with LPS can undergo initial laryngeal recalibration therapy (LRT) provided by a voice-specialized speech-language pathologist.
  • LRT involves combined voice therapy techniques, autonomic regulation and cognitive reframing to address perceived and confirmed irritation manifesting as cough, throat-clearing, dyspnea and voice change.
  • Behavioral therapies used to address psychological targets, such as cognitive behavioral therapy, can be used alone or in combination with other modalities in patients with LPS.
Neuromodulation
  • Neuromodulators may improve laryngeal hypersensitivity. These include tricyclic antidepressants, as well as gabapentin and pregabalin (particularly studied in cough).
  • Pharmacologic neuromodulation may attenuate laryngeal hypersensitivity and may be considered in LPRD patients with suboptimal response to anti-secretory therapy or SLP intervention and in isolated LPS with negative esophageal testing.
  • Tricyclic antidepressants, commonly prescribed for the treatment of functional GI disorders, should be used with caution because of anticholinergic effects which may cause dry mouth and aggravate LPS.
PRO Instruments & Risk Stratification Tools
  • Common PRO instruments for GERD (GERDQ, GERD-HRQL) are valuable in assessing the severity and treatment response of esophageal symptoms, but have suboptimal diagnostic yield for LPS and LPRD.
  • Currently available LPRD-specific PRO instruments should not be used to diagnose LPRD due to insufficient specificity.
  • Some clinical risk stratification tools (HAS-BEER, COuGH RefluX) were developed in recent years to help direct initial diagnostic and management strategies in patients with LPS.
Discussion
  • The San Diego Consensus for LPS & LPRD (SD-LPRD) is a modern-day evidence-based paradigm for evaluation and management of LPS.
  • The physiological mechanisms underlying “LPR” are often unclear and can be neurogenic, inflammatory, behavioral or motility related.
  • A major distinction is between presenting symptoms pertaining to the upper aerodigestive tract, termed LPS, from confirmed reflux-mediated pathophysiology based on objective findings, termed LPRD.
Key Distinctions
  • An expert evaluation of the larynx using laryngoscopy ± stroboscopy is an important step in evaluating any patient with LPS, not only to document findings, but also to rule out non-reflux etiologies for LPS.
  • The presence of typical esophageal symptoms should be assessed as evaluation and management algorithms differ for isolated LPS and concurrent LPS with esophageal reflux symptoms.
  • Prior to initiating anti-reflux therapy in isolated LPS, or when empiric treatment trials fail in LPS associated with esophageal symptoms, further esophageal evaluation includes upper GI endoscopy and ambulatory reflux monitoring.
Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
  • In planning ambulatory reflux monitoring, the diagnostic evaluation of LPS potentially related to reflux may require the determination of whether symptoms arise from a reflux mediated mechanism or a reflex mechanism.
  • pH-impedance monitoring, particularly using a HEMII-pH or dual pH impedance catheter, can distinguish between these mechanisms and provide definitive evidence of reflux events.
  • Prolonged wireless reflux monitoring provides precise information regarding the presence or absence of pathologic acid burden.
Laryngeal Hyperresponsiveness and Hypervigilance
  • In the case of isolated LPS or when LPS fail to respond to anti-reflux therapies even in confirmed LPRD, overlap with laryngeal hyperresponsiveness and hypervigilance should be considered, and behavioral therapy and/or neuromodulation recommended.
  • Laryngeal behavioral therapies offered through trained SLPs and other behavioral therapists can hold value in disorders with laryngeal hyperresponsiveness and hypervigilance independent of the underlying mechanism.
  • The LCAT holds potential to identify patients with LPS who could benefit from behavioral therapies and/or neuromodulation.
