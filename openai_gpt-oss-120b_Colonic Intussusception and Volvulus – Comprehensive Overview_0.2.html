Colonic Intussusception and Volvulus – Comprehensive Overview
  • Source: Surgical Textbook Chapter 155 (2025)
  • Topic: Adult colonic intussusception & colonic volvulus
1. Pathophysiology of Colonic Intussusception
  • Definition: proximal bowel segment (intussusceptum) telescopes into distal segment (intussuscipiens).
  • Lead‑point present in 86–90 % of adult cases; most often a GI neoplasm.
  • Proposed mechanism: intraluminal stimulus → proximal constriction + distal relaxation → invagination.
  • Types:
  • Enteric – jejunum/ileum only.
  • Colonic – includes ileocolic, colocolonic, colorectal configurations.
  • Etiology breakdown:
  • Malignancy → 56 % of colonic cases, ~19 % of enteric cases.
  • Benign lesions (adenoma, Meckel diverticulum, adhesions, HIV, sprue, idiopathic) → remainder.
2. Clinical Presentation
  • Accounts for 1 % of bowel obstructions; complete obstruction in <20 %.
  • Adult classic triad (pain, mass, bloody stool) rare; most common symptoms:
  • Crampy abdominal pain – 71 %.
  • Nausea/vomiting – 68 %.
  • Abdominal distention – 45 %.
  • Tenderness – 60 %.
  • History often reveals intermittent/sub‑acute obstruction over days–years.
  • Misdiagnosis frequent; many identified intraoperatively.
3. Diagnostic Imaging Modalities
3.1 Plain Radiography
  • Useful for enteric (non‑colonic) intussusception detection.
  • May show “stacked‑coin” / “coiled spring” on upper GI series.
3.2 Barium Studies
  • Barium enema: cup‑shaped filling defect marks distal intussusceptum.
  • Contraindicated if perforation/ischemia suspected.
3.3 Ultrasound (Pediatric‑focused)
  • Target/donut sign (transverse view).
  • Pseudo‑kidney sign (longitudinal view).
  • Operator dependent; limited in adults due to bowel gas & habitus.
3.4 Computed Tomography (CT) – Modality of Choice
  • “Target sign” / “bowel‑within‑bowel” axial appearance.
  • Mesenteric vessels compressed between bowel walls → pathognomonic.
  • Highest sensitivity/specificity for adult intussusception.
3.5 Endoscopy (Flexible Sigmoidoscopy/Colonoscopy)
  • Diagnostic in subacute/chronic colonic obstruction; biopsies avoided if ischemia/necrosis present.
4. Management Strategies – Adult Intussusception
  • Surgery is definitive; non‑operative reduction not recommended due to high malignancy risk, especially >60 yr.
  • Resection performed as formal oncologic procedure:
  • Segmental removal of involved bowel.
  • Lymphadenectomy of affected mesentery.
  • Reduction discouraged to avoid trauma/perforation of compromised wall.
  • Post‑resection: monitor for recurrence; ensure clear margins.
5. Colonic Volvulus – Epidemiology & General Pathophysiology
  • Represents 1.9 % of large‑bowel obstructions in the US; up to 50 % in Africa/Middle East/Latin America.
  • Torsion of a colon segment around its mesentery → closed‑loop obstruction.
  • Degrees of twist: 180° → clinical obstruction; 360° → strangulation, gangrene, perforation.
  • Predisposing anatomy: redundant colon + elongated mesentery + narrow base (sigmoid, cecum, transverse).
6. Sigmoid Volvulus
6.1 Pathophysiology
  • Chronic sigmoid elongation (“dolichomesocolic colon”) between distal descending mesocolon & rectosigmoid junction.
  • More common in males >30 yr; peaks in mid‑70s.
6.2 Presentation
  • Most frequent colonic volvulus – 50–90 % of cases.
  • Western subtype: elderly, diabetic, institutionalized, chronic constipation → gradual distention, obstipation, paradoxical diarrhea.
  • Endemic subtype (Africa/India/Middle East): young males, acute obstruction after high‑fiber diet.
6.3 Imaging Findings
  • Plain X‑ray: absent gas in left iliac fossa; “coffee‑bean” / “omega loop” sign – apex right upper abdomen (≈60 %).
  • Barium enema: “bird’s beak” at torsion point.
  • CT: mesenteric twist, sigmoid transition point, “X‑marks‑the‑spot,” “split wall” signs (≤50 %).
6.4 Treatment Algorithm
  • Stable patient without ischemia → endoscopic decompression (rigid sigmoidoscopy or flexible colonoscopy).
  • Visual inspection for mucosal viability; pass rectal tube (36 F) after detorsion.
  • Post‑decompression radiograph confirms detorsion.
  • Recurrence rate ≈71 %; mortality of recurrent episodes up to 36 %.
  • Definitive surgery: semi‑elective oncologic resection with primary anastomosis (preferred).
  • Non‑resective options (detorsion, sigmoidopexy) have recurrence 9–44 % and higher mortality; now rarely used.
7. Cecal Volvulus
7.1 Pathophysiology
  • Incomplete congenital fixation → mobile cecum predisposes to torsion.
  • Two forms: classic volvulus (whirl sign) & cecal bascule (folded cephalad “flap‑valve” obstruction).
7.2 Presentation
  • Younger cohort than sigmoid; female predilection; bimodal peaks (mid‑40s, late 70s).
  • Symptoms mimic small‑bowel obstruction: acute pain, vomiting, distention, possible peritonitis.
  • “Mobile cecum syndrome” – intermittent pain relieved by flatus in ~50 % of chronic cases.
7.3 Imaging Characteristics
  • Plain X‑ray: massively dilated cecum (“kidney‑bean”) under left diaphragm; paucity of gas RLQ.
  • Barium enema: ascending colon beak; hydrostatic reduction contraindicated (high perforation risk).
  • CT: “whirl sign” of twisted mesenteric vessels; cecal margin beaking, upstream ileal obstruction.
7.4 Treatment Principles
  • Endoscopic reduction ineffective (<30 %); not attempted due to thin‑walled cecum risk.
  • Operative resection with primary anastomosis is standard; recurrence after resection negligible.
  • Cecal bascule managed similarly – primary resection mandatory if ischemic/necrotic.
8. Rare Volvulus Variants
8.1 Transverse Colon Volvulus (≈3 % of colonic volvulus)
  • Normally fixed; occurs with congenital absence or loss of gastrocolic, lienocolic, phrenocolic ligaments → redundancy & mesenteric lengthening.
  • Early vomiting due to duodenojejunal compression.
  • Imaging: inverted coffee‑bean sign on X‑ray; CT/contrast enema confirm torsion point.
8.2 Ileosigmoid Knotting (Compound Midgut Volvulus)
  • Redundant sigmoid colon wrapped by mobile ileum → simultaneous small‑bowel & distal large‑bowel obstruction.
  • Predominantly in Asian/African endemic areas; male fourth decade, high‑fiber single‑meal cultures.
  • CT hallmark: dilated sigmoid + ileal loops with whirling of mesosigmoid and mesenteric vessels.
  • Endoscopic decompression contraindicated (necrosis seen 50–100 % at laparotomy).
  • Definitive therapy: emergent resection dictated by necrotic extent; mortality 20–100 % if non‑viable bowel, 6–8 % when viable.
9. Imaging & Diagnosis of Colonic Volvulus (General)
  • CT → ↑ diagnostic accuracy ~100 %; whirl sign confirms torsion.
  • Plain X‑ray – coffee‑bean/omega loop sign in sigmoid; kidney‑bean sign in cecal volvulus.
  • Barium enema – bird’s‑beak point; therapeutic only for temporary reduction.
10. Endoscopic Decompression of Sigmoid Volvulus (Initial Therapy)
  • Bedside rigid sigmoidoscopy w/ minimal insufflation → visual ischemia check.
  • Rectal tube (36 F catheter) placed after gentle passage through torsion point.
  • Post‑decompression radiograph confirms detorsion; decompression maintained for days awaiting definitive resection.
11. Surgical Treatment of Sigmoid Volvulus
  • Formal oncologic resection preferred – 89 % of cases managed this way.
  • Subtotal/total colectomy required if necrosis, megacolon, or colonic atony encountered.
  • Primary anastomosis safe with good judgment; diverting loop ileostomy favored over colostomy for ease of care.
12. Operative Options for Cecal Volvulus
  • Resection with primary anastomosis → standard; lymphadenectomy added if malignant lead‑point suspected.
  • Non‑resective detorsion + cecopexy → recurrence 12–13 %, high morbidity; now rarely employed.
13. Management of Transverse Colon Volvulus
  • Diagnosis often delayed due to atypical fixation loss; operative exploration mandatory when imaging inconclusive.
  • Resection (right hemicolectomy, segmental transverse colectomy, or left hemicolectomy) dictated by redundancy/ischemia extent.
14. Management of Ileosigmoid Knotting
  • Endoscopic reduction contraindicated – necrosis in >50 % at laparotomy.
  • Emergent operative resection based on necrotic burden; mortality ranges 20–100 % if non‑viable bowel, 6–8 % when viable.
