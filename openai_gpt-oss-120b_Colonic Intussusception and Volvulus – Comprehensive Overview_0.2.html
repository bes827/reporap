Colonic Intussusception and Volvulus – Comprehensive Overview
  • Source: Surgical Textbook Chapter 155 (2025)
  • Topic: Adult colonic intussusception & colonic volvulus
1. Pathophysiology of Colonic Intussusception
  • Mechanism: Proximal segment (intussusceptum) invaginates into distal lumen (intussuscipiens).
  • Lead‑point prevalence: 86–90 % have a pathologic lesion; 56 % malignant, ~19 % benign.
  • Stimulus hypothesis: Intraluminal irritant → proximal constriction + distal relaxation → telescoping.
  • Types:
  • Enteric: jejunum/ileum only.
  • Colonic: ileocolic, colocolonic, colorectal configurations.
  • Benign etiologies: benign GI neoplasms, adhesions, Meckel diverticulum, sprue, HIV, idiopathic.
2. Clinical Presentation
  • Incidence: 1 % of bowel obstructions; <20 % present with complete obstruction.
  • Typical adult symptoms (frequency in brackets):
  • Crampy abdominal pain (71 %).
  • Nausea/vomiting (68 %).
  • Abdominal distention (45 %).
  • Tenderness (60 %).
  • History pattern: Sub‑acute or intermittent obstruction over days–years; often misdiagnosed until laparotomy.
3. Diagnostic Imaging Modalities
3.1 Plain Radiography & Contrast Studies
  • Enteric intussusception: “stacked coin” / “coiled spring” on upper GI series.
  • Colonic involvement: Barium enema shows cup‑shaped filling defect at distal intussusceptum.
3.2 Computed Tomography (CT) – Modality of Choice
  • Target sign: Axial “bowel‑within‑bowel” with mesenteric vessels compressed between walls → pathognomonic.
  • Sensitivity/Specificity: >90 % for adult intussusception.
3.3 Ultrasound (Primarily Pediatric)
  • Transverse view: “target” / “donut” sign.
  • Longitudinal view: “pseudo‑kidney” sign.
  • Limitations in adults: Operator dependence, bowel gas, body habitus.
3.4 Endoscopy (Diagnostic Adjunct)
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy for suspected colonic malignancy or chronic obstruction.
  • Contraindications: Tissue ischemia/necrosis → avoid biopsy/polypectomy.
4. Treatment Principles for Adult Intussusception
  • Surgical resection is definitive; non‑operative reduction not recommended due to high malignancy risk, especially >60 yr.
  • Oncologic resection: Segmental removal with lymphadenectomy of involved bowel.
  • Reduction discouraged: Risk of perforation, trauma to compromised wall.
  • Post‑resection care: Monitor for anastomotic leak; consider adjuvant therapy if malignancy confirmed.
5. Colonic Volvulus – Epidemiology & General Pathophysiology
  • Overall prevalence: 1.9 % of large‑bowel obstructions in the U.S.; up to 10–50 % in Africa, Middle East, South America.
  • Mechanism: Torsion of a colonic segment around its mesentery (mesenterioaxial).
  • 180° twist → obstruction.
  • ≥360° twist → venous gangrene, ischemia, perforation.
  • Predisposing anatomy: Redundant colon + elongated mesentery + narrow fixation base.
  • Common sites: Sigmoid (most frequent), cecum, transverse colon.
6. Sigmoid Volvulus
6.1 Pathophysiology & Risk Factors
  • Acquired elongation of sigmoid between distal descending mesocolon and rectosigmoid junction (“dolichomesocolic” colon).
  • Higher incidence in males >30 yr; peaks in mid‑70s.
  • Contributing factors: chronic constipation, high‑fiber diet (endemic regions), postoperative adhesions, Hirschsprung disease, megacolon, pregnancy.
6.2 Clinical Presentation
  • Western subtype: Elderly, diabetic, institutionalized, psychoactive meds; chronic fecal loading → gradual obstruction.
  • Endemic subtype: Younger males (80 %); acute obstruction after high‑fiber intake.
  • Symptoms: Abdominal pain (often mild), obstipation, distention, paradoxical diarrhea, nausea/vomiting.
6.3 Diagnostic Imaging
  • Plain Radiography: “coffee bean” / “omega loop” sign; apex points to RUQ in ~60 % cases.
  • Barium Enema: “bird’s beak” at torsion point; therapeutic reduction possible.
  • CT Scan (≈100 % accuracy):
  • Mesenteric twist (“whirl sign”).
  • Sigmoid transition point (most sensitive).
  • “X‑marks‑the‑spot” & “split wall” signs (<50 % cases).
  • Ischemia on CT poorly correlates with clinical ischemia → rely on exam.
6.4 Management Algorithm
  • Initial step: Assess hemodynamic stability, exclude perforation/ischemia.
  • Endoscopic decompression (rigid sigmoidoscopy or flexible colonoscopy):
  • Success rate 60–90 %; place 36‑F rectal tube for maintenance.
  • Obtain mucosal inspection for ischemia or lesion.
  • Recurrence risk ≈71 %; mortality of recurrent cases up to 36 %.
  • Surgical intervention (semielective after decompression):
  • Preferred: Formal oncologic resection with primary anastomosis.
  • Indications for urgent surgery: Failed endoscopic reduction, perforation, gangrene, hemodynamic instability.
  • Resection extent dictated by necrosis; may require subtotal/total colectomy (≈16 % cases).
6.5 Operative Options & Outcomes
  • Resection with primary anastomosis: Standard of care; mortality ≈9.4 %.
  • Stoma creation (Hartmann’s) for diffuse peritonitis or high‑risk patients.
  • Nonresective procedures (detorsion ± fixation): Recurrence 9–44 %; mortality 7.8 %; less favored.
  • Laparoscopic approach: Feasible after decompression; no mortality benefit vs open but reduced wound complications.
7. Cecal Volvulus
7.1 Pathophysiology & Epidemiology
  • Accounts for 25–40 % of U.S. volvulus cases; 1–1.5 % of all intestinal obstructions.
  • Congenital mobility: Incomplete fixation of cecum → hypermobility (present in ~11 % autopsies).
  • Cecal bascule: Folding cephalad → “flap‑valve” obstruction; no whirl sign on CT.
7.2 Clinical Presentation
  • Younger cohort than sigmoid volvulus; female predilection; incidence peaks at mid‑40s and late‑70s.
  • Typical acute SBO symptoms: Crampy pain, vomiting, distention, possible peritonitis.
  • “Mobile cecum syndrome”: Chronic intermittent pain relieved by flatus (≈50 % of cases).
7.3 Diagnostic Imaging
  • Plain Radiography: Massive dilated cecum (“kidney‑bean” under left diaphragm); paucity of gas in RLQ.
  • Barium Enema: Ascending colon beak; hydrostatic reduction contraindicated (high perforation risk).
  • CT Scan:
  • “Whirl sign” of engorged mesenteric vessels (absent in bascule).
  • Beaked cecal margins, upstream ileal obstruction.
7.4 Management Strategy
  • Endoscopic reduction: Generally unsuccessful; contraindicated due to thin‑walled cecum risk.
  • Surgical treatment (first‑line):
  • Resection with primary anastomosis (standard).
  • Cecopexy or detorsion alone → high recurrence (12–13 %).
  • Cecostomy tube placement: High morbidity (fistula, abdominal wall complications).
  • Mortality: Overall ≈6.7 %; coagulopathy strongest predictor of death.
8. Rare Forms of Colonic Volvulus & Special Entities
8.1 Transverse Colon Volvulus
  • Incidence: ~3 % of colonic volvulus cases; extremely rare.
  • Pathogenesis: Absence or laxity of gastrocolic, lienocolic, phrenocolic ligaments → redundant transverse colon.
  • Presentation: Early vomiting (duodenojejunal compression), abdominal pain, distention.
  • Imaging: “Inverted coffee‑bean” sign on plain film; CT or contrast enema confirms torsion.
  • Treatment: Operative – segmental transverse colectomy, extended right hemicolectomy, or left hemicolectomy depending on viability; nonresective detorsion/colopexy described but recurrence common.
8.2 Ileosigmoid Knotting (Compound Volvulus)
  • Geography: Endemic in Asia, Africa, Middle East; rare in West.
  • Mechanism: Mobile ileum wraps around a redundant sigmoid base → “compound volvulus.”
  • Risk factors: High‑fiber diet, single large daily meal, Meckel diverticulum, pregnancy.
  • Clinical picture: Acute SBO with rapid progression to hypovolemic shock/peritonitis (>50 % present late).
  • Imaging: CT shows dilated sigmoid + ileal loops with whirling of both mesosigmoid and small‑bowel mesentery.
  • Management: Endoscopic decompression contraindicated (necrosis in 50–100 %); emergent laparotomy with resection of necrotic segments; mortality 20–100 % if viability lost vs 6–8 % when viable.
8.3 Other Rare Variants
  • Volvulus associated with pregnancy: Sigmoid volvulus accounts for ~45 % of obstetric bowel obstructions; maternal mortality ≈5 %, fetal demise up to 30 %.
  • Colonic volvulus in Hirschsprung disease: Massive dilation predisposes to torsion; reported cases managed with resection.

All diagnostic and therapeutic recommendations reflect current consensus for adult patients, emphasizing prompt imaging, avoidance of non‑operative reduction in suspected malignancy, and definitive oncologic resection when indicated.

9. Imaging Summary Tables (Key Signs per Modality)
  • CT: Target sign → intussusception; Whirl sign → volvulus; Mesenteric twist highly specific.
  • Plain X‑ray: Coffee bean (sigmoid), Kidney‑bean (cecal), Inverted coffee‑bean (transverse).
  • Contrast Enema: Bird’s beak (volvulus); Cup‑defect (intussusception).
10. Comparative Treatment Outcomes Across Entities
10.1 Intussusception vs Volvulus Mortality
  • Adult intussusception resection mortality ≈9 % (malignancy‑driven).
  • Sigmoid volvulus overall mortality 9.4 %; recurrent cases up to 36 %.
  • Cecal volvulus mortality 6.7 %; bascule similar when necrotic.
10.2 Recurrence Rates by Modality
  • Endoscopic decompression (sigmoid) → recurrence ≈71 %.
  • Nonresective detorsion (sigmoid/cecal) → recurrence 9–44 % (sigmoid), 12–13 % (cecal).
  • Surgical resection → negligible recurrence when oncologic margins achieved.
11. Post‑Operative Considerations & Follow‑Up
  • Monitoring: Serial abdominal exams, lactate, CBC to detect early ischemia or leak.
  • Stoma care: Hartmann’s for perforation; loop ileostomy preferred over colostomy when diversion needed.
  • Nutritional support: Prevent short‑gut syndrome after extensive resections.
  • Oncologic surveillance: Colonoscopy 6–12 mo post‑resection if malignancy identified; consider adjuvant chemotherapy/radiation per tumor staging.
12. Prevention & Patient Education
  • Encourage high‑fiber diet moderation in endemic regions to reduce redundancy.
  • Manage chronic constipation aggressively (osmotics, stool softeners) to avoid sigmoid elongation.
  • Educate at‑risk populations (elderly institutionalized, psychiatric patients) on early symptom reporting.
13. Key Take‑Home Points
  • Intussusception in adults → almost always a pathologic lead point; surgical resection mandatory.
  • Colonic volvulus → imaging (CT) essential for diagnosis; endoscopic decompression useful only for sigmoid when no ischemia.
  • Resection remains definitive therapy across all colonic volvulus subtypes; aim for oncologic margins and lymphadenectomy when malignancy suspected.
  • Rare variants (transverse, ileosigmoid knotting) require high clinical suspicion; operative management is standard of care.
