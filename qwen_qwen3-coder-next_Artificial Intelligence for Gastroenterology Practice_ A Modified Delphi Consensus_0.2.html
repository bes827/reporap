Artificial Intelligence for Gastroenterology Practice: A Modified Delphi Consensus
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, 2026 (Published Ahead of Print; DOI: 10.14309/ajg.0000000000003944)
  • Topic: AI Integration in Gastroenterology, Hepatology, and Endoscopy
  • Author(s): Seth A. Gross, MD, FACG; Aasma Shaukat, MD, MPH, FACG (co-first authors); plus 23 additional experts from NYU Langone, Mayo Clinic, Cedars-Sinai, and 20+ institutions across the U.S. and Portugal
1. Endoscopy: CADe, CADx, and Gastric Neoplasia Applications
  • CADe improves adenoma detection rate (ADR) in controlled trials: ↓ AMR from 35% to 16% when used first-line
  • Real-world ADR benefit remains unproven: nonrandomized studies show no significant improvement
  • CADx enables “leave-in-situ” strategy for diminutive non-neoplastic polyps → ↓ unnecessary polypectomies, ↓ cost
  • Critical gaps: suboptimal proximal colon performance; need for ≥90% sensitivity/specificity per ESGE standards
Sub-requirements for CADe/CADx validation
  • Must meet SPIRIT-AI and CONSORT-AI reporting standards
  • Training/test datasets must reflect diverse lesion types, populations, countries, and healthcare systems
  • Disclosure required: clinical population, disease prevalence, endoscopist experience, imaging tech, explainability, update protocols
  • Sessile serrated lesion detection remains inadequate → urgent need for device improvement
Gastric neoplasia surveillance
  • High miss rates due to limited training, subtle lesions, inadequate inspection time
  • AI must support accurate assessment of endoscopic resection eligibility → match experienced endoscopist judgment
2. Clinical Applications: Ambient AI, NLP, and Workflow Optimization
  • Ambient AI scribes reduce physician workload, ↑ patient engagement (Shah et al., JAMA Netw Open 2025)
  • NLP enables automated coding: GPT-4 model matches ICD coding + human review, faster and more cost-effective
  • AI-driven clinical decision support: big-data models outperform providers in predicting cancer therapy response (Huang et al., Cancer Lett 2020; Jiang et al., Nat Commun 2023)
  • Interoperability is non-negotiable: AI workflows must integrate seamlessly with EHR and endoscopy systems
Practice integration priorities
  • Seamless EHR integration → ↓ provider burden, ↑ clinical adoption
  • Workflow analysis for operational efficiency recommendations
  • Prior authorization automation → ↓ clinician/administrative burden
3. Training, Education, and Deskilling Mitigation
  • Structured AI curriculum required for GI fellows: foundational competency in AI principles and limitations
  • AI augmentation + feedback during endoscopy training → real-time learning enhancement
  • Simulator integration with AI tools → ↑ procedural skill development
  • Trainees must achieve core endoscopy competency without AI augmentation at graduation → avoid deskilling
Guardrails against overreliance
  • Overreliance risks: ↓ independent polyp detection, false-positive-driven interventions, performance drop in AI-limited settings
  • Training must clarify what AI can and cannot do; set clear expectations for trainee use
  • Human oversight, accuracy, and quality must be preserved
4. IBD, Hepatology, Equity, Ethics, and Implementation Governance
  • Multiomics AI tools for IBD: integrate genetic, microbiome, imaging, nutritional, clinical data → personalized care
  • AI in hepatology: improves HCC risk prediction, diagnosis, treatment planning via EHR + imaging + histopathology integration
  • Wearable + AI monitoring → decentralized IBD/liver disease care, real-time activity/QoL tracking
  • Current prognostic models insufficient for standalone use → require refinement before replacing conventional methods
Equity and ethical imperatives
  • Bias mitigation: systematic racial/ethnic bias documented in AI cardiovascular models (Eur J Radiol 2025)
  • Chain-of-custody data governance required for consent, privacy, IP protection
  • Patient-facing AI advice must be clinician-reviewed pre-deployment
  • Language barriers must be proactively addressed in patient-facing tools
  • Equitable access across all populations → avoid exacerbating disparities
Governance and reimbursement
  • Specialty societies (e.g., ACG, ASGE) must lead regulatory oversight of AI diagnostics
  • Conflicts in AI recommendations resolved via multi-stakeholder process (clinicians, regulators, patients, payors)
  • Reimbursement models must shift from fee-for-service to value-based: reward quality, outcomes, cost reduction
  • RVU valuation of E&M services must not decrease due to AI integration
5. Implementation Barriers and Operational Challenges
  • Reimbursement remains a major barrier: lack of specific CPT/HCPCS codes for CADe/CADx; current payment models disincentivize AI adoption
  • Regulatory uncertainty: FDA clearance pathways unclear for adaptive AI tools; lack of harmonization across international markets
  • Interoperability gaps: EHR system fragmentation impedes AI integration → need for FHIR-based standards (Tabari et al., JMIR Med Inform 2024)
  • Cybersecurity and data sovereignty: AI model training requires large datasets → risk of breaches without chain-of-custody protocols
Infrastructure and technical readiness
  • Legacy system incompatibility: many community practices lack API access or structured data pipelines needed for AI
  • Edge-case handling: AI tools fail silently on rare pathologies (e.g., flat adenomas, early gastric cancer) → need for robust failure modes
  • Real-time inference latency: >200ms delay in CADe feedback disrupts endoscopic workflow → must meet procedural timing constraints
  • Model drift without monitoring: performance degradation over time due to changing patient demographics or equipment upgrades
Workflow disruption risks
  • Cognitive offloading: overreliance on AI may ↓ endoscopist vigilance and pattern recognition
  • Alert fatigue: excessive false positives → clinicians disable or ignore AI prompts
  • Training burden on staff: onboarding requires dedicated time, ↓ immediate productivity
  • Liability ambiguity: unclear accountability when AI misses a lesion → “black box” problem impedes incident review
6. Evidence Gaps and Research Priorities
  • Longitudinal outcomes missing: no studies link CADe use to interval colorectal cancer rates
  • Cost-effectiveness evidence limited: modeling studies suggest savings (Areia et al., Lancet Digit Health 2022), but real-world RCTs lacking
  • Diverse population validation absent: most AI trained on single-center, high-resource data → poor generalizability
  • Comparative effectiveness unknown: no head-to-head trials of CADe vs. high-definition white-light colonoscopy in community practice
Priority research questions
  • Prospective multicenter trials needed for all AI tools before routine adoption
  • Health equity metrics: track outcomes by race, ethnicity, language, income, geography
  • AI-specific endpoints: define clinical utility beyond sensitivity/specificity (e.g., time to diagnosis, patient satisfaction, downstream resource use)
  • Human-AI team performance: quantify how AI changes clinician behavior, not just tool accuracy
Data infrastructure imperatives
  • Multi-institutional data-sharing consortia required for training and validation (Spann et al., Gastroenterology 2025)
  • Standardized data dictionaries for GI-specific variables (e.g., Paris classification, Boston Bowel Prep Scale, Mayo score components)
  • Longitudinal phenotyping: link endoscopy, pathology, genomics, EHR, and patient-reported outcomes
  • Bias auditing frameworks: mandatory pre-deployment assessment of performance across subgroups
7. Training, Competency, and Professional Development
  • AI literacy must be embedded in graduate medical education (GME) and maintenance of certification (MOC)
  • GI fellowship curricula should include:
  • Fundamentals of ML/DL, bias sources, validation metrics (AUC-ROC, PPV/NPV), explainability methods
  • Hands-on simulation with CADe/CADx tools under supervision
  • Case-based ethics modules on overreliance, accountability, and transparency
  • Continuing education required for practicing clinicians: annual AI competency refreshers
Competency frameworks and assessment
  • Trainee milestones: distinguish between AI-assisted vs. AI-independent procedural competence
  • Objective structured assessment of AI proficiency (OSAIP): simulate edge cases where AI fails
  • Performance dashboards: track individual endoscopist ADR, AMR, and AI interaction frequency
  • Certification pathways: optional subspecialty credential in AI-augmented endoscopy
Faculty development needs
  • Faculty training on AI pedagogy: how to teach critical appraisal of AI literature
  • Interdisciplinary teaching teams: include data scientists, ethicists, and patient advocates
  • Open-access training datasets with annotated failures to foster humility in AI use
  • Simulation labs equipped with multiple AI platforms for comparative evaluation
8. Ethical, Legal, and Policy Frameworks
  • Informed consent must evolve: patients should be told when AI is used, its limitations, and who oversees it
  • Algorithmic transparency mandates: developers must disclose training data composition, validation cohort, and known failure modes
  • Regulatory oversight structure: specialty societies (ACG, ASGE) should co-develop standards with FDA/ CMS
  • Conflict resolution protocols: multi-stakeholder committees to resolve AI-clinician disagreements
Governance and accountability
  • AI incident reporting system: mandatory near-misses and harms (analogous to FAERS for drugs)
  • Vendor liability: clear delineation between clinician and developer responsibility in adverse events
  • Patient access to AI decision trails: right to explanation under HIPAA §164.524
  • Bias mitigation as quality metric: tie reimbursement to documented bias assessments
Policy recommendations
  • CMS reimbursement pilots for AI-assisted colonoscopy and NLP documentation
  • Anti-kickback safe harbor for value-based AI tools that reduce total cost of care
  • Tax incentives for small practices adopting interoperable AI with equity safeguards
  • Federal grants for community health center AI implementation with safety net focus
9. Stakeholder-Specific Implementation Pathways
  • Clinicians: need AI tools that augment—not replace—clinical judgment; require clear scope-of-practice guidance
  • Health systems: must invest in interoperable infrastructure, change management, and clinician engagement
  • Payers (CMS, commercial insurers): need evidence of value—not just technical accuracy—to justify reimbursement
  • Patients: require education on AI’s role, rights to opt-out, and assurance of human oversight
Role-specific implementation requirements
For endoscopists
  • CADe as second-pass reader: reduce miss rate without disrupting workflow; avoid real-time “primary driver” use until validation complete
  • CADx only for diminutive polyps (≤5 mm) meeting ≥90% sensitivity/specificity thresholds
  • Mandatory training on overreliance risks: simulate AI failure scenarios during proctoring
  • ADR benchmarking with/without AI to assess individual impact
For gastroenterology fellows and trainees
  • AI augmentation prohibited during core competency assessments
  • Simulation-based training: use AI-enhanced simulators only after basic colonoscopy proficiency
  • Structured reflection on AI use: journal entries documenting decision changes due to AI prompts
  • Mentorship requirements: fellows must discuss AI use with attending during case review
For practice administrators
  • AI ROI framework: track ↓ documentation time, ↑ prior auth throughput, ↓ no-show rates via chatbot triage
  • Workflow mapping: identify high-burden steps (e.g., chart prep, coding, prior auth) for AI targeting
  • Vendor evaluation criteria: interoperability (FHIR), explainability, bias testing reports, incident response plan
  • Pilot evaluation metrics: clinician satisfaction (Likert 1–5), time per patient, error rate pre/post
For health system IT leadership
  • API-first procurement policy: require AI vendors to provide standardized FHIR-based interfaces
  • Data governance council: include GI clinicians, ethicists, and patient advocates in AI oversight
  • Model version control: track updates, retraining dates, performance drift alerts
  • Cybersecurity addendum to vendor contracts: specify encryption-in-transit/at-rest, breach notification timelines
10. Equity-Focused AI Deployment Strategies
  • Algorithmic bias must be quantified and mitigated before deployment—especially for race, ethnicity, language, SES
  • Diverse training data non-negotiable: minimum 30% underrepresented minority patients per NIH UNITE Initiative
  • Language-specific NLP models required for non-English-speaking patients to avoid misclassification
  • Low-resource setting adaptations: lightweight AI tools (e.g., smartphone-based image analysis) for global equity
Disparity mitigation protocols
  • Bias audits as pre-market requirement: report performance stratified by race, gender, age, insurance status
  • Real-world equity monitoring dashboard: track AI performance by demographic strata post-deployment
  • Community engagement in tool design: involve patient advocates from marginalized groups during development
  • Equity impact statements for all AI grants and procurement decisions
Access expansion initiatives
  • Federally qualified health center (FQHC) AI grants: subsidize deployment in safety-net hospitals
  • Tele-AI hubs: central AI inference servers serving rural clinics via secure cloud pipelines
  • Low-bandwidth-compatible tools: offline-capable models for areas with poor connectivity
  • Medicaid-specific validation: ensure models trained on Medicaid populations perform as well as Medicare/private
Patient-facing equity safeguards
  • Plain-language AI consent forms: translated into top 5 local languages per region
  • Opt-out without penalty: patients may refuse AI use without affecting care access
  • AI literacy materials for patients: explain benefits/risks in accessible formats (videos, infographics)
  • Bias complaint mechanism: dedicated channel for patients to report perceived AI-related inequity
11. Regulatory, Legal, and Reimbursement Landscape
  • FDA classification: most GI AI tools fall under Class II (special controls); some CADx may require Premarket Approval
  • CMS reimbursement pathways:
  • Current: no separate code for CADe; bundled into colonoscopy base code
  • Proposed: new G-codes for AI-assisted colonoscopy (e.g., G0555–G0559)
  • Value-based: bundled payment for IBD/liver disease AI care coordination (e.g., CPT 99487 extension)
  • Malpractice risk modifiers: AI use may reduce liability if used appropriately, increase if overrelied upon
Legal accountability frameworks
  • Three-tier liability model:
  • Tier 1: Clinician negligence (e.g., ignoring AI warning)
  • Tier 2: Vendor negligence (e.g., unvalidated algorithm, hidden bias)
  • Tier 3: Systemic failure (e.g., poor integration, lack of training)
  • AI incident taxonomy: define near-misses, false positives/negatives, workflow disruption, harm
  • Expert witness standards: require dual expertise (GI + AI validation methodology)
Policy recommendations
  • ACG-led AI regulatory task force: develop consensus on:
  • Minimum validation standards per application (e.g., CADx for diminutive polyps vs. gastric neoplasia)
  • Required documentation elements (e.g., AI version, training data source, bias report)
  • Mandatory disclosure language for informed consent
  • CMS collaboration: develop AI-specific RVU adjustments to avoid E&M devaluation
  • Antitrust safe harbor: allow specialty societies to jointly define AI quality metrics without violating competition laws
  • Federal AI liability shield: for clinicians using FDA-cleared tools in good faith
12. Future Directions and Long-Term Vision
  • Next-generation AI integration: move from assisted to predictive and prescriptive tools
  • Predictive: AI forecasting IBD flares, HCC risk, post-op recurrence
  • Prescriptive: AI suggesting personalized surveillance intervals, drug selection, lifestyle interventions
  • Multimodal fusion models: combine endoscopic video, histopathology, genomics, EHR, wearables
  • Digital twins for GI organs: simulate disease progression and treatment response per patient
Roadmap milestones
  • Short-term (0–2 years):
  • CADe reimbursement pilots in 5 health systems
  • National AI bias registry for GI tools
  • ACG-endorsed AI curriculum for 100% of accredited GI fellowships
  • Mid-term (3–5 years):
  • FDA-cleared CADx for “diagnose-and-leave” strategy
  • Interoperable AI platform across 50% of academic GI divisions
  • CMS coverage for AI-augmented IBD/liver disease management
  • Long-term (5–10 years):
  • AI as standard-of-care in colonoscopy (like high-definition imaging)
  • Real-time clinical decision support embedded in EHR workflow
  • Global AI governance consortium with ACG leadership
Unmet needs and emerging opportunities
  • AI for rare GI conditions: e.g., autoimmune enteropathy, complex fistulizing Crohn’s
  • AI in mobile endoscopy units: portable devices with edge AI for low-resource settings
  • Explainable AI (XAI) standards: clinician-facing visualizations of decision pathways
  • AI ethics review boards at institutional and national levels
Final implementation principles
  • Augmentation over automation: AI should enhance—not replace—clinician judgment
  • Transparency as non-negotiable: no black boxes in clinical decision support
  • Equity by design: bias mitigation and access expansion embedded from inception
  • Continuous validation: AI tools must be re-evaluated annually or after major system changes
  • Clinician ownership: AI development and governance must include practicing gastroenterologists at every stage
