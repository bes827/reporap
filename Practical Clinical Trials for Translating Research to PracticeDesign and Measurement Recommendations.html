Practical Clinical Trials for Translating Research to Practice
Design and Measurement Recommendations
  • - Source: Med Care, June 2005 (Glasgow et al.) & JAMA, September 2003 (Tunis et al.)
  • - Objective: Provide recommendations for designing and reporting Practical Clinical Trials (PCTs) to enhance external validity and bridge the gap between research and practice.
Knowledge Test
  • - What percentage of recommended care do patients typically receive, highlighting the 'chasm' between evidence and practice?
  • - In the SWEAT trial, what were the 18-month retention rates for the center-based vs. home-based exercise programs?
  • - How many of the 22 original CONSORT criteria specifically address external validity?
  • - What is a 'type III error' in the context of intervention implementation?
  • - The ALLHAT trial compared inexpensive diuretics to newer agents. How many primary care clinics were involved in this large-scale PCT?

The Problem: The Research-Practice Gap
  • - An enormous gap exists between evidence-based recommendations and actual clinical practice.
  • - Patients receive, on average, only ~50% of recommended care (McGlynn et al.).
  • - One major cause is that traditional clinical trials are often of little relevance to practicing clinicians and decision-makers.
Limitations of Traditional Trials
  • - Strict Inclusion Criteria: Exclude patients with comorbidities, non-study medications, or low adherence, who are common in primary care.
  • - Setting: Often conducted in tertiary care centers with paid research staff, not representative community settings.
  • - Focus: Prioritize internal validity over external validity (generalizability).
Example: Diabetes Prevention Program
  • - A landmark study that screened over 158,000 patients to randomize just 3,819.
  • - This highlights the highly selective nature of many efficacy trials.



The Solution: Practical Clinical Trials (PCTs)
  • - Definition: Trials where the hypothesis and study design are specifically formulated to answer practical questions faced by decision-makers (patients, clinicians, payers).
  • - Goal: To evaluate the risks, benefits, and costs of an intervention as it would occur in routine clinical practice.
PCTs vs. Explanatory Trials
  • - PCTs (Pragmatic): Address practical questions about real-world effectiveness.
  • - Explanatory Trials: Aim to understand how and why an intervention works under ideal conditions (efficacy).

Core Characteristics of PCTs
  • - PCTs are distinguished by four key features that enhance their relevance and generalizability.
1. Compare Clinically Relevant Alternatives
  • - Focus: Head-to-head comparisons of viable clinical strategies, rather than treatment vs. placebo.
  • - Rationale: Better represents the actual decisions facing clinicians and policymakers.
  • - A new/expensive treatment should be shown to be more effective than current or cheaper alternatives.
Example: BPH Treatment
  • - A VA study compared terazosin, finasteride, or both for BPH symptoms.
  • - It found terazosin was more effective than finasteride alone at 1 year, providing direct guidance for clinical choice.

Example: Low Back Pain
  • - Compared physical therapy, chiropractic care, and a self-care educational booklet.
  • - While PT and chiropractic care increased satisfaction, there were no differences in function or recurrence rates, and the booklet was substantially less expensive.


2. Enroll a Broad & Representative Study Population
  • - Goal: Enroll patients with characteristics reflecting the full range seen in routine clinical practice.
  • - Method: Use broad inclusion criteria and minimal exclusion criteria.
  • - It is critical to document the representativeness of participants vs. non-participants.
Example: Pharmacy Alert Intervention
  • - Magid et al. evaluated an intervention to detect medication prescribing errors.
  • - The project's reach extended to over 350,000 patients, with over 95% of the HMO's members participating.

Example: Smoking Cessation in Planned Parenthood
  • - Glasgow et al. identified smoking status for >99% of patients.
  • - All smokers were invited to participate, regardless of their desire to quit, achieving a 76% participation rate among low-income smokers.


3. Recruit From a Variety of Practice Settings
  • - Goal: Include a wide range of physicians and settings (e.g., community clinics, private offices) to enhance external validity.
  • - Key Question Answered: "Will this program work in settings like ours, with staff and resources similar to ours?"
  • - Avoid the temptation to only use large, well-resourced, or research-savvy settings.
Example: ALLHAT Trial
  • - Compared hypertension treatments in over 42,000 patients.
  • - Participants were enrolled from 623 primary care clinics, many of which had no prior research experience.
  • - Finding: Inexpensive diuretics resulted in a lower incidence of major cardiovascular events compared to newer, more expensive agents.


4. Measure a Broad Range of Relevant Health Outcomes
  • - Focus: Collect data on outcomes most relevant to decision-makers, beyond traditional mortality and morbidity.
  • - Includes: Quality of Life (QoL), symptom severity, patient satisfaction, and costs.
  • - Quality-Adjusted Life-Years (QALYs) provide a common metric to compare treatments across different conditions.
Example: Depression Treatment Costs
  • - A study compared fluoxetine vs. tricyclic antidepressants.
  • - Despite fluoxetine's higher drug cost, the total costs of care were no different.
  • - This was because fluoxetine patients had fewer adverse effects and fewer physician visits.

Example: Chronic Disease Self-Management
  • - Lorig et al. demonstrated that self-management programs can improve health status, reduce hospitalizations, and enhance self-efficacy at a relatively low cost.




Design & Reporting Recommendations for PCTs
  • - Specific recommendations to improve the design, conduct, and reporting of PCTs to maximize their practical value.
Expand CONSORT Criteria for External Validity
  • - The standard CONSORT criteria are excellent for internal validity but weak on external validity.
  • - Only 1 of the 22 original criteria addresses generalizability, and it is non-specific.
  • - Proposes 8 new items to be added to CONSORT to address this gap (See Table 1).
Proposed Additions (Table 1)
  • - Participants: State target population for patients, clinicians, and settings.
  • - Participant Flow (Patients): Report exclusion/participation rates and representativeness.
  • - Participant Flow (Staff): Report participation rate and characteristics of interventionists.
  • - Participant Flow (Settings): Report exclusion/participation rates and representativeness of settings.
  • - Outcomes (Implementation): Report fidelity of intervention delivery.
  • - Outcomes (Cost): Report time and costs to deliver the intervention.
  • - Outcomes (Sustainability): Report organizational continuance or adaptation of the intervention post-trial.
  • - Discussion: Describe barriers and facilitators to implementation.

Alternative Research Designs
  • - When randomization is not feasible or acceptable, several viable alternative designs provide high levels of experimental control.
Interrupted Time-Series Design
  • - Also called a "multiple baseline across settings" design (See Figure 2).
  • - Each setting (e.g., clinic) serves as its own control.
  • - The intervention is introduced at staggered, different times across settings to control for secular trends and other confounders.

Delayed Treatment Design
  • - All participants eventually receive the intervention, but the timing is staggered (and randomized if possible).
  • - Allows for evaluation and refinement of the intervention over time.

Regression-Discontinuity Design
  • - Useful when groups are selected based on a specific cutoff on a continuous variable (e.g., symptom severity).
  • - The cutoff variable is used to statistically model and control for selection effects.

Propensity Scores
  • - A statistical technique used to model variables that predict selection into a treatment group.
  • - The resulting score can be used as a control variable in regression analysis to estimate intervention effects.


Specific Measurement Recommendations (Table 2)
  • - PCTs should include a broader set of measures to inform clinical and policy decisions.
Key Measurement Areas
  • - 1. Contextual Factors: Organizational culture, setting characteristics.
  • - 2. Protocol Implementation: Fidelity of intervention delivery. A failure here is a Type III error (intervention fails because it's not delivered as intended).
  • - 3. Generalization: Use frameworks like RE-AIM (Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance).
  • - 4. Quality of Life: Patient-centered outcomes.
  • - 5. Economic Outcomes: Cost-effectiveness, cost-utility, return on investment.
  • - 6. Behavior Change: At both patient and staff levels.


Example of Clinically Relevant Design: SWEAT Trial
  • - Compared a standard center-based exercise program with a less expensive, home-based program for sedentary women.
  • - Results at 18 months:
  • - Retention: 81% (center-based) vs. 61% (home-based).
  • - Adherence: 65% of sessions completed (center-based) vs. 51% (home-based).
  • - Conclusion: Provided excellent data for decision-makers, showing the home-based option needed more work before replacing the standard of care.


Challenges and Strategies for Improvement
  • - Despite their importance, the supply of PCTs is inadequate due to funding and structural issues.
Why the Supply of PCTs is Inadequate
  • - High Cost: Large sample sizes and long follow-up are expensive. The ALLHAT study cost approximately $120 million.
  • - Funding Sources:
  • - NIH: Primarily focuses on basic biomedical research; lacks a systematic mechanism to prioritize PCTs.
  • - Industry: Focuses on trials for FDA regulatory approval (efficacy vs. placebo), not comparative effectiveness.

Proposed Strategies for Improvement
  • - A multi-pronged approach is needed to increase the production and impact of PCTs.
Systematic Prioritization
  • - Establish a neutral body (e.g., Institute of Medicine) to identify and prioritize key unanswered clinical questions for decision-makers.

Demand from Decision-Makers
  • - Payers, purchasers, and professional societies should consistently demand high-quality evidence from PCTs to inform coverage, payment, and guideline decisions.

Create Infrastructure
  • - Develop and expand durable research networks within primary care settings (e.g., HMO Research Network, practice-based research networks).

Address Methodological & Ethical Issues
  • - Refine methods for large, simple trials, equivalence trials, and cluster randomization.
  • - Develop efficient and adequate consent and IRB procedures for multi-site trials.

Expand Funding
  • - Explore new funding models, potentially linking research funding to health care spending, as seen in the VA system and other national health services.
