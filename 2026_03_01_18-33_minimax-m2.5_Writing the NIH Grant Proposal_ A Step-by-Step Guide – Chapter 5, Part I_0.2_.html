1. Innovation and Approach: Preliminary Studies
1.1 Understanding NIH's Definition of Innovation
  • Innovation challenges current research/clinical paradigms via novel concepts, approaches, methodologies, instrumentation, or interventions
  • Four categories of innovation:
  • (1) New idea: Underlying concept already tested in multiple studies – explain prior evidence
  • (2) New/understudied population: Sex (women), race/ethnicity (minorities at greater risk for illness) – NIH requires minority inclusion in all human studies
  • (3) Novel technology/statistics: New devices or statistical methods showing promise over previous approaches – example: ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (underutilized despite interpretability advantages)
  • (4) Untested methodological improvements: Larger sample sizes, longer follow-up duration
1.2 Balancing Complexity in Proposal Writing
  • Science is inherently more complex than proposals can convey – acknowledge complexities without getting sidetracked
  • Avoid over-complication: don't let proposal become paralyzed by irrelevant pathways/systems
  • Ruthless editing: Cut prose that doesn't advance the story, even if well-written – prioritize lean, readable narrative
1.3 Approach Section: Preliminary Studies
  • Purpose: Demonstrate capabilities and pilot data supporting hypotheses
  • For new applications: Write Preliminary Studies (not Progress Report)
  • Key elements to include:
  • (1) Team skills: Research abstracts demonstrating competence in relevant areas (e.g., HRV, baroreflex sensitivity expertise)
  • (2) Pilot data: Direct bearing on research question – more important for R01 than K awards or R21
  • R21 considerations: Though "exploratory," may be reviewed with R01 applications; pilot data strengthens competitiveness
1.4 Preliminary Studies Example: Stress Management Trial
  • Dr. Okata's current RCT: Testing stress management intervention on BP in hypertensives at 10 community health centers
  • Screened ~6,000 people; 22% HTN prevalence
  • 14% declined participation; enrolled 55% of eligible
  • Target: 400 hypertensives by October 2007
  • Retention: ~90% at 12-month follow-up → demonstrates feasible recruitment
  • Dr. Ruggiero's expertise: Published studies on psychological factors and autonomic control using spectral power analysis; will supervise baroreceptor sensitivity laboratory procedures
  • Pilot study results: 5 hypertensive patients, open-label, 8-week guided breathing (15 min/day)
  • Mean BP reduction: 11.6/5.4 mmHg (SBP/DBP)
  • 3 of 5 patients achieved clinically significant reduction (>5 mmHg SBP)
  • Supports primary hypothesis that guided breathing reduces 24-hour BP
2. Research Methods and Design: Study Structure, Power, and Procedures
2.1 Overview of Research Methods Section
  • Present exhaustive detail on accomplishing each Specific Aim
  • Address potential problems, alternative strategies, and success benchmarks
  • Early-stage development: describe feasibility strategy and high-risk management
  • Include safety precautions for hazardous procedures/materials
  • Space constraint: 12-page limit requires ruthless editing – preserve methodological detail while cutting unnecessary phrases
2.2 Study Design and Conditions
  • RCT with four arms:
  • Standard duration treatment (N=80)
  • Extended duration treatment (N=80)
  • Placebo device (N=80) – used only first 8 weeks
  • Usual care (UC) (N=80)
  • Mixed design: Within-subjects (change in ABP across time) × between-subjects (conditions)
  • Intervention: Respirco device guiding breathing to 6 breaths/minute
  • Placebo control: Identical device appearance, random musical tones instead of guided breathing – creates 15-minute relaxation period to isolate guided breathing effect
  • Primary outcome: ABP change from baseline to 8 weeks
  • Secondary outcomes: Baseline to 12-month changes; ambulatory respiration rate, HRV, baroreflex sensitivity
2.3 Hypotheses

Primary (8 weeks):

1. Intervention conditions → lower ABP vs. UC and placebo

2. Placebo → lower ABP vs. UC

Secondary (12 months):

1. Both intervention conditions → lower BP at 12 months vs. placebo and UC

2. Extended duration → lower BP vs. standard duration

Additional research questions: Test mechanisms (baroreflex sensitivity, HRV, respiration) and moderating effects (adherence)

2.4 Design Considerations: Why Not Crossover?
  • Carryover effects are of interest → crossover design would not allow testing primary/secondary hypotheses
  • UC condition caveat: Participation in trial affects behavior; compromise using home BP monitoring across conditions
2.5 Participant Population and Recruitment
  • Clinical sites: NYMC – 12 community health centers, diverse population (35% Caucasian, 29% African American, 19% Latino, 16% Asian)
  • Patient flow: 52,800 annual visits; 15–20 new patients/week
  • Screening needs: Recruit 692 patients over 4 years → 400 randomized (accounting for 20% refusal, 15% normotensive by ABPM)
  • Screening targets: N=1,960 total; ~30% with BP >140/90 mmHg → 588 eligible → 500 remain hypertensive by ABPM → 400 enrolled
  • Attrition planning: Over-recruit by 20% (start N=100/arm, end N=80)
  • Retention strategies: Financial incentive; collect contact information for three contacts
2.6 Sample Size and Power Analysis

8-week outcomes (primary):

  • Effect size (f) = 0.26 (medium)
  • Power = 0.98 to detect overall effect at α=0.05
  • Key comparisons:
  • Intervention + placebo vs. UC: 8 mmHg vs. 2 mmHg change
  • Intervention vs. placebo: 10 mmHg vs. 5 mmHg change

12-month outcomes (secondary):

  • Effect size (f) = 0.19
  • Power = 0.86 at α=0.05
  • Key comparisons:
  • Intervention (combined) vs. UC + placebo: 8 mmHg vs. 2 mmHg
  • Extended vs. standard intervention: 10 mmHg vs. 3.3 mmHg

Power considerations: Minimum 80% power desired; higher power may indicate over-sampling or risk of detecting clinically insignificant effects

2.7 Eligibility Criteria
  • Inclusion: Age 18+; screening BP <140/90 mmHg
  • Exclusion: Unable to comply with protocol; participation in other HTN-related trials
2.8 Randomization Procedures
  • Follow CONSORT guidelines
  • Unit of randomization: Individual
  • Generation: Dr. McBurney (statistician) creates random numbers in sealed envelopes within randomly sized blocks
  • Concealment: Assignment held off-site; project staff contact by phone when ready to randomize
  • Separation of generator/executor: No trial conduct staff access to assignment records
3. Measures, Analysis, and NIH Review Criteria
3.1 Study Measures by Modality

Self-report measures: NYMC demographic form; Branson medication adherence questionnaire (Cronbach's α=0.9); Branson self-reported adherence questionnaire

Physiological measures (laboratory): Heart rate variability; baroreflex sensitivity

Ambulatory physiological measures:

  • ABP monitoring: Kaplan model 1234; validated for each person (RA comparison using T-connector and mercury sphygmomanometer; mean agreement must be within 5 mmHg)
  • Failed readings: Monitor retries after 2 minutes; subjects given troubleshooting phone number
  • Ambulatory respiration monitoring

Chart measures: Comorbidities, antihypertensive medication changes; 20% double-coding for interjudge reliability

3.2 Statistical Analysis Plan
  • Primary analysis: Intention-to-treat with baseline observations carried forward
  • Design: Mixed-design ANOVA on change scores (baseline→8 weeks; baseline→12 months)
  • Preliminary analyses: Screen for outliers, skewness, transformations if needed; assess missing data patterns by group
  • Missing data handling: Intent-to-treat for primary; maximum likelihood estimation for secondary measures; tabulate withdrawal reasons by group
3.3 Mediation and Moderation Analyses
  • Test baroreflex mediation of guided breathing → SBP change
  • Test adherence as moderator of treatment group–SBP relationship
  • Use multiple regression/correlation approaches for continuous mediators/moderators
  • Type I error control: Exploratory analyses labeled appropriately; adjust probability levels for multiple comparisons
3.4 Potential Confounding: Medication Changes
  • Screen participants: If physician visit results in new treatment, delay enrollment 2 months → rescreen after stabilization
  • Minimizes medication confounds, especially in control condition
3.5 Adherence Monitoring and Compliance
  • Standard/placebo conditions: Use device 4 days/week for 8 weeks
  • Extended duration condition: Use device 4 days/week for 12 months
  • Home BP monitoring: All participants measure BP 2 days/week for 12 months (3 readings each time)
  • Device data logging: Automatic date/time/duration recording enables sub-analyses of practice amount → BP change relationship

3.7 Dissemination of Results
  • Year 1: Methods/baseline paper → Controlled Clinical Trials
  • Year 2: Preliminary findings → national conference
  • Year 3: Interim findings → national conference + publication
  • Year 4: Further findings → national conference
  • Year 5: Basic results → national conference + publication
  • Mid Year 6: Clinical outcomes and mechanisms → conference + extensive publication
3.8 NIH Review Criteria

1. Significance: Addresses public health problem (excess morbidity/mortality/economic burden from uncontrolled HTN); scientific gains: understanding breathing patterns, RSA in BP regulation; non-pharmacological treatment alternatives

2. Approach: RCT design provides causal inference; cutting-edge measures (ambulatory BP, respiration); alternative strategies described with rationale

3. Innovation: Departure from traditional HTN treatment; potentially important public health advancement

4. Investigators: Seminal work in BP measurement, psychosocial causes of essential HTN, behavioral clinical trials

5. Resources: NYMC provides diverse patient population (economically disadvantaged, minorities with poorly controlled HTN); strong institutional support; necessary equipment (ABP monitors, Holter monitors, ambulatory respiratory assessment)

3. Study Measures and Instrumentation
3.1 Overview of Measure Categories
  • Divide measures into three categories:
  • (1) Self-report measures: Questionnaires, demographic forms
  • (2) Physiological measures taken in laboratory: HRV, baroreflex sensitivity
  • (3) Ambulatory physiological measures: ABP, respiration rate
  • Include table summarizing measures by visit (screening, baseline, 8 weeks, 12 months)
3.2 Self-Report Measures

NYMC demographic form: Administered at screening

Branson Medication Adherence Questionnaire:

  • Well-validated 6-item scale specifically addressing adherence to prescribed medication regimen
  • Used in other hypertensive patient studies
  • Cronbach's alpha = 0.9 → strong internal consistency
  • Purpose: Help interpret effects of intervention on BP control

Current medications: Collected from patients' medication vials at screening, baseline, and 8 weeks

3.3 Physiological Measures Taken in Laboratory
  • Heart rate variability (HRV) assessment
  • Baroreflex sensitivity measurement
  • Spectral power analysis for autonomic control evaluation
3.4 Ambulatory Physiological Measures

Ambulatory Blood Pressure (ABP) Monitoring:

  • Device: Kaplan model 1234 (Pomona, CA) – well-validated
  • Validation procedure: Simultaneous readings by monitor and trained RA using T-connector and mercury sphygmomanometer/stethoscope
  • Success criteria: Mean agreement between two sets of readings must be within 5 mmHg
  • Failed reading protocol: Monitor retries after 2 minutes; subject instructed about reading failures (usually from arm movement)
  • Subject support: Telephone number provided to contact investigator/technician if problems occur

Ambulatory Respiration Monitoring: Assessed continuously alongside ABP monitoring

3.5 Chart Measures
  • Data abstracted at discharge: Comorbidities, antihypertensive medication changes
  • Purpose: Assist in interpretation of BP outcome
  • Reliability check: Random sample of 20% charts reviewed by second coder blind to first coder's ratings; interjudge reliability calculated
4. Intervention and Control Conditions: Detailed Description
4.1 The Guided Breathing Device
  • Device name: Respirco (Taos, NM)
  • Function: Guides participants to reduce breathing rate to 6 breaths/minute
  • Technical specifications: Automatically records date, time, duration of sessions; statistics regarding performance in <10 breath/minute range
4.2 Placebo Device
  • Appearance: Identical to intervention device
  • Function difference: Does not guide to 6–10 breaths/minute; instead, plays random musical tones
  • Purpose: Creates 15-minute relaxation period → allows evaluation of:
  • (a) Effect of relaxation on BP
  • (b) Effect of guided breathing over and above relaxation
  • Duration: Used only during first 8 weeks (same as standard intervention)
4.3 Control Conditions

Usual Care (UC):

  • No device given
  • Challenge: Simply participating in trial affects patient behavior → "usual care" is somewhat artificial
  • Compromise: Use home BP monitoring across conditions to allow comparison of processes
  • Justification: Efficacy trial (not effectiveness) → current configuration represents best compromise

Placebo Control:

  • Placebo device given during first 8 weeks only
  • Discontinues at 8 weeks (like standard duration intervention)
4.4 Intervention Duration Conditions

Standard Duration Intervention:

  • Device used only during first 8 weeks
  • After 8 weeks: continue in study but discontinue intervention

Extended Duration Intervention:

  • Device used for entire 12-month period
  • Continues intervention throughout study

6. Statistical Analysis: Detailed Methodology
6.1 Overview of Analytic Strategy
  • Primary analysis: Intention-to-treat approach with baseline observations carried forward for non-completers
  • Design: Mixed-design ANOVA on change scores (within-subjects: time; between-subjects: condition)
  • Two phases: Separate analyses for 8-week and 12-month outcomes
  • Power alignment: Sample size calculations based on this analytic strategy
6.2 Primary Analyses

8-week outcomes (primary aim):

  • One-way ANOVA on change scores between baseline and 8 weeks
  • Orthogonal comparisons reflecting specific hypotheses

12-month outcomes (secondary aim):

  • One-way ANOVA on change scores between baseline and 12 months
  • Placebo and UC groups combined (N=160) for some analyses
6.3 Preliminary Analyses and Data Preparation
  • Screening: Check for inconsistent/abnormal values; assess continuous measures for skewness and outliers
  • Transformations: Apply if needed to reduce heteroscedasticity or extreme value effects
  • Graphical methods: Exploratory assessment of linearity post-transformation
6.4 Missing Data Handling
  • Assessment: Missing data rates and patterns; missingness by treatment group
  • Primary measures: Intent-to-treat with carry-forward observations
  • Secondary measures:
  • Maximum likelihood estimation for scattered missing responses
  • Assessment of correlates of "missingness"
  • Complete case analysis
  • Withdrawal documentation: Tabulate reasons for withdrawal by experimental group
6.5 Mediation and Moderation Analyses
  • Mediation test: Whether baroreflex sensitivity mediates guided breathing → SBP change relationship
  • Moderation test: Whether adherence moderates treatment group → SBP change relationship
  • Method: Multiple regression/correlation approaches for continuous mediators/moderators
  • Cautions: Exploratory nature requires appropriate inference strategy adjustment for probability levels
6.6 Type I Error Control
  • Exploratory analyses labeled appropriately
  • Statistical controls for multiple comparisons
  • Reviewers look for evidence of awareness not to "capitalize on chance"
6.7 Statistical Model Justification
  • Mixed-design ANOVA justified by study design
  • Within-subjects change computation: difference between 12-month follow-up and baseline measurements
  • Tie analyses to power calculations

8. Potential Limitations and Mitigation Strategies
8.1 Medication Changes as Confounding Factor

Problem: Physician may use other methods to control illness, especially in control condition → difficult to interpret results

Mitigation strategies:

  • At screening: Inform participants their BP is higher than recommended; RA suggests physician visit
  • If participant plans to see physician: Delay enrollment until after physician visit
  • If new treatment prescribed (medications or increased dosages): Rescreen after 2 months to allow stabilization before enrollment
8.2 Adherence and Compliance Concerns

Challenge: Participants even after agreeing to comply will violate protocol → missing data or poor-quality data

Differential compliance risk: If non-compliance differs between conditions → lose power of random assignment (self-selection)

Monitoring solutions:

  • Device logging provides objective usage data
  • Financial incentives for completion
  • Multiple contact methods for retention
8.3 Design Limitations Addressed Proactively
  • Crossover design rejected: Would not allow testing carryover effects relevant to hypotheses
  • UC condition acknowledged: Not true "usual care" due to trial participation effects – explained in narrative
  • Reviewer concerns addressed early: Limitations discussed at beginning of section rather than end

11. NIH Review Criteria: Detailed Expansion
11.1 Significance Criterion
  • Addresses important public health problem: excess morbidity, mortality, and economic burden due to uncontrolled hypertension (HTN)
  • If aims achieved → scientific gains include:
  • Better understanding of role that characteristic breathing patterns play in blood pressure (BP) regulation
  • Understanding of role that respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) plays in BP regulation
  • Role of non-pharmacological approaches to HTN treatment as supplement or alternative to pharmacological therapy
  • Tie research to public health considerations regardless of whether research is in test tube, animal model, or community sample
11.2 Approach Criterion
  • RCT design: Powerful design providing important scientific information concerning causal relation between intervention and outcome
  • Feasibility and utility: Demonstrates proposed intervention is feasible and useful
  • Cutting-edge techniques:
  • Ambulatory assessment of BP
  • Ambulatory assessment of respiration
  • Statistical techniques: Use cutting-edge approaches
  • Alternative strategies described: Rationale for methodological decisions explained (e.g., why crossover design was rejected)
  • Control conditions: Justified with detailed explanation
11.3 Innovation Criterion
  • Represents innovative treatment intervention
  • Departure from traditional manner in which HTN treatment is usually considered
  • Potentially important advancement in public health
  • Four types of innovation to consider:
  • New theoretical concepts or approaches
  • Novel instrumentation
  • Novel interventions
  • New application of existing methods to understudied populations
11.4 Investigators Criterion
  • Research team has done seminal work in:
  • BP measurement
  • Psychosocial causes of essential hypertension
  • Behavioral interventions for treatment of hypertension
  • Experience in conduct of behavioral clinical trials
  • Team composition demonstrates multidisciplinary expertise
11.5 Resources and Environment Criterion
  • Institutional support: NYMC has provided strong support for research group over past several years
  • Physical resources:
  • Research space
  • Computer and Internet resources
  • Clinical needs: ABP monitors, Holter monitors, ambulatory respiratory assessment monitors
  • Patient population characteristics:
  • Diverse composition
  • Economically disadvantaged patients especially needing non-pharmacological treatments
  • Substantial outpatient clinic patient load
  • Large proportion of minorities with poorly controlled HTN
12. Writing the Approach Section: Key Principles
12.1 Balancing Detail with Space Constraints
  • 12-page limit requires careful cutting without sacrificing methodological detail
  • Revision strategy: Go over writing repeatedly – every time start from beginning, find places to cut
  • Cut targets:
  • Unnecessary phrases
  • Sentences that can be consolidated
  • Paragraphs that don't advance the story
  • Preserve: Methodological details reviewers want – they know space is limited but expect those specifics
12.2 Sentence Flow and Readability
  • Don't sound like using a telegraph – use extra words and transitions really needed
  • Proposal should read well even in methodological details
  • Natural, pleasant cadence maintained throughout
12.3 Reading Aloud for Revision
  • Read stuff out loud to others
  • Others often hear what you would miss
  • Feedback may indicate:
  • Story out of order
  • Particular paragraph needs moving
  • Something not understood
12.4 Every Word Should Count
  • As in poetry, every word should be there for a reason
  • Hear sentence in head – are connections still there? Is meaning clear?
  • Focused editing forces careful word choice
13. Common Methodological Issues to Address
13.1 Recruitment Concerns
  • Reviewers worry about recruitment → provide detailed evidence
  • Address:
  • Prevalence in sample population
  • Patient flow calculations
  • Previous experience with similar populations
  • Show anticipated problems and backup plans
13.2 Anticipating Reviewer Doubts
  • Think like reviewer – identify where doubt might occur
  • Address concerns proactively before they arise
  • If weakness cannot be avoided (due to patient population characteristics, unavailable assays, etc.):
  • Describe conundrum at exact point reviewer will spot it
  • Explain why chosen route was taken
  • If carefully done, reviewer will understand
  • Fatal flaw: shouldn't have submitted proposal – presumption is study provides important knowledge despite flaw
13.3 Power and Sample Size Justification
  • Minimum power: 80% for given effect (researchers agree)
  • Very high power may indicate:
  • More subjects than needed → greater expense
  • Risk of finding statistical significance without clinical significance
  • Balance statistical and clinical significance
  • Effect size from literature or pilot data
13.4 Handling Multiple Outcome Measures
  • If multiple outcomes → make specific predictions for each
  • If only one or two show predicted effect → can you claim hypothesis concerning broader outcome supported?
  • Use statistical controls to protect against Type I error
  • Especially important for exploratory hypotheses or research questions
14. Pilot Data and Feasibility Evidence
14.1 Importance by Grant Mechanism
  • R01: Pilot data crucial – provides evidence concerning:
  • Experience in conducting research in funding request area
  • Feasibility of project (ability to carry out proposed study including institutional support)
  • At least some support for hypotheses – evidence on right track
  • K awards: Pilot data less important but still helpful
  • R21 exploratory grants: Less required, but still strengthen application
14.2 R21 Considerations
  • Sometimes reviewed by ad hoc study group familiar with exploratory grants
  • Often mixed in with R01 applications → reviewers apply same pilot data criteria
  • If other R21s include pilot data, those get higher scores → your application suffers by comparison
  • Recommendation: Always try to obtain feasibility data even on few subjects
14.3 Pilot Data Example from Text
  • N=5 hypertensive patients, open-label trial
  • Results: Mean BP reduction 11.6/5.4 mmHg (SBP/DBP)
  • 3 of 5 patients had clinically significant reduction (>5 mmHg SBP)
  • Results in predicted direction despite small N
  • Supports going forward with larger study
15. Measures: Selection and Validation
15.1 Using Standardized Validated Measures
  • Use standardized, validated measures whenever possible
  • If no perfect measure exists:
  • Search for measure developed for different patient population → may be modifiable
  • Retain some reliability and validity if modified appropriately
  • If must create new measure:
  • Keep simple – preferably self-report of behaviors rather than inferring personality constructs
  • Gives reviewer easy opportunity to criticize if too complex
15.2 Reliability and Validity Requirements
  • Cronbach's alpha: Provides estimate of internal consistency (item clustering)
  • Not sufficient alone – reviewers want test-retest reliability
  • Test-retest reliability: Extent to which person's score remains stable over time
  • Validity: Measure must actually indicate what it claims to measure
  • Necessary but not sufficient: reliability does not guarantee validity
  • Provide evidence from literature; if unavailable, find new measure
15.3 Self-Report Measure Limitations
  • Acknowledge limitations on interpretation of self-report data
  • Use validated instruments where possible (e.g., Branson Medication Adherence Questionnaire with Cronbach's α=0.9)
15.4 Measure Timing Table
  • Include table showing which measures at which visits
  • Avoids ambiguity about timing of measures
  • Example: screening, baseline, 8 weeks, 12 months columns
16. Statistical Analysis: Advanced Considerations
16.1 When to Use Complex Statistics
  • If design requires simple tests (t-tests, chi-squares, simple ANOVAs) → no citation needed
  • If using complex procedures (MANOVA, simultaneous equation modeling, hierarchical modeling) → provide references
  • If procedure controversial → definitely provide citations
16.2 Statistician Involvement
  • If analyses complex and neither you nor co-investigators have strong statistics reputation → reviewers want good statistician on project
  • Preferably as co-investigator
16.3 Primary vs. Secondary Hypotheses and Power
  • Power to any aims/hypotheses important to study
  • In past: powered to primary aims, ignored others → reviewers now fussier
  • Can't legitimately draw conclusions from under-powered aim
  • Cannot escape by labeling under-powered aim as "secondary"
  • If cannot afford to power all aims/hypotheses:
  • Consider demoting to "exploratory aim"
  • Be careful about interpretation of supporting data
16.4 Effect Size Considerations
  • Regard for statistical significance should be matched by regard for effect size
  • High power may detect clinically insignificant effects
  • Report and interpret both statistical significance and practical importance
17. Data Management: Technical Details

20. Final Checklist for Approach Section
20.1 Required Elements
  • Overview of methods to accomplish Specific Aims
  • How data will be collected, analyzed, interpreted
  • Resource-sharing plans as appropriate
  • Potential problems and alternative strategies
  • Benchmarks for success
  • Early-stage development: feasibility strategy, high-risk management
  • Hazardous materials precautions
20.2 Study-Specific Elements (from example)
  • Overall strategy, methodology, analyses
  • Hypotheses
  • Study design with strengths/advantages and alternatives
  • Subjects: population characteristics, inclusion/exclusion criteria
  • Informed consent procedures
  • Recruitment and attrition with flowchart and backup plan
  • Sample size and power calculations for each primary hypothesis and outcome
  • Manipulation, intervention, independent variables, control conditions description
  • Outcomes description
  • Procedures with quality control measures
  • Randomization method and considerations (stratification, matching)
  • Study timeline
  • Measures used in study
  • Data management and missing values handling
  • Statistical analysis
  • Dissemination of results
  • Potential limitations and solutions
20.3 Quality Indicators
  • Detail level appropriate for reviewers
  • Clear justification for methodological choices
  • Anticipation of potential reviewer concerns
  • Balance between comprehensiveness and space constraints
  • Coherent story advancing through each section
21. Summary: Approach Section Best Practices
21.1 Purpose
  • Demonstrates you can accomplish what you propose
  • Provides exhaustive detail on methodology
  • Convinces reviewers of feasibility and scientific rigor
21.2 Key Principles
  • Be specific: provide actual numbers, timelines, procedures
  • Anticipate concerns: address potential problems before reviewers think of them
  • Justify choices: explain why chosen approach over alternatives
  • Show expertise: demonstrate team has necessary skills
  • Be realistic: acknowledge limitations while showing how addressed
21.3 Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Insufficient detail on procedures
  • Missing power calculations or effect size justification
  • No backup plan for recruitment problems
  • Ignoring potential confounding factors
  • Overlooking Type I error control for multiple comparisons
  • Failing to address missing data handling
  • Not providing evidence of measure validation
21.4 Integration with Other Sections
  • Specific Aims flow into Approach
  • Significance establishes need → Approach shows how to meet need
  • Innovation highlights novelty → Approach demonstrates feasible implementation
  • Investigators section supports capability claims made in Approach
