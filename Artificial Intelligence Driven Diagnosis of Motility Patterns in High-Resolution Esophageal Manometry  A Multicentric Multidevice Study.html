Esophageal Motility Disorders (EMDs)
  • Common in clinical practice → high symptomatic burden + significant impact on quality of life.
  • Diagnosis often delayed/missed early → early, accurate diagnosis essential for patient outcomes + effective treatment/follow-up.
Physiology
  • Esophagus: muscular tube w/ 2 sphincters.
  • Primary function: transport food/liquids to stomach → coordinated contractions + sphincter relaxation.
Diagnostic Categories
  • Disorders of esophagogastric junction (EGJ) outflow.
  • Disorders of peristalsis.
High-Resolution Esophageal Manometry (HREM)
  • Gold standard for evaluating functional esophageal disorders.
  • Complex procedure: data analysis + accessibility challenges.
  • Limitations: high intra-/inter-observer variability, intrinsic difficulty of data analysis/interpretation → suboptimal diagnostic accuracy.
Standard Protocol (CC v4.0)
  • Patients fasted ≥4 hours pre-procedure.
  • Initial supine position.
  • Stabilization period ≥60 seconds.
  • ≥3 deep inspirations to confirm catheter position.
  • Baseline period ≥30 seconds: identify UES, LES, RIP, basal EGJ pressure.
  • Ten 5ml wet swallows (ambient temp water/saline), ≥30 seconds interval.
  • Multiple Rapid Swallow (MRS): five 2ml wet swallows, 2–3 seconds apart.
  • Upright position: ≥60 seconds adaptation, catheter reassessment, ≥30 seconds baseline.
  • Upright: ≥5ml wet swallows, 30 seconds interval.
  • Rapid Drink Challenge (RDC): 200ml water quickly via straw.
  • Additional supportive maneuvers if no major disorder/inconsistent findings.
  • Each HREM: ≥15 swallows (10 supine, 5 upright) → high-resolution spatiotemporal pressure signals across 36 channels.
Challenges for AI
  • HREM generates complex, high-dimensional spatiotemporal pressure signals.
  • Requires tailored approaches: data preprocessing, feature extraction, model design.
  • Noise + artifacts (patient movement, sensor misalignment) → affect data quality.
  • Analysis is time-consuming + depends on specialized clinical expertise.
  • Limits availability of large, high-quality datasets for training/validation.
Chicago Classification (CC) v4.0
  • Standardized approach to HREM interpretation.
  • Provides algorithmic assignment of motility patterns.
  • Worldwide accepted system.
AI/Machine Learning (ML) in HREM
  • Potential to overcome HREM limitations: standardize interpretation, reduce interobserver variability.
  • Aims to improve diagnostic accuracy + reproducibility.
Previous AI Studies
  • Few studies in motility field → significant literature gap.
  • Pilot studies: demonstrated utility in identifying normal motility patterns + swallowing types.
  • Recent models: differentiate EMDs per CC.
  • Limitations of prior studies: mostly single-center, single-device, based on outdated CC v3.0.
Study's Contribution
  • First multicenter, multidevice study to evaluate ML models for differentiating peristalsis + EGJ outflow disorders in HREM.
  • Developed + validated ML model using multicenter, multidevice dataset.
  • Aligned w/ most recent Chicago Classification (v4.0).
  • Enhances model's potential for interoperability + external validity.
Study Design & Methods
  • Retrospective analysis of 618 HREM exams.
  • Multicenter: 3 centers (Spain, USA).
  • Multidevice: Medtronic ManoScan™ ESO, Laborie Solar™ GI.
Patient Cohort
  • 618 HREM procedures retrospectively reviewed.
  • From 3 reference centers: Hospital Universitario La Princesa (Spain), Hospital Universitario Puerta del Hierro Majadahonda (Spain), University Hospital of South Alabama (USA).
  • Eligibility: patients >18 years, HREM for clinical indication.
  • Exclusions: pediatric, pregnant, ineligible per center's clinical eval.
  • Inclusions: active opioid use, prior esophageal surgery (to reflect real-world practice).
  • Devices: Medtronic ManoScan™ ESO, Laborie Solar™ GI.
Data Labeling
  • Procedures independently reviewed + labeled by 2 expert gastroenterologists.
  • Labels based on Chicago Classification v4.0.
  • Anatomic landmarks manually assessed + confirmed by experts.
  • Discrepancies resolved via joint consensus.
  • Only studies w/ complete agreement on landmark ID + swallow pattern classification used.
  • Excluded exams w/o consensus.
  • Final labels reflected 2 diagnostic categories: EGJ outflow disorders (achalasia types I, II, III; EGJ outflow obstruction) & peristalsis disorders (absent contractility, distal esophageal spasm, hypercontractile esophagus, ineffective esophageal motility).
ML Model Development
  • Two independent ML models developed: one for EGJ outflow disorders, one for peristalsis disorders.
  • Human-in-the-loop/active learning strategy: prioritized informative data for training due to class imbalance.
  • Data split: 80% training/validation, 20% testing (patient-level stratified).
  • Cross-validation applied within training phase (repeated 10-fold).
  • Input features: >1,600 variables extracted from manometry time-series (resting phase + swallowing maneuvers).
  • Features include: basic statistics (mean, SD), rolling-window measures, wavelet-based metrics.
  • Output: binary (Yes/No).
  • Initial 'base model': DecisionTreeClassifier.
  • Optimization: evaluated XGBoost, LightGBM, AdaBoostClassifier, Gaussian Naive Bayes, Gradient Boosting Classifier, CatBoostClassifier.
  • Final model architecture trained + evaluated over 10 repeated runs w/ different random seeds.
  • Performance metrics: mean accuracy + AUC-ROC values w/ standard deviations.
  • System: 2.1 GHz Intel Xeon Gold 6130 processor, dual NVIDIA Quadro RTX 8000 GPUs.
Statistical Analysis
  • ML model performance evaluated by accuracy + AUC-ROC.
  • Differentiating disorders of peristalsis vs. EGJ outflow.
  • Metrics compared against expert consensus labels.
Results
  • Total 618 HREM exams analyzed.
  • Mean age: 58 ± 14 years; 56% female.
  • Expert-classified EMDs: EGJ outflow disorders (54 cases), peristalsis disorders (187 cases).
Model Performance: EGJ Outflow Disorders
  • Best model: GradientBoostingClassifier.
  • Accuracy: 0.942 ± 0.015.
  • AUC-ROC: 0.921 ± 0.041.
  • 54 cases classified as EGJ outflow disorders by experts.
Model Performance: Peristalsis Disorders
  • Best model: xGBClassifier.
  • Accuracy: 0.809 ± 0.029.
  • AUC-ROC: 0.871 ± 0.027.
  • 187 cases classified as peristalsis disorders by experts.
Performance Consistency
  • Consistent performance across repeated validations → model robustness + generalization.
  • LGBMClassifier for EGJ outflow: Accuracy 0.940 ± 0.014, AUC-ROC 0.931 ± 0.038.
  • CatBoostClassifier for Peristalsis: Accuracy 0.797 ± 0.033, AUC-ROC 0.839 ± 0.033.
  • Further validation per center planned to assess generalizability.
Discussion & Clinical Implications
  • Feasibility of AI models for global application in HREM.
  • Potential to improve diagnostic accuracy + reduce variability in EMD identification.
  • AI-driven HREM → standardized, reproducible, automated analysis.
Addressing HREM Limitations
  • HREM interpretation is expert- + manual-dependent → substantial intra-/inter-observer variability.
  • AI models aim to reduce variability + improve reproducibility/accessibility.
  • Manual interpretation limits accuracy + widespread HREM implementation.
Future Clinical Integration
  • Potential application: second-reader tool within HREM analysis platforms.
  • Flag cases w/ uncertain/borderline findings for additional expert review.
  • Resource-limited settings: accessible decision-support tool for less experienced practitioners.
  • Requires rigorous prospective validation, intuitive user interfaces, seamless interoperability w/ existing reporting systems.
  • Goal: transparent, reproducible, actionable insights complementing clinician expertise.
Study Limitations
  • Absence of fully independent external validation cohort.
  • Class imbalance, especially for EGJ outflow disorders.
  • Variability in patient cooperation + anatomical characteristics.
Generalizability Concerns
  • All participating centers contributed data to both training + testing phases.
  • May overestimate model’s generalizability.
  • Future research needs independent dataset from centers not involved in model development.
Data Exclusions
  • Only exams w/ full expert consensus included in training.
  • May reduce generalizability in ambiguous/borderline real-world scenarios.
Technical Limitations
  • Exclusive reliance on pressure-based metrics.
  • EGJ distensibility (FLIP) + impedance not incorporated, despite importance for certain diagnoses.
  • Future studies should explore multimodal data integration.
  • Lack of transparency of ML models ('black-box' systems) → challenges in trust.
  • Interpretability hindered by subjectivity of certain exam findings.
  • Future plans: incorporate explainability techniques (SHapley Additive exPlanations), visual representation (heatmaps, temporal importance plots).
