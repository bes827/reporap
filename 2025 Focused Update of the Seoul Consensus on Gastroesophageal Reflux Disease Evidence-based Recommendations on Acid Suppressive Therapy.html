2025 Focused Update of the Seoul Consensus on Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease: Evidence-based Recommendations on Acid Suppressive Therapy
  • Article Type: Review or Meta-Analysis.
  • Journal: Journal of Neurogastroenterology and Motility (JNM).
  • Publication Status: Online First, Ahead of Print.
  • Received: July 16, 2025; Accepted: August 4, 2025.
  • Purpose: Refine GERD management strategies by incorporating latest evidence on acid suppressive therapies & regional considerations in Asian populations.
  • Builds on: 2020 Seoul Consensus; integrates P-CABs & adopts Lyon Consensus 2.0 diagnostic framework.
  • Target Audience: Gastroenterologists & general healthcare providers.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What specific diagnostic framework, updated in 2023, is incorporated into the 2025 Seoul Consensus to guide individualized GERD therapy?
  • 2. In patients with severe erosive esophagitis (LA grades C and D), which class of acid-suppressive therapy has demonstrated superior mucosal healing rates compared to the other, particularly at 4 and 8 weeks?
  • 3. For long-term management of non-erosive reflux disease (NERD) or mild erosive esophagitis (LA grades A and B), what specific dosing strategy is recommended for PPIs or P-CABs?
  • 4. What is the minimum percentage of expert agreement required for a recommendation statement to reach consensus using the modified Delphi method in these guidelines?
  • 5. Beyond symptom relief, what are two major chronic complications of GERD that effective long-term treatment aims to prevent?
Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) Overview
  • GERD: chronic, relapsing GI disorder → reflux of gastric contents into esophagus/beyond.
  • Leads to: troublesome symptoms &/or complications.
  • Treatment essential for: prompt symptom relief, mucosal healing, prevention of chronic complications.
Definition & Classification (Statement 1)
  • GERD: condition characterized by regurgitation of gastric contents into esophagus/mouth → troublesome symptoms/complications.
  • Proven GERD: requires conclusive evidence of reflux esophagitis (LA grades B, C, D), Barrett’s esophagus, ulceration/stricture on endoscopy; OR pathological GER confirmed by ambulatory pH-impedance monitoring.
  • Subclassified into: Non-Erosive Reflux Disease (NERD) & Erosive Reflux Disease (ERD) based on erosive esophagitis (EE).
Erosive Esophagitis (EE) Grading
  • Characterized by: mucosal breaks.
  • Graded by: Los Angeles (LA) classification.
  • Mild EE: LA grades A & B.
  • Severe EE: LA grades C & D.
  • Note: LA grade A alone may not define proven GERD per Lyon Consensus 2.0, but remains clinically relevant for subclassification & phenotype-based treatment.
Lyon Consensus 2.0
  • Published: 2023.
  • Introduced: revised diagnostic framework emphasizing objective physiologic testing.
  • Concept: 'actionable GERD' to guide individualized therapy.
  • Thresholds for esophageal reflux testing in Asians: may differ from Western populations; warrants further investigation.
Acid-Suppressive Therapies
  • Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs): cornerstone of GERD management for >2 decades.
  • Potassium-Competitive Acid Blockers (P-CABs): emerging alternatives, first introduced in Asia.
PPI Limitations
  • Requires: proton pump activation.
  • Onset of action: delayed.
  • Duration of action: insufficient in some patients.
  • Symptom relief: incomplete in some patients.
  • Long-term adverse effects: growing concern.
Potential Long-term PPI Adverse Effects
  • Bone fractures.
  • Micronutrient deficiencies.
  • Pneumonia.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD).
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).
  • Clostridium difficile infection.
  • Hypergastrinemia.
P-CAB Advantages
  • Mechanism: reversibly inhibit H⁺, K⁺-ATPase.
  • Acid suppression: more potent & sustained.
  • Stability: in acidic environments.
  • Onset of action: rapid.
  • Half-life: longer.
  • Evidence: increasing RCTs, real-world studies, meta-analyses support role in initial & maintenance treatment.
P-CAB Long-term Safety
  • Data needed: further research on long-term safety.
  • Concerns: potential risks like hypergastrinemia, gastric malignancy, kidney dysfunction.
Guidelines Development Methodology
  • Working Group: Gastroenterologists from Korean Society of Neurogastroenterology and Motility (KSNM) & methodology experts.
  • Methodology: Hybrid approach combining systematic reviews, meta-analyses, & expert consensuses.
  • Process: Modeled after 2020 Seoul Consensus & recent Korean guidelines; 9 online meetings (started May 2024).
Key Clinical Questions
  • Developed using: PICO format (Population, Intervention, Comparator, Outcome).
  • Focus areas: 1) Initial treatment strategies for GERD based on disease severity; 2) Long-term maintenance approaches (on-demand vs continuous, optimal dosing).
Literature Search & Selection
  • Databases: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library.
  • Search period: Up to December 2024.
  • Study type: Randomized controlled trials (RCTs).
  • Comparisons: On-demand vs continuous PPI maintenance; P-CAB vs PPI for initial/maintenance GERD treatment.
  • Inclusion criteria: RCTs on GERD patients (NERD, ERD, EE, endoscopy-negative), specified comparisons, full-text articles.
  • Exclusion criteria: Abstract-only, unpublished, case reports, narrative reviews, systematic reviews/meta-analyses (unless used as source for evidence), conference proceedings, study protocols, insufficient data.
Evidence & Recommendation Strength
  • Framework: GRADE (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluation).
  • Evidence Levels: High, Moderate, Low, Very Low (based on study design, bias risk, consistency, directness, precision, publication bias).
  • Recommendation Strength: Strong (desirable effects clearly outweigh undesirable); Weak (trade-offs less certain due to low-quality evidence or uncertainty).
Consensus Process
  • Method: Modified Delphi method.
  • Evaluation: 5-point Likert scale (1=strongly disagree, 5=strongly agree).
  • Consensus defined as: ≥80% agreement (score of 4 or 5).
  • Round 1: 59 expert panel members participated; all statements reached consensus.
Treatment of GERD: Initial Strategies
  • Recommendations stratified by disease severity (NERD, mild EE, severe EE).
NERD / Mild Erosive Esophagitis (EE) (Statement 2)
  • Recommendation: Standard dose PPIs / P-CABs once daily for 4-8 weeks.
  • Level of Evidence: High.
  • Strength of Recommendation: Strong.
  • Expert Opinion: Strongly agree 70.7%; Agree w/ reservation 25.9%.
Efficacy: PPIs vs P-CABs
  • Mild EE healing rates @ 8 weeks: Comparable (pooled RR = 1.00; 95% CI, 0.99-1.02).
  • Mild EE healing rates @ 4 weeks: Comparable (pooled RR = 1.01; 95% CI, 0.97-1.06).
  • Vonoprazan subgroup: Comparable (RR = 1.00; 95% CI, 0.98-1.01).
  • Other P-CABs (fexuprazan, keverprazan, zastaprazan): Comparable (RR = 1.01; 95% CI, 0.98-1.05).
  • Tegoprazan (all EE grades): Comparable efficacy to PPI @ 4 & 8 weeks (pooled RR = 1.03; 95% CI, 0.97-1.10).
  • NERD symptom relief: P-CABs superior vs placebo.
Safety: PPIs vs P-CABs
  • Overall incidence of treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) over 8 weeks: Comparable (pooled RR = 1.04; 95% CI, 0.94-1.15).
Standard Doses Examples
  • PPIs: lansoprazole 30 mg, esomeprazole 40 mg, pantoprazole 40 mg.
  • P-CABs: tegoprazan 50 mg, fexuprazan 40 mg, zastaprazan 20 mg, vonoprazan 20 mg.
Severe Erosive Esophagitis (EE) (Statement 3)
  • Recommendation: Standard dose P-CABs / PPIs once daily for 8 weeks.
  • Level of Evidence: High.
  • Strength of Recommendation: Weak.
  • Expert Opinion: Strongly agree 50.0%; Agree w/ reservation 37.9%.
Consideration
  • P-CABs may offer superior mucosal healing efficacy compared to PPIs in patients w/ severe EE.
Efficacy: PPIs vs P-CABs
  • Severe EE healing rates @ 8 weeks: Superior w/ P-CABs (pooled RR = 1.10; 95% CI, 1.00-1.20).
  • Severe EE healing rates @ 4 weeks: Superior w/ P-CABs (pooled RR = 1.14; 95% CI, 1.03-1.26).
  • Vonoprazan subgroup: Superior efficacy vs PPIs (RR = 1.13; 95% CI, 1.04-1.23).
  • Other P-CABs (fexuprazan, keverprazan, zastaprazan): Comparable efficacy (RR = 1.04; 95% CI, 0.81-1.34).
  • Treatment failure rates (failure to heal EE): Significantly lower w/ P-CABs (8.9% vs PPI 19.4%; P < 0.0001).
Safety: PPIs vs P-CABs
  • Overall incidence of TEAEs over 8 weeks: Comparable (pooled RR = 1.04; 95% CI, 0.94-1.15).
Preference
  • P-CABs may be preferred over PPIs due to superior healing efficacy in severe EE & comparable safety profiles.
Treatment of GERD: Long-term Maintenance Strategies
  • Essential for managing GERD → high relapse rates after acid-suppressive therapy cessation.
  • Strategies: continuous, on-demand, intermittent, threshold therapies.
  • Most widely adopted: continuous & on-demand.
NERD / Mild Erosive Esophagitis (EE) (Statement 4)
  • Recommendation: On-demand therapy w/ PPI / P-CAB.
  • Level of Evidence: Moderate.
  • Strength of Recommendation: Weak.
  • Expert Opinion: Strongly agree 63.8%; Agree w/ reservation 29.4%.
PPI On-demand vs Continuous
  • Treatment failure rates: Comparable (pooled RR = 1.17; 95% CI, 0.65-2.13).
  • Adverse events: Similar incidence (RR = 0.98; 95% CI, 0.89-1.09).
  • Pill burden: Significantly lower w/ on-demand therapy (risk difference = –0.52; 95% CI, –0.62 to –0.42).
P-CAB On-demand Therapy
  • Evidence: Remains limited for long-term maintenance.
  • Effectiveness: Suggested by some studies in NERD/mild EE.
  • Support: Individual studies, meta-analyses, & superior pharmacological properties (rapid onset, long-lasting effects).
  • P-CAB vs PPI (mild EE): P-CAB superior in preventing EE recurrence over 24 weeks (pooled RR = 0.60; 95% CI, 0.38-0.94).
Severe Erosive Esophagitis (EE) (Statement 5)
  • Recommendation: Continuous daily therapy w/ P-CAB / PPI.
  • Level of Evidence: Low.
  • Strength of Recommendation: Weak.
  • Expert Opinion: Strongly agree 46.6%; Agree w/ reservation 37.9%.
Rationale for Continuous Therapy
  • Elevated risk of complications: Barrett’s esophagus, peptic strictures, esophageal cancer.
  • High rate of: symptomatic & endoscopic relapse following treatment discontinuation.
  • Reflected in: most international guidelines.
Continuous PPI vs On-demand
  • Effectiveness: Continuous PPI therapy may be more effective.
  • Relapse rates: Significantly higher in on-demand group, particularly in severe EE.
  • Treatment failure & symptom relief: Continuous maintenance therapy more effective.
P-CAB vs PPI
  • EE recurrence prevention over 24 weeks: P-CAB superior (pooled RR = 0.60; 95% CI, 0.44-0.83).
  • Treatment failure rates (endoscopic recurrence): Significantly lower w/ P-CABs (23.3% vs PPI 39.9%; P = 0.001).
  • Long-term safety: Further studies required to establish profiles.
Dose Optimization for Long-term Safety (Statement 6)
  • Recommendation: Lowest effective dose that controls symptoms & maintains mucosal healing.
  • Level of Evidence: Low.
  • Strength of Recommendation: Weak.
  • Expert Opinion: Strongly agree 83.1%; Agree w/ reservation 15.2%.
Rationale
  • Avoid: overuse & potential risks associated w/ long-term acid suppression.
  • Periodic reassessment: therapeutic indications, doses, durations of therapy.
Long-term PPI Risks
  • ↑ risks of: enteric infection, kidney dysfunction, nutrient malabsorption, cardiovascular events, dementia, gastric malignancy, hypomagnesemia, vitamin B12 deficiency, bone fracture.
Long-term P-CAB Risks
  • Concerns: hypergastrinemia, gastric malignancy, kidney dysfunction.
  • Research needed: further clarification on long-term safety, especially given recent clinical adoption.
Guideline Emphasis
  • Japanese guidelines: minimize dose & duration, allow long-term maintenance when clearly indicated.
  • American Gastroenterological Association: reassess appropriateness & dosing within 12 months, ideally via endoscopic & reflux monitoring.
  • American College of Gastroenterology: PPIs generally safe, but potential risks for long-term use must be considered.
  • Adjustment: based on individual clinical responses, risk factors, & evolving indications.
Conclusion & Perspectives
  • Guidelines provide: evidence-based approach for GERD diagnosis & management.
  • Emphasis: recent advances in acid-suppressive therapy & evolving GERD definition.
  • Integration: P-CAB data w/ established PPI evidence.
  • Recommendations stratified by: disease severity (NERD, mild EE, severe EE) for initial & long-term maintenance.
  • Future Research: ongoing research, especially on long-term P-CAB safety, is essential.
  • Further studies warranted for: pediatric GERD & extraesophageal manifestations.
  • Revision: guidelines will be periodically revised w/ accumulating evidence for optimal clinical outcomes.
