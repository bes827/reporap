Vonoprazan–tetracycline dual regimen as rescue therapy for Helicobacter pylori infection: randomized controlled trial
  • Source: Gastroenterology (pre‑proof), Accepted 6 Jan 2026, DOI 10.1053/j.gastro.2026.01.008
  • Topic: Rescue eradication therapy for H. pylori after ≥1 prior failure
  • Authors: Gao W et al.
1. Study Design & Population
  • Prospective, open‑label, non‑inferiority RCT; 1:1 randomization to VT dual therapy vs. bismuth quadruple therapy (BQT) for 14 days.
  • Inclusion: adults 18–80 y, confirmed H. pylori (13C‑UBT), ≥1 prior eradication failure.
  • Exclusion: recent antibiotics/PPI/bismuth (<4 wks), severe organ disease, pregnancy/lactation, known drug hypersensitivity, concurrent trial participation, psychiatric conditions impairing compliance.
  • Randomization: computer‑generated block size 4; allocation concealed in opaque envelopes; investigators & participants unblinded, outcome assessors (UBT technicians) blinded.
2. Interventions & Dosing
  • VT dual therapy: vonoprazan 20 mg BID + tetracycline 500 mg TID (total tetracycline 1.5 g/day).
  • BQT: lansoprazole 30 mg BID + colloidal bismuth 150 mg TID + tetracycline 500 mg TID + metronidazole 400 mg TID (standard 14‑day regimen).
  • Administration timing: acid‑suppressor/bismuth ~30 min pre‑meal; antibiotics immediately post‑meal.
3. Outcomes & Statistical Plan
  • Primary endpoint: non‑inferiority of eradication rate (13C‑UBT at 8 weeks) between VT and BQT. Non‑inferiority margin = 10%; one‑sided α = 0.025; Bonferroni‐adjusted α per comparison = 0.0125; power = 80%.
  • Secondary endpoints: incidence & severity of treatment‑emergent adverse events (TEAEs), medication adherence (≥80% doses).
  • Populations: ITT (all randomized, loss → failure), modified ITT (received ≥1 dose + UBT), PP (completed ≥80% meds + follow‑up).
  • Analyses: categorical variables – χ² or Fisher; continuous – t‑test or Wilcoxon; CI 95%; non‑inferiority tested with two‑sided tests for each population.
4. Baseline Characteristics (n=350)
  • Age mean ± SD: VT 48 ± 13.4 y (range 18–77); BQT 45.7 ± 12.1 y (18–73).
  • Sex M/F: VT 60/115; BQT 66/109.
  • BMI mean ± SD: both groups 23 ± ≈3.5 kg/m².
  • Penicillin allergy: VT 63 (36%); BQT 56 (32%).
  • Prior eradication attempts median IQR: VT 2 (1–3); BQT 2 (1–2).
  • No significant differences in smoking, alcohol use, comorbidities, endoscopic diagnoses, or concomitant medications (all p > 0.05).

  • Sub‑analysis by penicillin allergy: VT eradication 91.8 % (no allergy) vs. 88.3 % (allergy); BQT 89.4 % vs. 89.3%; differences not significant (p > 0.5).
6. Safety & Adherence
  • TEAEs overall: VT 10.9 % (19/175) vs. BQT 45.7 % (80/175); P < 0.001.
  • Grade distribution (VT/BQT): Mild 13/54 (P = 0.000), Moderate 4/10 (NS), Severe 2/16 (P = 0.001).
  • Most frequent AEs (VT): nausea 3, constipation 5, dizziness 2; (BQT): nausea 37, diarrhea 12, abdominal pain 9, bitter taste 8.
  • Discontinuations due to AEs: VT 0 % vs. BQT 8.6 % (15/175); P < 0.001.
  • Adherence ≥80%: VT 96.0 % (168/175) vs. BQT 87.4 % (153/175); P = 0.006.
7. Limitations Highlighted by Authors
  • Single‑center, open‑label design → potential reporting bias, limited external validity.
  • No individual H. pylori susceptibility testing; reliance on regional resistance data.
  • Non‑inferiority margin of 10 % considered relatively wide; stricter margins may be needed for definitive equivalence.
  • Sample size insufficient for stratified analysis by number of prior failures or specific prior regimens.
  • Gut microbiota impact not assessed; long‑term ecological effects unknown.
8. Discussion & Interpretation
  • VT dual therapy met non‑inferiority criteria (P < 0.01) despite a modestly lower absolute eradication rate in PP analysis (–1.1 %).
  • Simplified regimen removed bismuth and metronidazole, reducing pill burden from 8 tablets/day (BQT) to 5 tablets/day (VT).
  • Authors argue that potent acid suppression by vonoprazan (maintains gastric pH ≥6) enables a single low‑resistance antibiotic (tetracycline) to achieve reliable eradication.
9. Mechanistic Rationale & Antibiotic Resistance Context
  • Tetracycline resistance in Asia‑Pacific (1990–2022): ~4 %, consistently lower than metronidazole (52 %), levofloxacin (26 %), clarithromycin (22 %).
  • Vonoprazan, a potassium‑competitive acid blocker, provides stronger and more sustained pH elevation than double‑dose PPIs, enhancing tetracycline activity.
  • No cross‑resistance reported between tetracycline and amoxicillin; VT may serve as an alternative for patients failing high‑dose amoxicillin dual therapy.
10. Subgroup & Ancillary Analyses (Exploratory)
  • Prior regimen distribution (VT vs. BQT, n=175 each): Clarithromycin‑based ≈ 90 % in both groups; Levofloxacin‑based ~ 40 %; Metronidazole‑based ~ 50 %; Furozolidone‑based 10–14 %.
  • Patients with ≥2 prior failures: VT group 41.1 % vs. BQT 35.4 % (p = 0.07, not significant).
  • Early discontinuations (poor adherence) detailed in Table 3: VT 2 patients stopped (ages 34 M, 27 F); both had ≥80 % adherence before stop; one achieved eradication, the other failed. BQT 16 patients stopped early (various AEs), overall cure rate among them 62.5 % (10/16).
11. Clinical Implications & Recommendations
  • VT dual therapy offers a penicillin‑free, simplified rescue option especially valuable for patients with documented penicillin allergy or intolerance to amoxicillin.
  • Lower adverse event profile and higher adherence suggest improved patient comfort and potential cost savings (fewer clinic visits, less need for supportive care).
  • Findings support the broader concept that potent acid suppression + a single low‑resistance antibiotic can replace complex quadruple regimens in rescue settings.
12. Future Research Directions (Authors’ Proposals)
  • Conduct multicenter, double‑blind trials with matched placebos to eliminate reporting bias and assess generalizability across diverse geographic resistance patterns.
  • Incorporate culture‑based susceptibility testing or molecular resistance profiling to correlate individual strain resistance with eradication outcomes.
  • Evaluate narrower non‑inferiority margins (e.g., 5 %) and larger sample sizes to confirm equivalence with high statistical rigor.
  • Perform stratified analyses by number of prior failures, specific previous regimens, and severity of gastric pathology (e.g., atrophic gastritis vs. ulcer disease).
  • Assess gut microbiota composition pre‑/post‑therapy to determine ecological impact of the simplified dual regimen versus BQT.
13. Key Take‑Home Messages (What You Need to Know)
  • Background: Rescue therapy traditionally relies on multi‑drug quadruple regimens that are hard to tolerate.
  • New Finding: VT dual therapy achieved eradication rates non‑inferior to BQT (≈90 % mITT) while causing ~¾ fewer adverse events and yielding higher adherence.
  • Limitation: Open‑label, single‑center, no resistance testing → caution when extrapolating to regions with different tetracycline resistance patterns.
  • Clinical Relevance: VT dual therapy can be considered a practical alternative for patients struggling with pill burden or those with penicillin allergy; may also serve as a rescue option after amoxicillin‑based failures.

15. Microbiological & Pharmacologic Context
  • Acid suppression intensity → gastric pH ≥6 critical for tetracycline activity; vonoprazan maintains higher intragastric pH than standard PPIs, explaining superior outcomes vs. omeprazole‑tetracycline dual therapy in earlier studies.
  • Tetracycline primary resistance in China <4 % (regional data); stable even after multiple treatment failures → rationale for empiric use without susceptibility testing.

16. Author Conclusions & Clinical Recommendations
  • Fourteen‑day VT dual therapy is an effective, well‑tolerated rescue regimen for H. pylori after prior failure.
  • Eradication rates non‑inferior to standard BQT (difference ≤1.2 % across all analyses).
  • Simplified dosing substantially lowers adverse event burden (10.9 % vs. 45.7 %) and improves adherence (96 % vs. 87 %).
  • Authors suggest VT dual therapy be considered a practical, penicillin‑free alternative, especially for patients with documented amoxicillin intolerance or those unable to manage multi‑pill quadruple regimens.
