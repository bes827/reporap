Introduction to PHPT
  • - Pelvic Health Physical Therapy (PHPT) is an essential component for treating pelvic floor disorders across all three pelvic compartments.
Gastroenterology-Related Conditions Treated
  • - Constipation
  • - Fecal urgency and frequency
  • - Fecal and anal incontinence
  • - Prolapse
  • - Anorectal pain disorders

Identified Knowledge Gap
  • - Many referring providers lack a clear understanding of what PHPT entails, its evidence, and the referral process.


What is Pelvic Health Physical Therapy (PHPT)?
  • - PHPT is a specialized form of physical therapy provided by a licensed physical therapist with advanced, post-doctorate training for pelvic floor disorders.
Core Principles
  • - Addresses underlying neuromusculoskeletal causes of pelvic floor disorders.
  • - Utilizes a patient-centered, trauma-informed care approach with consent.

Key Assessment Method
  • - Primarily uses internal vaginal and/or anorectal examinations to assess pelvic floor muscles (PFM).

Multifaceted Interventions (Table 1)
  • - Thorough patient education on findings and their correlation to symptoms.
  • - Behavioral changes and habit retraining.
  • - Neuromuscular re-education / biofeedback.
  • - Manual therapy techniques (soft tissue, joint mobilization).
  • - Therapeutic exercises and an individualized home program.

Hallmark of PHPT: Assessment of PFM Control
  • - A key skill is assessing a patient's voluntary abdominal and PFM control and their ability to sense muscle movement.
Assessment Process
  • - Patient performs a PFM contraction, relaxation, and simulated defecatory push effort.
  • - The therapist's examination finger provides real-time assessment while monitoring abdominal activity.
  • - Patient receives simultaneous sensory and verbal feedback to enhance performance.
This process is known as:
  • - Biofeedback or Neuromuscular Re-education.




PHPT vs. "Biofeedback"
  • - The term "biofeedback" is often used synonymously with PFM rehabilitation, which can cause confusion.
Biofeedback: Definition & Goal
  • - Definition: A conditioning treatment that converts typically unknown bodily process information into simple visual, auditory, or tactile cues.
  • - Purpose: To bring physiological responses to a conscious level, empowering the individual to modify them.
Goal in Defecatory Disorders
  • - To create effective and synergistic coordination of the abdomen, anorectum, and PFM.
  • - To enhance anorectal sensory perception to promote defecation, continence, or reduce pain.


Biofeedback Approaches (Table 4)
  • - Biofeedback can be delivered through instrumented or non-instrumented methods.
Non-Instrumented
  • - Digital Rectal Exam (DRE): A skilled examiner provides simultaneous verbal and tactile feedback.
  • - Self-tactile cues: Patient's hands on abdomen/anus to monitor motion.
  • - Mirror visualization: To observe perineal movement.

Instrumented
  • - sEMG: Surface electromyography using internal or external sensors.
  • - Rectal balloon catheter: For sensation and/or expulsion training.
  • - Manometry equipment: Intra-anal pressure sensors.


The Role of Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)
  • - A skilled DRE is the cornerstone of a pelvic health physical therapist’s practice.
  • - It is an efficient and effective biofeedback technique, often trusted more by therapists than equipment.
DRE Assessment & Biofeedback Example (Table 2)
  • - Assessment: Patient performs contraction, relaxation, and push efforts while the therapist palpates anorectal and abdominal muscles.
  • - Biofeedback: If PFM contracts instead of opening during a push, the therapist uses verbal/tactile cues (e.g., changing breath, hip position) to help the patient achieve the correct coordination, which both parties can feel in real-time.
Common Dysfunctions Identified by DRE
  • - Contraction: Bearing down instead of squeezing.
  • - Relaxation: Muscles fail to return to baseline.
  • - Defecatory Push: PFM and anus contract/lift instead of opening/loosening.



Evidence & Consensus on Biofeedback
  • - For adults with dyssynergic defecation, there is Level 1 Grade A evidence for biofeedback.
  • - There is currently no standard device or treatment protocol for biofeedback use.
ASCRS 2023 Consensus
  • - Biofeedback with a trained physical therapist is considered best practice.
  • - The choice between instrumented or non-instrumented biofeedback should be based on the patient's clinical presentation.

Limitations of Home Devices
  • - Most available home devices have been evaluated for intra-vaginal use for urinary incontinence, not for the coordinated lengthening/relaxation needed for defecatory dysfunction.


Distinguishing PHPT from Traditional "Biofeedback" (Table 3)
  • - PHPT is a comprehensive, individualized treatment plan, whereas "biofeedback" is often narrowly interpreted as a scripted, sEMG-only protocol.
PHPT is a Broader Approach
  • - Biofeedback/neuromuscular re-education is just one component of PHPT.
  • - PHPT also includes manual therapies, behavioral retraining, and therapeutic exercises.
  • - Emerging evidence suggests these multimodal plans are more effective than sEMG only.


Ultimate Goal & A Key Pitfall
  • - Goal: Consistent independent performance and awareness of muscle control without the need for external biofeedback confirmation.
  • - Pitfall: Prolonged biofeedback use can inhibit the development of an internal reference of correct performance, impairing long-term motor learning.


Evidence for PHPT in Gastroenterology
  • - Robust evidence supports PHPT as a first-line intervention for various GI, urological, gynecological, and sexual symptoms.
Proven Efficacy
  • - Retraining the PFM to restore neuromuscular coordination has demonstrated efficacy in clinical trials for improving symptoms.
Conditions Improved by PHPT
  • - Constipation / dyssynergic defecation
  • - Fecal incontinence and urgency
  • - Incomplete evacuation
  • - Rectocele
  • - Anorectal pain



Referral to PHPT
  • - A referral is appropriate once a musculoskeletal reason for a patient's GI complaint is suspected.
Pre-Referral Testing Considerations
  • - No specific tests are mandatory prior to a PHPT referral.
  • - While guidelines suggest anorectal physiological testing is "gold standard," it is not considered essential by therapists.
Reality of Anorectal Testing
  • - Contrary to guidelines, 98% of patients with chronic constipation never undergo anorectal function testing.
  • - Testing has limitations: sensitivity, specificity, availability, and patient comfort.


Importance of a Multidisciplinary Approach
  • - A collaborative approach is integral for comprehensive treatment and best outcomes.
Example: Dyssynergic Defecation
  • - Two-thirds of patients with dyssynergic defecation also have slow transit motility.
  • - This requires simultaneous management: a prescribed bowel regimen from the physician and motor dysfunction treatment from the PT.

Other Team Members (Table 1)
  • - Gastroenterologist, Colorectal surgeon, Registered dietician, Gynecologist, Behavioral health provider, etc.


Patient Selection Criteria
  • - Success with PHPT depends on accurate patient selection.
Key Factors
  • - Cognitive State: Patient must have the cognitive processing and attention required for PHPT.
  • - Motivation: Patient's motivation to participate is vital for success.


The Referral Process (Table 5)
  • - A provider can refer a patient to PHPT, but it's important to note that all 50 states allow direct access for patients to seek PT care without a medical provider's referral.
Finding a PHPT Specialist
  • - Look for specialists with certifications like WCS, PRPC, or CAPP-Pelvic.
  • - It's important to verify the therapist can provide an internal rectal approach if appropriate.

Insurance Coverage
  • - Most insurance plans provide coverage for Physical Therapy services, which includes PHPT.



Conclusion
  • - Many bowel-related symptoms have a musculoskeletal component that can benefit from PHPT.
  • - A hallmark treatment strategy is neuromuscular re-education/biofeedback to re-establish voluntary PFM control.
  • - These conditions are best managed with a tailored and multidisciplinary approach to improve symptoms and enhance quality of life.
