Clostridioides difficile Infection (CDI) Overview
  • Most common healthcare-associated infection in US.
  • Recurrence is a major challenge.
  • Treatment goals: treat active infection + prevent recurrences.
Recurrence Rates
  • ≈25% of patients recur after standard antimicrobials.
  • Of those, ≈35% will subsequently recur.
  • Thereafter, ≈60% will recur, creating a challenging cycle.
Historical Advancements
  • Last 20 years: research focused on ↓ recurrence.
  • Approaches: highly selective antimicrobials (e.g., fidaxomicin), toxin-targeting infusions (e.g., bezlotoxumab), fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) for microbiota diversity restoration.
Key Treatment Modalities & Changes
  • Treatment options rapidly changing (2024-2025+).
  • Clinicians must understand available options + optimal usage.
Fidaxomicin
  • FDA approved: 2011; first next-gen therapeutic.
  • Mechanism: novel, highly selective therapy targeting C. difficile.
  • Benefit: limited collateral microbiome disruption → maintains colonization resistance → ↓ recurrence.
Effectiveness
  • Generational treatment w/ significantly ↓ recurrent rates vs. vancomycin.
  • Consistent superiority vs. vancomycin.
  • Metronidazole: remarkably ↓ efficacy.
Current Status & Future
  • Preferred antimicrobial for initial + recurrent CDI.
  • High costs hampered widespread usage despite guideline inclusion.
  • Expected to come off patent → anticipated ↓ costs + ↑ availability → broader patient benefit.
Regimens
  • Standard: 200 mg PO BID x 10 days.
  • EXTEND regimen: 200 mg PO BID x 5 days, then QOD for days 7–25 (total 20 doses).
Bezlotoxumab
  • FDA approved: May 2016.
  • Mechanism: infusion targeting CDI toxins, given during standard antimicrobial therapy.
  • Benefit: associated w/ ↓ rates of recurrence.
Target Population
  • Essential addition for select patients.
  • Benefit in age ≥65, immunosuppressed, or severe CDI.
  • Added importance during COVID-19 pandemic due to FMT unavailability.
Availability Change
  • Merck & Co. ceased production/marketing.
  • Removed from market: January 31, 2025.
  • Impact: creates significant gap in treatment options, especially for severely immunocompromised (FMT not recommended for this cohort).
Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT) & Microbiota-Based Therapies
  • FMT: safe + effective method to remarkably ↓ rCDI when administered following standard antimicrobial.
  • FDA approvals aimed to broaden clinician comfort + access.
FDA Approved Products
  • Fecal microbiota, live-jslm (RBL): broad consortium, enema-based formulation.
  • Fecal microbiota spores, live-brpk (VOS): narrow consortium of Firmicutes, encapsulated form.
Openbiome Changes
  • Largest US stool bank.
  • September 2024: advised voluntary cessation of distributing treatments for rCDI prevention.
  • As of 12/31/24: no longer available generally, except for severe + fulminant disease.
  • Impact: conventional FMT will largely sunset, leaving a hole in treatment for fulminant CDI.
Role in Fulminant CDI
  • No data for RBL or VOS in fulminant patient population.
  • Their role for fulminant disease is unclear.
  • Openbiome's sunsetting narrows options for sickest patients.
Current Availability
  • RBL + VOS: excellent options to prevent rCDI for other cohorts.
Approach to CDI Treatment in Evolving Landscape
  • Need to understand best approach for patients given changing options.
  • Adapt to evolving options.
First-Line Therapy
  • If fidaxomicin costs significantly reduced (post-patent expiration):
  • Should be first-line option for both initial + recurrent disease, as per guidelines.
Add-on Therapies (Post-Antimicrobial)
  • Losing bezlotoxumab is challenging.
  • FDA-approved donor-derived microbiota therapies (RBL, VOS) are available.
Immunocompetent / Mildly Immunocompromised
  • Patients at greatest risk for future recurrence (e.g., multiply recurrent CDI, concomitant IBD).
  • Previously considered for bezlotoxumab.
  • Now: should receive one of the FDA-approved microbiota-based therapeutics (RBL or VOS).
Severely Immunocompromised
  • Not eligible for FDA-approved donor-derived microbiota therapies.
  • Need more creative antimicrobial usage or novel approaches.
Vancomycin Tapers with Pulses
  • Example: 125 mg PO QID x 14 days, then 125 mg PO BID x 7 days, then 125 mg PO QD x 7 days, then 125 mg PO QOD x 8 days, then 125 mg PO Q3D x 15 days (total 86 doses).
Fidaxomicin EXTEND Regimen
  • 200 mg PO BID x 5 days, followed by 1 tablet every other day for days 7–25.
Severe or Fulminant Disease (Post-Openbiome Sunset)
  • Consider usage of unapproved FMT or RBL administration through colonoscopy.
  • No data for RBL/VOS in this population; VOS (capsule) cannot be administered via colonoscopy.
Fischer's Protocol (for RBL via colonoscopy)
  • Administer every other to every third day until pseudomembranes disappear.
  • Consider repeat RBL or unapproved FMT treatment several weeks after hospital discharge.
Guidelines & Practical Considerations
  • All should consider following American Gastroenterological Association Guidelines.
  • Become more comfortable using RBL + VOS, even as early as first recurrence in high-risk patients.
  • Insurance challenges remain + will likely continue to evolve.
