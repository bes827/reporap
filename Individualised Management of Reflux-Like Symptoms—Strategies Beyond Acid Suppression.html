Individualised Management of Reflux-Like Symptoms—Strategies Beyond Acid Suppression
  • Review article published in Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics, 2025.
  • Authors: Peter J. Kahrilas, Laurie Keefer, Rena Yadlapati, et al.
  • Addresses the complexities of reflux-like symptom pathophysiology and management strategies beyond acid reflux.
Introduction
  • Reflux-like symptoms are frequently treated with PPIs, often long-term & at high doses.
  • Symptoms are often conflated with reflux oesophagitis, assuming similar responsiveness to acid suppression.
  • However, many patients report persistent symptoms despite optimized PPI therapy.
Problem
  • Differential clinical responsiveness between reflux oesophagitis and persistent reflux-like symptoms suggests differing pathophysiology.
  • This calls into question the shared management strategy.
Aim
  • Examine persistently symptomatic patients with non-erosive disease from a primary care/generalist perspective.
  • Consider both acid and non-acid targeted interventions based on individual clinical presentation and circumstances.
Methods
  • Panel of ten expert clinicians (primary care, gastroenterology, psychology) held online meetings.
  • Shared perspectives on pathophysiology and management of reflux-like symptoms when PPIs are ineffective or provide partial relief.
  • Focus: Enhance care through personalized strategies from a primary care/generalist perspective.
Process
  • Key themes for exploration: underlying contributors of reflux-like symptoms/non-acid-mediated symptoms & alternative (non-acid suppressive) management strategies.
  • Members reviewed topics, presented findings, and identified content gaps.
  • Output: Examines complexities of reflux-like symptom pathophysiology and management strategies.
Pathophysiology
  • Understanding the distinct pathophysiologies is crucial for effective management.
Reflux Oesophagitis
  • Characterized by endoscopically visible mucosal breaks in the distal oesophagus (Los Angeles Classification: mild A, B; severe C, D).
  • Pathophysiology: Incompetence of anti-reflux barrier @ oesophagogastric junction -> hypotensive LES, hiatal hernia -> excessive acid reflux & poor oesophageal clearance.
  • Results in increased distal oesophageal acid exposure -> inflammation & mucosal erosions.
Treatment & Management
  • Can almost uniformly be healed with potent antisecretory therapy (PPIs or PCABs).
  • Healing rates parallel antisecretory potency.
  • Irreversible physiological defects (esp. severe oesophagitis) mandate maintenance antisecretory therapy or alternative strategies (weight loss, surgery).
'Typical' Reflux-Like Symptoms
  • Montreal Consensus: Typical oesophageal symptoms = heartburn, chest pain, regurgitation.
  • Most individuals w/ typical symptoms do NOT have reflux (erosive) oesophagitis -> NERD (non-erosive reflux disease).
  • Physiological testing (pH-metry or pH-impedance) reveals heterogeneity.
NERD Subtypes
  • True NERD: Abnormal oesophageal acid exposure.
  • Reflux hypersensitivity: Normal oesophageal acid exposure, significant correlation between symptoms and reflux events.
  • Functional heartburn: Normal oesophageal acid exposure, no significant correlation between symptoms and reflux events.
Problems with Classifications
  • Poor correlation between defining symptoms and reflux events; most reflux events are asymptomatic, most symptoms not temporally linked to reflux.
  • Disconnect between patient-reported symptom severity and physiological abnormality (oesophageal pH-metry).
Explanation
  • Complex process of oesophageal event perception depends on peripheral and central neurologic processes.
  • Psychological stressors, cognitive-affective factors, and aberrant coping behaviors are important modulators of symptom experience.
  • Neural plasticity leads to hypersensitivity (perception of stimuli not normally perceived).
Gut Microbiome Impact
  • Gut microbiome (shaped by environment, life experiences, social factors) may impact visceral sensitivity and CNS processing.
Oesophageal Hypervigilance and Anxiety Scale (EHAS)
  • Quantifies oesophagus-focused anxiety and symptom vigilance.
  • 15-item questionnaire, 5-point Likert scale (0-60).
  • EHAS score correlates with symptom severity (GERDQ) regardless of NERD subtype (true NERD, reflux hypersensitivity, functional heartburn).
  • Shorter 7-item version (EHAS-7) performs as well.
Study Example
  • Study of 105 patients w/ reflux-like symptoms (no oesophagitis): EHAS score correlated w/ symptom severity, regardless of classification by pH-impedance testing.
Psychosocial Factors
  • Detailed evaluation of 393 'PPI-refractory' patients w/ pH-impedance monitoring & psychometric testing.
  • Machine-learning model (88% variance in symptom severity) showed psychological variables were most important.
  • Top variables: depressive symptoms (0.210), illness behaviour (0.165), PTSD symptoms (0.128), GI-specific anxiety (0.117), pain catastrophizing (0.114).
  • Reflux-related variables had lower predictive worth (total 0.109).
Psychosocial Impact Study
  • Study of 123 patients w/ abnormal acid exposure & 116 w/ normal acid exposure but positive symptom correlation.
  • Oesophageal hypervigilance was persistent across patients, irrespective of pH-metry results, and significantly predicted symptom severity.
  • Psychosocial factors are important across the entire spectrum of GERD, including reflux oesophagitis.
Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction (DGBIs)
  • Psychological comorbidities and visceral hypersensitivity play a dominant role in symptom perception.
  • DGBIs (functional heartburn, dyspepsia, IBS) commonly overlap and coexist with somatic symptoms (headache, backache, insomnia, fibromyalgia, dizziness).
  • UK study: DGBIs affect 1 in 3 adults, sparsely recognised/taught in medical education.
Patient Adaptation
  • Patient's adaptation responses influence symptom experience.
  • Rationalizing symptoms -> better coping.
  • Flight-or-fight, fear, stress response -> impaired QoL.
  • Perception of symptoms as threatening -> avoidance behavior -> reinforcement of fear & restricted life activities.
Oesophageal Hypervigilance
  • Behavioral reaction: increased attention to oesophageal symptoms & settings -> 'expect' symptoms &/or engage in maladaptive coping behaviors to avoid symptoms.
'Atypical' Reflux Symptoms
  • Proposed 'atypical' or extra-oesophageal GERD syndromes: laryngitis, pharyngitis, post-nasal drip, otitis, asthma.
  • Patients rarely have reflux oesophagitis on endoscopy -> establishing causative role of reflux is problematic.
  • Oesophageal pH-metry or pH-impedance metry are mainstays of evaluation, but interpretation lacks consensus.
Challenges
  • Examples of reflux leading to extra-oesophageal syndromes exist, but establishing reflux as a dominant contributor in clinical setting remains problematic.
  • Psychosocial factors on symptom burden have received inadequate attention.
  • Laryngeal symptoms study: elevated hypervigilance & anxiety (EHAS) in those w/ & w/o abnormal pH-metry.
Heterogeneity
  • Substantial heterogeneity exists, with some phenotypes driven by abnormal reflux physiology and some by hypersensitivity and behavioural factors.
General Principles
  • Uncommon for reflux to be dominant cause of extra-oesophageal syndromes in patients without typical oesophageal symptoms.
  • Even when reflux is involved, it is usually one of multiple contributors and often not the dominant one.
  • Diagnostics (pH-metry or pH-impedance metry) are more helpful in ruling out GERD than ruling it in.
  • Strong tendency to over-treat these patients with PPIs.
Overlapping Gastrointestinal Symptoms
  • Dyspeptic symptoms frequently overlap with reflux-like symptoms and are often present in PPI 'non-responders'.
  • Functional heartburn has more in common with functional dyspepsia than reflux disease (more postprandial fullness, bloating, early satiety, nausea).
  • IBS overlaps more frequently with functional heartburn than true NERD and reflux hypersensitivity.
Clinical Implications
  • Question patients to differentiate between dyspeptic and reflux-like symptoms.
  • Avoid medical jargon; use easy-to-understand tools (pictograms) to simplify communication.
  • H. pylori testing is important in dyspepsia management but generally unnecessary in patients with only typical reflux-like symptoms.
Potential Lifestyle Contributors to Reflux-Like Symptoms
  • Lifestyle factors can significantly influence reflux symptoms.
Dietary Habits
  • Poor dietary habits (eating past satiety, eating close to bedtime) are associated with gastro-oesophageal reflux.
  • Large meals cause gastric distention, promote acid secretion, trigger transient LES relaxations, and make reflux from the postprandial acid pocket more likely.
  • Dietary triggers vary widely among patients.
Common Dietary Triggers
  • Coffee, alcohol, chocolate, diets rich in fat or carbohydrate, fried foods, highly processed foods (high sugar, salt, saturated fats).
  • Carbonated beverages increase heartburn risk during sleep.
Weight Gain, Obesity and Hiatal Hernia
  • Reflux-like symptoms increase progressively with BMI increases.
  • Obesity is an important risk factor for reflux complications (oesophagitis, Barrett's, adenocarcinoma).
  • Hiatal hernia and increased intra-abdominal pressure are important physiological risk factors for reflux and are linked to obesity.
Hiatal Hernia
  • Spectrum of disruption of the diaphragmatic hiatus culminating in hiatal hernia is age-related and is the likely dominant pathophysiological factor linking advancing age to reflux.
  • Excessive abdominal straining (weight training) can also cause hiatal hernia.
Individualised Management Strategies
  • Personalized strategies are key to effective management.
Expectation Setting and Counselling
  • Reflux-like symptoms have a multifactorial pathophysiology and wax and wane over time.
  • Decision to continue PPI long-term will be individualised, based on symptomatic benefit and patient preference.
  • Management should be patient-centred, reflective of physiological, behavioural and dietary factors.
Addressing Misconceptions
  • Complex pathophysiology is often oversimplified as excessive acid exposure.
  • 'PPI failure' may be more a problem of unrealistic expectations than PPI efficacy.
  • Over-the-counter products and direct-to-consumer advertising can boost expectations.
Patient Fears
  • Unmet expectations can generate fear about alternative reasons for refractory symptoms (cancer, heart disease).
Clinical Approach
  • Identify specific contributing factors: understand patient's circumstances, perceptions, symptom type, severity, comorbidities, treatment history, alarm features.
  • Counsel patients on multi-factorial aetiology, including the gut-brain connection.
  • Explain that a single intervention is unlikely to be a perfect solution.
Gut-Brain Axis Explanation
  • Explain the gut-brain axis in patient-friendly language as a two-way communication pathway.
  • Certain thoughts, feelings, and behaviours can increase the perception of and response to symptoms in the GI tract.
  • Reassure patients that symptoms are not 'all in the mind'.
Treatment Aims
  • Clinicians should reassure patients that the sensory pathways that are triggering symptoms can be modified.
  • Treatment aims to achieve steady and continued improvement rather than immediate resolution.
Doctor-Patient Relationship
  • Key to holistic management based on patient experience.
  • Active listening, compassion, rapport building to elicit information (life stressors, anxiety, trauma).
  • PRO tools (EHAS, GERDQ, GIS, QOLRAD) can make consultations more interactive, effective, and efficient.
Reassurance
  • Reassure patients that the risk of developing oesophageal cancer is extremely low.
  • UK National Endoscopy Database: low diagnostic yield from gastroscopy for symptom evaluation (0.2-0.3% positive predictive value for cancer in reflux/dyspepsia).
  • Investigation often required for reassurance.
Diet and Lifestyle Changes
  • Key element of care for all patients with reflux-like symptoms.
  • Rigorous evidence from RCTs is scarce, but the concept that dietary and lifestyle changes can lower the risk of reflux-related symptoms is well established.
  • Relevant contributors are highly individualised.
Recommendations
  • Avoid high-fat foods and potential dietary triggers (citrus, tomatoes, spiced/fried foods, chocolate, alcohol, coffee, carbonated drinks), overeating, and recumbency after meals.
  • Simple strategies: smaller plates, cut-off time for last meal (3-h interval before bedtime), raising head of bed.
Mindful Eating
  • Lessons from Japanese practice of 'mindful eating': heightened awareness of satiety cues by paying attention to flavours, textures, and chewing.
  • Pre-meal mantra 'hara hachi bu' or “eat until you are 80% full”.
Weight Management
  • Recommended for overweight/obese patients and those with recent weight gain.
  • Weight loss reduces symptoms dose-dependently and increases the chance of treatment success with acid-reducing/neutralising medicines.
Comparative Study
  • Symptomatic overweight patients with reflux oesophagitis: PPI + tailored dietetic/aerobic exercise program -> greater symptom improvement & >50% able to discontinue PPI vs. no patients on PPI alone.
Interventions
  • Specific weight management intervention will depend on local services.
  • GLP-1RA may be available to support weight loss as an adjunct to calorie-restricted diet and exercise.
  • Specific clinical data for these drugs for overweight patients with reflux-like symptoms is not yet available.
GLP-1RA Risk
  • Some short-acting GLP-1RAs may increase the risk of oesophagitis and Barrett's oesophagus (with and without dysplasia) in patients with type 2 diabetes.
Atypical (Laryngopharyngeal) Symptoms
  • PPI treatment is a common approach, but evidence from RCTs demonstrates little, if any, benefit over placebo.
  • Disconnect between objective findings and symptom severity.
Anti-Reflux Surgery Study
  • Patients with 'PPI-refractory' laryngopharyngeal symptoms undergoing anti-reflux surgery: laryngeal signs improved in 80%, but only 10% showed symptomatic improvement.
Paradigm Shift
  • Moving toward a phenotype-driven, personalised approach where the role of psychosocial factors on symptom perception and burden is acknowledged.
  • Vehicle of chronicity is laryngeal hypersensitivity amplified by anxiety, hypervigilance, and learned detrimental behaviours (throat clearing, chronic cough).
Laryngeal Cognitive-Affective Tool (LCAT)
  • 15-item questionnaire validated for measurement of laryngeal hypervigilance and symptom-specific anxiety in patients with chronic laryngeal symptoms.
Psychological Interventions
  • Brain-gut behaviour therapies (BGBT) may be beneficial in patients who identify a link between stress and symptoms, those with difficulty coping, and those who exhibit avoidance behaviours.
  • BGBTs reduce both reflux symptom severity and psychological symptoms (GI-specific anxiety) -> improved QoL.
  • EHAS tool can gauge levels of symptom-associated anxiety and hypervigilance.
Candidate Selection
  • Good candidates: accept and show interest in the gut-brain connection, acknowledge that coping could be improved, and are willing to invest time in behaviour change.
  • BGBT techniques may be combined with pharmacological approaches (tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs/SNRIs).
BGBT Techniques
  • Nurse-led GI self-management skills training, GI-focused CBT, gut-directed hypnotherapy, mindfulness-based interventions.
  • Oesophageal-directed hypnotherapy study: significant improvement in heartburn impact (QOLRAD, p = 0.01), visceral anxiety (p = 0.01), and emotional QoL (p = 0.05).
Individualized Therapy
  • Combination of techniques can be used to individualise therapy to a patient's specific needs or therapeutic targets (avoidance behaviour, visceral hypersensitivity, trait anxiety).
  • Primary care is ideally placed to implement nurse-led self-management skills training.
  • Stress management and relaxation techniques may be key components of self-management programmes.
Atypical Symptoms
  • BGBT may also be relevant for patients with chronic atypical symptoms who have developed laryngeal-specific anxiety and hypervigilance.
  • Laryngeal recalibration therapy (cognitive behavioural element): 85% symptomatic response, whether or not they had proven underlying reflux disease.
Diaphragmatic Breathing
  • May be effective for reducing reflux-like symptoms.
  • Activates parasympathetic nervous system, increases LES pressure and crural diaphragm tension, and may be especially useful for patients with regurgitation.
  • Can also help relaxation by increasing vagal tone, which is disrupted in DGBIs.
On-Demand Symptom Control Measures
  • Growing resistance to maintenance PPI therapy has led to PPI deprescribing initiatives.
  • Alternative to continuous maintenance treatment: on-demand symptom control strategy.
  • Antacid or alginate-antacid products are useful for postprandial acid reflux and regurgitation and for those with a hiatal hernia.
Alginate-Antacid Mechanism
  • Forms a viscous pH-neutral raft on contact with the postprandial acid pocket.
  • Reduces the proximal migration of acid reflux episodes and displaces the acid pocket below the diaphragm in patients with a hiatal hernia.
  • Meta-analyses show efficacy for symptom control versus placebo and non-inferiority to some PPIs.
Mucosal Protection
  • Alginates and other bioadhesive treatments that coat the oesophagus may reduce symptoms through protecting the mucosa.
  • Hyaluronic acid–chondroitin sulphate-based bioadhesive formulation has demonstrated superior reduction in reflux-like symptoms compared with placebo in patients with NERD.
Adjunct Therapy
  • On-demand therapy can also be useful as an alternative to dose escalation in patients being maintained on acid suppressive therapy.
  • Topical mucosal preparations containing alginate or protective bioadhesive agents added to a PPI in patients with residual symptoms show benefit.
  • Alginate preparation added to a PPI significantly reduced the number of nights with reflux-like symptoms compared with a PPI alone.
Summary
  • PPIs are extremely effective for healing reflux oesophagitis, but less so for resolving persistent reflux-like symptoms and even less for atypical reflux symptoms.
  • Discrepancy is related to differences in pathophysiological determinants.
  • Anatomical/physiological degradation of the anti-reflux barrier dominates in reflux oesophagitis; cognitive-affective and behavioural processes increase symptom burden.
Key Factors
  • Hypersensitivity, hypervigilance, and anxiety are important contributors that correlate closely with symptom severity and persistence.
  • Management strategies should be individualised, beginning with expectation setting and counselling.
Effective Management
  • Utilise behaviour modification (diet/lifestyle changes, weight control), education regarding brain-gut interactions, psychological interventions, and on-demand symptom control measures as either a substitute or adjunct.
  • Acid suppression is just one of several interventions that can be utilised as part of an individualised approach to care.
