Tom W's Specialty: An Experiment on Base Rates vs. Representativeness
  • - An experiment designed to pit statistical base rates against stereotypes (representativeness).
Task 1: Judging by Base Rate
  • - Participants are asked to rank 9 fields of graduate study by the likelihood of a random student, Tom W, being in them.
  • - With no specific information, the correct approach is to use base rates ---> the number of students enrolled in each field.
  • - Most people correctly rank fields like humanities and education as high likelihood due to larger enrollment.

Task 2: Judging by Similarity (Representativeness)
  • - A personality sketch of Tom W is provided, designed to fit a stereotype.
  • - Sketch Details: High intelligence, lacks creativity, needs order, dull writing, corny puns, sci-fi imagination, little sympathy for others, deep moral sense.
  • - Participants are asked to rank fields based on how similar Tom W is to the typical student in that field.
Typical Ranking by Similarity
  • - 1. Computer Science (due to 'nerdiness' hints like 'corny puns').
  • - 2. Engineering (due to 'neat and tidy systems').
  • - 9. Social Science and Social Work (due to 'little feel and little sympathy for other people').
  • - This task activates System 1's automatic association with stereotypes.


Task 3: The Critical Prediction
  • - Psychology graduate students, who know about base rates and were told the sketch has uncertain validity, were asked to predict Tom W's actual field.
  • - The critical error: They ignored the base rates and the low quality of evidence.
The Result: Representativeness Wins
  • - The students' probability rankings were identical to the similarity rankings.
  • - They substituted the difficult question (probability) with an easier one (similarity).
  • - This is a serious mistake because judgments of similarity and probability follow different logical rules.
  • - Even a sophisticated statistician, Robyn Dawes, intuitively fell for it, guessing computer scientist.



The Representativeness Heuristic
  • - A mental shortcut where we judge the probability of something by how similar it is to our stereotype or prototype.
  • - It involves System 1 generating a quick impression of similarity, often ignoring other relevant data.
  • - Example: Judging a candidate's leadership by the shape of their chin.
Virtues of Representativeness
  • - Intuitive impressions are often more accurate than chance.
  • - Examples:
  • - - People who act friendly are usually friendly.
  • - - A very tall, thin athlete is more likely to play basketball than football.

Sins of Representativeness (Cognitive Biases)
  • - Exclusive reliance on this heuristic leads to grave errors in statistical logic.
Sin 1: Excessive Willingness to Predict Unlikely Events
  • - People neglect low base-rate information when presented with a representative story.
  • - Example: Guessing a shy poetry lover studies Chinese literature instead of business administration, even though there are far more business students.
Overcoming Base-Rate Neglect
  • - Base rates are used correctly when there is no other information.
  • - They are often ignored when specific, individual information is available.
  • - Activation of System 2 can improve accuracy.
Experimental Findings
  • - Instructing people to 'think like a statistician' enhances base-rate use.
  • - Instructing them to 'think like a clinician' has the opposite effect.
  • - Forcing a frown (which increases System 2 vigilance) led to some sensitivity to the base rates in the Tom W problem.



Sin 2: Insensitivity to the Quality of Evidence
  • - System 1's rule is WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is).
  • - It processes available information as if it were true, even if you're told it's unreliable.
  • - In the Tom W problem, participants trusted the 'uncertain' personality sketch over the hard statistical base rates.
How to Discipline Intuition
  • - When evidence quality is low, your judgment of probability should stay close to the base rate.
  • - This requires significant effort and self-control from System 2.




Bayesian Reasoning: The Disciplined Approach
  • - A logical framework for how to change your mind in light of evidence.
  • - It specifies how to combine prior beliefs (base rates) with the diagnosticity of evidence.
Two Key Principles
  • - 1. Base rates matter, even when you have case-specific evidence.
  • - 2. Intuitive impressions of evidence are often exaggerated. The combination of WYSIATI and associative coherence makes us believe our own stories too much.

Practical Steps
  • - Anchor your judgment on a plausible base rate.
  • - Question the true diagnosticity (informativeness) of your evidence.
Bayesian Example: Tom W
  • - Base Rate: 3% of grad students are in computer science.
  • - Evidence: Description is 4 times more likely for a computer scientist.
  • - Bayes's rule conclusion: The probability Tom W is a computer scientist is now 11% (not the intuitive 80-90%).



Linda: The Conjunction Fallacy
  • - An experiment designed to provide conclusive evidence of heuristics and their incompatibility with logic.
The Setup
  • - A description of Linda is given: 31, single, outspoken, bright, philosophy major, concerned with social justice, anti-nuclear activist.
  • - This description creates a strong stereotype of a feminist.
  • - Participants are asked to rank 8 scenarios by probability.

The Twist: Logic vs. Representativeness
  • - The list includes two critical items:
  • - - 'Linda is a bank teller.'
  • - - 'Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement.'
  • - Logic: The set of 'feminist bank tellers' is a subset of 'bank tellers'. Therefore, P(Feminist Bank Teller) â‰¤ P(Bank Teller).
  • - Representativeness: The 'feminist bank teller' story is more coherent and plausible given Linda's description.
The Result: Representativeness Wins Again
  • - In multiple experiments, a vast majority ranked 'feminist bank teller' as more probable than 'bank teller'.
  • - This error is called the Conjunction Fallacy: judging a conjunction of two events to be more probable than one of the events.
  • - Undergraduates: 89% violated the logic.
  • - Doctoral students in decision science: 85% also violated the logic.

Plausibility vs. Probability
  • - The most coherent stories are not the most probable, but they are plausible.
  • - People easily confuse plausibility, coherence, and probability.
  • - Trap for forecasters: Adding detail to scenarios makes them more persuasive but less likely to come true.



Less Is More: Averaging vs. Adding
  • - A pattern where removing items from a set can increase its perceived value, because System 1 tends to average instead of add.
Experiment 1: Dinnerware Sets
  • - Set A: 40 pieces, including 9 broken ones.
  • - Set B: 24 pieces, all intact.
  • - Joint Evaluation (seeing both sets): Set A is valued higher ($32) than Set B ($30), because more items have value (logic rules).
  • - Single Evaluation (seeing only one set): Set B is valued much higher ($33) than Set A ($23), because the broken dishes in Set A lower its average value (intuition rules).

Experiment 2: Die Rolls
  • - A die has 4 green (G) and 2 red (R) faces. Which sequence is more likely to appear in 20 rolls?
  • - 1. RGRRR (unrepresentative)
  • - 2. GRGRRR (more representative)
  • - 3. GRRRRR
  • - The Logic: Sequence 2 contains Sequence 1, so it must be less likely.
  • - The Result: Almost two-thirds of respondents bet on Sequence 2 because it was more representative of the die.

Reducing the Conjunction Fallacy
  • - The fallacy is reduced when the question is phrased in terms of frequencies instead of percentages.
  • - 'How many of 100 people...' is easier than 'What percentage...'.
  • - This phrasing helps people visualize that one group is a subset of another.
  • - Error rate dropped from 65% (percentage) to 25% (frequency).


Causes Trump Statistics
  • - The mind is hungry for causal stories and treats different types of base rates differently.
The Cab Accident Problem
  • - A witness identifies a cab in a hit-and-run as Blue. Witness is 80% reliable.
Version 1: Statistical Base Rate
  • - Data: 85% of cabs in the city are Green, 15% are Blue.
  • - This is a statistical fact about the city, not causally linked to the accident.
  • - Result: People tend to ignore this base rate and focus only on the witness testimony.

Version 2: Causal Base Rate
  • - Data: The two companies have equal cabs, but Green cabs are involved in 85% of accidents.
  • - This creates a causal story: Green drivers are reckless.
  • - Result: People give this base rate considerable weight. Their average judgment is close to the Bayesian solution (the probability of the cab being Blue is 41%).


Types of Base Rates
  • - Statistical Base Rates: Facts about a population. Generally underweighted or ignored when specific information is available.
  • - Causal Base Rates: Change your view of how the case came to be. Treated as information about the individual and easily combined with other facts.
Stereotypes as Causal Base Rates
  • - Stereotypes are representations of categories as norms or prototypes.
  • - They are a basic function of System 1.
  • - In the cab problem, stereotyping Green drivers as reckless improves judgment accuracy.
  • - However, in sensitive social contexts, we reject causal base rates to create a more equal society, even if it leads to suboptimal judgments.



Teaching Psychology: The 'Helping Experiment'
  • - An experiment demonstrating that people often fail to draw correct inferences from statistical base rates, especially when those inferences conflict with their beliefs about themselves.
The Experiment Setup
  • - Participants in booths hear a confederate have a (fake) seizure and ask for help.
  • - They know others are also listening.
  • - The Surprising Result: Only four of the fifteen participants responded immediately. Most did not help at all or helped very late.
  • - The Lesson: The presence of others diffuses the sense of personal responsibility.

The Failure to Learn from Statistics
  • - Nisbett and Borgida tested if students learned this lesson.
  • - One group was told the procedure and the statistical results. Another group was not.
  • - Both groups were shown videos of two (uninformative) participants and asked to predict their behavior.
  • - Result: Knowing the statistics had no effect. Both groups predicted the individuals would help, ignoring the base rate of unhelpfulness. They 'quietly exempted themselves' from the conclusion.

The Power of Surprising Individual Cases
  • - A new experiment was run to find a better teaching method.
  • - Students were shown the videos first and told that these two nice individuals did not help.
  • - They were then asked to guess the overall statistics of the experiment.
  • - Result: Their guesses were extremely accurate. They learned the general principle from the surprising particular cases.
Key Conclusion
  • - People are unwilling to deduce the particular from the general (e.g., apply statistics to an individual).
  • - People are very willing to infer the general from the particular (e.g., create a general rule from one or two surprising examples).
  • - The test of learning is a change in your understanding of situations, not just learning a new fact.
