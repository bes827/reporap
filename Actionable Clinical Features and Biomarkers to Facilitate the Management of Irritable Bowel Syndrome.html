Paradigm Shift in IBS Management
  • - Current care for Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is often a 'trial and error' approach based on symptom subtypes (IBS-C, IBS-D, etc.).
  • - There is a major clinical need for a personalized medicine approach that targets specific underlying pathophysiological mechanisms.
Limitations of Rome IV Criteria
  • - The Rome IV criteria are excellent for making a positive diagnosis of IBS and reducing unnecessary testing.
  • - However, they do not identify the specific mechanisms operating in an individual patient.


Clinical Traits as Actionable Biomarkers
  • - Clinicians can identify distinct clinical phenotypes within the IBS population that go beyond the traditional Rome-based classifications.
  • - Recognizing these traits can guide initial therapy choices. (See Figure 1)
ðŸ§  Psychological Comorbidity
  • - A significant phenotypic biomarker in IBS, heavily associated with care-seeking and treatment outcomes.
  • - Latent class analysis identified stable clusters of IBS patients distinguished by psychological burden.
Treatment Implications
  • - Early identification of patients with significant psychological comorbidity should prompt a different initial care plan.
  • - Offer brain-gut behavior therapies and/or centrally-acting neuromodulators early, not just after other therapies fail.
  • - Refer to mental health specialists for specific conditions like eating disorders, severe anxiety, PTSD, or OCD.
Neuromodulator Selection (Table 1)
  • - The optimal choice should consider the agent's peripheral effects on gut function.
  • - Example: Mirtazapine may be useful in those with prominent dyspepsia and/or nausea.



âš¡ Autonomic & Neurologic Burden
  • - Unique clusters of IBS patients exist with increased neurological burden, including migraines and autonomic dysfunction.
  • - A common manifestation is Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS).
POTS Profile
  • - Definition: Chronic orthostatic intolerance with excessive tachycardia without hypotension.
  • - Symptoms: Orthostatic (blurred vision, lightheadedness, syncope) and non-orthostatic (urinary retention, dyspepsia, nausea).
  • - Classic Patient: Typically a young female with overlapping fibromyalgia, migraines, and/or chronic fatigue syndrome.

Treatment Strategies for Autonomic Dysfunction (Table 1)
  • - Non-pharmacologic: Physical conditioning, exercise, increased fluid (2L/day) and salt (10g/day) intake.
  • - Pharmacologic: Pyridostigmine can be a useful addition to the gastroenterologist's arsenal.
Pyridostigmine Details
  • - Improves non-GI POTS symptoms while also improving GI symptoms like constipation.
  • - Dose: up to 60 mg PO TID.



ðŸ§¬ Hypermobility, POTS & Mast Cell Activation ('The Triad')
  • - Hypermobility Spectrum Disorders (HSD) are highly associated with IBS.
  • - HSD are non-inflammatory connective tissue disorders from abnormal collagen synthesis, clinically assessed using the Beighton score.
  • - Nearly one third of patients in GI clinics have undiagnosed HSD.
The 'Triad' as a Biomarker
  • - A recognizable subtype of IBS patient is characterized by a triad of clinical traits: hEDS (hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos), POTS, and Mast Cell Activation.
  • - These patients often have rashes, asthma-like symptoms, and a history of intolerance to many medications and foods.

Mast Cell Activation & Treatment (Table 1)
  • - Mast cell activation in the gut is a key pathophysiological mechanism in this subgroup.
Diagnostic Assays
  • - Serum: Tryptase.
  • - Urine: Histamine, leukotriene C4, and prostaglandin D2.

Treatment Strategies
  • - Histaminergic Blockade: A non-sedating H1-receptor antagonist (e.g., cetirizine) AND an H2-receptor antagonist (e.g., famotidine).
  • - Leukotriene-receptor Antagonists: e.g., montelukast sodium.
  • - Mast Cell Stabilizers: Gut-selective agents like oral cromolyn (100-200 mg PO TID with meals and at bedtime).




Physiological Biomarkers
  • - Objective tests can help identify underlying mechanisms and guide more targeted therapies.
ðŸ§ª Bile Acid Malabsorption (BAM)
  • - Occurs in a quarter to a third (25-33%) of patients with DGBIs and diarrhea.
  • - Excess colonic bile acids increase colonic motility and secretion.
  • - Normally, ~95% of bile acids are reabsorbed in the terminal ileum.
Testing for BAM (Table 1)
  • - Stool: Elevated total and primary bile acids in a 48-hour collection.
  • - Combined Serum/Stool: Fasting serum C4 level combined with % primary bile acids from a single stool sample.
  • - Imaging: 75SeHCAT retention test (not available in the US).

Treatment & Response
  • - Testing for BAM predicts benefit from bile acid sequestrants (e.g., cholestyramine, colestipol, colesevelam).
  • - Patients with elevated stool bile acids have a clinical response rate of ~70%.
  • - Emerging therapy: GLP-1 agonists (e.g., liraglutide) may be useful, especially in obese patients with BAM.

  • - Focuses on the role of diet and gut microbiome interactions in generating IBS symptoms.
FODMAPs
  • - Diets low in Fermentable Oligo-, Di-, Mono-saccharides And Polyols are an established treatment.
  • - Effective for many patients, regardless of IBS subtype.
  • - Evidence is mixed on whether any clinical feature can predict responsiveness.

Sucrase-Isomaltase Deficiency (SID)
  • - Affects ~5%-10% of IBS patients, typically with IBS-D.
  • - Deficiency in brush border enzymes impedes digestion of disaccharides and starches, leading to laxative effects and fermentation.
Testing for SID (Table 1)
  • - Invasive: Disaccharidase enzymatic assays or immunostaining of duodenal biopsies.
  • - Non-invasive: Breath testing using a sucrose substrate.

Treatment for SID (Table 1)
  • - Reduced dietary sucrose intake.
  • - Sacrosidase enzyme replacement therapy.


SIBO & IMO
  • - Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) and Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO) are linked to IBS subtypes.
Gas Associations
  • - Hydrogen sulfide overproduction is linked to diarrhea.
  • - Methane overproduction is linked to constipation.

Testing & Treatment (Table 1)
  • - Testing: Hydrogen, methane, and hydrogen sulfide breath tests are widely available.
  • - Treatment: Positive breath tests predict an increased clinical response to rifaximin in IBS-D.
  • - Other strategies include elemental diets.



ðŸ¦  Post-Infection IBS (PI-IBS)
  • - A history of GI infection is a major risk factor, but many patients cannot recall a specific event.
Biomarkers for PI-IBS
  • - Pathophysiology involves the bacterial neurotoxin CdtB and the human protein vinculin.
  • - Antibody titers to CdtB and vinculin are significantly higher in patients with IBS-D compared to controls.
  • - These markers have a reasonably high predictive value for IBS-D but await validation in broader IBS populations.

Treatment Implications (Table 1)
  • - Specific treatment for PI-IBS: Glutamine therapy.
  • - Consider enhanced prophylaxis for traveler's diarrhea in these patients.


ðŸ§± Intestinal Epithelial Permeability ('Leaky Gut')
  • - Altered intestinal barrier function has been linked to visceral hypersensitivity and pain in IBS.
Prevalence of Barrier Dysfunction
  • - Higher in diarrhea-predominant forms of IBS.
  • - IBS-D: 37-62%
  • - PI-IBS: 17-50%
  • - IBS-C: up to 25%

Testing Methods
  • - Most tests (e.g., Ussing chambers, biopsy analysis) are not easily applied in clinical settings.
  • - Blood/stool markers like zonulin are not sufficiently validated for clinical use.
  • - The Lactulose/Mannitol (L/M) oral test is the most common non-invasive method.

  • - Recent studies challenge the traditional interpretation of the L/M test.

Treatment Implications (Table 1)
  • - There are no FDA-approved treatments specifically for impaired permeability in IBS.
  • - A small trial showed that Glutamine treatment in PI-IBS improved both symptoms and L/M test measures.



Conclusion: A Framework for Personalized IBS Care
  • - The goal is not to perform indiscriminate biomarker testing on all IBS patients.
  • - Instead, use the discussion of potential mechanisms as a framework for shared decision-making to pursue selective testing.
  • - Linking treatments to the most dominant pathophysiological mechanism(s) should increase clinical response rates and patient satisfaction.
