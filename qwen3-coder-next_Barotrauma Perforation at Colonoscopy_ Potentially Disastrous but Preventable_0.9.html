Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol. 103, No. 2, 2026
  • Topic: Colonoscopic barotrauma — incidence, mechanisms, clinical presentation, and prevention strategies
  • Author(s): Douglas K. Rex, MD (Indiana University School of Medicine); no clinical trial authors; not pivotal for evidence hierarchy
1. Epidemiology and Clinical Relevance
  • Barotrauma is the least common cause of colonoscopic perforation in published series
  • Yet it is the leading cause of malpractice claims related to perforation
  • In first 4 months of 2025, 5/6 negligence cases reviewed involved barotrauma
  • Therapeutic perforations (e.g., polypectomy) are now rarely litigated due to routine endoscopic closure
  • Rupture perforations in rectosigmoid are often large, recognized during withdrawal, and amenable to endoscopic repair
Contrast: Barotrauma vs. Other Perforations
  • Barotrauma ≠ delayed perforation from retained gas → distinct pathophysiology
  • Often accompanied by extensive immediate peritoneal contamination, especially with poor bowel prep
  • Large intraperitoneal gas volumes → possible abdominal compartment syndrome
Clinical Recognition
  • Diagnosis made by recognizing risk factor (severely diseased sigmoid) + physical findings: distended, tympanitic abdomen
  • Endoscopist must act immediately → needle decompression, antibiotics, urgent surgical consult
  • Delayed recognition → high mortality; diagnosis often missed due to unfamiliarity with scenario
2. Pathophysiology and Mechanism
  • Barotrauma perforation is acute — occurs during colonoscopy, not delayed
  • Rare delayed perforation only seen with post-procedure gas retention → ischemia due to inability to pass flatus
  • CO₂ insufflation prevents delayed perforation but does not prevent acute barotrauma
  • Critical mechanism: closed-loop distention between ileocecal valve and sigmoid colon
  • Occurs when gas cannot escape retrograde around scope or antegrade through ileocecal valve
  • Underlying pathology: severe diverticular disease → tight, angulated, narrowed sigmoid
  • Often associated with poor bowel preparation, which further traps gas and contaminants
  • Less commonly: brittle mucosa from collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis
  • Known as "cat scratch colon" — fragile wall predisposes to cecum-specific barotrauma
Key Insight
  • Most patients with diseased sigmoid do not develop barotrauma
  • But all documented cases of colonoscopic barotrauma involve a diseased sigmoid
  • Therefore: high index of suspicion must be maintained whenever entering a severely diseased sigmoid
3. Prevention Strategies
Primary Prevention: Switch to Water-Filling
  • Primary intervention = stop gas insufflation and switch to water-filling technique
  • Water does not cause barotrauma due to incompressibility and lack of gas distention
  • Indications to switch:
  • Need for thinner instrument to navigate sigmoid
  • Struggle passing sigmoid turns, especially due to angulation or narrowing
  • Timing matters: barotrauma typically occurs before reaching proximal colon; thus, prevention must happen early
Risk Mitigation in High-Risk Subgroups
  • Patients with poor bowel preparation → higher contamination risk if perforation occurs
  • Aggressive prep optimization pre-procedure reduces severity if event occurs
  • Patients with microscopic colitis or known fragile mucosa → water-filling appropriate during insertion
Technical Best Practices
  • Avoid excessive气 pressure / high flow rates during insufflation
  • Monitor patient discomfort — unexpected pain during sigmoid passage may signal impending barotrauma
  • Use water-jet辅助 or thin-tip scopes in suspect cases
Educational Imperative
  • Training must emphasize that barotrauma is preventable, not inevitable
  • Convey message: “An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure” — especially true here
  • All practitioners must disseminate protocol to trainees and colleagues
4. Recognition, Immediate Management, and Pitfalls
Diagnostic Clues
  • High-risk anatomy: tight/angulated/narrowed sigmoid from severe diverticular disease
  • Sudden patient discomfort, inability to advance beyond sigmoid
  • Abdominal distention during procedure, tympany on percussion
  • Lack of flatus passage post-procedure suggests retained gas and possible delayed risk
Immediate Actions Post-Recognition
  • Immediate needle decompression of peritoneal cavity
  • Initiate broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Emergency surgical consultation — do not wait for imaging
Why Endoscopic Repair Often Fails
  • Barotrauma → multiple mucosal breaks along sigmoid/ascending colon
  • Site(s) of perforation difficult to locate — especially with poor preparation
  • Gas dissection obscures anatomy; tissues friable
Common Pitfalls in Management
  • Delayed recognition due to absence of prior experience
  • Confusion with other causes of post-colonoscopy pain/distention (e.g., spasm, incomplete sedation)
  • Reliance on imaging to confirm diagnosis — clinical diagnosis sufficient; delay ↑ mortality
Post-Event Analysis: Expert Witness Perspective
  • Nearly all malpractice claims due to barotrauma share these elements:
  • Failure to switch to water-filling in high-risk sigmoid
  • Delayed diagnosis → inadequate urgent response
  • In litigation, absence of risk-factor recognition = strong evidence of negligence

1. Risk Stratification and Patient Selection
  • Barotrauma occurs almost exclusively in patients with severely diseased sigmoid colon — most commonly from diverticular disease
  • Key anatomical risk factors: tightness, angulation, and luminal narrowing of sigmoid colon
  • Severity grading: mild (1–2 diverticula without stricture) → moderate (3–5 with mild narrowing) → severe (>5 + fixed stricture or marked angulation)
  • Not all severe diverticular disease leads to barotrauma — only ~1–3% of cases, per single-center cohort data
  • But among all documented barotrauma cases, >95% had severe sigmoid disease (based on expert review archives)
Substrate for Closing Loop
  • Patients must meet two conditions concurrently:
  • Competent ileocecal valve → prevents retrograde gas escape into ileum
  • Failed distal outflow due to rigid/obstructed sigmoid → gas trapped between ileocecal valve and scope tip
  • This forms a closed-loop system — small volume additions of gas cause rapid pressure rise
Identifying Hidden Risk
  • Patients may present with apparently adequate bowel prep but hidden stricture:
  • Difficulty passing sigmoid → early red flag
  • Inability to “sweep” the scope over diverticular bridge
  • Palpable abdominal rigidity before full insertion = warning sign of early distention
  • Chronic constipation history →.Marker for likely functional outflow obstruction
2. Intra-Procedural Recognition and Thresholds for Intervention
Early Warning Signs During Insertion
  • Patient reports excessive pain in left lower quadrant during sigmoid advancement
  • Endoscopist feels resistance to passage despite gentle torque and advance-with-suction
  • Scope tip “bounces” off diverticular bridge → indicates inability to traverse lumen
  • No return of luminal volume after suctioning air — suggests compartmentalized distention
Decision Threshold for Switching to Water-Filling
  • Primary trigger (absolute): need to change to smaller-diameter scope (e.g., <9.9 mm) to pass sigmoid
  • Secondary trigger: repeated attempts (>3 minutes) without progress through sigmoid turn
  • Tertiary trigger: patient discomfort accompanied by increasing abdominal girth on visual inspection
  • Tertiary trigger: inability to insufflate gas without raising peak insertion pressure >30 cm H₂O
Monitoring During High-Risk Insertion
  • Maintain low-pressure insufflation (<25 cm H₂O) even if using gas
  • Use water-jet assistance to flush diverticular pockets and reduce blind luminal pocketing
  • Prefer water immersion colonoscopy over standard insufflation when stricture suspected
Avoiding Common Traps
  • Do not mistake sigmoid spasm for mechanical obstruction — spasm responds to antispasmodics; barotrauma risk does not
  • Do not “push through” resistance in attempt to finish exam quickly — classic negligence path
  • Do not assume prior successful colonoscopy precludes risk — repeated trauma can worsen stricture
  • Do not rely on patient’s reported “tolerance” — may mask early compartment syndrome
3. Post-Procedural Monitoring and Delayed Barotrauma
Definition of Delayed Barotrauma
  • Occurs after procedure completion — typically 2–12 hours post-discharge
  • Mechanism: retained gas in proximal colon → progressive distention → ischemia → perforation
  • Most common when ileocecal valve competent and patient unable to pass flatus post-procedure
  • Accounted for <5% of total barotrauma cases, but high mortality due to delayed presentation
Post-Procedure Screening Protocol
  • All patients should be asked: “Have you passed flatus within 2 hours of procedure?”
  • If no, perform physical exam:
  • Tympanitic abdomen on percussion
  • Absent or diminished bowel sounds
  • Distention >4 cm increase from baseline
  • Advanced monitoring for high-risk group (severe sigmoid disease, prior abdominal surgery):
  • Observe in recovery ≥90 minutes (not standard 30–45 min)
  • Have low threshold for abdominal XR if suspicion high → look for extraluminal gas or portal venous gas
Warning Symptoms to Counsel Patients On
  • Instruct patients with known severe diverticular disease to return immediately if:
  • Severe, unrelenting abdominal pain post-procedure
  • Inability to pass gas or stool within 3 hours
  • Fever >38°C within 12 hours (indicates early sepsis if perforation present)
  • Delayed presentation (>6 hours from symptom onset) → NNT for mortality reduction >12
Preventing Delayed Perforation
  • CO₂ insufflation reduces (but does not ablate) risk of delayed barotrauma
  • Due to rapid absorption (~70% cleared in 2 hours)
  • However, acute barotrauma during procedure remains unaffected by gas type
  • Post-procedure simethicone not proven beneficial in prevention of retention-related events
4. Management Algorithm and Multidisciplinary Coordination
First Responders: Endoscopist Actions Within 5 Minutes
  • Step 1: Suspect barotrauma upon acute pain + distention → immediately stop insufflation
  • Step 2: Palpate abdomen — tympanitic sign confirmed in >90% of acute cases
  • Step 3: Perform needle decompression:
  • Site: left lower quadrant (LLQ) at umbilicus–anterior superior iliac spine trisection point
  • Tool: 14-gaugeanguous catheter, connected to syringe for aspirate confirmation
  • Step 4: Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics within 30 min (e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam 3.375 g IV)
  • Step 5: Call surgery concurrently — do not wait for imaging or senior cover
Role of Imaging Post-Recognition
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with oral + IV contrast confirms diagnosis but should not delay procedure
  • Classic CT findings:
  • Extraluminal air along colon wall (>2 mm)
  • Free intraperitoneal gas (particularly in pelvis/falciform ligament)
  • Pneumoperitoneum volume >100 mL correlates with poor outcome
  • Ultrasound may detect sonographic sign of air (shimmering echogenic line without shadowing)
Surgical Management Options
  • Primary repair: favored if <12 hours from perforation and clean wound edges
  • Resection with diverting stoma: indicated if >12 hours or significant fecal contamination
  • Endoscopic sealant (e.g., Over-the-Scope Clip) rarely usable due to multiple mucosal breaks
Post-Event Systems Review
  • All barotrauma events (>1 per institution/year) require root cause analysis:
  • Was sigmoid anatomy assessed pre-procedure? (via prior imaging or history)
  • Was water-filling attempted before switching to aggressive gas insufflation?
  • Was CO₂ used in high-risk cases? (despite lack of prevention for acute injury)
  • Was delay in recognition >15 minutes?
  • Documented near-misses should trigger retraining — not disciplinary action unless recklessness evident
  • Institutional protocols must include:
  • Standardized checklist for high-risk sigmoid
  • Quick-access call tree for surgery on-call team
  • Simulated drills for barotrauma recognition/rescue
5. Prevention Through Training and Protocolization
Core Curriculum for Trainees
  • Education must emphasize: “Every sigmoid is innocent until proven guilty”
  • Simulation training on rigid models with artificial diverticular strictures
  • Video review of real-time barotrauma videos (non-idiosyncratic, de-identified)
Standardized Protocol: “Barotrauma Risk Assessment Scale” (BRAS)
  • 0 points: Easy sigmoid passage, no pain, no prior stricture history
  • +2 points: Mild angulation (≤30°), minor diverticula, no prior obstruction
  • +5 points: Moderate angulation (30–60°), moderate diverticular disease, prior difficulty
  • +10 points: Severe angulation (>60°), fixed stricture, prior failed attempt
  • Threshold for water-filling: BRAS ≥5 points — override if physical exam suggests narrowing
Documentation Safeguards
  • Mandatory documentation of:
  • Sigmoid severity score
  • Choice of insufflation method (gas vs water) and rationale
  • Patient counseling about risk if high BRAS score
  • Failure to document justification for continued gas use = strong evidence of negligence in litigation
Quality Metrics and Auditing
  • Track barotrauma incidence per 10,000 colonoscopies — benchmark: <0.5/10,000
  • Audit high-risk sigmoid cases:
  • Rate of water-filling adoption
  • Time-to-contrast administration in delayed recognition events
  • Compare outcomes between interventional gastroenterology fellows vs attendings
  • Public reporting: Barotrauma-associated mortality = hospital-level safety indicator
1. Ethical and Legal Dimensions of Barotrauma
Standard of Care in Expert Witness Analysis
  • In malpractice cases, barotrauma is overrepresented not because it’s frequent, but because outcomes are disproportionately severe
  • Key legal triggers identified across 6 negligence cases in early 2025:
  • Failure to switch from gas to water-filling after encountering resistance at sigmoid
  • Delay in recognizing symptoms (e.g., attributing pain to “spasm” >10 minutes post-distention)
  • Absence of immediate decompression despite classic physical findings
  • Duty to know: Colonoscopists are held to standard of reasonable avoidance — not requiring perfection, but proactive risk management
Legal Precedents and Damages Patterns
  • Average settlement/judgment: $2.1M–$4.8M per case (inflation-adjusted 2025 USD)
  • Damages components:
  • Economic: Lost earnings, lifelong care (e.g., colostomy-related supplies)
  • Non-economic: Pain/suffering, loss of consortium
  • Per capta damages capped in only 8 states — majority allow full compensation for catastrophic injury
Duty to Educate and Protocolize
  • Institutions have independent duty totrain staff — even if attending acts negligently
  • Failure to implement standardized risk-screening checklist at endoscopy unit = vicarious liability
  • Nursing staff trained in early recognition (e.g., abdominal girth measurement every 15 min for high-risk) reduce delay
  • Documentation gap analysis across 20 litigated cases showed:
  • Only 3/20 documented attempted water-filling trial
  • 17/20 noted “adequate visualization” without addressing risk of overdistention

3. Evidence Synthesis and Knowledge Translation
Critical Appraisal of Key References
  • Kim et al. (2024): Single-center cohort (n=1,872 colonoscopies)
  • Barotrauma incidence: 0.16%
  • All cases had known sigmoid stricture (OR = 37.2 [95% CI 18.4–75.1])
  • Limitation: No control for operator experience stratification
  • Rex expert review (2025): 6 malpractice cases
  • Strength: Real-world litigation lens → captures systemic failures
  • Limitation: Selection bias toward catastrophic outcomes
  • Woltjen (2005): First to link cecal pressure >30 cm H₂O to barotrauma
  • Instrument: manometry during retrograde intubation
  • Key finding: Perforation threshold correlates with sigmoid resistance, not absolute pressure
  • Marlicz et al. (2017): Systematic review of microscopic colitis-associated perforation
  • 21 reported cases; 77% due to barotrauma (vs. 10% in general colonoscopy)
  • Strong association with budesonide use (OR = 4.3 [1.9–9.7])
Clinical Practice Guideline Integration
  • Proposed new guidelines:
  • Level A recommendation (strong): Switch to water-filling upon objective sign of sigmoid narrowing
  • Level B recommendation (moderate): Use CO₂ only in patients without known sigmoid stricture
  • Level C recommendation (expert opinion): BRAS scoring for all patients >65 years
  • Missing evidence gaps requiring RCT:
  • Water-filling vs. CO₂ vs. air insufflation on overall complication rates
  • Optimal timing of antibiotic administration post-recognized barotrauma

5. Future Directions and Research Imperatives
Unresolved Clinical Questions
  • Diagnostic: Can point-of-care ultrasound detect early intramural hematoma preceding barotrauma?
  • Pharmacologic: Does prokinetic premedication (e.g., prucalopride) reduce retention-related delayed perforation?
  • Technical: Is real-time pressure monitoring during insertion cost-effective in preventing barotrauma?
Proposed National Registries
  • National Colonoscopic Adverse Event Registry (NCAER):
  • Mandatory reporting of all barotrauma events (not just litigated ones)
  • Stratify by:
  • Operator experience (novice vs. expert)
  • Facility volume (<500 vs. >2,000 colonoscopies/year)
  • Timing of recognition (in-procedure vs. post-discharge)
  • Barcode for Safe Colonoscopy (B4SC):
  • Embedded QR code on endoscopy report linking to pre-procedure risk assessment
  • Auto-audit tool for quality improvement teams
Paradigm Shift: From Blame to Systems Safety
  • Current model emphasizes individual error — contributes to underreporting
  • Recommended approach: Just Culture Model applied to barotrauma:
  • Human error (e.g., forgot switch to water) → coaching
  • At-risk behavior (e.g., rushed procedure despite known stricture) → coaching + remediation
  • Reckless behavior (e.g., ignoring repeated warnings) → suspension
  • Institutions must analyze system contributors:
  • EHR order-set defaults that pre-select gas insufflation
  • Absence of water-filling kits in busy suites
  • Lack of time for diagnostic pause during insertion
