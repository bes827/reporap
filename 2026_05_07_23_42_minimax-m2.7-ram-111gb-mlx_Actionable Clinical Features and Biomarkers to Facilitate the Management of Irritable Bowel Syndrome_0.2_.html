Actionable Clinical Features and Biomarkers to Facilitate the Management of Irritable Bowel Syndrome
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, Vol. 121, May 2026
  • Topic: IBS biomarkers and personalized medicine approaches
  • Author(s): Andrea Shin, MD, MS; Kyle Staller, MD, MPH; David J. Levinthal, MD, PhD
IBS Definition and Diagnostic Challenges
  • IBS --> common chronic disorder of gut-brain interaction (DGBI)
  • Core features --> abdominal pain + altered bowel movements
  • Rome IV criteria --> positive diagnosis with minimal testing (in patients lacking red flags)
  • Rome criteria ↓ likelihood of missing: celiac disease, pancreatic insufficiency, IBD
  • Biopsychosocial model --> does NOT identify specific pathophysiological mechanisms in individual patients
  • Multiple distinct mechanisms contribute to IBS --> different subsets of patients respond to different treatments
  • Current care --> trial and error approach based on IBS subtype
  • Need --> personalized medicine linking treatments to dominant mechanisms
Clinical Phenotypes Beyond Rome Subtypes
1. Significant Psychological Burden
  • Key feature --> marked anxiety, depression, or PTSD; fear of eating; agoraphobia
  • Associated with: care seeking behavior + treatment outcomes
  • IBS and mood disorders --> shared genetic origins affecting central neuronal circuits
  • UK registry latent class analysis --> 7 distinct patient clusters
  • Distinguished by: bowel habits, symptom severity, psychological burden
  • Clusters --> stable over time
  • High psychological comorbidity clusters showed:
  • ↑ symptom severity
  • ↑ healthcare resource utilization
  • ↓ quality of life
  • UK population finding --> patients with high psychological burden remained on bowel-centric treatments (e.g., loperamide for IBS-D)
  • These patients --> never received psychological therapy
  • Recommendation --> early identification + offer brain-gut behavioral therapies and/or centrally-acting neuromodulators
  • Optimal neuromodulator choice --> incorporate GI symptoms for greatest benefit
2. Autonomic/Neurologic Symptoms
  • Latent cluster analysis --> unique clusters with increased neurological burden
  • Includes: migraines, other neurological disorders
  • POTS definition --> chronic orthostatic intolerance with excessive tachycardia without hypotension
  • Classic IBS + autonomic dysfunction patient --> young, female, overlapping fibromyalgia, migraines, chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Autonomic symptoms to inquire about:
  • Orthostatic: blurred vision, lightheadedness, presyncope/syncope, tremors/shakiness, dyspnea, paresthesia, palpitations
  • Non-orthostatic: urinary retention, dyspepsia, nausea
  • Recommended interventions:
  • Exercise training
  • ↑ fluid intake (2 L/d) + ↑ salt intake (10 g/d)
  • Pyridostigmine (up to 60 mg PO TID)
  • Pyridostigmine --> improves POTS symptoms + simultaneously improves GI symptoms (constipation)
  • Generally favorable side effect profile
3. hEDS/POTS/Mast Cell Activation Triad
  • Hypermobility spectrum disorders (HSD) --> highly associated with IBS
  • HSD characteristics:
  • Non-inflammatory connective tissue disorders
  • Abnormal collagen synthesis → generalized joint hypermobility
  • Best characterized clinically using Beighton score
  • Diagnostic standards for HSD --> evolving
  • Common presentations: benign joint hypermobility disorder, hEDS
  • GI clinic findings --> ~1/3 of patients have undiagnosed HSD based on clinical criteria
  • Many with HSD experience concurrent:
  • Autonomic symptoms
  • Musculoskeletal symptoms
  • Chronic pain
  • Patients with HSD + autonomic dysfunction --> often exhibit immune dysfunction (mast cell activation)
  • Mast cell activation triad: hEDS + POTS + mast cell activation
  • Clinical manifestations:
  • Rashes
  • Asthma-like respiratory symptoms
  • History of intolerance to medications and many foods
  • Validated serum/urine assays for mast cell mediators:
  • Serum tryptase
  • Urinary histamine
  • Leukotriene C4
  • Prostaglandin D2
  • Mast cell activation treatment options:
  • Nonsedating H1-receptor antagonist (e.g., cetirizine)
  • H2-receptor antagonist (famotidine)
  • Leukotriene-receptor antagonists (montelukast sodium)
  • Gut-selective mast cell stabilizing agents (oral cromolyn 100–200 mg PO TID with meals and qhs)
Bile Acid Malabsorption (BAM) as Biomarker
Prevalence and Significance
  • BAM occurs in 25%–33% of patients with DGBIs + diarrhea
  • Assessment of bile acid physiology --> important biomarker for IBS evaluation
Bile Acid Physiology
  • Primary bile acids --> synthesized in liver → pass into small intestine
  • ~95% of bile acids --> active reuptake in terminal ileum
  • Residual bile acids → colon → further biotransformation by colonic bacteria → excreted in stool
BAM and IBS-D
  • Both total + primary bile acids --> ↑ in IBS-D
  • Elevated bile acids predict clinically significant changes:
  • ↑ stool weight
  • ↑ stool frequency
  • ↑ stool consistency
  • Elevated stool bile acids in IBS-D --> independent of cholecystectomy history
Diagnostic Methods for BAM
  • 48-hr stool collection --> elevated total + primary bile acids
  • Fasting serum C4 level
  • % primary bile acids from single stool sample
  • Combined fasting serum C4 + primary bile acids from single stool --> diagnostic approach
  • Alternative: 75-selenium homocholic acid taurine (75SeHCAT) retention test --> not available in US
Treatment Implications
  • Testing predicts benefit with:
  • Dietary modifications
  • Bile acid sequestrant therapy (cholestyramine, colestipol, or colesevelam)
  • Analysis of >1,000 patients with chronic unexplained diarrhea:
  • ~70% clinical response to bile acid sequestrant therapy
  • ~3-fold ↑ likelihood of clinical response vs. those without elevated bile acid levels
  • Emerging data: GLP-1 agonists (e.g., liraglutide) --> alternative treatment for BAM
  • Particularly useful in obese patients with BAM-associated IBS-D
Biomarkers of Gut Luminal Interactions
FODMAPs and Diet
  • Low FODMAP diets --> well-established IBS treatments
  • Effective for many patients regardless of IBS subtype
  • Prominent bloating/gassiness after carbs/sugars --> suggests FODMAP contribution
  • Evidence is mixed on whether clinical features predict low FODMAP diet responsiveness
  • Emerging data: microbiome + metabolomic characteristics may influence response
  • Not readily available in clinical practice
  • Recent data: immunological profiles could tailor diet-based therapies
Sucrase-Isomaltase (SI) Deficiency
  • ~5%–10% of patients with IBS have congenital or acquired SI deficiency
  • Typically IBS-D predominant pattern
  • SI deficiency --> impedes digestion/absorption of disaccharides + dietary starches
  • Couples to symptom generation via:
  • Laxative effects
  • Fermentation
  • Diagnostic methods:
  • Disaccharidase enzymatic assays from duodenal biopsies
  • Immunostaining of duodenal biopsies for SI expression
  • Breath testing (sucrose substrate)
  • Treatment options:
  • Reduced dietary sucrose intake
  • Sacrosidase enzyme replacement therapy
SIBO and Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO)
  • Small intestinal dysbiosis + bacterial overgrowth --> determines IBS subtype
  • Specific microbial communities linked to:
  • Hydrogen → diarrhea
  • Methane → constipation
  • Hydrogen sulfide
  • Breath testing biomarkers now widely available (in-clinic or at-home)
  • Diarrhea --> strongest association with likelihood of SIBO presence
  • Rifaximin --> first-line treatment for IBS-D
  • Positive breath tests predicted ↑ clinical response to rifaximin in patients meeting Rome III criteria for IBS-D
  • Breath test-based biomarkers --> guide initial choice of rifaximin therapy
  • Alternative: elemental diets shown promise in SIBO/IMO
Post-Infection IBS Biomarkers
Clinical Context
  • Previous GI infection --> well-known risk factor for IBS development
  • PI-IBS clinical course may differ from non-PI-IBS
  • Specific treatments for PI-IBS exist (e.g., glutamine)
  • Patients with PI-IBS may warrant enhanced prophylaxis against traveler's diarrhea
  • Most patients cannot reliably recall specific enteric infection before IBS onset
  • Biomarkers provide prognostic information and impact management decisions
Cytolethal Distending Toxin B (CdtB) and Vinculin
  • CdtB --> neurotoxin produced by food-borne bacteria:
  • Campylobacter jejuni
  • Salmonella
  • Escherichia coli
  • Shigella
  • CdtB interacts with cell-adhesion protein vinculin
  • Antibody titers to:
  • Anti-CdtB
  • Anti-vinculin
  • Much higher in patients with IBS-D vs. IBD, celiac disease, or healthy controls
  • Presumably defines subset of PI-IBS patients
Evidence Summary for Antibody Biomarkers
  • High predictive value in IBS-D patients
  • Awaiting validation in broad populations with IBS (all subtypes)
  • Other PI-IBS biomarker candidates:
  • Altered intestinal proteolytic activity
  • Presence of stool bacterial superantigens
  • Role in clinical care --> not yet determined
Intestinal Permeability Biomarkers
Prevalence and Associations
  • Altered intestinal permeability (leaky gut) linked to:
  • Visceral hypersensitivity
  • Pain
  • Systematic review findings:
  • IBS-D: 37%–62% prevalence of barrier dysfunction
  • PI-IBS: 17%–50% prevalence
  • IBS-C: up to 25%
  • Associated with specific IBS symptom patterns
Diagnostic Challenges
  • Most permeability tests --> not easily applied in clinical care settings
  • Barrier function studies of biopsy samples (Ussing chambers)
  • Quantification of tight junction-related genes/proteins from intestinal biopsies
  • Application of fecal/biopsy supernatants to cellular monolayers
  • Confocal laser endomicroscopy --> promising point-of-care tool
  • Still in development
  • Indirect approaches (blood/stool lipopolysaccharide, intestinal fatty-acid binding protein, zonulin) --> not sufficiently validated
Lactulose/Mannitol (L/M) Testing
  • Most common non-invasive method for intestinal permeability assessment
  • Test principle:
  • Smaller molecule mannitol → absorbed through pore pathway
  • Larger molecule lactulose → absorbed through leak pathway
  • Recent study challenged assumptions:
  • Low lactulose excretion (<1%) over 24 hours in healthy volunteers + IBS-D patients
  • Recommended 13C-mannitol mass excretion at 0–2 hours as more reliable marker
  • Highlights: reproducibility + feasibility
  • Limitations:
  • L/M testing may be confounded by diet + medications
  • No FDA-approved treatments specifically for impaired permeability in IBS
Treatment Implications
  • Glutamine treatment in PI-IBS:
  • Improved both symptoms + L/M measures
  • Demonstrated in small clinical trial
Treatment Recommendations by Biomarker
Neuromodulators for Psychological Comorbidity
  • SNRIs, SSRIs, TCAs
  • Selection influenced by:
  • Predominant bowel pattern
  • Key clinical features
  • Mirtazapine --> for prominent dyspepsia and/or nausea
  • Referral to psychologist for:
  • Eating disorders
  • Severe anxiety/depression
  • PTSD
  • OCD
Autonomic Dysfunction Management
  • Pyridostigmine (up to 60 mg PO TID)
  • Particularly useful in IBS-C
  • Improves both POTS symptoms + GI symptoms
Mast Cell Activation Treatments
  • Cetirizine (nonsedating H1-receptor antagonist)
  • Famotidine (H2-receptor antagonist)
  • Montelukast sodium (leukotriene-receptor antagonist)
  • Oral cromolyn 100–200 mg PO TID with meals and qhs (gut-selective)
BAM Treatments
  • Cholestyramine
  • Colestipol
  • Colesevelam
  • GLP-1 agonists (e.g., liraglutide) --> for obese patients
SIBO/IMO Treatments
  • Rifaximin
  • Elemental diets
PI-IBS Treatment
  • Glutamine therapy
  • Enhanced prophylaxis for traveler's diarrhea to endemic areas
Leaky Gut Treatment
  • Glutamine therapy --> improves symptoms + L/M measures
Conclusions and Clinical Implementation
Diagnostic Paradigm Limitations
  • Current Rome IV criteria --> standardize diagnosis
  • Do NOT identify specific mechanisms in individual patients
Authors' Approach
  • Do NOT pursue biomarker-based testing in all IBS patients
  • Do NOT recommend indiscriminate testing for all possible mechanisms
Recommended Approach
  • Use biomarker-based testing selectively
  • Discussion of potential disease mechanisms --> framework for patient discussions
  • Facilitates shared decision-making to pursue selective testing
Clinical Utility
  • Recognition of clinical features + actionable biomarkers --> basis for personalized medicine
  • Links treatments to most dominant pathophysiological mechanisms
  • Goal: ↑ clinical response rates, patient satisfaction, quality of life
