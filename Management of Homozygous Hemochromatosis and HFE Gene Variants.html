Management of Homozygous Hemochromatosis and *HFE* Gene Variants
  • This document outlines the primary treatment strategies and management considerations for homozygous hemochromatosis, particularly focusing on individuals with the C282Y variant in the *HFE* gene.
  • It also addresses the management of heterozygous individuals and compound heterozygotes.

Homozygous Hemochromatosis (C282Y Variant) Management
  • Primary treatment for homozygous hemochromatosis, especially C282Y homozygotes, is therapeutic phlebotomy.
  • This method ↓ body's iron stores; considered safe & cost-effective.
  • Goal: prevent organ dysfunction ← excess iron / improve organ function if damage occurred.
Indications for Treatment (Phlebotomy)
  • C282Y homozygote w/ ↑ serum ferritin → assumed iron overload; phlebotomy indicated.
  • Treatment initiated urgently in symptomatic patients / significant iron overload.
Evidence of Iron Overload
  • Ferritin >300 ng/mL (males) / >200 ng/mL (females); especially if ≥1000 ng/mL.
  • Tissue injury: ↑ hepatic transaminase, ↓ hormone levels, ↓ cardiac ejection fraction.
  • ↑ tissue iron: identified by MRI, other imaging, or tissue biopsy.
Urgency & Benefit
  • Symptomatic patients / serum ferritin >1000 mg/L (or >1000 ng/mL) → urgent treatment.
  • Mildly ↑ iron stores (e.g., ferritin 300–500 mg/L) also benefit.
  • Benefits: ↓ cardiovascular disease, ↓ extra-hepatic malignancies.
Phlebotomy Procedure (Depletion Phase)
  • Involves weekly phlebotomy sessions by experienced team.
  • Goal: gradually ↓ iron stores until target ferritin reached.
Volume & Frequency
  • Volume: Typically 500 mL blood/session.
  • Some individuals (e.g., <50 kg, older, cardiopulmonary disease) may tolerate 250 mL better.
  • Frequency: Initial once-weekly phlebotomy recommended.
  • Severe iron overload / organ injury: twice-weekly possible if tolerated & hemoglobin >11 g/dL.
Monitoring & Duration
  • Monitoring: CBC, hemoglobin/hematocrit checked monthly / @ each phlebotomy.
  • Serum ferritin monitored monthly; every two weeks as target approaches.
  • Duration: Depends on accumulated iron load.
  • Each 500 mL unit removes ≈ 200-250 mg elemental iron.
Target Ferritin & Maintenance Therapy
  • Phlebotomy continues until iron stores reduced to low normal range.
  • Maintenance phase prevents iron reaccumulation.
Target Ferritin Level
  • Many studies suggest target serum ferritin of 50 mg/L (or 50 ng/mL).
  • Other experts: maintain between 50 and 150 ng/mL.
  • Advised to avoid inducing iron deficiency.
Maintenance Phase
  • Once target ferritin reached, maintenance phlebotomies performed periodically.
  • Typically: one unit of blood removed ≈ 3-6 times/year (once every 2-4 months).
  • Voluntary blood donation encouraged; ideal solution, transfusion services often accept.
Personalization
  • Maintenance therapy personalized.
  • Required interval varies based on patient's age, sex, initial ferritin presentation.
Management for Non-Expressing Homozygotes
  • C282Y homozygotes w/ normal serum ferritin & transferrin saturation are 'non-expressing'.
  • These individuals need observation, not treatment.
  • Adults w/ normal ferritin @ diagnosis unlikely to develop iron overload later.
Monitoring & Advice
  • Periodic screening of ferritin recommended (minimum every few years).
  • Voluntary blood donation encouraged for this group.
Other Management Considerations
  • Beyond phlebotomy, lifestyle modifications & screening for complications are crucial.
  • These measures aim to mitigate risks associated w/ iron overload.
Alcohol and Diet
  • Alcohol intake: major risk factor for liver disease in HH; limit, especially until iron removed.
  • Iron overload potentiates alcoholic liver disease.
  • Diet: balanced, healthy diet w/o specific restrictions (e.g., red meat).
Avoidances
  • Avoid vitamins/supplements containing extra iron.
  • Avoid uncooked seafood until iron overload treated → ↑ risk of infection from siderophilic organisms (e.g., *Vibrio vulnificus*).
Screening for Complications
  • All HH patients screened for liver fibrosis.
  • Established cirrhosis → mandatory screening for hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
Liver Disease Screening
  • HCC screening: 6-monthly ultrasound + alpha-fetoprotein levels.
Cardiac Disease Screening
  • Cardiac testing reserved for severe iron overload / dyspnea.
  • Assessment (echocardiogram / axial imaging) warranted in young patients w/ severe iron overload / ferritin >2000 mg/L.
Arthropathy/Bone Screening
  • Consider baseline bone density DEXA scan in C282Y homozygote adults >40 years.
  • X-rays of hands considered for symptomatic arthropathy.
  • Note: arthritis least likely HH manifestation to improve w/ iron removal.
Family Screening
  • Patients advised to notify siblings & adult children for hemochromatosis testing.
  • Newly diagnosed C282Y homozygote → family history; siblings & parents request *HFE* (C282Y) genetic test.
  • Screening of non-adult children not recommended.
Alternatives to Phlebotomy
  • For rare individuals intolerant to phlebotomy, other options exist.
  • Generally not preferred due to efficiency or side effect profiles.
Erythrocytapheresis
  • Automated red blood cell exchange.
  • Removes up to 800 mL blood/procedure.
  • Removes 2.3x amount of iron vs. 500 mL phlebotomy.
  • Efficient iron removal w/ maintained blood volume.
Iron Chelation
  • Oral iron chelator Deferasirox studied in HH but NOT approved for treatment.
  • Reserved for iron overload + anemia / in combination w/ phlebotomy in life-threatening cardiac dysfunction.
  • Less efficient than phlebotomy; frequent adverse effects reported.
Management of *HFE* Gene Heterozygosity
  • Heterozygosity for common *HFE* variants (C282Y/wild type or H63D/wild type) → low risk of iron overload.
  • Requires observation & addressing associated risk factors, not active phlebotomy.
Risk and Clinical Significance
  • Heterozygous individuals rarely develop iron overload.
  • C282Y heterozygosity generally produces an unaffected carrier state.
Biochemical Findings
  • Mild ↑ serum ferritin / transferrin saturation (TSAT) in ≈10% of C282Y heterozygotes.
  • Usually not clinically significant.
  • Elevations almost always associated w/ other risk factors (e.g., metabolic syndrome, obesity w/ fatty liver, daily alcohol).
Significant Iron Overload
  • If heterozygous individual develops significant iron overload, evaluate for other contributing factors.
Management Approach for Heterozygotes
  • Focus on monitoring, risk factor modification, & selective testing if iron overload suspected/established.
  • Iron studies generally not required unless indicated for another reason.
Monitoring and General Advice
  • Most pragmatic approach: treat other metabolic risk factors.
  • Address comorbidities: alcohol-related / metabolic liver disease.
  • Abstinence from alcohol use counseled, especially w/ liver disease.
  • Voluntary blood donation recommended to potentially normalize iron stores.
Dealing with High Ferritin
  • If high ferritin present in heterozygote, look for concomitant liver disease / other causes.
  • Often due to inflammation / metabolic issues, not hemochromatosis itself.
  • If high ferritin due to other liver disease (e.g., viral hepatitis, NAFLD, alcohol) → patient NOT diagnosed w/ HH.
  • Treatment directed @ underlying condition.
Assessment for Iron Overload
  • Uncertainty re: degree of iron overload (e.g., ferritin above normal but <1000 ng/mL) → MRI liver / ultrasound-based hepatic elastography.
  • Determines presence of iron deposition / fibrosis.
Phlebotomy (Rarely Indicated)
  • Phlebotomy generally NOT required for heterozygous individuals.
  • If serious iron overload (TSAT >45%; ferritin >1000 ng/mL; tissue iron on MRI/biopsy) → investigate for compound heterozygosity (common + rare *HFE* variant) / pathogenic variant in non-*HFE* gene.
  • If severe iron overload confirmed, treatment (phlebotomy) warranted; additional genetic analyses may be performed.
  • Venesection not proven beneficial in most heterozygous patients.
Compound Heterozygotes (C282Y/H63D)
  • Inheritance of both C282Y and H63D variants.
  • Rarely leads to severe disease; warrants slightly more attention.
Risk
  • Rarely develop iron overload / clinical symptoms.
  • Mild biochemical iron overload seen in ≈10% of cases.
  • Risk of iron overload lower than for C282Y homozygotes.
Management
  • If iron overload present, generally less severe than C282Y homozygotes.
  • Accurately investigate & treat other causes of liver disease.
  • Complications typically develop only w/ excess alcohol use / other comorbidity (e.g., diabetes, hepatic steatosis).
  • If iron overload persists, manage w/ monitoring & controlled phlebotomy if justified by severe accumulation.
Counseling Relatives
  • Appropriate for C282Y heterozygotes to inform first-degree relatives.
  • Inform about risk of inheriting variant & possibility of iron overload.
  • Facilitates shared decision-making re: genetic testing / iron studies for relatives.
  • First-degree relatives of *HFE* heterozygote have 50% chance of carrying variant.
