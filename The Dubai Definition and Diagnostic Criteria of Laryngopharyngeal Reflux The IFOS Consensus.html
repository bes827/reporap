The Dubai Definition and Diagnostic Criteria of Laryngopharyngeal Reflux: The IFOS Consensus
  • - Journal: Laryngoscope
  • - Publication Date: 2023
  • - Objective: To propose a global consensus definition and diagnostic approach for Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR) to guide primary care and specialist physicians.
Knowledge Test

Definition, Pathophysiology & Comparison with GERD
  • - This section outlines the core definition of LPR, its underlying mechanisms, and its relationship with Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD).
Definition of LPR (Statement 2)
  • - A disease of the upper aerodigestive tract.
  • - Results from the direct and/or indirect effects of gastroduodenal content reflux.
  • - Induces morphological and/or neurological changes in the upper aerodigestive tract.
Alternative Names (Statement 1)
  • - Silent reflux
  • - Pharyngolaryngeal reflux
  • - Extraesophageal reflux
  • - Atypical GERD
  • - Reflux laryngitis


Pathophysiology
  • - Pepsin is involved in the inflammatory reaction of the laryngopharyngeal mucosa.
  • - Bile salts may be found in higher concentrations in the saliva of LPR patients.
  • - Neurological impact can manifest as laryngeal sensitivity and abnormalities of the upper esophageal sphincter (UES).

LPR vs. GERD (Statement 3)
  • - LPR and GERD share common pathophysiological mechanisms but may present with different clinical pictures.
  • - LPR involves the additional process of retrograde flow above the UES into the laryngopharynx.
  • - Typical esophageal symptoms of GERD (e.g., heartburn) may be absent in some LPR patients.
Correlation
  • - The severity of GERD correlates with the development of LPR.
  • - Patients with both GERD and LPR show higher numbers of pharyngeal reflux events on HEMII-pH testing.



Contributing Factors & Associations
  • - This section details factors that may contribute to the development of LPR and its association with other otolaryngologic diseases.
Contributing Factors (Statements 4-5)
  • - Factors that may impair gastroesophageal function and be associated with LPR development.
Lifestyle & Diet
  • - Tobacco use
  • - Obesity
  • - Alcohol consumption

Dietary Factors
  • - Diets high in fat, salt, sugar, mint, or acidic content.
  • - Diets low in protein (high-protein foods may increase LES tonicity).

Physiological Factors
  • - Primary esophageal dysmotility.


Associations with Otolaryngologic Diseases
  • - LPR has been implicated in various conditions, though evidence levels vary.
Plausible Associations (Statements 7-8)
  • - Vocal process granuloma: LPR may be associated with its development.
  • - Asthma: LPR may impact the clinical course and therapeutic response.

Associations Requiring Clarification (Statement 9)
  • - Paradoxical vocal fold movement
  • - Laryngotracheal stenosis
  • - Spasmodic dysphonia
  • - Obstructive sleep apnea
  • - Laryngopharyngeal cancer



Clinical Presentation: Symptoms & Findings
  • - LPR symptoms and findings are notably non-specific and can overlap with other common conditions.
Symptoms (Statements 10-13)
  • - Given the non-specific nature, validated patient-reported outcome (PRO) questionnaires (e.g., RSI, RSS) are ideally employed for diagnosis and follow-up.
Common ENT Symptoms
  • - Dysphonia
  • - Dysphagia
  • - Throat pain
  • - Globus sensation
  • - Throat clearing
  • - Troublesome cough (especially after lying down/eating)

GERD-like Symptoms
  • - Heartburn and regurgitations may be present in some LPR patients.

Symptoms Requiring Further Study
  • - Odynophagia
  • - Ear pain
  • - Burning tongue
  • - Halitosis
  • - Dyspnea


Findings (Signs) (Statements 14-17)
  • - No specific laryngeal signs are diagnostic of LPR.
  • - Diagnosis should NOT be made based on laryngeal findings alone.
  • - Use of a validated instrument (e.g., RFS, RSA) is ideal for assessment.
Commonly Observed Signs
  • - Arytenoid erythema
  • - Posterior commissure hypertrophy
  • - Vocal fold edema/erythema
  • - Endolaryngeal mucus
  • - Pharyngeal erythema

Vocal Process Granuloma
  • - Can be a sign of LPR.
  • - Its presence should prompt LPR evaluation or treatment if symptoms are suggestive.

Weak Correlation
  • - Laryngeal findings have weak correlations with reported symptoms, esophageal endoscopy findings, and reflux-monitoring results.



Diagnostic Workup
  • - This section covers the recommended additional examinations for diagnosing LPR.
Upper GI Endoscopy / Transnasal Esophagoscopy (TNE) (Statements 18-20)
  • - Findings may be normal in LPR patients.
  • - Erosive esophagitis is found in only 10%-30% of LPR patients.
  • - Barrett’s metaplasia is even less common (<10%).
Indications for Endoscopy
  • - Patients who do not respond to an empirical therapeutic trial.
  • - Patients with "alarm" features: severe dysphagia, hematemesis, unexplained weight loss, or a family history of upper GI tract cancer.


Esophageal Manometry (High-Resolution) (Statement 21)
  • - Not recommended as a primary diagnostic tool for LPR.
Indications
  • - To evaluate for esophageal dysmotility in patients who do not respond to empiric treatment.
  • - To assist in the placement of a reflux monitoring probe.

Potential Findings
  • - Ineffective esophageal motility is identified in 31%-41% of patients with LPR symptoms.


Hypopharyngeal-Esophageal Multichannel Intraluminal Impedance-pH (HEMII-pH) (Statements 22-33)
  • - An objective tool to identify esophago-pharyngeal reflux events (acid, weakly acidic, or nonacid).
  • - Should be performed OFF acid suppression medications, which should be stopped at least 7 days prior to the study.
Diagnostic Criteria for LPR
  • - Diagnosis can be suggested when there is >1 hypopharyngeal reflux event in 24 hours.
  • - This cutoff is supported by the median number of pharyngeal reflux events in healthy individuals being 1.

Reflux Event Classification (pH based)
  • - Acid: pH < 4.0
  • - Weakly Acid: pH between 4.0 and 7.0
  • - Alkaline (Nonacid): pH > 7.0

Technical Requirements
  • - Proximal pH sensor placement: 0.5 cm to 1 cm above UES.
  • - Distal pH sensor placement: as close to 5 cm above LES as possible.
  • - Manual analysis of the recording is required to verify computer-reported events.

Limitations & Considerations
  • - The severity of LPR on HEMII-pH is not necessarily correlated with the severity of symptoms and findings.
  • - Correlation between HEMII-pH findings and treatment outcomes remains limited.

Alternative/Inferior Methods
  • - Single/dual-channel pH probes: Inadequate for diagnosing LPR as they miss non-acid events and lack a pharyngeal sensor.
  • - Triple-channel pH-only studies: Insufficient as they may miss weakly acidic and nonacidic events.


Other Diagnostic Tests
  • - This section covers emerging or adjunctive diagnostic methods.
Pepsin Saliva Detection (Statements 34-37)
  • - A noninvasive, adjunctive test; it is not a primary diagnostic test.
  • - Can demonstrate reflux in the pharynx but cannot confirm the patient’s symptoms are due to LPR.
  • - A patient may have a negative pepsin test but have LPR confirmed by HEMII-pH.
Influencing Factors
  • - The patient eating before the saliva sample is produced.
  • - The time of day the sample is acquired.


Oropharyngeal pH-testing (Statement 38)
  • - Remains controversial in its ability to truly diagnose LPR.
  • - Diagnostic criteria established for HEMII-pH are not transferable to an oropharyngeal-only pH study.



Executive Summary of Key Statements (Table II)
  • - A summary of the high, moderate, and low-evidence findings from the consensus.
GRADE A: High-Evidence Findings
  • - LPR results from reflux inducing morphological/neurological changes.
  • - LPR and GERD can have different clinical pictures; heartburn may be absent in LPR.
  • - Upper GI endoscopy may be normal in LPR patients.
  • - Endoscopy is indicated for "alarm" features.

GRADE B: Moderate-Evidence Findings
  • - Tobacco, alcohol, obesity, and certain diets are associated with LPR.
  • - LPR diagnosis can be based on >1 pharyngeal reflux event (acid, weakly acid, or non-acid) on 24-hour HEMII-pH.
  • - Use of clinical instruments for non-specific symptoms/findings is recommended.

GRADE C-D: Low-Evidence Findings
  • - Associations between LPR and conditions like paradoxical vocal fold movement, sleep apnea, and laryngeal cancer are plausible but not yet confirmed.
