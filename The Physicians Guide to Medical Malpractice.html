The Physician's Guide to Medical Malpractice

Knowledge Test
  • - What is the deadline in Texas state court for a defendant to file an answer to a petition after being served?
  • - By how many days does sending a notice letter extend the standard 2-year statute of limitations?
  • - Until what age is a medical malpractice claim viable for a minor plaintiff?
  • - What is the common term for the stressful period of waiting for a jury's decision?
  • - What is the consequence if a plaintiff fails to produce a qualified expert report within the statutory 180-day period?

The Medical Malpractice Insurance Policy
  • - Understanding the policy is crucial for navigating a legal claim.
Duties of the Carrier (Insurance Company)
  • - The carrier has two primary obligations: Defense and Indemnity.
Duty to Defend
  • - Carrier must retain a lawyer to defend legal claims against the physician.
  • - Carrier must pay all expenses related to the defense.

Duty to Indemnify
  • - Carrier must pay for a settlement or judgment on any covered claim, up to the policy limits.

Assignment of Counsel
  • - Carrier typically assigns an approved lawyer.
  • - A physician's request for a specific attorney is often honored.
  • - A physician may hire a personal lawyer, but the carrier will not pay those fees.

Consent to Settle Clause
  • - Some policies require the physician's consent to settle a case.
  • - Giving consent transfers the decision-making power to the insurance company.
  • - Important: Settlements, like adverse judgments, are reported to the National Practitioner Data Bank.


Duties of the Insured Physician
  • - The policy requires the physician to fulfill two key duties to maintain coverage.
Prompt Notice
  • - Insureds must provide the carrier with prompt notice of any potential claims or lawsuits.
  • - Failure to provide prompt notice could jeopardize the carrier's obligations to both defend and indemnify.

Cooperation
  • - A 'cooperation clause' requires insured physicians to cooperate in the defense of a legal claim.



Phase One: The Presuit Notice Period
  • - This is the most critical phase because the physician must react before having a lawyer's guidance.
The Notice Letter
  • - A letter from a plaintiff's attorney indicating an intent to bring a suit.
  • - Required by Texas law (Article 4590i) to encourage presuit negotiations.
  • - Key Point: It is NOT a lawsuit and is not filed with the court.

Immediate Actions Upon Notice
  • - Two immediate and critical steps must be taken upon receiving a notice letter.
1. Immediately Notify the Carrier
  • - Forward any relevant papers to the insurance carrier without delay.
  • - Delaying notification can jeopardize coverage.
  • - Also notify the carrier if contacted by a plaintiff's lawyer who is just 'considering' a claim.
Best Strategy if Contacted by a Lawyer
  • - Refrain from discussing the case.
  • - Immediately call the insurance carrier.


2. Secure the Patient's Chart
  • - Pull the patient's chart and place it somewhere safe.
  • - CRITICAL: Make no changes, alterations, or deletions to the chart.
  • - Even the *appearance* of an alteration can have a devastating impact on the defense.


Discussing the Claim
  • - Avoid the temptation to discuss the claim with colleagues.
  • - Such conversations would likely be discoverable in a lawsuit.
  • - Discuss claims only with your insurance carrier and your lawyer.


Phase Two: The Life of a Lawsuit
  • - This phase begins with the formal filing of a petition and involves significant time and effort from the physician.
Lawsuit Initiation
  • - A lawsuit formally begins with the filing of a petition and service of a citation on the physician.
  • - Action: Immediately notify the carrier by phone, then forward copies of all documents.
Answer Deadline (Texas)
  • - A defendant's answer must be filed by the Monday following 20 days after service.
  • - Failure to file on time can result in a default judgment against the physician.


Discovery Phase
  • - The process where each party obtains relevant information and documents from the other parties.
  • - The standard is broad: anything 'likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence'.
  • - Preparing for a deposition requires the physician's undivided focus.
Forms of Discovery
  • - Discovery is conducted through several methods:
Interrogatories
  • - Written questions served by one party on another.

Requests for Disclosure
  • - Standardized requests for basic information (witnesses, experts, damages).
  • - Must be produced without objection.

Requests for Production
  • - Requests for written documentation.

Requests for Admissions
  • - Require a party to 'admit' or 'deny' specific facts.
  • - Highly time-sensitive: Failure to respond can result in the admissions being deemed against the party.

Expert Reports
  • - Parties must exchange reports containing the expert's opinion and its basis.

Depositions
  • - Question-and-answer sessions where witnesses provide sworn testimony.



Primary Defenses
  • - Key legal arguments used to defend against a malpractice claim.
Failure of Plaintiff's Expert
  • - A plaintiff must present a qualified expert to testify on both negligence and proximate cause.
Negligence
  • - Defined as the failure to use ordinary care.
  • - i.e., failing to do what a physician of ordinary prudence would have done in the same or similar circumstances.

Proximate Cause
  • - Defined as the cause that, in a continuous sequence, produces an event foreseeable by the physician.

Expert Qualifications
  • - Must be a physician practicing at the time of testimony OR at the time of the care in question.
  • - Factors include board certification, substantial training/experience, and active practice relevant to the claim.


Statute of Limitations
  • - The time period within which a plaintiff must file a lawsuit.
  • - Generally, this is 2 years from the date of the treatment in question.
Exceptions & Adjustments
  • - Three situations can adjust the strict 2-year rule:
Notice Letter
  • - Sending a notice letter extends the 2-year period by 75 days.

Minor Plaintiff
  • - The 2-year statute is 'tolled' (paused) until the minor turns 18.
  • - A claim is therefore viable until the minor turns 20.
  • - Exception: Claims for medical bills for a minor must be filed by parents within 2 years of treatment.

Discovery Rule
  • - Applies when a plaintiff could not reasonably discover the basis of the lawsuit within 2 years (e.g., a retained surgical instrument).
  • - Allows filing within a 'reasonable time' after discovery.




Mediation
  • - A nonbinding process where a neutral third-party mediator facilitates settlement discussions.
  • - The mediator can only persuade; they cannot impose a settlement.
  • - Typically takes a half or full day and requires attendance of all parties, lawyers, and an insurance representative with settlement authority.

Pretrial Modes of Disposition
  • - Methods by which a lawsuit can be resolved before reaching a trial.
Motion for Summary Judgment
  • - A dispositive motion that, if granted, is a final judgment on the merits.
  • - Often used to assert defenses like the statute of limitations or to challenge the plaintiff's expert.

Motion to Dismiss
  • - A dispositive motion typically filed when a plaintiff fails to produce a qualified expert report within the 180-day statutory period.

Voluntary Nonsuit
  • - When a plaintiff decides to drop the claim.
  • - This is not a dismissal on the merits and can be refiled if the statute of limitations has not expired.

Settlement
  • - Parties execute a compromise agreement.
  • - Can include language stating the physician does not admit negligence.
  • - The case is formally closed by a judge's order of nonsuit.



Phase Three: Trial
  • - An extremely demanding undertaking that is emotionally, physically, and psychologically exhausting.
Trial Setting & Timeline
  • - It can take several years for a case to go to trial after being filed.
  • - Typical timelines in Texas:
  • - Large county district court: 1.5 to 3 years
  • - Large county court at law: 1 to 1.5 years
  • - Small county district court: 1 to 2 years

The Physician's Role at Trial
  • - Requires complete focus and concentration.
  • - It is best for the physician to be present at the counsel table for the entire trial, which may take 1 to 2 weeks.
  • - The stressful wait for a jury's decision is termed 'sweating a jury'.


Conclusion & Key Takeaways
  • - Knowledge of the legal process allows physicians to respond appropriately to a claim.
  • - A lawsuit will require a significant investment of time, effort, and focus.
Best Practices for Physicians
  • - Involve your carrier immediately.
  • - Be open and candid with your lawyer.
  • - Be compliant, available, and ready to devote time and effort.
  • - Rely on your legal team to manage the defense so you can continue to practice medicine.
