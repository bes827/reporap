AGA Clinical Practice Guideline on the Prevention and Treatment of Hepatitis B Virus Reactivation in At-Risk Individuals
  • Source: AGA Institute, February 2025
  • Topic: Hepatitis B Reactivation Prevention and Treatment
Background & Aims
Overview of HBV Reactivation:
  • Hepatitis B virus reactivation (HBVr) occurs due to immune-modulating exposures, such as immunosuppressive therapies or disease states.
  • Baseline risk depends on serology status: HBsAg-positive vs anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative.
Aim of Guideline:
  • Update evidence-based recommendations for managing HBVr risk in at-risk individuals, incorporating new studies since the 2014 publication.
Key Objectives:
  • Address various exposures, including immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), anti-TNF therapies, and others.
  • Provide guidance on antiviral prophylaxis vs monitoring alone based on risk categories.
Methods
Framework Used:
  • Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE) approach to assess evidence quality.
Evidence Synthesis:
  • Systematic review of studies since 2014, including randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and observational data.
  • Focus on critical outcomes like HBVr incidence, hepatitis flares, chemotherapy interruption, and adverse events.
Risk Categories:
  • Defined low (<1%), moderate (1%–10%), and high (>10%) risk thresholds for HBVr.
  • These categories guide recommendations: strong for high-risk, conditional for moderate and low-risk scenarios.
Certainty Assessment:
  • Effect estimates from pooled RRs, with concerns about imprecision limiting certainty in some cases.
  • Used GRADEpro software to evaluate evidence across domains like bias, inconsistency, and feasibility.
Other Considerations:
  • Universal CDC screening guidance for HBV in adults aged ≥18 years.
  • Equity and cost-effectiveness analyses incorporated to inform recommendations.
Recommendation 1: For individuals at high risk of HBVr, the AGA recommends antiviral prophylaxis over monitoring alone. (Strong recommendation, moderate certainty evidence)
Implementation Considerations:
  • This recommendation assumes the use of antivirals with a high barrier to resistance.
  • Antiviral prophylaxis should be started before the initiation of risk-imposing therapy and continued for at least 6 months after discontinuation (at least 12 months for B-cell depleting agents).
Recommendation 2: For individuals at moderate risk of HBVr, the AGA suggests antiviral prophylaxis over monitoring alone. (Conditional recommendation, moderate certainty evidence)
Implementation Considerations:
  • This recommendation assumes the use of antivirals with a high barrier to resistance.
  • Patients who prioritize avoiding long-term antiviral use or its costs may opt for monitoring, performed at 1- to 3-month intervals including viral load assessment.
Recommendation 3: For individuals at low risk of HBVr, the AGA suggests monitoring alone over using antiviral prophylaxis. (Conditional recommendation, moderate certainty evidence)
Implementation Considerations:
  • This recommendation assumes regular and sufficient follow-up for continued monitoring.
  • Patients may choose antiviral therapy if they value avoiding reactivation risks, despite the low baseline probability.
Recommendation 4: For individuals at potential risk of HBVr, the AGA recommends testing for hepatitis B. (Strong recommendation, moderate certainty evidence)
Implementation Considerations:
  • Testing should follow CDC guidelines for universal screening in adults ≥18 years.
  • Initial serologic markers followed by viral load testing if positive or equivocal.
Risk Categories Summary:
High-Risk Exposures (Baseline risk >10%):
  • Anthracycline derivatives in HBsAg-positive patients.
  • B-cell depleting agents (rituximab, ofatumumab) in any serology status.
  • Anti-TNF therapy in HBsAg-positive patients.
Moderate-Risk Exposures (Baseline risk 1%–10%):
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors in HBsAg-positive patients.
  • Anti-TNF therapy in anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative patients with moderate baseline risk.
  • CAR-T cell therapy in anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative patients.
Low-Risk Exposures (Baseline risk <1%):
  • Methotrexate, 6-MP, azathioprine in anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative patients.
  • Corticosteroid therapy (<10 mg dose) in anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative patients.
  • Low-dose corticosteroid therapy (<10 mg) in anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative patients.

Key Findings:
  • The baseline risk of HBVr varies significantly by exposure type and patient serology.
  • For high-risk exposures, antiviral prophylaxis reduces the risk of reactivation by approximately 82%.
  • For moderate-risk exposures, antiviral prophylaxis reduces the risk by approximately 72%.
  • For low-risk exposures, antiviral prophylaxis reduces the risk by approximately 50%.
Limitations and Gaps:
Uncertainty in Risk Categories:
  • The certainty of evidence for some risk categories is limited due to concerns about imprecision.
Need for Further Research:
  • More robust data is needed, particularly for exposures with very low baseline risk.
Practical Considerations:
  • The guideline acknowledges that long-term monitoring may not be feasible for all patients.
Conclusion:
Evidence Summary:
  • The panel found moderate certainty evidence supporting antiviral prophylaxis for high-risk patients.
Clinical Recommendations:
  • Use preemptive antiviral prophylaxis for high-risk exposures.
Monitoring Strategy:
  • For moderate and low-risk patients, implement a structured monitoring plan with clear intervals.
