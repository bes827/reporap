Colonic Intussusception and Volvulus
  • Source: Chapter 155, Section IV – Colon, Rectum, and Anus
  • Topic: Adult colonic intussusception and volvulus – pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management
  • Author(s): Katerina Wells (chapter author); references include multiple studies from 1967–2015
1. Colonic Intussusception: Pathophysiology and Etiology
  • Defined as invagination of proximal bowel segment (intussusceptum) into distal segment (intussuscipiens)
  • Accounts for 1% of bowel obstructions; 86–90% associated with a pathologic lead point
  • 56% of colonic intussusceptions due to malignancy; ~19% of enteric cases linked to malignancy
  • Benign causes: benign GI neoplasms, adhesive disease, Meckel diverticulum, sprue, HIV, idiopathic
  • Proposed mechanism: luminal stimulus → proximal constriction + distal relaxation → invagination
2. Clinical Presentation and Diagnostic Challenges
  • Adults rarely present with classic pediatric triad (pain, mass, hematochezia); instead:
  • Crampy abdominal pain (71%), nausea/emesis (68%), distention (45%), tenderness (60%)
  • Often subacute/intermittent obstruction over days to years → high misdiagnosis rate
  • >50% diagnosed intraoperatively due to nonspecific symptoms and delayed presentation
3. Imaging Modalities for Diagnosis
  • CT scan: most accurate modality; pathognomonic “bowel-within-bowel” sign with compressed mesenteric vessels
  • Also shows “target sign” in axial view
  • Ultrasound: used mainly in pediatrics; adult accuracy limited by operator experience, body habitus, bowel gas
  • Transverse: “target” or “donut” sign
  • Longitudinal: “pseudo-kidney” sign
  • Plain radiography: helpful for enteric (not colonic) intussusception; “stacked coin” or “coiled spring” appearance
  • Barium enema: contraindicated if perforation/ischemia suspected; shows cup-shaped filling defect in colonic obstruction
4. Treatment Principles and Surgical Management
  • Surgery is primary treatment for adult colonic intussusception; reduction discouraged due to high malignancy risk
  • Formal oncologic resection with lymphadenectomy required; avoid reduction to prevent perforation/seeding
  • Enteric intussusception: may allow reduction if benign etiology confirmed or resection risks short gut
  • Postoperative/adhesion-related cases: reduction acceptable (benign assumption)
  • Barium studies and hydrostatic reduction contraindicated in adults due to perforation risk
5. Colonic Volvulus: Overview and Epidemiology
  • Represents 1.9% of large bowel obstructions in the U.S., but up to 10–50% in Africa, Middle East, South America
  • Defined as torsion of colon segment along its own mesentery (mesenterioaxial)
  • 180° torsion → clinical obstruction; 360° → strangulation, venous gangrene, ischemia, perforation
  • Common sites: sigmoid colon (most common), cecum, transverse colon — due to redundant loops + elongated mesentery
  • Perforation occurs at point of torsion, within closed loop, or in thin-walled proximal cecum
6. Sigmoid Volvulus: Pathophysiology, Subtypes, and Predisposing Factors
  • Accounts for 50–90% of colonic volvulus cases; two distinct subtypes:
  • Western (sporadic) subtype: elderly, diabetic, institutionalized, psychoactive medication users; chronic fecal loading from constipation → sigmoid elongation
  • Endemic subtype: Africa, India, Middle East, Latin America; young males (80%), high-fiber diet → chronic distention and elongation
  • Incidence peaks in mid-70s for both genders; increasing incidence linked to westernization/dietary shifts
  • “Dolichomesocolic colon”: vertically longer than wide sigmoid mesocolon; more common in males >30
  • Congenital theories: incomplete embryologic fixation of cecum during counterclockwise rotation
  • Other risk factors: postoperative adhesions, megacolon (especially Hirschsprung disease), pregnancy
  • Pregnancy accounts for 45% of intestinal obstructions in pregnant women; maternal mortality ~5%, up to 50% if perforated
  • Fetal demise ~30%, due to reduced placental flow from massively dilated sigmoid
7. Sigmoid Volvulus: Clinical Presentation and Diagnostic Imaging
  • Western subtype: delayed presentation (mean 3–4 days), obstipation, paradoxical diarrhea, abdominal distention
  • Abdominal pain less severe due to subacute dilation
  • Endemic subtype: acute obstruction with severe pain, distention, obstipation; often preceded by milder episodes
  • Plain radiography findings:
  • Absence of bowel gas in left iliac fossa (sigmoid rotated away)
  • “Coffee bean” sign or “omega loop” (apex pointing to right upper quadrant) in 60% of cases
  • Barium enema:
  • “Bird’s beak” sign at point of torsion/obstruction
  • Diagnostic accuracy 100% when combined with plain film findings
  • May be therapeutic (temporary reduction)
  • CT findings:
  • Mesenteric twist = highly specific for diagnosis
  • Sigmoid transition point = most sensitive finding
  • “X-marks-the-spot” sign: complete twist of adjacent sigmoid loops in “twist-tie” configuration
  • “Split wall” sign: thickened wall with fat stranding/hypoenhancement
  • Radiographic ischemia signs poorly correlate with clinical ischemia → clinical suspicion > imaging
8. Sigmoid Volvulus: Nonoperative and Operative Management
  • Endoscopic decompression (nonoperative therapy):
  • Contraindicated if ischemia/perforation suspected
  • Success rate: 60–90% in stable patients without ischemia
  • Rigid sigmoidoscopy: bedside decompression with minimal insufflation; passage through torsion allows rectal tube placement (36-French)
  • Post-procedure plain film confirms detorsion
  • Flexible endoscopy: guidewire across torsion point, then rectal tube over wire
  • Allows time for colon prep, screening colonoscopy, and surgical optimization
  • Recurrence: up to 71% after decompression; mortality in recurrent cases up to 36%
  • Recurrence interval: 2–35 months → supports urgent operative planning
  • Operative therapy:
  • Resection preferred in 89% of cases; subtotal/total colectomy needed in 16% (cecal necrosis, megacolon, colonic atony)
  • Stomas still used in ~50%; primary anastomosis safe with good judgment
  • Diverting loop ileostomy preferred over colostomy (easier care, closure feasible)
  • Nonresective options (less common):
  • Detorsion ± fixation: recurrence 9–44%, mortality 7.8%
  • Sigmoidopexy: lower mortality than detorsion alone
  • Sigmoidostomy: discouraged (high recurrence/morbidity; 13% mortality in nonfunctional patients)
  • Overall mortality: 9.4%; highest with diffuse feculent peritonitis or gangrene
  • Hartmann procedure (end sigmoid colostomy + rectal stump) safest in severe cases
  • Laparoscopy: increasing use; ideal after endoscopic decompression reduces sigmoid size
  • Not predictor of mortality, but requires adequate working space
9. Cecal Volvulus: Pathophysiology and Subtypes
  • Accounts for 25–40% of volvulus cases in U.S., 1–1.5% of all intestinal obstructions
  • Two entities:
  • Cecal volvulus: incomplete congenital fixation → cecum mobile; torsion along mesentery
  • Cecal bascule: cecum folds cephalad → “flap-valve” obstruction (no mesenteric torsion)
  • Congenital hypermobility common at autopsy (11%), but not independently causative
  • Biphasic incidence: mid-40s and late 70s; female predilection
  • 5.53% annual increase in U.S. cases (NIS data)
  • Predisposing factors: chronic constipation/cathartic use, distal obstruction, psychiatric illness, prior abdominal surgery (30–70%)
  • Postoperative adhesions act as lead point
  • Pregnancy association: gravid uterus displaces cecum, stretches ileocolic mesentery → torsion risk
  • Most proximal obstruction point: terminal ileum → symptoms mimic small bowel obstruction
10. Cecal Volvulus: Clinical Features and Imaging Diagnosis
  • Acute onset: abdominal pain, emesis, distention → may progress to peritonitis if ischemic
  • “Mobile cecum syndrome”: chronic intermittent pain relieved by flatus passage (50% of acute cases)
  • Plain radiography:
  • Massively dilated cecum resembling “kidney-bean” under left diaphragm
  • Paucity of bowel gas in right lower quadrant (cecum deviated medially)
  • Proximal small bowel dilation with air-fluid levels
  • Cecal bascule: dilated cecum in midabdomen/pelvis (no “whirl sign”)
  • Barium enema:
  • Beaking of ascending colon
  • Useful to exclude distal obstructing lesions
  • Hydrostatic reduction contraindicated (rarely successful, high perforation risk)
  • CT findings:
  • “Whirl sign” = torsion of engorged mesenteric vessels (absent in bascule)
  • Beaking of cecal margins with upstream ileal obstruction
  • Vascular insufficiency: wall thickening, fat stranding, hypoenhancement
11. Cecal Volvulus: Treatment and Outcomes
  • Endoscopic decompression not recommended: <30% success; high perforation risk in thin-walled cecum
  • Operative therapy is mainstay:
  • Resection with primary anastomosis = accepted standard; recurrence negligible post-resection
  • Cecostomy: nonresective option with high recurrence (12–13%) and complications (50% abdominal wall issues/fistula)
  • Detorsion ± cecopexy: recurrence 12–13%
  • Indications for urgent resection: ischemia, perforation, necrosis
  • Overall mortality: ~6.7%
  • Strongest predictor of death: coagulopathy (reflects severe sepsis/metabolic derangement)
  • Emphasizes need for timely diagnosis and intervention

12. Rare Volvulus Variants: Transverse Colon and Ileosigmoid Knotting
  • Transverse colon volvulus:
  • Accounts for 3% of all colonic volvulus cases
  • Normal anatomic attachments (gastrocolic, lienocolic, phrenocolic ligaments) prevent torsion
  • Occurs in setting of:
  • Congenital absence of colonic attachments
  • Acquired conditions causing colonic distention + mesenteric lengthening (e.g., chronic constipation, megacolon)
  • Presenting symptoms: similar to sigmoid/cecal volvulus; vomiting earlier due to duodenojejunal junction compression by dilated transverse colon
  • Plain radiography: “inverted coffee-bean” sign — dilated transverse colon with apex pointing to pelvis
  • Diagnosis challenges: CT or contrast enema diagnostic, but atypical findings cause delays
  • Misdiagnosed as sigmoid volvulus; failed endoscopic decompression should prompt suspicion of proximal obstruction
  • Treatment: operative intervention required
  • Resection often necessary due to redundancy/ischemia
  • Options: extended right hemicolectomy, segmental transverse colectomy, left hemicolectomy
  • Nonresective alternatives:
  • Detorsion alone
  • Detorsion + anterior abdominal wall colopexy
  • Parallel colopexy: suturing redundant proximal/distal transverse colon to ascending/descending colon
  • Recurrence likely high with nonresective approaches (assumed from cecopexy data)
  • Ileosigmoid knotting:
  • Variant of midgut volvulus; rare in West, common in endemic areas (Asia, Africa, Middle East)
  • Pathophysiology: length of ileum wraps around base of redundant sigmoid mesentery → “compound volvulus”
  • Peristalsis tightens knot → combined small bowel + distal large bowel obstruction
  • Etiology unclear; higher incidence in:
  • Males in fourth decade
  • Cultures with single daily meal (bulkier stool → distended sigmoid)
  • Predisposing factors: Meckel diverticulum, pregnancy
  • Presentation:
  • Acute small bowel obstruction symptoms
  • >50% present with late sequelae: hypovolemic shock, peritonitis
  • Diagnosis:
  • Plain radiography: dilated sigmoid with air-fluid levels in small bowel; descending colon medially deviated (tethering of sigmoid mesentery to ileal mesentery)
  • CT findings:
  • Dilated sigmoid colon
  • Dilated ileal loops
  • Whirling of mesosigmoid and small bowel mesentery
  • Treatment:
  • Endoscopic decompression contraindicated
  • Necrosis at laparotomy in 50–100% of cases
  • Should be considered if sigmoid volvulus fails endoscopic reduction
  • Mortality starkly stratified by viability:
  • Bowel necrosis at surgery: 20–100% mortality
  • Intestinal viability maintained: 6–8% mortality
  • Operative therapy = mainstay; resection dictated by extent of necrosis
13. Comparative Summary: Intussusception vs. Volvulus in Adults
  • Intussusception:
  • Primary driver: pathologic lead point (malignancy in 56% colonic cases)
  • Imaging hallmark: “bowel-within-bowel” on CT, “target/donut/pseudo-kidney” on US
  • Treatment: resection without reduction (avoid perforation/seeding)
  • Endoscopic/reduction attempts high-risk in adults; reserved for select enteric cases with benign etiology
  • Volvulus:
  • Primary driver: anatomical redundancy + mesenteric elongation → torsion
  • Imaging hallmark: “whirl sign” (cecal), “X-marks-the-spot”/“split wall” (sigmoid), “bird’s beak” (barium enema)
  • Treatment:
  • Sigmoid volvulus: endoscopic decompression first-line in stable patients → urgent semielective resection
  • Cecal volvulus: immediate surgery (endoscopy ineffective, high perforation risk)
  • Rare variants: surgical exploration + resection based on viability
  • Key contrasts in management:
  • Intussusception: avoid reduction → oncologic resection
  • Sigmoid volvulus: decompress endoscopically first → plan resection during index admission
  • Cecal volvulus: skip decompression → proceed directly to surgery
  • Recurrence risk stratification:
  • Intussusception: low post-resection (if complete oncologic resection)
  • Sigmoid volvulus: 71% recurrence after decompression alone
  • Cecal volvulus: 12–13% recurrence after detorsion ± cecopexy
  • Transverse volvulus: assumed high recurrence with nonresective procedures
14. Special Populations and High-Risk Scenarios
  • Pregnancy:
  • Sigmoid volvulus = most common non-obstetric cause of intestinal obstruction (45%)
  • Maternal mortality: 5% overall, up to 50% with perforation
  • Fetal demise: ~30%, due to reduced placental perfusion from mass effect
  • Management: prioritize maternal stabilization; endoscopic decompression possible in stable patients → urgent resection if ischemia
  • Elderly/comorbid patients:
  • Western-type sigmoid volvulus: often delayed diagnosis due to comorbid senility/psychiatric illness
  • High postoperative complication rate: respiratory, renal, infectious, thromboembolic
  • Mortality remains high (9.4% overall) despite modern techniques
  • Postoperative/adhesion-related cases:
  • Intussusception: often benign etiology → reduction acceptable
  • Volvulus (especially cecal/transverse): high likelihood of structural abnormality → resection preferred
  • Hirschsprung disease:
  • Can present as sigmoid volvulus in adults (case reports cited)
  • Requires careful preoperative evaluation for megacolon extent
  • May necessitate total colectomy if colonic atony present
15. Imaging Modalities: Comparative Diagnostic Accuracy and Limitations
  • CT:
  • Highest accuracy for both intussusception (gold standard) and volvulus (~100% accuracy)
  • Advantages: rapid, widely available, assesses ischemia (wall thickening, fat stranding, hypoenhancement), identifies complications
  • Limitations: radiation exposure; false-negative ischemia if clinical suspicion high despite normal imaging
  • Ultrasound:
  • Pediatric intussusception: sensitivity >95% in expert hands
  • Adult use limited by operator dependence, obesity, bowel gas
  • Key signs: “target” (axial), “donut” (transverse), “pseudo-kidney” (longitudinal)
  • Barium enema:
  • Diagnostic for colonic obstruction: “cup-shaped” defect (intussusception), “bird’s beak” (volvulus)
  • Contraindicated if perforation/ischemia suspected
  • Therapeutic potential in sigmoid volvulus (temporary reduction), but not definitive
  • Plain radiography:
  • First-line for suspected obstruction:
  • Intussusception: “coiled spring” (enteric only)
  • Sigmoid volvulus: “omega loop”/“coffee bean” (60% sensitivity)
  • Cecal volvulus: “kidney-bean” sign
  • Transverse volvulus: “inverted coffee bean”
  • Often insufficient alone; used to triage for CT/enema
16. Surgical Techniques and Decision Algorithms
  • Intussusception resection principles:
  • Formal oncologic resection with lymphadenectomy for malignant lead points
  • Segmental bowel resection based on location (right/colectomy, sigmoidectomy, etc.)
  • Avoid manual reduction due to perforation risk from edematous/ischemic bowel
  • Sigmoid volvulus surgical options:
  • Resection first-line (89% of cases)
  • Subtotal/total colectomy if cecal necrosis, megacolon, or colonic atony
  • Primary anastomosis feasible with adequate prep and low tension
  • Diverting loop ileostomy preferred over colostomy (easier care, higher closure rates)
