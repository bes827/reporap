IBD & Extraintestinal Manifestations (EIMs) Overview
  • - Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) is an immune-mediated disease with chronic, relapsing-remitting gastrointestinal inflammation.
  • - The clinical picture is shaped by both GI symptoms and multiple EIMs.
IBD Subtypes
  • - Ulcerative Colitis (UC): Nontransmural mucosal inflammation restricted to the colon.
  • - Crohn’s Disease (CD): Transmural inflammation with skip lesions, affecting any part of the GI tract (mouth to anus).
  • - Unclassified Colitis: Affects 10% to 15% of IBD cases, with overlapping features of UC and CD.

EIMs Classification (ECCO Guidelines)
  • - EIMs are grouped into 4 categories:
Classical (Reactive)
  • - Inflammatory process at distant sites with shared pathophysiology but different histology.

Associations
  • - Coexistence with other immune-mediated disorders.

Complications
  • - Includes treatment-induced manifestations.

Wider Associations
  • - Other related conditions.



Cutaneous Manifestations of IBD
  • - Prevalence ranges from 10% to 30% in IBD patients.
  • - Onset often coincides with disease flares but not always.
  • - Prognosis does not always correlate with IBD clinical course.
1. Reactive (Classical) Manifestations
  • - Same pathophysiologic mechanisms as IBD but different histopathology.
Erythema Nodosum (EN)
  • - The most common cutaneous EIM of IBD.
Epidemiology
  • - Overall prevalence: 1% to 15% in IBD patients.
  • - Recent meta-analysis: 3% in CD and 1% in UC.
  • - More common in women and linked to colonic illness.

Pathophysiology
  • - A type IV (delayed) hypersensitivity reaction to various antigens.
  • - Histopathology: Septal inflammation with neutrophils (early) and lymphocytes/giant cells (late).
  • - Polarized Th1 response with overexpression of IL-2 and IFN-γ.

Clinical Presentation
  • - Symmetric, tender, erythematous, warm nodules and raised plaques (1-5 cm).
  • - Lesions never ulcerate and heal without atrophy or scarring.
  • - Typically located on shins, knees, and ankles.

Diagnosis & Treatment
  • - Diagnosis is clinical. Biopsy only for persistent (>8 weeks) or atypical cases.
  • - Treatment focuses on controlling underlying IBD.
  • - Lesions self-resolve in 3 to 6 weeks.
  • - Symptomatic relief: bed rest, compression, analgesics, potassium iodide.
  • - Painful lesions: short courses of oral corticosteroids, hydroxychloroquine, anti-TNF agents.

Relevance to IBD
  • - Typically follows the clinical course of IBD.
  • - CD may raise the risk of EN (unidirectional causality).
  • - Associated IBD susceptibility loci: PTGER4, ITGAL, IKZF1, SOCS5.


Pyoderma Gangrenosum (PG)
  • - The second most common cutaneous EIM.
Epidemiology
  • - Prevalence: 0.6% to 2.6% among IBD patients.
  • - Significantly associated with female sex, CD, and other EIMs (ocular, joint).
  • - More common in Black Africans and those with a family history of UC.

Pathophysiology
  • - Involves dysregulated neutrophils and T cells (predominantly Th1).
  • - Key cytokines: IL-1α, IL-17, IL-23, IL-6, IL-8.
  • - Associated genes: PTPN6, PSTPIP1, IL8RA.

Clinical Presentation
  • - Starts as a papule or pustule ---> progresses to an ulcer with erythematous to violaceous undermined borders and a purulent base.
  • - Predilection for trunk, extensor surfaces, and peristomal areas.
  • - Exhibits pathergy (lesion enlargement after minor trauma).
Clinical Variants
  • - Ulcerative: Most common.
  • - Pustular: Almost exclusively seen in acute IBD exacerbations (usually UC).
  • - Other variants: Bullous, vegetative, peristomal, postoperative.


Diagnosis
  • - A diagnosis of exclusion; no specific lab or clinical findings.
  • - Biopsy should be avoided as it may cause lesion enlargement due to pathergy.

Treatment (See Figure 2)
  • - Requires interdisciplinary care with dermatologists.
Local Care
  • - Wound care: Gentle cleansing, sterile dressings. No debridement due to pathergy.
  • - Topical: Superpotent corticosteroids, tacrolimus.
  • - Intralesional: Corticosteroids, methotrexate, ciclosporin A.

Systemic Therapy
  • - First line: Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., oral prednisone 0.5-2 mg/kg/day).
  • - Biologics: Anti-TNF agents (Infliximab is the first drug with RCT for PG), Ustekinumab, IL-1/IL-23 inhibitors.
  • - Small Molecules: JAK inhibitors (e.g., Tofacitinib).
  • - Caution: IL-17 inhibitors should not be used in IBD patients due to risk of IBD exacerbation.


Relevance to IBD
  • - Clinical course may or may not follow intestinal disease activity.
  • - Can precede IBD diagnosis in ~15% of patients.
  • - Associated with increased risk of pouch failure in UC patients with ileal pouch-anal anastomosis.


Sweet Syndrome (SS)
  • - A rare neutrophilic dermatosis in IBD.
Epidemiology
  • - Prevalence: <0.3% of IBD patients.
  • - More frequent with colonic involvement, especially in CD.
  • - Can occur before (5.3%), alongside (29.5%), or after (64.2%) IBD diagnosis.

Pathophysiology
  • - Classified as classic (idiopathic), malignancy-associated, or drug-induced.
  • - IBD-related SS is the classic type.
  • - A drug-induced form can be caused by azathioprine (AZA), which is a hypersensitivity reaction independent of thiopurine S-methyltransferase levels.

Clinical Presentation
  • - Rapid development of tender, erythematous to violaceous papules, plaques, or nodules.
  • - Associated with fever and leukocytosis.
  • - Lesions typically on upper extremities, face, and neck.

Diagnosis & Treatment
  • - Diagnosis of exclusion; biopsy recommended.
  • - Histopathology: Dense diffuse neutrophilic infiltrate in the upper dermis.
  • - Treatment: Focus on controlling underlying IBD.
  • - First line: Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day).
  • - Second line: Biologics (infliximab, ustekinumab, vedolizumab).

Relevance to IBD
  • - Tends to parallel underlying disease activity.
  • - May be a positive prognostic factor for avoiding bowel penetrations or strictures in CD with colonic involvement.



2. Specific Manifestations
  • - Share similar histopathologic features with intestinal disease.
Extraintestinal Crohn's Disease
  • - Arises outside the GI tract.
  • - Locations: Oral, perianal, or vulvar CD.


3. Associated Disorders
  • - Inflammatory or autoimmune disorders that coexist with IBD and may share risk factors.
Hidradenitis Suppurativa (HS)
  • - Chronic, inflammatory, recurrent follicular skin disease.
Epidemiology & Pathophysiology
  • - Elevated risk in both CD (OR 2.12) and UC (OR 1.51) patients.
  • - Pathophysiology: Follicular hyperkeratosis ---> occlusion ---> rupture and inflammation.
  • - Shared factors with IBD: Gut-skin axis, upregulated TNF-α and IL-17.

Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis
  • - Painful, deep-seated, inflamed lesions in apocrine gland areas (axillary, inguinal, anogenital).
  • - Progresses to abscesses, interconnected sinus tracts, and scars.
  • - Diagnosis is clinical; no pathognomonic tests.

Treatment & IBD Relevance
  • - Treatment: Lifestyle changes (smoking cessation, weight loss), antibiotics, biologics.
  • - For HS with IBD: Adalimumab (first line) and infliximab can manage both conditions.
  • - Relevance: IBD is a causal factor for HS (but not vice versa). HS can also be a paradoxical reaction to anti-TNF therapy.


Psoriasis
  • - Chronic inflammatory papulosquamous disease.
Epidemiology & Pathophysiology
  • - Occurs in 3.6% of CD and 2.8% of UC patients.
  • - Bidirectional causality with IBD has been demonstrated.
  • - Shared pathogenesis: JAK/STAT pathway, macrophages, CD4+ T cells.

Treatment & IBD Relevance
  • - Treatment: Anti-TNF agents are recommended for both diseases.
  • - Warning: Avoid IL-17A inhibitors due to possible IBD exacerbations.
  • - Relevance: IBD may causally increase the risk of psoriasis. Psoriasiform lesions are a common paradoxical effect of anti-TNF therapy.


Other Associated Disorders
  • - A summary of other less common associated conditions.
Rosacea
  • - IBD patients are 86% more likely to develop rosacea (especially UC).
  • - IBD is considered the causal factor, not the reverse.
  • - Can also be an adverse event of anti-TNF treatment.

Atopic Dermatitis (AD)
  • - Bidirectional relationship suggested, but studies are contradictory.
  • - Children with AD may have an increased risk of developing IBD later in life.

Acne Conglobata (AC)
  • - Severe inflammatory acne; increased risk in IBD patients.
  • - First-line treatment is oral isotretinoin (0.5-1.0 mg/kg/day).

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)
  • - Higher prevalence of CD reported in SLE patients.
  • - However, one study suggests SLE is associated with a lower IBD risk in European populations.

Vitiligo
  • - Affects ~0.3% of IBD patients (UC=CD).
  • - No direct causal relationship demonstrated.

Chronic Urticaria (CU)
  • - Significant association found, but no genetic causal relationship demonstrated.

Leukocytoclastic Vasculitis (LCV)
  • - In UC, it is a true association, often occurring during a flare.
  • - In CD, it is mostly secondary to biologic therapy (infliximab, adalimumab).

Alopecia Areata (AA)
  • - Affects 6 in 1000 IBD patients.
  • - No causative relationship has been demonstrated.



4. Complications: Side Effects of IBD Therapies
  • - Biologics can cause "paradoxical" adverse events, resembling conditions they normally treat.
Paradoxical Cutaneous Reactions
  • - Occur in 25.9% of patients on anti-TNF in prospective studies.
  • - Pediatric patients seem more affected.
Implicated Drugs
  • - Anti-TNF: Infliximab (67.5%) and adalimumab (30.5%) are most common.
  • - Vedolizumab: Reports of induced PG, SS, and psoriasiform dermatitis.
  • - Ustekinumab/Risankizumab: Rare reports of paradoxical psoriasis.

Common Manifestations
  • - Most reported: Psoriasis/psoriasiform rash.
  • - Others: HS, PG, EN, vasculitis, and SLE/lupus-like lesions.

Pathophysiology & Risk Factors
  • - Mechanism: Cytokine imbalance (increased IFN-α) ---> Th17-induced inflammation.
  • - Risk Factors for psoriasiform lesions: female sex, younger age, smoking, and ileocolonic CD.

Management
  • - Avoid discontinuation of anti-TNF if possible.
  • - Most cases respond well to topical dermatological treatments.
  • - Severe cases: Switch to another biologic (e.g., ustekinumab, vedolizumab).


JAK Inhibitor-Associated Reactions
  • - Associated with increased risk for Herpes Zoster (HZ).
  • - Also associated with acneiform reactions (9.7% of patients on upadacitinib).



Herpes Zoster (HZ) in IBD
  • - Infectious vesiculopapular skin disease from reactivation of Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV).
Risk in IBD Patients
  • - IBD patients have a 1.68-fold higher risk for HZ compared to the general population.
  • - Risk Factors: Age >50, immunosuppression, active IBD, Asian race.
Drug-Specific Risks
  • - Increased risk linked to: corticosteroids, thiopurines, and combination therapies.
  • - JAK Inhibitors: A meta-analysis identified a significant association of HZ with tofacitinib (10mg twice a day) and upadacitinib (45mg every day).
  • - Safest option: Anti-IL-23 agents showed no significant rise in HZ risk.


Clinical Presentation & Complications
  • - Unilateral, dermatomal vesiculopapular rash, preceded by neuropathic symptoms.
  • - Complications: Postherpetic neuralgia is the most frequent. IBD patients have increased risk for all HZ complications except ophthalmic ones.

Diagnosis & Treatment
  • - Diagnosis: Mostly clinical. PCR for VZV DNA from active lesions is the gold standard.
  • - Treatment: Initiate systemic antivirals <72 hours after rash onset.
Antiviral Regimens
  • - Immunocompetent: Oral valaciclovir 1000mg 3 times a day for 7-10 days.
  • - Immunosuppressed: Intravenous aciclovir (8-12mg/kg 3 times a day for 7 days).


Prevention
  • - Vaccination can greatly reduce risk.
  • - The recombinant zoster vaccine (Shingrix) is preferred over live attenuated vaccines, which are contraindicated in immunocompromised individuals.
  • - Recommend vaccination for all IBD patients >50 years and high-risk patients regardless of age.


Special Considerations: Pregnancy
  • - Pregnancy can affect IBD course via hormonal alterations and may increase EIM risk.
Common Dermatoses
  • - Erythema Nodosum (EN) is the most common shared dermatosis between IBD and pregnancy.
  • - Pyoderma Gangrenosum (PG) is much rarer.

Treatment Safety in Pregnancy
  • - Considered Safe: Anti-TNF agents (though may increase risk of abortion/preterm birth), 5-ASA, sulfasalazine, thiopurines.
  • - Note: Certolizumab does not cross the placenta.
  • - Use with Caution: Corticosteroids (short courses for flares).
  • - ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED: Methotrexate (MTX) and thalidomide due to teratogenic effects.
  • - Not Recommended: JAK inhibitors and ozanimod due to limited data.


Interdisciplinary Care: Role of Dermatologists
  • - Essential for diagnosing and managing IBD-associated skin disorders.
  • - Expertise allows for accurate diagnosis, avoiding unnecessary IBD treatment modifications.
  • - Helps manage paradoxical reactions, often allowing continuation of systemic IBD therapy.
