Pearls of Wisdom in Anorectal Manometry

  • Focus: Pediatric and adult anorectal manometry.
  • Moderators: Jason Baker and Darren Brenner.

Anatomy and Physiology
  • GI tract arises from endodermal tissue as early as week 3 of embryology.
  • Anal canal separates from bladder as early as week 7 of embryology.
  • Lower GI tract: cecum, colon, rectum, anal canal → storage & elimination of waste.
Colon Function
  • Fine-tunes water absorption.
  • Contents move back & forth on the right, then to the left.
  • Transit time varies: 3 bowel movements/day or 3 bowel movements/week can be normal.
Anal Rectum
  • Regulation & defecation maintenance & continence.
  • Two sphincters: internal (involuntary) & external (voluntary).
  • Stool stretches rectal wall → reflex to brain → internal sphincter relaxes.
External Sphincter
  • Voluntary control.
  • Conscious decision to defecate or hold.
  • Holding can lead to constipation.
Anorectal Manometry
  • Techniques exist for >50 years.
  • Most widely performed test for anorectal function assessment.
  • Initially: conventional anorectal manometry w/ water perfusion catheters.
Evolution of Technology
  • Advanced to high-resolution manometry (HRM).
  • Now: high-definition manometry → pressure converted to color contour plot.
  • Measures: anal sphincter pressure, sphincter length, reflex function, sensation, defecation coordination.
Equipment
  • Three main HRM systems: Solar GI Manometry, ManoScan, Insight Manometry.
  • All use solid-state flexible catheters.
  • Closely spaced microtransducers measure pressure changes.
Catheter Details
  • Sensors are visible as small silver components.
  • Balloons are tied to the tip of the catheter.
  • Size depends on weight in pediatrics: smaller balloon ≤25 kg, adult balloon >25 kg.
Solar GI Manometry
  • Catheters are 12 French.
  • Offers high-resolution & 3D catheters.
  • 8-12 sensors provide detailed information.
Procedure
  • Catheter is lubricated & inserted into rectum w/ patient in left lateral position.
Indications
  • Suspected Hirschsprung's disease → assess rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR).
  • Non-relaxing internal anal sphincter diagnosis (anal achalasia).
  • Post-surgery evaluation (imperforate anus, rectal surgery) for fecal incontinence.
Further Indications
  • Disorders of defecation dynamics/pelvic floor → biofeedback/pelvic floor PT referral.
  • Anorectal function assessment in trauma, congenital anomalies, myelodysplasia.
  • Conscious rectal sensitivity threshold & retentive encopresis evaluation.
General Assessment
  • Sphincter strength (squeeze pressure).
  • Patient's conscious rectal sensitivity threshold.
Procedure Prep
  • Pediatrics: explained in office visit, rectal exam to assess suitability.
  • Enema before procedure (hospital setting preferred).
  • Infants <1 year: 10 mL/kg saline enema (pharmacy regulations).
Enema Details
  • If saline enema ineffective → pediatric suppository (consent required).
  • Ages 1-2: pediatric fleet enema; over 2: adult fleet enema.
  • NPO guidelines: 2 hours before (awake), anesthesia policy (anesthesia).
Anesthesia Considerations
  • Awake procedure preferred (≥5 years).
  • Infants (rule out Hirschsprung's): sweeties for distraction.
  • Anesthesia if Hirschsprung's suspected & biopsy needed.
Adult Prep
  • Rectal exam on day of manometry.
  • Enema if stool felt during exam (wait ≥30 minutes).
  • Home enemas (1-2) 1.5-2 hours before procedure.
Adult Considerations
  • Medications allowed 2 hours before.
  • Light breakfast allowed, then NPO until procedure.
Procedural Anxiety
  • Low-risk procedure, high anxiety in both pediatric & adult populations.
  • Pediatrics: oral anxiolytics (midazolam/ativan) considered, but can affect sensation results.
  • Parents present in room; multidisciplinary team (nursing, child life, provider).
Child Life Specialist Role
  • Emotional & developmental support.
  • Honest, age-appropriate wording.
  • Distraction: music, videos, stress balls, iPad games.
Adult Considerations
  • TV/iPad for distraction.
  • Rectal exam beforehand (assess for trauma/abuse).
  • Explain procedure steps to avoid surprises.
Trauma Assessment
  • Assess for trauma/abuse history.
  • Offer specific gender provider if preferred.
  • Resources available for abuse history disclosure.
Rectal Exam Details
  • Inspection: perineum for fissures, hemorrhoids, skin tags, soiling.
  • Assess pelvic floor tone.
  • Squeeze: assess anal sphincter & puborectalis contraction.
Rectal Exam Steps
  • Squeeze: feel muscle lift finger toward navel.
  • Bear down: simulate defecation; sphincter & puborectalis relax; finger moves toward expulsion.
  • Palpate posterior rectal wall for tenderness/polyps.
Abdominal Assessment
  • Place hand on abdomen & ask to simulate defecation.
  • Assess core function strength for bowel movement.
Manometry Measurements
  • Resting pressure.
  • Rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR).
  • Cough reflex.
  • Squeeze pressure.
Further Measurements
  • Rectal sensation.
  • Bear down/push.
Pitfalls
  • Lack of standardized normative data sets across institutions.
  • Fur London Classification: first classification system for anorectal function diagnosis.
Fur London Classification Subtypes
  • Disorders of rectoanal inhibitory reflex.
  • Disorders of anal tone & contractility.
  • Disorders of rhino-rectal-anal coordination.
  • Disorders of rectal sensation.
International Anorectal Physiology Working Group
  • Guidance for equipment, protocol, measurements, & interpretation.
  • Addresses limited standards across institutions.
Standardized Protocol
  • Stabilization: ≥3 minutes after catheter placement.
  • Resting pressure: 60 seconds.
  • Short squeezes: 3 sets of 5-second squeezes w/ 30-second recovery.
Further Protocol Steps
  • Long squeeze: assess squeeze endurance.
  • Coughs: 2 coughs.
  • Pushes: 3 pushes over 15 seconds each w/ 30-second recovery.
  • Rectal sensation & RAIR.
Procedure Details
  • Resting Pressure: allow patient to relax (several minutes).
  • Color Contour Plot: blue = lightest pressure, red/purple = highest pressure.
  • Anxiety can ↑ pressure in pediatrics.
Resting Pressure Parameters
  • Duration: 30-60 seconds.
  • Anesthesia can ↓ resting pressure.
  • Norms: based on adult studies (40-90 mmHg).
High Pressure Concerns
  • Hirschsprung's disease, anal achalasia, anal spasms.
  • Low Pressure Concerns: anesthesia effects, spinal cord abnormalities.
Sphincter Length
  • Light blue to top of green on color contour plot.
  • Long sphincter: difficulty pushing.
  • Short sphincter: mega rectum, anorectal surgery, leakage.
Length Norms
  • Younger babies: 2.0-4.0 cm.
  • Older kids/adults: 2.5-4.5 cm (variations exist).
Rectoanal Inhibitory Reflex (RAIR)
  • Performed after resting pressure (pediatrics).
  • Stool triggers reflex → internal sphincter relaxes.
  • Relaxation visible on color contour plot.
RAIR Interpretation
  • Clear relaxation indicates normal sphincter function.
  • Relaxation significantly reduces concern for Hirschsprung's.
Cough Reflex Test
  • Quick spinal check.
  • Anal pressure > rectal pressure.
  • High pressures (red) = cough; low pressures (dark blue) = balloon/rectal pressure.
Cough Reflex Interpretation
  • Darker colors at bottom, lighter at top.
  • Weak cough → spinal imaging (tethered cord).
  • Cough reflex modulated by spine.
Squeeze Pressure
  • Voluntary squeeze for 15-30 seconds (endurance squeeze).
  • Squeeze pressure should double resting pressure.
  • Assess ability to hold squeeze.
Squeeze Interpretation
  • Inability to hold squeeze → leakage.
  • Focus for physical therapy: training external sphincter.
  • Provider observation: ensure correct muscle use.
Rectal Sensitivity
  • Use patient's home language for clarity.
  • Ask about: sensation, urge to defecate, discomfort/emergency.
  • Younger kids: hard to differentiate sensation/urge.
Sensation Explanation
  • Explain feeling the catheter/tube.
  • Ask when they feel something different, urge to poop, emergency.
  • Repeat test ≥2 times for consistency.
Sensation Norms
  • Depends on balloon size (150 cc or 400 cc max).
  • Normal sensation: 20-60 cc.
  • Urge: 40-80 cc.
  • Discomfort: 60-120 cc.
Sensitivity Interpretation
  • Rapid sensation/urge/discomfort → urgency.
  • No sensation even at max inflation → learning body signals.
Bear Down/Push
  • Attempt to defecate, push catheter out.
  • Relax pelvic floor.
  • Squeeze & push look identical → incorrect pushing technique.
Bear Down Interpretation
  • Good abdominal core pressure + relaxed pelvic floor.
  • Inability to perform maneuver → dyssynergic defecation.
  • Performed on left side (not typical defecation position).
Balloon Expulsion
  • Catheter inserted w/ 50 mL warmed water.
  • Patient sits on toilet in proper position w/ footstool.
  • Time to push out balloon.
Balloon Expulsion Norms
  • Normal: <1 minute.
  • Up to 5 minutes allowed for delayed reaction.
  • Combined w/ anorectal manometry → coordination of muscles for defecation.
Biofeedback
  • Biofeedback & pelvic floor physical therapy referral for dyssynergic defecation.
Trauma and Anorectal Manometry
  • Importance of addressing trauma history (physical, emotional, sexual abuse).
  • Asking about trauma can change thought process regarding constipation, testing, & treatment.
  • Non-judgmental approach: binary (yes/no) answer.
Procedure Modifications
  • Show catheter online/in lab.
  • Walk through steps of process.
  • Match gender of provider to patient.
Behavioral Therapy
  • Engage behavioral therapists.
  • Sessions may be needed before testing.
  • Therapist may accompany patient during study.
Cough Reflex Abnormalities
  • Anal pressure > rectal pressure during cough.
  • Opposite → incontinence episodes.
  • Ratio of pressures is important.
Clinical Correlation
  • Higher anal sphincter pressure over pressure coming down → maintain continence during laughing, giggling, sneezing, coughing.
Pediatric Perspective
  • Assess for spinal abnormalities (tethered cord).
  • MRI of L-spine if needed.
Extreme Sensitivity/Rectal Hypersensitivity
  • Initial sensation with minimal inflation.
  • Pattern over time is important.
  • Rectal hypersensitivity can be linked to IBS or inflammatory conditions.
Differential Diagnosis
  • Ulcerative proctitis, post-radiation effects (hypocompliant rectum).
  • Urge fecal incontinence.
Pediatric Handling
  • Less common in pediatrics.
  • Mega rectum more common (inflate to max, no sensation).
  • Solitary rectal ulcer: rectal treatment first before ARM.
Abnormal RAIR vs. Anal Spasm
  • High pressures during RAIR: anxiety, squeezing sphincter.
  • Higher balloon volumes may be needed to overcome pressure.
  • Spasms during conscious rectal sensitivity: wait for it to pass.
Adult Perspective
  • Ensure adequate latency periods between inflations (30 seconds).
  • Wait for pressure to return to baseline.
RAIR Interpretation
  • Initial contraction followed by relaxation.
  • If relaxation seen at lower volumes, RAIR is intact.
Lack of Pediatric Norms
  • No great pediatric norms.
  • Norms based on middle-aged women study.
  • Allow larger range slightly.
Clinical Correlation
  • Correlate findings clinically to patient's symptoms.
  • Extremes are easier to identify.
London Consensus
  • Use upper/lower level thresholds due to uncertainty of normal values.
Water vs. Air Inflation
  • Warm water for balloon expulsion.
  • Water not used in HRM due to company recommendations.
  • Temperature sensitivity in some children (autistic spectrum).
Clinical Observations
  • Water balloon expulsion more uncomfortable than air.
  • No studies differentiate air vs. water in decent population.
Seated Position
  • Add seated position for bear downs.
  • Air-filled balloon for ARM, water for expulsion.
  • Water adds more volume/depth, sensation like passing stool.
Balloon Inflation Volume
  • >25 kg: do not necessarily inflate all the way to 400 cc.
  • Depends if they feel sensation or not.
  • Stop if discomfort felt at 120 cc.
Adult Considerations
  • Literacy of understanding instructions.
  • Talkback method: patient repeats definitions back.
Constipation and Defecatory Patterns
  • Reconciling normal defecatory pattern with abnormal balloon expulsion test.
  • Balloon expulsion testing has best sensitivity/specificity for pelvic floor dysfunction.
Clinical Practice
  • If manometry okay but can't pass balloon in 3 minutes, refer for physical therapy/biofeedback.
Pediatric Approach
  • ARM, balloon expulsion, biofeedback gain evaluation.
  • Compare all three results.
  • Two out of three abnormal → abnormal dynamics.
