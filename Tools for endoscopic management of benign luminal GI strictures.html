Tools for endoscopic management of benign luminal GI strictures
  • Source: ASGE TECHNOLOGY COMMITTEE
  • Journal: GASTROINTESTINAL ENDOSCOPY
  • Publication Year: 2025
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the reported median perforation rate for pneumatic balloon dilation in achalasia when performed by an experienced endoscopist?
  • 2. In refractory peptic esophageal strictures, what was the key finding of a randomized trial comparing intralesional steroid injection to sham injection?
  • 3. What is the "rule of 3" in bougie dilation, and what have recent large studies shown about adherence to it?
  • 4. For which type of benign esophageal stricture has electrosurgical incision therapy been shown to provide a longer remission of dysphagia compared to bougienage?
  • 5. A meta-analysis comparing bougie and balloon dilators for benign esophageal strictures found no difference in major outcomes, but which method was associated with less post-procedure pain?
Introduction to Benign Luminal GI Strictures
  • Can occur in any part of the GI tract.
  • Arise from benign & malignant causes.
  • Dilation is necessary when there is impaired passage of luminal contents, need for diagnosis, or therapy beyond the stricture.
Dilators for the GI Lumen
  • Dilation is achieved by applying expansible forces against a stenosis.
  • Two main categories: fixed-diameter push-type dilators & radial-expanding balloon dilators.
Bougie Dilators
  • Primarily used for esophageal strictures.
  • Exert both radial forces & longitudinal shearing forces as they are advanced.
Non–wire-guided
  • Examples: Hurst (blunt tip) & Maloney (tapered tip) dilators.
  • Characteristics: Flexible, radiopaque, tungsten-filled rubber bougies.
  • Use: Passed blindly w/ patient upright; rarely used for self-dilation now due to safety concerns.
Wire-guided
  • Flexible, tapered, polyvinyl chloride tubes w/ a central channel for a guidewire.
  • Examples: Savary-Gilliard (most common), American Dilation System, SafeGuide.
Features
  • Savary-Gilliard: 20-cm long tapered tip, radiopaque band at maximal diameter.
  • American Dilation System/SafeGuide: Shorter tapered tip, radiopaque band throughout length.
  • CleanGuide: New disposable wire-guided bougie.
Reprocessing & Durability
  • Most bougies are reusable, but reprocessing is challenging.
  • Heightened cleaning/sterilization standards → ↓ durability & cost-effectiveness of reusable devices.
  • Push for disposable dilators vs. 'green endoscopy' focus on reducing waste.
Balloon Dilators
  • Exert only radial forces when expanded within a stenosis.
  • Available in various designs, lengths, and calibers.
Through-the-scope (TTS) Balloons
  • Facilitate direct visual control during dilation.
  • Can be used w/ or w/o wire guidance.
  • Disposable, made of low-compliance thermoplastic polymers for uniform expansion.
Technique
  • Expanded by pressure injection of liquid (water or radiopaque contrast).
  • Pressure monitored manometrically.
  • Inflation w/ contrast enhances fluoroscopic assessment.
Achalasia Balloon Dilators
  • Large-diameter (30, 35, 40 mm) polyethylene balloons.
  • Wire-guided, disposable, do not pass through the endoscope.
  • Positioned across the esophagogastric junction under fluoroscopy.
Use
  • Primary indication: Pneumatic dilation for achalasia.
  • Off-label use: Stenosis after gastric sleeve or fundoplication.
BougieCap
  • Single-use, transparent, conical hard plastic cap mounted on the endoscope tip.
  • The endoscope itself becomes the dilator, applying radial & longitudinal shear forces.
  • Available in sizes 7-18 mm.
Advantages
  • Direct visual control & tactile feedback → may prevent overdilation.
  • Real-time evaluation immediately after dilation.
  • Advantageous for high cervical esophageal strictures.
Evidence & Safety
  • Two prospective multicenter studies (50 patients each) showed technical feasibility, clinical efficacy, & safety.
  • No significant adverse events reported.
  • Adverse Event: Slipping of the cap occurred in 3 cases.
EsoFLIP
  • Novel esophageal dilator incorporating high-resolution impedance planimetry.
  • Provides real-time graphic illustration of the esophageal lumen (diameter, cross-sectional area, etc.).
  • Enables therapeutic hydraulic dilation.
Indications & Sizes
  • Use: Achalasia & benign esophageal strictures (GERD, radiation, surgery).
  • Sizes: Up to 30-mm diameter for achalasia; up to 20-mm for benign strictures.
Potential Advantages
  • Avoids fluoroscopy → ↓ radiation risk.
  • Controlled dilation over a wide range of diameters w/ a single balloon.
  • Objective measurement of stricture size & immediate response to dilation.
Potential Disadvantages
  • Not a TTS catheter.
  • Requires an external pressure monitor.
  • Guidewire must be removed before dilation.
  • Time required to fill & empty the balloon.
Self-expandable Stents
  • Used for refractory benign strictures, accomplishing dilation via their radial expansion forces.
  • Types: Covered self-expandable metal stents (SEMSs), self-expandable plastic stents, lumen-apposing metal stents (LAMSs).
  • Biodegradable stents are available but not in the United States.
Topical Use of Antifibrotic Drugs
  • Used in refractory or recurrent benign esophageal strictures to reduce restenosis.
Steroids (Intralesional/Oral)
  • Use: Peptic, radiation-induced, caustic, & anastomotic strictures.
  • Also used for prevention of stricture formation after EMR/ESD.
Efficacy
  • Randomized trial (peptic strictures): Intralesional steroid injection → fewer dilations over 1 year vs. sham.
  • Systematic review: Advantage for peptic, radiation-induced, & caustic strictures; less clear for anastomotic.
  • Network meta-analysis (post-ESD): Oral or intralesional steroids → ↓ stenosis rate & number of required dilations vs. placebo.
Mitomycin C (MMC)
  • Chemotherapy agent w/ antifibrotic properties.
  • Common technique: 4-quadrant injections of 0.4 mg/mL MMC in 0.5 mL aliquots.
Efficacy
  • Systematic review (adults & children): Complete response 73%, partial response 21%.
  • Study (anastomotic/post-ESD strictures): Dysphagia-free period was longer in MMC group (4.88 months) vs. dexamethasone (4.02 months) & dilation alone (2.41 months).
Paclitaxel
  • Inhibits proliferation & migration of fibroblasts.
  • Application: Paclitaxel-coated balloons.
Evidence
  • Pilot study: High technical success, no adverse events or stricture recurrence.
  • Further large-scale trials are needed.
PuraStat
  • Self-assembling peptide hypothesized to act as a biological wound dressing.
  • Protects wound from luminal stress, reducing inflammation & fibrosis.
  • Studies show safety & ease of application; long-term data needed.
Technique of Dilation
  • Choice of dilator is determined by endoscopist preference & clinical scenario.
  • Dilator diameter measured in mm or French (1 mm = 3F).
General Principles
  • Pre-procedure assessment: Location, length, diameter, etiology.
  • Biopsy to rule out malignancy.
  • Fluoroscopy guidance for complex strictures (angulated, long, very narrow).
The 'Rule of 3'
  • Traditional rule for bougie dilation: After moderate resistance, pass no more than 3 dilators of progressively increasing diameter (≤3 mm per session).
  • Recent evidence: At least 2 large retrospective studies showed non-compliance did not ↑ risk of perforation or other adverse events.
Specific Scenarios & Modifications
  • Schatzki ring: A single large-diameter dilator (16-20 mm) is advocated to disrupt the ring.
  • Very narrow strictures: Initial dilation should be restricted to 10-12 mm.
  • Refractory strictures: May require steroid/antifibrotic injection, electrocautery incision, or stent placement.
Do's and Don'ts
  • DO: Perform comprehensive pre-procedure assessment & obtain biopsies.
  • DO: Use wire-guidance & fluoroscopy for complex cases to enhance safety.
  • DON'T: Overdilate; perform graded dilation.
  • DO: Consider alternative therapies (stents, surgery) for refractory strictures.
Outcomes & Comparative Data
  • Data varies based on stricture location and etiology.
Esophagus
  • Most common cause of benign strictures: GERD.
  • Simple strictures (<2 cm, straight) vs. Complex (>2 cm, tortuous, narrow).
Bougie vs. Balloon Dilation
  • Meta-analysis (5 trials, 461 patients): No significant difference in symptomatic relief, 1-year recurrence, bleeding, or perforation rates.
  • Finding: Balloon dilation associated w/ less severe post-procedure pain.
Schatzki Ring
  • Treatment: Rupture of the ring.
  • Effective methods: Single large-caliber dilation (16-20 mm), biopsy disruption, electrosurgical incision.
  • Electrosurgical incision: May offer longer dysphagia remission vs. bougienage.
  • Medical therapy: Long-term PPI therapy significantly ↓ risk of relapse.
Anastomotic Strictures
  • First-line therapy: Endoscopic interventions (balloon dilation, electrosurgical incision).
  • Radial incision & cutting: May have higher 6-month & 12-month patency rates vs. repeated balloon dilation for refractory cases.
Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE)
  • Esophageal dilation is a safe & effective treatment combined w/ medical/dietary therapy.
  • Bougie & balloon dilators have similar safety & efficacy profiles in EoE.
Refractory Strictures
  • Rescue strategy: Temporary placement (6-12 weeks) of fully covered SEMS (FCSEMS).
  • Pooled clinical success rate for stents: 40.5%.
  • LAMS: Clinical success rate of 63%, but w/ an adverse event rate of 27.9%.
Achalasia
  • Pneumatic balloon dilation (≥30 mm) is an accepted primary therapy.
  • Symptom relief: 74% (30 mm), 86% (35 mm), 90% (40 mm) of patients.
  • Long-term efficacy: Equivalent to laparoscopic Heller myotomy at 10-year follow-up.
Stomach & Small Bowel
  • Causes: PUD, NSAIDs, postsurgical changes, pancreaticobiliary disease.
  • Typically treated w/ TTS balloon dilators.
Gastric Outlet Obstruction (GOO)
  • First-line treatment: Endoscopic balloon dilation.
  • Failure more likely in: Long strictures (>3 cm), caustic ingestion, chronic pancreatitis.
Crohn's Disease
  • Endoscopic balloon dilation is efficacious, especially for strictures <4 cm.
  • Success rates: Technical 92.6%, Clinical 70.2%.
  • Adverse events: Overall 6.4%, Perforation 3%.
  • Long-term outcome: Cumulative surgery rate at 5 years is 75%.
Stents in Crohn's Disease
  • FCSEMS are not first-line therapy.
  • ProtDilat trial: Endoscopic balloon dilation was superior to FCSEMS; 80% of balloon group vs. 51% of stent group were free of re-intervention at 1 year.
Colon
  • Etiologies: Anastomotic, NSAIDs, diverticulitis, radiation, IBD.
  • Balloon or bougie-type dilators are effective.
Comparative Data
  • Prospective trial (anastomotic strictures): Large pneumatic balloon (35 mm) required fewer sessions (1.6 vs 2.6) & had longer response (560 vs 245 days) compared to smaller TTS balloon (18 mm).
Safety & Adverse Events
  • Endoscopic dilation is generally safe when performed by experienced endoscopists.
  • Minor mucosal tears & bleeding are expected.
Perforation
  • Most clinically significant adverse event.
  • Overall rate for esophageal strictures: 0.1% to 0.6%.
Risk Factors
  • Stricture-related: Narrow, angulated, caustic, or radiation-induced.
  • Patient-related: Large hiatal hernia, esophageal diverticula.
  • Operator-related: Inexperience.
Specific Scenarios
  • EoE: Dilation is NOT associated w/ a higher perforation risk.
  • Achalasia: Median perforation rate w/ pneumatic dilation is 1.9% (range 0-16%).
  • GOO: Perforation occurred in 3.4% of cases in a large series.
Other Adverse Events
  • Bacteremia: Transient bacteremia reported in up to 23% of cases; prophylactic antibiotics not routinely recommended.
  • Bleeding: Clinically significant bleeding occurs in <0.5% of cases.
  • Electrosurgical Incision: Associated w/ a perforation risk of up to 3.5%.
Financial Considerations
  • Reusable dilators offer cost benefits over disposable devices, even with reprocessing expenses.
  • Stents are significantly more expensive than dilation catheters & balloons.
CPT Codes & Reimbursement (2023 Medicare National Average)
  • Esophageal Dilation (Balloon <30mm): CPT 43220/43249
  • Esophageal Dilation (over guidewire): CPT 43226
  • Achalasia Dilation (Balloon >30mm): CPT 43233
  • Gastric Outlet Dilation: CPT 43245
  • Colonoscopy w/ Balloon Dilation: CPT 45386
Areas for Future Research
  • Optimal technique for refractory strictures needs clarification.
  • Prospective studies needed on antifibrotic drugs to reduce recurrence.
  • Head-to-head comparisons between newer stents (e.g., LAMSs) and traditional dilation techniques are needed.
Conclusions
  • Fixed-diameter push-type dilators & radial expansion balloon dilators are safe & effective for benign GI strictures.
  • Both approaches have similar efficacy & safety profiles.
  • Newer technologies (EsoFLIP, BougieCap, stents, electroincision) address limitations of older tools.
