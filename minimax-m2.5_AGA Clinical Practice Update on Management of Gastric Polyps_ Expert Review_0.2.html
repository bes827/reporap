AGA Clinical Practice Update on Management of Gastric Polyps: Expert Review
  • Source: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology 2026 (AGA Institute)
  • Topic: Diagnosis and Management of Gastric Mucosal Polyps
  • Author(s): Anna M. Buchner, Robert J. Huang, Gregory Y. Lauwers, Hashem B. El-Serag
1. Overview and Scope
  • CPU provides framework for understanding natural history, epidemiology of gastric polyps
  • Advises on: endoscopic detection, classification, resection, and surveillance
  • Addresses sampling/surveillance of mucosal pathology giving rise to polyps (atrophy, pseudo-pyloric/intestinal metaplasia)
  • Complements AGA documents on gastric intestinal metaplasia, atrophic gastritis, high-quality upper endoscopy, and gastric cancer screening
2. Epidemiology, Risk Factors, and Natural History (BPA 1–3, 5–6)
2.1 Prevalence and Distribution
  • Gastric polyps frequently identified during upper endoscopy exams
  • In Western countries: 70%–94% are FGPs or GHPs
  • Multiple polyp types coexist in 2%–3% of patients
  • Prevalence study (2007–2008): 6.35% of patients had gastric polyps
  • 77% FGPs, 17% GHPs, 0.69% GAs, 0.1% inflammatory fibroids
  • Higher prevalence (2008–2013): 7.72% FGPs, 1.79% GHPs, 0.09% GAs, 0.06% type 1 G-NETs
  • Trend shift: GHPs decreased from 65% to 15%; FGPs increased from 19% to 77% (2004–2013, Beijing study)
  • Prevalence of GAs, GHPs, neuroendocrine lesions ↑ with age; FGPs ↓ after age 60
  • Female predominance for FGPs; equal/slight female predominance for GAs and GHPs
2.2 Fundic Gland Polyps (FGPs)
  • Prevalence: 47%–77% of polyps
  • Site: body; can be multiple; small, sessile, usually <1 cm, glassy surface
  • Associated with: PPI use, FAP, GAPPS
  • Surrounding mucosa usually normal without inflammatory changes
  • Dysplasia in up to 48% of FAP-associated FGPs; <1% in sporadic lesions
  • Gastric adenocarcinoma progression in up to 15% of FAP cases
  • Risk factors for dysplasia: mucosal carpeting by polyps, size >1 cm
  • Risk inversely associated with gastric atrophy, H pylori infection, GIM
  • Positive associations: GERD and PPI use
2.3 Gastric Hyperplastic Polyps (GHPs)
  • Prevalence: 17%–55% of polyps
  • Common in regions with high H pylori prevalence
  • Site: antrum > body; single or multiple; sessile or pedunculated, <20 mm
  • Surface erosions may occur in larger polyps
  • Associated with: chronic gastritis (H pylori-related, autoimmune)
  • Dysplasia detected in ~4% of polyps
  • Malignant transformation: 0.8%–10%
  • Neoplastic polyps tend to be >20 mm
  • H pylori eradication leads to 59% reduction in GHP prevalence (95% CI, 43%–75%)
  • Successful eradication: 79% reduction (95% CI, 72%–86%)
2.4 Gastric Adenomas (GAs)
  • Prevalence: 1%–10% of polyps
  • Site: antrum > body; usually single; flat or sessile, <20 mm
  • Associated with: chronic gastritis (H pylori-related, autoimmune gastritis)
  • Subtypes: intestinal-type (56%), foveolar-type (41%)
  • Progression risk: 3%–4% for low-grade lesions; 5%–30% for high-grade
  • Risk ↑ with polyp size, particularly >20 mm
  • Foveolar-type GA in H pylori-naïve stomach: very low to nonexistent transformation risk (raspberry-like appearance)
  • Surrounding gastric mucosa carries higher risk of malignant transformation
2.5 Pyloric Gland Adenomas
  • Prevalence: <3% of polyps
  • Site: body > antrum; usually single; sessile, pedunculated, or masses; 1–10 cm
  • Associated with: autoimmune gastritis; second most common polyp in FAP; also seen in Lynch syndrome, juvenile polyposis
  • High-grade dysplasia in up to 42% of cases
  • Progression: 12%–30% of cases
  • Risk factors for high-grade dysplasia: larger polyp size, tubulo-villous architecture, autoimmune gastritis
  • In Lynch syndrome: higher-grade associated with male sex
2.6 Oxyntic Gland Adenomas
  • Rare, slow-growing neoplasm
  • Site: body; polypoid or raised flat; solitary; 2–20 mm, typically <10 mm
  • Unique: arises from oxyntic mucosa; multilineage phenotype (chief cells, parietal cells, mucous neck cells)
  • Usually normal adjacent gastric mucosa
  • Large polyps and cases with mucus neck cell differentiation likely to progress
2.7 Hamartomatous Polyps
  • Juvenile polyps: rare; body > antrum; pedunculated or sessile; 2–20 mm
  • Peutz-Jeghers polyps: rare; any site; polypoid, lobulated on short broad stalk; solitary or multiple; 1–3 cm
  • Cowden syndrome-associated: rare; any site; small, sessile, polypoid; multiple
  • Cronkite-Canada syndrome-associated: rare; any site; sessile or flat with diffuse mucosal thickening
  • Progression: juvenile up to 14%; Peutz-Jeghers 2%–3%; Cowden low risk; Cronkite-Canada very rare
2.8 Gastric Neuroendocrine Tumors (G-NETs)
  • Uncommon; up to 2% of all gastric polyps
  • Types:
  • Type 1: body atrophy/GIM with ECL cell proliferation; >70% of cases; multiple, ≤2 cm
  • Type 2: hypertrophic with ECL hyperplasia; MEN-1 and gastrinoma; multiple, ≤1 cm
  • Type 3: normal surrounding mucosa; solitary, >2 cm; worst prognosis
  • Most (>70%) arise in setting of autoimmune gastritis (type 1)
  • Limited metastasis for types 1 and 2; higher rate for type 3
2.9 PPI Use and Gastric Polyps
  • PPI use associated with 1.5–2.5-fold ↑ risk of FGPs
  • PPI use ≥12 months: up to 5-fold increase in FGP risk
  • Meta-analysis (12 studies through 2016): significant association 1.43 (95% CI, 1.24–1.64) fixed-effects; 2.
3. Endoscopic Detection and Characterization of Gastric Polyps (BPA 4, 7–8)
3.1 Systematic Endoscopic Examination Requirements
  • BPA 4: systematic endoscopic examination of polyps and surrounding gastric mucosa essential for assessing underlying pathology (H pylori-related gastritis, autoimmune gastritis, GIM)
  • Determines subsequent management: biopsies of polyps, surrounding mucosa, and polyp resection
  • BPA 8: evaluation should include complete inspection with HD-WLE and enhanced imaging (virtual chromoendoscopy)
  • Endoscopists must recognize and photo-document endoscopic features of polyps and surrounding mucosal abnormalities
  • Entire gastric mucosa should be carefully inspected using HD-WLE with adequate stomach distension, clearance of mucus debris
  • Photo documentation required for gastric landmarks, lesions, and mucosal alterations
  • HD-WLE alone is suboptimal for diagnosing mucosal alterations (gastric atrophy, GIM) or distinguishing dysplastic/cancerous polyps
  • Rate of neoplastic lesions missed on upper endoscopy resulting in cancer diagnosis within 3 years: 4.7% to 11.3%
  • Subtle mucosal lesions surrounding GIM associated with interval development of early cancer
3.2 Polyp Characteristics by Type (BPA 7)
  • GHPs: sessile or pedunculated, <20 mm diameter, typically single polyp in antrum; can arise as multiple polyps anywhere in gastric mucosa
  • FGPs: usually small, multiple, sessile; uniquely located in gastric body and fundus; in FAP setting can coalesce and become dysplastic
  • Hamartomatous polyps: typically sessile, solitary, predominantly in gastric body; not easily distinguishable from GHPs
  • GAs: typically single, flat, <20 mm, occur in gastric antrum; larger sizes >20 mm with villous histology have higher cancer risk
  • G-NETs: small type 1 or type 2 sessile lesions associated with autoimmune gastritis or Zollinger-Ellison syndrome with MEN-1; can exist with/without central depression; occur in gastric body/fundus
  • Type 3: ~15% of NETs; solitary, larger polyps up to 5 cm; not associated with hypergastrinemia; worst prognosis
3.3 Image-Enhanced Endoscopy (IEE) Techniques
  • Virtual chromoendoscopy with IEE includes: NBI, BLI, LCI with or without magnifying endoscopy
  • Improves detection and characterization of dysplastic gastric lesions and abnormal gastric mucosa
  • Enhances color contrast between dysoplastic areas and surrounding mucosa
  • Enables clear visualization of surface structures and blood vessels during close-up observations
  • LCI: highlights purple color of GIM, distinguishing from inflammatory gastric mucosa; aids identification of early dysplastic/neoplastic lesions adjacent to GIM
3.4 NBI Classification System
  • Validation study by Pimentel-Nunes et al proposed simplified classification:
  • Pattern A: regular vessels with circular mucosa → normal histology (accuracy 83%; 95% CI, 75%–90%)
  • Pattern B: tubulo-villous mucosa → linked to GIM (accuracy 84%; LR+, 4.75)
  • Pattern C: irregular vessels and mucosa → associated with dysplasia (accuracy 95%; 95% CI, 90%–99%; LR+, 44.33)
  • "Light-blue crest" sign: moderate reliability (k = 0.49) but specific (87%) for GIM
  • Variable vascular density: best feature for identifying H pylori-related gastritis (accuracy 70%)
3.5 Early Gastric Cancer Detection
  • Endoscopic diagnostic criteria (vessel-plus-surface classification): irregular microvascular pattern and/or irregular microsurface pattern with demarcation line separating early cancerous lesion from normal mucosa
  • Magnifying BLI and NBI: similar sensitivities for detecting demarcation line (96.1% vs 98.1%)
  • Irregular microvessel patterns: sensitivities 95.1% (BLI) and 96.2% (NBI)
  • Irregular microsurface patterns: 97.1% with BLI vs 78.8% with NBI
3.6 Additional Endoscopic Classifications
  • Kimura-Takemoto classification: for gastric atrophy (not routinely applied in US practice)
  • EGGIM (endoscopic grading of GIM): for gastric intestinal metaplasia assessment
  • Interobserver reliability of endoscopic findings: moderate overall; depends on years of experience and training
  • Future implementation could help standardize findings, develop targeted biopsy guidance, optimize procedures
3.7 Guidelines and Recommendations
  • AGA, American College of Gastroenterology, European organizations recommend routine use of IEE as guiding tool for assessing atrophic/metaplastic changes and targeting dysplastic lesions
  • Computer-assisted AI systems introduced for early detection of dysplastic lesions
  • IEE commonly used in Eastern countries; underutilized by US endoscopists
  • Until US endoscopists acquire skills to distinguish mucosal abnormalities using IEE: meticulous evaluation and random/targeted biopsies of polyps and surrounding mucosa remain essential
4. Endoscopic Management of Gastric Polyps and Surrounding Gastric Mucosa (BPA 9–11)
4.1 Resection Techniques Overview
  • BPA 9: endoscopic resection includes traditional techniques (snare, biopsy forceps, mucosal resection) or ESD
  • Goal: completely remove lesion while providing tissue for accurate pathological assessment of most advanced features
  • Technique choice incorporates: presumed polyp pathology, size, location, endoscopist expertise
4.2 Biopsy Forceps Polypectomy
  • Little direct data on cold forceps for small gastric polyps ≤3 mm; supported by colon literature
  • Large prospective study (colonic polyps ≤3 mm): cold forceps polypectomy (2.4-mm-diameter large forceps) noninferior to cold snare for complete resection; faster procedure
  • Polyps ≥4 mm should NOT be resected using forceps technique
4.3 Snare Polypectomy and EMR
  • Prospective studies evaluated forceps vs EMR for larger polyps (≥5 mm): all 4 studies showed forceps cannot reliably establish underlying
4.3 Snare Polypectomy and EMR (continued)
  • neoplasia in larger polyps
  • For polyps 4–10 mm: cold snare or EMR are appropriate
  • Taiwan study: cold snare noninferior to EMR regarding adverse events (most commonly bleeding) in gastric polypectomy
  • Taiwanese study: cold snare safe and effective for removing FGPs 3–8 mm in size
  • EMR adverse events: largest study shows bleeding rate ~7%, majority managed endoscopically; perforation rare
4.4 Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD)
  • Should be restricted to polyps where neoplasia is diagnosed or strongly suspected
  • Single-center Korean study of 282 gastric adenomatous polyps removed by ESD:
  • High complete resection rate (96%)
  • Low bleeding rate (1.4%)
  • No perforations
  • Lesion size >20 mm predictive of early gastric cancer
  • Two reports: gastric ESD can be performed safely and efficiently in the West
  • Both EMR and ESD allow for en bloc resection depending on polyp size/morphology; even >20 mm pedunculated polyp can be resected en bloc by EMR
  • Referral to endoscopist with advanced resection expertise advised if polyp size exceeds 20 mm
4.5 Management Strategy for Newly Diagnosed Polyps
  • All newly detected solitary polyps should be resected at index endoscopy (diagnostic and therapeutic intent)
  • For multiple gastric polyps: use knowledge of regional polyp epidemiology, clinical history, polyp characteristics to inform approach
  • If all suspected to be FGPs: resect largest suspected FGP; other suspected FGPs can be resected if large (>10 mm) or visually atypical
  • If not all polyps believed to be FGPs:
  • At minimum: resect largest suspected non-FGP polyp to establish histologic diagnosis and assess for deeper neoplasia
  • If procedural factors allow: complete removal of all suspected non-FGP polyps may obviate need for second procedure
  • Use separate specimen jars for each suspected polyp type
4.6 Biopsy Sampling Protocols (BPA 11)
  • Suspected abnormalities in surrounding mucosa (GIM or AG) should undergo targeted biopsies according to existing protocols
  • If suspected non-FGP detected on index endoscopy: mucosal sampling using updated Sydney System or MAPPS II protocol
  • If solitary gastric polyp observed: systematic mucosa sampling recommended
  • Topographic pattern of inflammation, atrophy, GIM from protocols helps distinguish H pylori-related vs autoimmune gastritis
  • Updated Sydney System: biopsies from five zones (antrum lesser/greater curves, incisura, body lesser/greater curves); biopsies placed in separate jars (antrum and body); targeted biopsies of visible lesions in separate jar
5. Post-Endoscopic Treatment Management and Surveillance (BPA 12–14)
5.1 Surveillance Framework Based on Polyp Type
  • BPA 12: surveillance plans formulated based on histopathological type of polyps and surrounding gastric mucosa
  • Accurate histological classification crucial for determining therapeutic and surveillance strategies
  • Comprehensive histological assessment of polyp and surrounding gastric mucosa needed to evaluate malignant progression risk
  • Ethnic origin and family history of gastric cancer associated with risk but should not alter surveillance frequency (mediated by gastric mucosal status)
  • Polyp size (irrespective of type) and presence of dysplasia guide management
5.2 FGP Surveillance
  • Non-syndromic FGPs devoid of dysplasia: surveillance not usually needed
  • Syndromic patients with dysplastic FGP, large polyps (>10 mm), or mucosal carpeting: watchful follow-up advised (associated with gastric cancer development)
  • >10 mm, no dysplasia: surveillance in 1 year; consider PPI discontinuation
  • Mucosal carpeting (>20 polyps): surveillance in 1 year; discontinue PPI and consider evaluation for familial polyposis syndrome
  • With dysplasia: 6 months for high-grade, 1 year for low-grade; evaluate for familial polyposis syndrome
5.3 GHP Surveillance
  • <10 mm, no dysplasia: surveillance not clearly indicated if all polyps resected; ensure H pylori negativity
  • If gastric premalignant conditions present: follow AGA advice
  • >10 mm, no dysplasia: endoscopic surveillance in 1 year
  • With dysplasia: 6 months for high-grade, 1 year for low-grade
5.4 Adenoma Surveillance (BPA 13)
  • Includes intestinal-type, pyloric gland, and oxyntic gland adenomas
  • Ensure complete removal of index adenoma; consider referral to endoscopist with advanced resection expertise
  • High-grade dysplasia: initial follow-up at 6 months, then annually thereafter
  • Low-grade dysplasia: initial follow-up at 12 months, then annually
  • If polyp biopsied or resection incomplete: follow-up within 3 months for high-grade dysplasia, 6 months for low-grade dysplasia
  • Ensure H pylori negativity; if gastric premalignant conditions present, follow AGA advice
5.5 G-NET Surveillance
  • Type I or II:
  • If ≥2 cm: consider referral to oncologic center with expertise
  • If <2 cm: every 1–2 years
  • If autoimmune gastritis present: follow existing AGA advice
  • Type II: evaluation for source of inappropriate gastrin production
  • Type III: referral to oncologic center with expertise
5.6 Pyloric Gland and Oxyntic Gland Adenoma Management
  • Same approaches as other adenomas recommended
5.7 Tattooing Recommendations
  • Routine tattooing not advised for most diagnostic or therapeutic gastric polypectomies
  • Can be considered (optional) for: site of incompletely resected large polyps, suspected malignant polyps, when surgery or EMR/ESD follow-up planned
5.8 Assessment of Surrounding Gastric Mucosa (BPA 14)
  • Endoscopic surveillance advised when histopathology of adjacent mucosa confirms GIM and/or atrophic gastritis
  • Patients with longstanding H pylori-related or autoimmune gastritis who developed mucosal atrophy, pseudo-pyloric metaplasia, or GIM have increased risk of gastric polyps
  • Obtain biopsies from gastric antrum and corpus at minimum
  • Successful H pylori eradication: leads to regression of hyperplastic polyps, improves AG, decreases gastric cancer risk
  • Targeted biopsies every 3 years for: persistent H pylori infection, advanced AG with incomplete GIM detected, mild/focal GIM with family history of gastric cancer
  • Duration of surveillance not yet determined
  • Clinicians should assess multiple high-risk features and engage in shared decision-making
6. Conclusion
  • CPU summarizes BPA statements for diagnosing and managing gastric polyps
  • Gastric polyps are among most encountered lesions detected during upper endoscopy
  • Proposed management includes: endoscopic characterization of polyps and adjacent mucosa using image-enhancement tools, followed by biopsy, resection, appropriate follow-up surveillance exams
  • Systematic evaluation of polyps and surrounding mucosa essential for accurate diagnosis and management
  • Image-enhanced endoscopy improves detection and characterization of dysplastic lesions and mucosal abnormalities
  • Surveillance intervals based on polyp histology, dysplasia presence, and surrounding gastric mucosa status
  • Management requires individualized approach considering polyp characteristics, patient factors, and available expertise
