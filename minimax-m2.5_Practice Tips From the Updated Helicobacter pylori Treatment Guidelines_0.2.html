Practice Tips From the Updated Helicobacter pylori Treatment Guidelines
  • Source: Gastroenterology & Hepatology, Volume 22, Issue 2, February 2026
  • Topic: Updated ACG H. pylori Treatment Guidelines (2024 vs 2017)
  • Author(s): Martin Ventoso, MD, and Steven F. Moss, MD
1. Background & Epidemiology
  • Disease burden: H. pylori causes chronic gastritis → peptic ulcer disease, gastric adenocarcinoma, MALT lymphoma
  • Extra-gastric associations: iron-deficiency anemia, idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP), dyspepsia
  • Global prevalence trend: ↓ from 58.2% (1980–1990) to 43.1% (2011–2022)
  • US prevalence: ~one-third of adults infected; precise recent estimates lacking
  • High-risk US groups: elderly (age cohort effect), minority populations, first-generation immigrants from endemic areas (Central/South America, East Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Eastern Europe)
2. Guideline Overview & Methodology
  • 2024 ACG guidelines: 5 recommendations for treatment-naive patients + 7 for refractory cases + 6 key concepts
  • Development: GRADE methodology; clinical experts + gastroenterology methodologists
  • Key change: shift from empiric to biology-based (antimicrobial susceptibility-guided) therapy
  • Comparison base: 2017 ACG guidelines relied heavily on non-North American data
3. First-Line Treatment: Optimized Bismuth Quadruple Therapy
3.1 Rationale for Preference
  • Clarithromycin resistance >30% in US → clarithromycin triple therapy unacceptable efficacy
  • Supported by 3 US retrospective series (Rhode Island, US military, national claims database)
  • Eradication rates: 85–87% (bismuth quadruple) vs 76–80% (clarithromycin triple)
  • Duration: 14 days (standard for all 2024 regimens); exception: Pylera 10-day pack
3.2 Optimized Regimen Specifications
Drug Dosing Notes
PPI Twice-daily standard dose, 30–60 min before breakfast/dinner Esomeprazole or rabeprazole preferred; pantoprazole nonpreferred
Bismuth ≥300 mg bismuth subsalicylate 4× daily OR 120–300 mg bismuth subcitrate 4× daily Salicylate allergy: substitute bismuth subcitrate or use branded Pylera
Tetracycline 500 mg 4× daily Do NOT substitute doxycycline
Metronidazole 500 mg 3–4× daily (>1.5 g/day) Overcomes in vitro resistance
  • Penicillin safety: Bismuth quadruple contains no penicillin → safe for true penicillin allergy
4. New FDA-Approved Treatment Regimens
4.1 Rifabutin Triple Therapy (Talicia)
  • Composition: rifabutin 150 mg + amoxicillin 3 g + omeprazole 120 mg daily
  • Efficacy: 84% eradication (modified ITT) vs 58% in comparator arm (amoxicillin + omeprazole only)
  • Generic substitution: omeprazole 40 mg + amoxicillin 1 g each 3× daily; rifabutin 150 mg 2× daily (not once daily)
  • PK rationale: Generic rifabutin 150 mg QD inferior to 50 mg TID (as in combination tablet)
  • Limitation: Cannot use with confirmed penicillin allergy
4.2 Vonoprazan-Based Therapy (P-CAB)
  • Mechanism: Potassium-competitive acid blocker; targets parietal cell proton pump
  • Advantages over PPI: More potent, faster-acting, efficacy independent of meal timing
  • Trial results (US/Europe, treatment-naive):
  • Vonoprazan + clarithromycin + amoxicillin: 81% eradication
  • Lansoprazole + clarithromycin + amoxicillin: 69% eradication
  • Vonoprazan + amoxicillin dual therapy: 77% eradication → FDA-approved both regimens
  • Limitation: Not directly compared with bismuth quadruple therapy; real-world performance TBD
4.3 Guideline Recommendations for New Regimens
  • Strength: Conditional (suggested) recommendations; weaker than "recommended" (bismuth quadruple)
  • Evidence quality: Low to moderate
  • Rifabutin triple therapy: Suggested as empiric first-line alternative (non-penicillin-allergic patients)
  • Vonoprazan-amoxicillin dual therapy: Suggested first-line option
  • Dual PPI-amoxicillin therapy: NOT recommended
  • Penicillin allergy: Neither rifabutin nor vonoprazan regimens suitable (both require amoxicillin)
5. Antimicrobial Resistance & Susceptibility Testing
5.1 US Resistance Rates (2011–2021 meta-analysis)
  • Metronidazole: 42.1%
  • Levofloxacin: 37.
5.1 US Resistance Rates (continued)
  • Clarithromycin: 31.5%
  • Amoxicillin: 2.6%
  • Tetracycline: 0.87%
  • Rifabutin: 0.17% (lowest resistance)
  • Dual clarithromycin + metronidazole resistance: 11.7%
  • Alternative study (907 US/EU participants from vonoprazan trial): clarithromycin 22.2%, amoxicillin 1.2%, metronidazole 69.2%
5.2 Clinical Implications of Resistance Data
  • High clarithromycin/levofloxacin resistance: Underlies strong recommendation against empiric use
  • 2017 approach: Used prior antibiotic exposure as surrogate predictor of resistance
  • 2024 approach: Encourages antimicrobial susceptibility testing, especially in refractory cases and definite penicillin allergy
5.3 Susceptibility Testing Methods
  • Traditional culture: Limited by exacting gastric biopsy requirements and specimen transport challenges
  • Molecular methods (preferred): PCR and next-generation sequencing using gastric biopsies or stool specimens
  • Practical barriers: Costs and reimbursement issues remain unresolved
  • Guideline recommendation: Providers should explore local testing availability; consult infectious disease, microbiology, and pathology colleagues
6. Refractory/Salvage Treatment Algorithms
6.1 Algorithm Without Susceptibility Testing
  • Penicillin-allergic patients: Limited options → essentially only optimized bismuth quadruple therapy available
  • No prior bismuth quadruple: Suggest optimized bismuth quadruple over quinolone-based therapy
  • Prior PPI-clarithromycin triple failure: Suggest optimized bismuth quadruple therapy
  • Prior bismuth quadruple failure: Suggest rifabutin triple therapy
6.2 Algorithm With Susceptibility Testing
  • Clarithromycin-containing regimens: Avoid unless susceptibility confirmed
  • Levofloxacin-based therapy: Suggest only with known levofloxacin-sensitive strains; reserved for when other treatments failed or unavailable (adverse event profile + black-box warning concerns)
  • Guided regimen selection: Based on individual susceptibility patterns
6.3 Evidence Quality for Salvage Therapy
  • All refractory treatment recommendations: conditional (weaker)
  • Evidence base: Low to very low quality
  • Key gap: Lack of US clinical trials for salvage treatments
7. Gastric Cancer Prevention & Screening
7.1 Evidence for Eradication Benefit
Study Type Risk Reduction
RCTs in H. pylori-positive healthy individuals (meta-analysis) 36% reduction
RCTs in patients post-endoscopic gastric cancer resection (meta-analysis) 48% reduction
RCT in first-degree relatives of gastric cancer patients 55% reduction
Population screening/treating (Matsu Islands, Taiwan) 50% reduction
Retrospective: VA cohort 76% reduction
Retrospective: Kaiser Permanente Northern California (≥8 years follow-up) 63% reduction (noncardia cancer)
7.2 US-Specific Data
  • VA cohort (371,813 patients): Higher gastric cancer risk in racial/ethnic minorities and smokers; H. pylori eradication → 75% risk reduction
  • Kaiser Permanente (716,567 enrollees): Eradication therapy → 63% reduced incidence of noncardia gastric adenocarcinoma after 8 years
7.3 Screening Recommendations
  • Universal screening: NOT currently recommended in ACG 2024 guidelines
  • Focused testing and treatment: Appropriate starting at age 45–50 years
  • Target populations: Non-White racial/ethnic groups, immigrants from high cancer incidence regions
  • Rationale: These groups have highest US gastric cancer rates
7.4 International Context
  • Countries with screening programs: Japan, Korea
  • European trials: Several ongoing H. pylori screening trials

8.2 Other Guidelines
  • WGO Global Guideline: Intended for resource-poor countries
  • ACG iterations: 2007 → 2017 → 2024
8.3 Guideline Limitations & Criticism
  • Common criticisms: Outdated, inaccessible, irrelevant, subject to bias
  • Conflict of interest mitigation: Open declaration for peer judgment
  • Purpose: Complement clinical judgment, not replace it
9. Implementation Gap & Practice Patterns
9.1 Current US Prescribing Reality
  • Primary care: Majority of H. pylori prescriptions; may not follow latest guidelines
  • 2017 guideline recommendation: Confine clarithromycin triple therapy to patients with no macrolide exposure + low clarithromycin resistance areas
  • Market reality: Clarithromycin-PPI triple therapy comprised >80% of US treatments (2016–2019)
  • Failure handling: ~50% of clarithromycin-PPI triple therapy failures received exact same prescription again
9.2 Dissemination Challenges
  • Specialty journal limitation: Published in American Journal of Gastroenterology (gastroenterology-focused)
  • Wider dissemination needs:
  • Update platforms (UpToDate, OpenEvidence)
  • Modify EMR care algorithms
  • Change pharmacy/insurance plan recommendations

10.1 Identified Gaps (continued)
  • Refractory treatment efficacy: Lack of US data on salvage treatments is disappointing and should be addressed
  • Susceptibility testing trials: Need for RCTs comparing susceptibility testing-based treatment vs empiric therapy to formally test hypothesis that tailored therapy improves outcomes
  • Treatment registry: Ongoing efforts to establish US H. pylori treatment registry (modeled on Europe and Latin America) may help address data gaps
10.2 Future Directions
  • P-CABs likely to replace PPIs: Given importance of acid suppression in vivo bacterial killing
  • Recent network meta-analysis (post-guideline): Recommended P-CAB–amoxicillin as most efficacious regimen overall
  • Caveat: 24 of 25 included studies from East Asia where dual therapies perform better than in West (reasons unclear)
  • Novel combinations needed: To achieve ≥90% eradication rates in US → combining P-CAB–amoxicillin dual therapy with rifabutin and/or bismuth and/or tetracycline
11. Conclusions
  • 2024 ACG guidelines: Major shift from empiric to biology-based (susceptibility-guided) therapy
  • First-line preference: Optimized bismuth quadruple therapy (14 days)
  • New alternatives: Rifabutin triple therapy, vonoprazan-based regimens (suggested, not recommended)
  • Key principle: Avoid clarithromycin/levofloxacin unless susceptibility confirmed
  • Screening focus: Targeted testing in high-risk groups (age 45–50, non-White/ethnic minorities, immigrants from endemic areas) for gastric cancer prevention
  • Implementation gap: >80% of US prescriptions still clarithromycin-based; major dissemination needed to primary care
  • Evolution expected: Field will continue rapid evolution; P-CABs likely central to future regimens
