Diagnosis & Treatment of Alcohol-Associated Liver Disease (ALD) & Alcoholic Hepatitis (AH)
  • - A synthesized mind map based on two key sources:
  • - AASLD 2019 Practice Guidance | Hepatology | Jan 2020
  • - Medical Management of Severe AH (Mitchell & McClain) | Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol | Jan 2017
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the Maddrey Discriminant Function (MDF) cutoff for initiating corticosteroid treatment in severe Alcoholic Hepatitis (AH)?
  • - What is the recommended daily protein intake for patients with AH?
  • - Which FDA-approved medication for Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD) has no hepatic metabolism and is considered probably safe in ALD?
  • - What is the Lille score cutoff that indicates non-response to corticosteroids and supports their cessation?
  • - What percentage of patients transplanted for ALD return to drinking within the first 5 years?

Overview & Epidemiology
  • - ALD is a spectrum of liver injury from alcohol use, ranging from steatosis to hepatitis and cirrhosis.
  • - Terminology is shifting from "alcoholic" to "alcohol-associated" to reduce stigma (e.g., ALD, AC).
  • - ALD is a major cause of liver disease worldwide, both alone and as a co-factor.
Disease Burden
  • - ALD is a leading indication for liver transplantation (LT) in the US, surpassing HCV.
  • - Mortality from alcohol-associated cirrhosis (AC) is increasing, especially in younger patients aged 25-34.
  • - AC deaths account for ~10% of all alcohol-attributable deaths worldwide.

Prevalence Challenges
  • - True prevalence is likely underestimated due to:
  • - Difficulty identifying early, asymptomatic stages.
  • - Stigma and underreporting of alcohol use.
  • - Often not counted if other liver diseases (HCV, NASH) are present, despite high rates of co-existence.


Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD)
  • - AUD replaces the former terms "alcohol abuse" and "dependence".
  • - It is characterized by negative consequences and symptoms of alcohol use.
  • - Rates of AUD and high-risk drinking are rising dramatically, especially among women and minorities.
Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
  • - Based on 11 criteria experienced in the past year (See Table 1).
  • - Severity Classification:
  • - Mild: 2-3 symptoms.
  • - Moderate: 4-5 symptoms.
  • - Severe: 6 or more symptoms.

Screening & Intervention (SBIRT)
  • - Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment.
  • - A nonjudgmental, open interview style is crucial to maintain therapeutic alliance.
Screening Tools
  • - NIAAA Single-Question Screen: "How many times in the past year have you had 5 or more drinks in a day (men) or 4 or more (women)?"
  • - If answer is ≥1, proceed to a full screening tool.
  • - AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Inventory Test): 10 questions. Score >8 suggests harmful use; >20 suggests dependence.
  • - AUDIT-C: First 3 questions of AUDIT. A brief, effective screen for problem drinking. Score of ≥4-5 may indicate harmful use.


Biomarkers of Alcohol Use
  • - Used to aid diagnosis and support recovery, not to "catch" patients.
  • - Should be combined with clinical interview and other labs.
  • - Traditional markers (GGT, macrocytosis) are suggestive but not specific.
Carbohydrate-Deficient Transferrin (CDT)
  • - Half-life: 2-3 weeks.
  • - Limitations: Low sensitivity (25%-50%) and false positives in severe liver disease without alcohol use.

Ethyl Glucuronide (EtG) & Ethyl Sulfate (EtS)
  • - Measured in urine, blood, or hair.
  • - Urine EtG/EtS: Detects use within ~3 days. High sensitivity (~80-90%) and specificity (~90-99%).
  • - Detection window can be prolonged in renal failure.
  • - Hair EtG: Detects use over months but is costly and has limited availability.

Phosphatidylethanol (PEth)
  • - Formed in erythrocyte membranes; measured in blood.
  • - Half-life: ~10-14 days.
  • - Advantages: Not influenced by liver or kidney disease. High sensitivity (~100%) and specificity (~96%) in ALD patients.
  • - Considered one of the best current biomarkers.



Pathophysiology & Risk Factors
  • - Only a minority of heavy drinkers develop significant liver disease.
  • - Pathophysiology involves fat accumulation, changes in gut permeability ---> increased endotoxin ---> innate immune activation, inflammation, and fibrosis.
Alcohol Consumption Thresholds
  • - Risk increases beyond a threshold, not linearly.
  • - Safe Limits (General Population): ≤1 standard drink/day for women, ≤2 for men.
  • - Standard Drink (US): Contains 14g of alcohol.
  • - Binge Drinking: Pattern that brings BAC to 0.08 g/dL (typically after 4 drinks for women, 5 for men, in ~2 hours).
  • - Guidance: There is no safe level of drinking for patients with existing ALD or other liver diseases.

Key Risk Factors
  • - Gender: Women have greater risk for any level of drinking.
  • - Consumption Pattern: Daily drinking and binge drinking increase risk.
  • - Genetics: Polymorphisms in PNPLA3, TM6SF2, and MBOAT7 increase risk. A variant in HSD17B13 is protective.
  • - Comorbidities: Risk is amplified by NAFLD/NASH, chronic viral hepatitis (HCV), and hemochromatosis.
  • - Other Factors: Smoking, increased BMI.
Protective Factors
  • - Coffee consumption protects against cirrhosis from many causes, including ALD.



Clinical Spectrum & Diagnosis of ALD
  • - ALD presentation is often nonspecific and can mimic other liver diseases.
  • - A high index of suspicion for AUD is required.
  • - Signs can range from none to overt liver failure (See Table 6).
Alcohol-Associated Steatosis
  • - Usually asymptomatic, may have hepatomegaly.
  • - AST and GGT are often elevated.
  • - Readily identified on imaging (ultrasound, CT, MRI).
  • - Reversible with alcohol cessation.

Alcohol-Associated Cirrhosis (AC)
  • - Often diagnosed at time of decompensation (ascites, encephalopathy, variceal bleed).
  • - Prognosis assessed by CPT and MELD/MELD-Na scores.
  • - Abstinence is the most crucial factor influencing outcome.

Alcoholic Hepatitis (AH)
  • - An acute, severe inflammatory syndrome in patients with heavy alcohol use.
  • - Represents a serious decompensation of ALD, often superimposed on underlying cirrhosis.
  • - Characterized by rapid onset of jaundice, malaise, and features of SIRS.
Definition & Diagnosis
  • - A consensus definition helps standardize diagnosis for clinical care and trials.
Clinical Criteria (Probable AH)
  • - Onset of jaundice within 60 days of heavy alcohol consumption.
  • - Heavy consumption: >50 g/day for a minimum of 6 months.
  • - Serum bilirubin >3 mg/dL.
  • - AST 50-400 U/L with an AST:ALT ratio >1.5.
  • - No other obvious cause for hepatitis.

Diagnostic Categories (AASLD)
  • - Definite AH: Requires liver biopsy confirmation.
  • - Probable AH: Meets clinical criteria without confounding factors.
  • - Possible AH: Clinical criteria met but confounding factors present (e.g., DILI); biopsy recommended to guide therapy.

Histology (ASH)
  • - Features include: steatosis, hepatocyte ballooning, Mallory-Denk bodies, neutrophilic infiltration, and pericellular fibrosis.
  • - Cirrhosis is present in the vast majority of severely ill patients.
  • - Limitation: Liver biopsy cannot reliably distinguish between ASH and NASH.


Assessing Prognosis in AH
  • - Several validated scoring systems predict short-term mortality and guide treatment decisions.
Static Scores (at presentation)
  • - Maddrey Discriminant Function (MDF):
  • - Uses bilirubin and prothrombin time.
  • - Purpose: Identifies patients with high short-term mortality who may benefit from corticosteroids.
  • - Cutoff: MDF ≥32 defines severe AH.
  • - MELD Score:
  • - Uses bilirubin, INR, and creatinine.
  • - Purpose: General prognosis for liver disease; predicts 90-day mortality.
  • - Cutoff: MELD >20 also suggests severe AH and prompts consideration of steroid treatment.
  • - Glasgow Alcoholic Hepatitis Score (GAHS):
  • - Adds age and WBC to MELD components.
  • - Purpose: Further refines identification of patients with MDF ≥32 who will benefit from steroids (GAHS ≥9). Not widely validated outside the UK.
  • - ABIC Score:
  • - Uses Age, Bilirubin, INR, Creatinine.
  • - Purpose: Provides three-tiered risk stratification for prognosis.

Dynamic Score (on treatment)
  • - Lille Score:
  • - Purpose: Assesses response to corticosteroids after 7 days of treatment.
  • - Incorporates change in bilirubin.
  • - Cutoff: Score ≥0.45 indicates non-response, predicts very high mortality, and supports stopping corticosteroids.

Key Complications
  • - Infection: Common at presentation (12-26%) and during treatment. A high index of suspicion is required.
  • - Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): High risk due to HRS and SIRS. Avoid nephrotoxins.
  • - Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS): Presence at admission predicts multi-organ failure (MOF) and high mortality, even without infection.




Management of Alcoholic Hepatitis (AH)
  • - A multidisciplinary approach is essential, focusing on abstinence, nutrition, and targeted medical therapy for severe cases.
General & Supportive Care (Best Supportive Care)
  • - Improvements in supportive care have lowered the baseline mortality of AH, making it harder for clinical trials to show benefit.
Abstinence
  • - The single most important factor for both short-term and long-term survival.
  • - Continued drinking increases risk of all liver-related complications.

Nutritional Therapy
  • - Malnutrition is nearly universal and impairs recovery.
  • - Goal: 1.0-1.5 g/kg protein and 30-40 kcal/kg body weight daily.
  • - Intake <21.5 kcal/kg/day is associated with significantly increased mortality.
  • - Enteral nutrition (via feeding tube if needed) is preferred. Parenteral nutrition alone is inadequate.

Infection Management
  • - Screen for infection at admission with cultures (blood, urine, ascites).
  • - Treat confirmed infections promptly with appropriate antibiotics.
  • - Empiric antibiotics are being studied but are not yet standard of care.


Pharmacotherapy for Severe AH (MDF ≥32 or MELD >20)
  • - Focuses on suppressing the intense inflammatory response.
Corticosteroids
  • - Agent: Prednisolone 40 mg/day orally (or methylprednisolone 32 mg/day).
  • - Mechanism: Broad anti-inflammatory effects.
  • - Consensus View: Offers a modest improvement in 28-day survival but no benefit at 90 days or 1 year.
Evidence & Controversy
  • - Multiple small trials and meta-analyses show conflicting results.
  • - The largest RCT (STOPAH trial) did not show a statistically significant 28-day survival benefit (p=0.06).
  • - However, post-hoc analyses of STOPAH and large meta-analyses of individual patient data do show a significant short-term benefit.
  • - The lack of significance in STOPAH may be due to lower-than-expected mortality in the placebo group.

Clinical Application
  • - Use: Should be considered for patients with severe AH (MDF ≥32) without contraindications.
  • - Contraindications: Uncontrolled infection, active GI bleeding, severe AKI, concomitant HBV/TB.
  • - Monitoring: Calculate the Lille score at day 7. If ≥0.45, stop steroids due to non-response and futility.


N-Acetylcysteine (NAC)
  • - Agent: Intravenous NAC, typically given with corticosteroids.
  • - Mechanism: Antioxidant, restores glutathione levels.
  • - Evidence: A single RCT showed co-administration with prednisolone reduced 1-month mortality (8% vs 24%) and rates of infection/HRS compared to prednisolone alone.
  • - Guidance: The addition of IV NAC to prednisolone may improve 30-day survival and can be considered, but requires further validation.

Pentoxifylline (PTX)
  • - Mechanism: Weak TNF inhibitor.
  • - Evidence: Early small trials suggested benefit, particularly in reducing HRS.
  • - However, larger, higher-quality trials (including STOPAH) and meta-analyses have failed to show any survival benefit.
  • - Guidance: Pentoxifylline is no longer recommended for the treatment of AH.

Treatments of Unlikely Benefit
  • - Anti-TNF agents (Infliximab, Etanercept): Terminated early in trials due to increased infection-related mortality.
  • - Antioxidants (Vitamin E, S-adenosylmethionine): Failed to show survival improvement.

Future & Investigational Therapies
  • - Granulocyte-Colony Stimulating Factor (G-CSF): Pilot studies suggest benefit by promoting liver regeneration; requires more study.
  • - Gut-directed therapies: Fecal microbiota transplantation, probiotics.
  • - Repurposing drugs developed for NASH.



Treatment of Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD) in ALD
  • - Abstinence is paramount; multidisciplinary management with addiction specialists is mandatory for patients with ALD.
Psychosocial & Behavioral Approaches
  • - Integrated Care: Integrating AUD treatment providers within the hepatology clinic produces better abstinence rates than external referral.
  • - Modalities: Cognitive-Behavior Therapy (CBT), Motivational Enhancement Therapy (MET), 12-step facilitation (e.g., Alcoholics Anonymous).
  • - No single modality has been shown to be consistently superior.

Pharmacotherapy (Relapse Prevention)
  • - Most medications have not been formally studied in patients with advanced liver disease (AH or cirrhosis).
FDA-Approved Medications
  • - Naltrexone: Opioid antagonist. Undergoes hepatic metabolism; hepatotoxicity is a concern. Not studied in ALD.
  • - Acamprosate: NMDA receptor antagonist. Has no hepatic metabolism and is renally excreted. Probably safe in ALD based on limited data.
  • - Disulfiram: Not recommended for use in patients with ALD due to hepatotoxicity.

Off-Label Medications
  • - Baclofen: GABA-B agonist. A single RCT in patients with AC showed improved abstinence rates with an acceptable safety profile (patients with HE were excluded).
  • - Gabapentin: Modulates GABA activity. Renally excreted. Not studied in ALD.
  • - Topiramate: GABA action augmentation. Not studied in ALD.

Guidance Summary
  • - Based on limited data, acamprosate or baclofen can be considered for AUD treatment in patients with ALD.



Liver Transplantation (LT) for ALD
  • - ALD is a leading indication for LT in the US.
  • - Liver allograft and recipient survival for ALD are among the highest of all indications.
Referral & Candidate Selection
  • - Referral Triggers: New onset decompensation (ascites, HE, variceal bleed), SBP, HCC, or MELD-Na ≥21.
  • - Patients with CPT class C cirrhosis who fail to improve after 3 months of abstinence should be considered.
  • - Selection requires a comprehensive psychosocial assessment by an addiction expert.
The "6-Month Rule"
  • - A traditional requirement of 6 months of documented abstinence before listing for LT.
  • - Rationale: Allows time for potential liver recovery and demonstrates commitment to abstinence.
  • - Current View: The rule is an inadequate predictor of post-LT relapse and penalizes patients unlikely to survive 6 months. Consensus is diminishing for its strict application; selection should not be based solely on a fixed interval of abstinence.


Outcomes & Relapse
  • - Approximately 20%-25% of ALD recipients return to some form of drinking within the first 5 years post-LT.
  • - It is crucial to distinguish between a minor "slip" and a sustained, harmful relapse.
  • - Harmful Relapse: Can lead to recurrent fibrosis and cirrhosis in the allograft in as little as 5 years.

Early LT for Severe AH
  • - An emerging indication for patients with severe AH who are not responding to medical therapy (Lille ≥0.45) and have a grim short-term prognosis (~70% 6-month mortality).
Evidence
  • - A seminal French/Belgian study showed dramatically improved 6-month survival with early LT vs. historical controls (77% vs. 23%).
  • - A multicenter US study confirmed excellent outcomes: 94% 1-year and 84% 3-year survival.

Candidate Selection (Crucial & Strict)
  • - Patients must be carefully selected with a favorable psychosocial profile to minimize relapse risk.
  • - Key Criteria:
  • - Severe AH as the first liver decompensating event.
  • - No prior diagnosis of liver disease.
  • - Good insight into AUD and strong social support.
  • - A predictive model (SALT score) has been proposed to identify low-risk candidates but requires validation.
