Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE): A Consolidated Guideline View
  • - This mind map synthesizes and compares two major clinical guidelines on Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE).
  • - Source 1 (ACG): ACG Clinical Guideline: Diagnosis and Management of Eosinophilic Esophagitis (Dellon et al., Am J Gastroenterol 2025).
  • - Source 2 (BSG): British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG) and British Society of Paediatric Gastroenterology, Hepatology and Nutrition (BSPGHAN) joint consensus guidelines (Dhar et al., Gut 2022).
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the BSG's specific recommendation for PPI withdrawal before a diagnostic endoscopy for EoE?
  • - What is the histologic remission target for treated EoE, according to the BSG?
  • - What is the ACG-cited efficacy of a 6-food elimination diet (6FED) in achieving histologic remission?
  • - According to the ACG, what is the FDA-approved weekly dose of dupilumab for adults with EoE?
  • - Both guidelines agree that esophageal dilation is safe, but what is the perforation risk cited by the ACG?

Definition & Diagnosis
  • - EoE is a chronic, immune/antigen-mediated esophageal disease.
Core Diagnostic Criteria (Consensus)
  • - 1. Symptoms of esophageal dysfunction (e.g., dysphagia, food impaction).
  • - 2. Histology: Esophageal biopsy showing a peak eosinophil count of ≥15 eosinophils/high-power field (eos/hpf).
  • - 3. Exclusion: Evaluation for other causes of esophageal eosinophilia (e.g., GERD, eosinophilic gastroenteritis, Crohn's disease).
Histologic Threshold Nuances (BSG)
  • - BSG proposes updating the definition to account for digital microscopy.
  • - Recommends expressing the count as ≥15 eosinophils/0.3 mm² or >60 eosinophils/mm².


Role of PPIs in Diagnosis (Paradigm Shift)
  • - Consensus: A trial of Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) is no longer required to diagnose EoE.
  • - The term 'PPI-responsive esophageal eosinophilia' (PPI-REE) is obsolete.
  • - PPIs are now considered a treatment for EoE, not a diagnostic tool.
Pre-Diagnostic PPI Use: A Point of Difference
  • - BSG (Strong Recommendation): PPIs should be withdrawn for at least 3 weeks prior to a diagnostic endoscopy to avoid false negatives.
  • - ACG (Advisory): Advises performing endoscopy on no treatment (including no PPIs) to maximize diagnostic sensitivity, but does not specify a mandatory withdrawal period.


Clinical Presentation
  • - Symptoms vary significantly with age.
Adults & Adolescents
  • - Most common: Dysphagia and food impaction.
  • - Other symptoms: Heartburn, chest pain.
  • - ACG highlights assessing for 'IMPACT' behaviors (Imbibe fluids, Modify foods, Prolong meals, Avoid hard textures, Chew excessively, Turn away tablets) which can mask dysphagia severity.

Children
  • - Infants/Young Children: Feeding intolerance, food refusal, failure to thrive, poor growth.
  • - School-aged Children: Abdominal pain, vomiting, regurgitation, heartburn.
  • - BSG notes symptoms are often non-specific and vary with age.


Risk Factors & Associations
  • - Atopy (Consensus): Strong association with other atopic diseases (asthma, allergic rhinitis, eczema). ACG notes 60%-80% of EoE patients have concomitant allergic conditions.
  • - Genetics (Consensus): Family history increases risk. BSG notes a first-degree relative increases risk significantly.
  • - Demographics (BSG): More common in men (3:1 ratio) and people of white ethnic origin.


Endoscopic & Histologic Evaluation
  • - Endoscopy with biopsy is essential for diagnosis, monitoring, and assessing treatment response.
Endoscopic Findings
  • - Typical features: Edema, fixed rings, white exudates, linear furrows, strictures, crepe-paper mucosa.
  • - Consensus Recommendation: Use a systematic scoring system at every endoscopy.
EoE Endoscopic Reference Score (EREFS)
  • - Specifically recommended by ACG for standardizing reports.
  • - Grades 5 features: Edema, Rings, Exudates, Furrows, Strictures.
  • - Score range: 0-9. A post-treatment score of ≤2 is a proposed threshold for response.

Endoscopic Underestimation (BSG)
  • - BSG emphasizes that endoscopists can underestimate the frequency of strictures and narrow-lumen esophagus.
  • - ACG concurs, citing studies where endoscopy had poor sensitivity (15%) for detecting narrowing compared to esophagram.


Biopsy Protocol (Consensus)
  • - Recommendation: Obtain at least 6 esophageal biopsies.
  • - Biopsies should be from at least 2 different levels (e.g., proximal/mid and distal esophagus).
  • - ACG advises targeting visible abnormalities (furrows, exudates) to increase diagnostic yield.
Biopsy During Food Impaction
  • - Consensus: Biopsies should be taken at the time of endoscopy for food bolus obstruction after the bolus is cleared.
  • - This is a critical opportunity for diagnosis that is often missed.


Histologic Assessment
  • - Primary Feature: Peak eosinophil count.
  • - Consensus: Eosinophil counts must be quantified on every biopsy report, not just stated as '>15'.
Other Histologic Features
  • - Both guidelines note other important features beyond eosinophils.
  • - BSG: Basal cell hyperplasia, edema (spongiosis), eosinophil microabscesses, subepithelial sclerosis.
  • - ACG: Mentions the EoE Histologic Scoring System (EoEHSS) which assesses 8 features, including lamina propria fibrosis.

Defining Histologic Remission (BSG)
  • - BSG provides specific definitions for clinical use:
  • - Remission: Maximum eosinophil count <15 eos/0.3 mm².
  • - Deep/Complete Remission: Maximum eosinophil count <5 eos/0.3 mm².



Management: The 3 D's (Drugs, Diet, Dilation)
  • - Treatment goals: Improve symptoms, resolve inflammation (histologic/endoscopic remission), and prevent complications (fibrostenosis).
  • - Consensus: Treatment choice should be based on shared decision-making between the clinician and patient.
Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)
  • - Consensus: PPIs are an effective first-line treatment for EoE.
  • - Mechanism is beyond acid suppression; includes anti-inflammatory effects like decreasing eotaxin-3 expression and improving barrier function (ACG).
Efficacy
  • - ACG meta-analysis: Pooled histologic remission (<15 eos/hpf) of 50.5% and clinical response of 60.8%.
  • - BSG meta-analysis: Similar pooled rate of 51% for histologic remission.

Dosing Recommendations
  • - ACG: Recommends 'high-dose' PPI, which is double the approved reflux dose (e.g., omeprazole 20mg twice daily or 40mg once daily).
  • - BSG: Recommends twice-daily dosing for at least 8-12 weeks before assessing response.


Topical Corticosteroids (STCs)
  • - Consensus: A highly effective first-line treatment for inducing clinical and histologic remission.
Efficacy
  • - ACG: Meta-analyses show histologic response rates in the 60%-70% range. Phase 3 trials of newer formulations show rates from 62% to 95%.
  • - BSG: Rates STCs as more effective than PPIs but less effective than topical budesonide preparations in a meta-analysis.

Formulations & Dosing
  • - Common agents: Budesonide and Fluticasone.
  • - ACG: Mentions FDA-approved Budesonide Oral Suspension (BOS) and EMA-approved Budesonide Orodispersible Tablet (BOT), as well as off-label slurries and swallowed inhalers.
  • - BSG: Supports use of orodispersible budesonide (BOT) for induction in adults and oral viscous budesonide (OVB) for children.

Adverse Effects
  • - Consensus: Most common side effect is oropharyngeal/esophageal candidiasis.
  • - ACG: Cites rates from 3.8% (BOS) to 23.7% (BOT) in trials.
  • - Adrenal Insufficiency: Uncommon. ACG notes it's ≤5% in trials. BSG recommends monitoring for it in children/adolescents on long-term therapy.


Dietary Elimination Therapy
  • - Consensus: An effective treatment option, but requires significant patient commitment and dietitian support.
Empiric vs. Allergy Test-Directed Diets
  • - Strong Consensus: Do not use currently available allergy testing (skin prick, patch, serum IgE/IgG) to guide food elimination for EoE.
  • - Allergy test-directed diets have the lowest response rates (~45%).
  • - Empiric elimination is the recommended approach.

Types of Empiric Diets & Efficacy
  • - Elemental Diet: Amino acid-based formula. Highest efficacy (>90%) but poor palatability and adherence.
  • - 6-Food Elimination Diet (6FED): Eliminates milk, wheat, soy, egg, nuts, fish/shellfish. Efficacy ~70%.
  • - 4-Food (4FED) & 2-Food (2FED): Less restrictive. Efficacy ~40-50%.
  • - 1-Food (1FED): Milk elimination alone. Efficacy ~35-45%.
Approach to Diet Selection
  • - ACG: Suggests starting with a less restrictive diet (1FED or 2FED) is a reasonable initial choice.
  • - BSG: Recommends a 'step-up' approach, starting with a 2FED and escalating if no response.


Food Reintroduction
  • - Consensus: After a response to an elimination diet, foods must be reintroduced systematically to identify triggers.
  • - This requires a repeat endoscopy with biopsies after each food/food group is added back for 6-8 weeks.


Biologics
  • - A newer class of therapy for moderate-to-severe or refractory EoE.
Dupilumab (Anti-IL-4/IL-13)
  • - ACG (Conditional Recommendation): Suggests dupilumab for patients ≥1 year old who are non-responsive to PPI therapy.
  • - Efficacy (Adults/Adolescents): Achieved histologic remission (≤6 eos/hpf) in 60% of patients vs. 5% in placebo.
  • - Dosing: 300mg subcutaneously weekly for patients ≥40kg. Dosing is every other week for younger/lighter patients.

Other Biologics
  • - Eosinophil-Depleting Agents (Benralizumab, Lirentelimab): ACG notes these achieve high rates of histologic response but often without significant symptom improvement.
  • - Anti-IL-13 (Cendakimab): Showed promise in Phase 2 trials (ACG).
  • - BSG: Notes that novel biologics show promise but cannot yet be recommended outside of specific circumstances (e.g., co-existing allergic disease).


Esophageal Dilation
  • - Consensus: An effective and safe adjunctive therapy for treating fibrostenotic disease (strictures, narrowing) causing dysphagia.
Indications & Approach
  • - Used for patients with dysphagia from strictures.
  • - Consensus: Dilation should be combined with effective anti-inflammatory therapy, as it does not treat the underlying inflammation.
  • - ACG: Recommends a 'start low and go slow' approach, with a goal diameter of ≥16 mm in adults.

Safety
  • - Consensus: The procedure is safe.
  • - ACG: Cites a perforation risk of <0.5%.
  • - BSG: Cites a perforation risk of 0.38% in a meta-analysis, noting the risk is no higher than for other benign conditions.



Maintenance & Monitoring
  • - EoE is a chronic disease that requires long-term management and monitoring.
Maintenance Therapy (Consensus)
  • - Strong Recommendation: Effective therapy (dietary or pharmacologic) should be continued long-term.
  • - Disease activity almost universally recurs when treatment is stopped.
  • - Long-term treatment may prevent or slow fibrostenotic progression.
Dose Reduction
  • - ACG: For pharmacologic treatments, consider identifying the lowest effective dose for long-term use, confirmed by endoscopy.
  • - BSG: Notes high relapse rates after STC withdrawal and recommends maintenance therapy.


Monitoring Response (Consensus)
  • - Strong Recommendation: Evaluate response using a combination of symptoms, endoscopy, and histology.
  • - Monitoring symptoms alone is insufficient due to poor correlation with underlying inflammation.
Timing of Follow-up Endoscopy
  • - ACG: Generally 8-12 weeks after starting a new therapy (PPI, STC, diet). May be 12-24 weeks for dupilumab.
  • - BSG: Recommends endoscopy with biopsy 8-12 weeks after initiating therapy.

Non-Invasive Monitoring (Future Direction)
  • - Both guidelines acknowledge the need for less invasive monitoring.
  • - ACG: Mentions promising techniques like unsedated transnasal endoscopy, Cytosponge, and the Esophageal String Test.



Pediatric-Specific Considerations
  • - Management in children has unique goals and challenges.
Treatment Goals (Consensus)
  • - In addition to symptom/histologic control, key goals are ensuring normal growth, development (including eating skills), and nutrition.
  • - ACG: Suggests evaluation by a feeding therapist and/or dietitian for children with feeding dysfunction.

Diagnostic Approach (BSG)
  • - Strong Recommendation (BSG): All children undergoing endoscopy for any upper GI symptoms should have esophageal biopsies taken to screen for EoE.
  • - This is due to the non-specific nature of symptoms in this population.

Evaluating Fibrostenosis
  • - ACG (Conditional Recommendation): Suggests an esophagram for children with dysphagia to evaluate for fibrostenotic disease, which can be missed on endoscopy.

Transition of Care (BSG)
  • - BSG highlights the importance of a formal transition of care from pediatric to adult services.
  • - A structured transition may improve symptom control, adherence to therapy, and reduce emergency presentations.


Complications
  • - Untreated or undertreated EoE can lead to significant complications.
Fibrostenotic Disease (Strictures)
  • - Consensus: A major complication of untreated, chronic inflammation.
  • - BSG: A delay in diagnosis is associated with a higher risk of stricture formation. A delay of >20 years had a stricture rate of 71% vs. 17% for a delay of <2 years.

Food Bolus Obstruction (FBO)
  • - Consensus: EoE is a leading cause of FBO in adults.
  • - BSG: Recommends maintenance therapy with topical steroids to reduce the risk of recurrent FBO.

Spontaneous Perforation
  • - BSG: EoE is the most common cause of spontaneous esophageal perforation.
  • - It typically involves small tears with limited extravasation, unlike a Boerhaave's perforation.
  • - Management is often conservative with multidisciplinary input.

Psychological Impact (BSG)
  • - BSG highlights the significant psychological impact of EoE, particularly from dietary therapy.
  • - Anxiety, depression, and social restrictions are common and can be alleviated by effective therapy.
