Liver Disease in Pregnancy: A Clinical Mind Map
  • - This mind map synthesizes clinically critical information from two key sources:
  • - Brady CW. Liver Disease in Pregnancy: What’s New. Hepatology Communications, 2020.
  • - EASL Clinical Practice Guidelines on the management of liver diseases in pregnancy. Journal of Hepatology, 2023.
Knowledge Test
  • - Test your knowledge on key aspects of liver disease in pregnancy before you begin.
Question 1

Question 2

Question 3
  • - Mycophenolate mofetil is a teratogenic immunosuppressant used in some autoimmune conditions. How long before conception should it be stopped?

Question 4
  • - What is the definitive, curative therapy for both HELLP syndrome and Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP)?

Question 5
  • - In a pregnant woman with cirrhosis, a pre-conception MELD score above what value is predictive of hepatic decompensation during pregnancy?


General Considerations & Physiology
  • - Understanding baseline changes is crucial for accurate diagnosis.
Epidemiology
  • - Rates of chronic liver disease (CLD) in women of childbearing age (15-39 years) are rising, from 10.4% (1988-1994) to 24.9% (2007-2012) (Brady, citing NHANES).
Most Common Liver Diseases
  • - Gestational Liver Disorders: Affect 3% of pregnancies (EASL).
  • - NAFLD/MASLD: Most common CLD, prevalence rose from 6.8% to 18.1% in women 15-39 (Brady).
  • - Gallstone Disease: Second most common hepatic issue during pregnancy hospitalizations (Brady).


Physiological Changes in Pregnancy
  • - Pregnancy induces a hyperdynamic circulatory state mimicking CLD signs (e.g., palmar erythema, spider naevi) due to increased cardiac output and hyper-oestrogenic state (EASL).
Expected Laboratory Changes
  • - Increase: Alkaline Phosphatase (placental origin), plasma volume.
  • - Decrease: Albumin, peripheral vascular resistance.
  • - No Change / Slight Decrease: AST, ALT, Bilirubin, GGT, Platelets, Prothrombin Time (Brady, Table 1; EASL, Table 2).


Diagnostic Modalities
  • - Initial assessment should always begin with non-invasive testing (Brady).
Imaging
  • - Ultrasound: First-line and safe at any gestation (Brady & EASL).
  • - MRI: Acceptable without contrast. Gadolinium is associated with teratogenicity and should be avoided (Brady).
  • - CT Scan: Use must be weighed against risks of ionizing radiation, especially between 8-15 weeks gestation (Brady).

Invasive Procedures
  • - Endoscopy (OGD): Acceptable, typically recommended in the second trimester. Patient should be in left lateral decubitus position to avoid IVC compression (Brady & EASL).
  • - Liver Biopsy: Can be performed if clinically necessary and benefits outweigh risks. Transjugular route confers radiation exposure (Brady & EASL).
  • - ERCP: Can be performed, ideally in 2nd/3rd trimester. Fetal radiation is well below the teratogenic threshold of 50 mGy (EASL).

Transient Elastography (FibroScan)
  • - Not FDA-approved for use in pregnancy (Brady).
  • - EASL notes it is safe, but liver stiffness may physiologically increase in the third trimester (mean 4.5 kPa in 2nd trimester to 6.0 kPa in 3rd).



Liver Diseases Unique to Pregnancy
  • - Disorders that arise specifically due to the pregnant state.
Hyperemesis Gravidarum (HG)
  • - Intractable nausea and vomiting, occurring in 0.3-3% of pregnancies (Brady & EASL).
  • - Typically presents in the first trimester and resolves by 20 weeks gestation (Brady).
Liver Involvement
  • - Occurs in 40-60% of patients with HG (Brady & EASL).
  • - Biochemistry: Mildly elevated AST/ALT, but can rise to 1,000 IU/L. Jaundice is rare (Brady).
  • - Liver enzymes normalize after HG resolves; no long-term sequelae (Brady).

Management
  • - Supportive Care: IV fluids, antiemetics, vitamin/mineral supplementation.
  • - Key Supplements: Emphasize Thiamine and Folic Acid to prevent complications like Wernicke's encephalopathy (Brady & EASL).
  • - Antiemetics: Metoclopramide, promethazine, ondansetron are considered compatible (Brady). EASL provides a tiered approach (See Fig 6).


Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy
  • - Includes preeclampsia/eclampsia and HELLP syndrome.
Preeclampsia / Eclampsia
  • - Preeclampsia: New onset hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg) after 20 weeks with proteinuria (>300mg/day) or end-organ dysfunction (Brady & EASL).
  • - Eclampsia: Development of generalized seizures during preeclampsia (Brady).
Prevention in High-Risk Patients
  • - Low-dose aspirin (81-150mg daily): Recommended to be started between 12 and 16 weeks gestation (Brady & EASL).
  • - Reduces risks of preeclampsia, fetal growth restriction, and fetal death (Brady).


HELLP Syndrome
  • - Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets.
  • - Occurs in 0.2-0.9% of pregnancies, complicates 10-20% of severe preeclampsia cases (Brady & EASL).
  • - Typically presents in the 3rd trimester (28-36 weeks), but 30% of cases are postpartum (Brady).
Diagnosis & Classification
  • - Considered a manifestation of severe preeclampsia (EASL).
  • - Two main classification systems exist (Brady, Table 3).
Tennessee Classification
  • - Complete HELLP: AST ≥70, LDH ≥600, Platelets ≤50 x 10⁹/L.
  • - Partial HELLP: One or two of the above criteria.

Mississippi Triple Class System
  • - Class 1 (Severe): Platelets ≤50 x 10⁹/L.
  • - Class 2 (Moderate): Platelets 50-100 x 10⁹/L.
  • - Class 3 (Mild): Platelets 100-150 x 10⁹/L.


Prognostic Markers
  • - Platelet Count: A count < 100 x 10⁹/L is associated with increased risk of abnormal coagulation and adverse maternal outcomes (EASL).
  • - Liver Enzymes: Raised enzymes predict adverse maternal outcomes better than fetal outcomes, but should not be used in isolation (EASL).

Management
  • - Definitive therapy is delivery of the fetus (Brady & EASL).
  • - Labs may worsen for 48-72 hours postpartum before improving (Brady).
Medical Therapy
  • - Antihypertensives: To maintain BP below 160/110 mmHg. Options include labetalol, nifedipine (Brady & EASL).
  • - Magnesium Sulfate: For seizure prevention (Brady & EASL).
  • - Corticosteroids: NOT for improving maternal outcomes. Used for fetal lung maturity if delivery is planned before 35 weeks (EASL).

Hepatic Complications
  • - Subcapsular hematoma or hepatic rupture are rare but life-threatening.
  • - Requires surgical intervention or hepatic arterial embolization (Brady).
  • - Ultrasound is recommended for patients with severe RUQ/epigastric pain to screen for hematoma (EASL).




Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP)
  • - Rare (1:7,000 to 1:15,000 pregnancies) but severe disorder of microvesicular fat deposition in the liver, leading to hepatic failure (Brady).
  • - Typically occurs in the third trimester (Brady & EASL).
Pathophysiology
  • - Caused by inherited defects in fetal mitochondrial fatty acid oxidation, most commonly LCHAD deficiency (long-chain 3-hydroxyacyl-coenzyme A dehydrogenase) (Brady).
  • - Toxic fatty acid metabolites accumulate in the mother, causing hepatotoxicity (Brady).

Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis
  • - Presents with nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, anorexia, and signs of liver failure (jaundice, encephalopathy, coagulopathy) (Brady & EASL).
  • - Can overlap with HELLP, but AFLP typically shows more profound hepatic synthetic dysfunction (coagulopathy, hypoglycemia, hyperammonemia) (Brady).
Swansea Criteria
  • - Diagnostic tool combining clinical, lab, and imaging features.
  • - Diagnosis made if ≥6 features are present (Brady, Table 4).
  • - High sensitivity (100%) and negative predictive value (100%) (Brady).

Prognostic Markers
  • - Poor prognosis associated with:
  • - Encephalopathy
  • - Elevated serum lactate (> 2.8 mg/dl)
  • - MELD score > 30
  • - Swansea score > 7 (EASL).


Management
  • - Prompt delivery of the fetus is the cornerstone of management (Brady & EASL).
  • - Clinical abnormalities may persist for up to a week postpartum (Brady).
Supportive Care
  • - Requires intensive care for managing coagulopathy, hypoglycemia, renal failure, and other complications.
  • - Plasma exchange: May be considered post-delivery to improve recovery time in severe cases (EASL).
  • - N-acetylcysteine: No specific data for AFLP, but can be considered in ICU setting for non-paracetamol acute liver failure (EASL).
  • - Liver Transplantation: Required in rare cases of progression to irreversible liver failure (Brady & EASL).

Fetal & Recurrence Risk
  • - Fetuses are at risk for liver failure, cardiomyopathy, and myopathy (Brady).
  • - Recurrence risk exists; up to 25% of children born to mothers with AFLP will carry a fatty acid oxidation defect (Brady).



Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy (ICP)
  • - Most common pregnancy-related liver disease, prevalence 0.1-2% (Brady).
  • - Presents in 2nd or 3rd trimester with pruritus (classic symptom) and elevated serum bile acids (Brady & EASL).
Pathophysiology
  • - Thought to be due to genetic mutations in bile salt transporters (e.g., ABCB4, ABCB11) unmasked by hormonal changes of pregnancy (Brady & EASL).

Risk Stratification & Fetal Outcomes
  • - Serum bile acid level is the key predictor of fetal risk. Transaminases and bilirubin are not significant predictors (Brady & EASL).
Bile Acid Thresholds
  • - > 40 μmol/L: Increased risk of spontaneous preterm birth, meconium-stained fluid, and fetal distress (Brady & EASL).
  • - > 100 μmol/L: Significantly increased risk of stillbirth. Risk rises markedly after 35 weeks gestation (Brady & EASL).


Management
  • - Goal is to manage symptoms and mitigate fetal risk.
Pharmacotherapy: Ursodeoxycholic Acid (UDCA)
  • - Standard dose is 10-15 mg/kg/day (Brady).
  • - Brady (2020): Notes that UDCA reduces pruritus and improves LFTs, but a recent trial (PITCHES) showed it did not significantly reduce adverse perinatal outcomes.
  • - EASL (2023): Recommends UDCA for pruritus and notes it should be offered for women with bile acids > 40 μmol/L to reduce spontaneous preterm birth risk and may also protect against stillbirth.

Delivery Planning
  • - This is the intervention with clearly demonstrated benefit (Brady).
  • - Bile acids > 100 μmol/L: Plan delivery at 35-36 weeks (EASL).
  • - Bile acids 40-99 μmol/L: Consider delivery by 37-39 weeks (Brady & EASL).
  • - Bile acids < 40 μmol/L: Consider induction by 39 weeks (EASL).


Long-term Maternal Risks
  • - A history of ICP is associated with later development of hepatobiliary cancer, cardiovascular disease, and immune-related diseases (e.g., diabetes, thyroid disease) (Brady).



Pre-existing Liver Diseases in Pregnancy
  • - Management of chronic or acute liver diseases that occur coincidentally with pregnancy.
Viral Hepatitis
  • - Management focuses on maternal health and preventing mother-to-child transmission (MTCT).
Hepatitis A (HAV)
  • - MTCT is rare. Caesarean section and avoidance of breastfeeding are not recommended (EASL).
  • - Infection in 2nd/3rd trimester can cause complications like preterm labor (EASL).
  • - Post-exposure prophylaxis: Both HAV vaccine and immunoglobulin can be used in pregnancy (EASL).

Hepatitis B (HBV)
  • - Obstetric outcomes not significantly affected, but MTCT is a major concern (Brady).
  • - Risk of MTCT is highest with positive HBeAg and high HBV DNA (Brady).
Preventing MTCT
  • - Infant Immunoprophylaxis: Crucial. Hepatitis B immunoglobulin (HBIG) and HBV vaccine within 12 hours of birth reduces transmission to 5-10% (Brady & EASL).
  • - Maternal Antiviral Therapy: Recommended for mothers with HBV DNA > 200,000 IU/mL (or >5.3 log10 IU/mL) (Brady & EASL).
  • - Timing: Start at 24-28 weeks gestation and continue up to 12 weeks postpartum (EASL).
  • - Preferred Agent: Tenofovir is preferred due to potency and low resistance (Brady & EASL).

Delivery & Breastfeeding
  • - Delivery: Caesarean section is not routinely recommended to reduce MTCT (EASL).
  • - Breastfeeding: Not contraindicated if the infant has received immunoprophylaxis, even if the mother is on antiviral therapy (Brady & EASL).

Amniocentesis
  • - Associated with a higher risk of MTCT, especially with high maternal viral load. Non-invasive prenatal testing is preferred (EASL).


Hepatitis C (HCV)
  • - Prevalence is increasing in women of childbearing age due to injection drug use (Brady).
  • - MTCT risk is 5-6% with HCV monoinfection, but rises to 22% with HIV co-infection (Brady).
Screening & Treatment
  • - Universal HCV screening for all pregnant women is recommended (Brady & EASL).
  • - Treatment: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) are generally not recommended during pregnancy due to lack of safety data. Treatment should occur before pregnancy or after delivery/breastfeeding (Brady & EASL).
  • - Emerging data (e.g., ledipasvir/sofosbuvir) show promise for safety and efficacy in pregnancy, but more research is needed (Brady & EASL).

Delivery & Breastfeeding
  • - Delivery: Mode of delivery based on obstetric indications. Avoid fetal scalp monitoring (Brady).
  • - Breastfeeding: Not discouraged. No association with transmission risk (EASL).


Hepatitis D (HDV)
  • - MTCT is exceptional. Measures to prevent HBV transmission are effective for preventing HDV (EASL).

Hepatitis E (HEV)
  • - High mortality in pregnancy, especially with genotype 1 in endemic areas (EASL).
  • - Can cause acute liver failure, particularly in the third trimester (EASL).
  • - Management of Severe HEV: Early delivery/termination of pregnancy can be considered to reduce maternal mortality in cases with severe hepatitis and encephalopathy (EASL).


Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)
  • - AIH activity is variable during pregnancy.
Flares
  • - Flares can occur during pregnancy (20%) but are more common postpartum (30-50%) (Brady).
  • - Risk is higher if AIH was not well-controlled in the year before pregnancy (Brady).

Management
  • - Continue immunosuppression. Do not stop therapy. Dose may need to be increased postpartum to prevent flares (Brady & EASL).
  • - Safe Medications: Corticosteroids (Prednisone/Prednisolone), Azathioprine, Budesonide (in non-cirrhotics) (Brady & EASL).
  • - Unsafe Medication: Mycophenolate mofetil is teratogenic and must be stopped at least 12 weeks before conception (EASL). Switch to Azathioprine (Brady).

Maternal & Fetal Outcomes
  • - Increased rates of gestational diabetes, hypertensive disorders, preterm birth, and fetal growth restriction (EASL).
  • - Outcomes are worse in women with AIH-related cirrhosis (Brady).


Pre-existing Cholestatic Liver Disease (PBC, PSC)
  • - Includes Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC) and Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC).
Maternal Outcomes
  • - ~50% experience worsening or de novo pruritus during pregnancy (EASL).
  • - Up to 70% have postnatal deterioration of liver tests (EASL).
  • - In PSC, worsening cholestasis should prompt imaging (US or MRCP) to rule out dominant strictures (EASL).

Fetal Outcomes
  • - Impaired outcomes with lower live birth rates (58-76%in PBC) and increased preterm delivery (6-33%) (EASL).
  • - Higher maternal bile acids are associated with shorter gestation length (EASL).

Management
  • - Focuses on symptom control and maintaining stable liver function.
Medications
  • - UDCA: Should be continued during pregnancy and breastfeeding. It is safe and associated with stable liver tests (EASL).
  • - Obeticholic Acid: Not recommended due to lack of safety data (EASL).
  • - Fibrates (e.g., Bezafibrate): May be used after the first trimester if benefits outweigh risks (EASL).
  • - Rifampicin: For pruritus, can be used at 300-600 mg daily. Neonates should receive vitamin K (EASL).
  • - Anion Exchange Resins (Cholestyramine): For pruritus. Give at least 4 hours after UDCA (EASL).



NAFLD / MASLD
  • - Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease (MASLD), formerly NAFLD.
  • - Incidence in women of childbearing age is at least 10% (Brady & EASL).
Pathophysiology & Associations
  • - Pregnancy-related insulin resistance and changes in lipid metabolism may predispose to or worsen MASLD (Brady).
  • - Strongly associated with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) (EASL).

Maternal & Fetal Outcomes
  • - Maternal: Increased risk of gestational diabetes and preeclampsia (Brady & EASL).
  • - Fetal: Associated with increased odds of preterm birth, cesarean section, and low birth weight (Brady).
  • - Maternal obesity/MASLD is associated with future MASLD in the offspring (EASL).

Management
  • - Preconception: Optimize metabolic comorbidities (diabetes, obesity) (EASL).
  • - During Pregnancy: Lifestyle modifications (diet, avoiding excessive weight gain) are advised (EASL).
  • - Postpartum: Breastfeeding is encouraged as it is associated with a lower incidence of metabolic syndrome and MASLD in the mother (EASL).


Cirrhosis and Portal Hypertension
  • - Pregnancy is less common due to cirrhosis-related anovulation but rates are rising (Brady & EASL).
  • - Portal pressures increase due to plasma volume expansion and IVC compression (Brady).
Risk Stratification (Pre-conception)
  • - MELD Score: Score ≥10 is a significant predictor of liver-related adverse events (variceal hemorrhage, ascites, encephalopathy) (Brady).
  • - Albumin-Bilirubin (ALBI) Score: Score < -2.7 (Grade 1) is predictive of a live birth (Brady).
  • - Platelet Count: < 110 x 10⁹/L predicts the presence of varices on second-trimester endoscopy (Brady).

Variceal Hemorrhage
  • - Historically high rates reported, but recent data suggest a lower rate of 5% (Brady).
  • - Mortality from bleeding is < 20% in recent series (EASL).
Management of Varices
  • - Screening Endoscopy: Recommended in the second trimester if not performed within 1 year of conception (Brady & EASL).
  • - Prophylaxis: Non-selective beta-blockers can be used. Propranolol is preferred over Nadolol (Brady). Carvedilol may also be used (EASL).
  • - Treatment: Endoscopic band ligation should be performed for high-risk varices with a goal of eradication (Brady & EASL).
  • - Acute Bleed: Manage with octreotide, antibiotics, and band ligation. Avoid terlipressin due to risk of uterine contraction (EASL).


Delivery
  • - Vaginal delivery is preferred where possible (EASL).
  • - A shortened second stage of labor (e.g., with instrumental assistance) is recommended to reduce Valsalva and bleeding risk (EASL).


Wilson's Disease
  • - Autosomal recessive disorder of copper metabolism.
Management
  • - Therapy must be continued. Discontinuation is associated with hepatic deterioration and death (EASL).
  • - Zinc: Considered safe and can be continued (EASL).
  • - Chelators (D-penicillamine, trientine): Continue, but with dose reduction in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters to avoid fetal copper deficiency (EASL).
  • - Breastfeeding: Not discouraged, as copper/zinc levels in breastmilk are normal (EASL).


Vascular Liver Disease
  • - Includes Budd-Chiari syndrome and portal vein thrombosis (PVT).
  • - Pregnancy is a prothrombotic state, and these disorders may first manifest during or after pregnancy (EASL).
Outcomes
  • - Associated with high rates of preterm birth (76% in one Budd-Chiari study) and fetal loss (EASL).
  • - Maternal mortality is low in well-controlled, pre-existing cases (EASL).

Management
  • - Thromboprophylaxis with low molecular weight heparin is advisable in most cases throughout pregnancy (EASL).


Hepatic Tumors
  • - Majority are benign and managed conservatively.
Hepatocellular Adenoma (HCA)
  • - Benign tumors, strongly associated with oral contraceptive use and metabolic disorders (EASL).
Risk Based on Size
  • - < 5 cm: Low risk. Pregnancy does not increase complications. Growth occurs in ~25% of cases, so ultrasound monitoring is recommended (EASL).
  • - > 5 cm: Increased risk of enlargement and hemorrhage (25-30% risk). Treatment prior to pregnancy is recommended (EASL).


Hemangioma
  • - Most common benign liver tumor. Pregnancy is not precluded (EASL).
  • - Can increase in size. Risk of rupture is low (3.2% for giant >4cm tumors), but higher for large, peripheral, or exophytic lesions. Monitoring with ultrasound is recommended for high-risk cases (EASL).

Focal Nodular Hyperplasia (FNH)
  • - Second most common benign tumor. A benign course is expected during pregnancy (EASL).
  • - Pregnancy is not contraindicated, and routine imaging to monitor size is not recommended (EASL).

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
  • - Rare in pregnancy, associated with poor outcomes and risk of spontaneous rupture (12.5%) (EASL).
  • - Management is individualized and may include surgery or radiofrequency ablation (EASL).
  • - Ultrasound surveillance should be maintained in at-risk patients (e.g., cirrhosis) during pregnancy (EASL).


Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI)
  • - A diagnosis of exclusion. A careful history of all medications and supplements is critical (EASL).
Common Culprits in Pregnancy
  • - Antihypertensives: Methyldopa, hydralazine.
  • - Antithyroid: Propylthiouracil.
  • - Antibiotics: Tetracycline, amoxicillin-clavulanic acid (EASL).


Alcohol-Related Liver Disease (ALD)
  • - Alcohol use is increasing among women of childbearing age (EASL).
Management
  • - Screening: All pregnant women should be screened for alcohol use (EASL).
  • - Conception: Delaying conception until abstinence is achieved is recommended (EASL).
  • - Medications: Disulfiram is contraindicated. Naltrexone or acamprosate use must be individualized, weighing risks vs. benefits (EASL).


Post-Liver Transplant
  • - Fertility often returns within a year of successful transplant.
Timing of Pregnancy
  • - It is recommended to delay pregnancy for at least 1 year following transplant to ensure stable graft function and reduced immunosuppression (EASL).
  • - Conception >2 years post-transplant is associated with the best outcomes (EASL).

Maternal & Fetal Risks
  • - Maternal: Increased risk of gestational hypertension (~30%), preeclampsia (7-12%), GDM, and renal impairment (EASL).
  • - Fetal: Increased rates of preterm birth (14-53%) and fetal growth restriction (EASL).

Immunosuppression Management
  • - DO NOT STOP maintenance immunosuppression. Rejection rates are 0-20% during pregnancy (EASL).
Safe Medications
  • - Calcineurin Inhibitors: Tacrolimus, Cyclosporine.
  • - Antiproliferatives: Azathioprine.
  • - Corticosteroids: Prednis(ol)one.

Contraindicated Medications
  • - Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF): Teratogenic. Must be stopped at least 12 weeks before conception (EASL).
  • - mTOR inhibitors (Sirolimus, Everolimus): Limited data, not routinely recommended (EASL).




Multidisciplinary Care & Planning
  • - A collaborative approach is essential for optimizing outcomes.
Pre-conception Counseling
  • - Strongly recommended for all women with chronic liver disease or a history of a gestational liver disorder (EASL).
  • - Allows for risk stratification, medication optimization (e.g., stopping MMF), and patient education (EASL).

Contraception
  • - Most liver disorders do not impact contraceptive choices, but there are key exceptions (EASL, Table 7).
Caution/Contraindication for Combined Hormonal Contraception (CHC)
  • - History of ICP: Use with caution; ~10% experience cholestatic injury.
  • - Decompensated Cirrhosis: Risks may outweigh benefits.
  • - Hepatocellular Adenoma: Contraindicated due to estrogen-driven growth.
  • - History of Thrombosis (e.g., PVT): Contraindicated unless on adequate anticoagulation.


Delivery Considerations
  • - Timing and mode of delivery are tailored to the specific liver condition (EASL, Table 8).
Timing of Delivery
  • - Early Delivery Indicated: HELLP, AFLP, decompensated cirrhosis.
  • - ICP: Deliver at 35-36 weeks if bile acids >100 μmol/L.
  • - Most Pre-existing CLD: Plan for early term (38-39 weeks).

Mode of Delivery
  • - Vaginal Delivery Preferred: For most conditions, including cirrhosis with portal hypertension.
  • - Shortened 2nd Stage: Recommended for women with varices to reduce bleeding risk.
  • - Cesarean Section: Generally for obstetric indications. May be considered in AFLP based on some data showing improved outcomes.
