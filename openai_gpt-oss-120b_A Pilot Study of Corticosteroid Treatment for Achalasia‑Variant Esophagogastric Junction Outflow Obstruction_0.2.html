3. Background & Rationale
  • EGJOO definition: impaired LES relaxation with some preserved peristalsis (HRM, Chicago Classification v4.0).
  • Overlap with achalasia: clinical similarity; possible early/variant form.
  • Hypothesis: subset of achalasia‑like EGJOO driven by type 2 (allergic) inflammation → eosinophil/mast cell infiltration → neuronal damage.
  • Prior evidence: eosinophil infiltrates in esophagectomy specimens, mast cell degranulation in LES muscle, Th2 cytokine–induced eotaxin‑3 secretion, strong epidemiologic link between achalasia and EoE/allergic disorders.
  • Prednisone (20 mg daily ×14 days) proposed to test anti‑inflammatory responsiveness.
4. Study Design & Methods
4.1 Patient Selection
  • Adults with dysphagia and/or non‑cardiac chest pain; EGJOO confirmed by HRM (CC v4.0) and either abnormal timed barium esophagram/retained tablet or reduced EGJ opening on EndoFLIP.
  • Exclusions: prior foregut surgery, botulinum toxin ≤6 mo, hiatal hernia >2 cm, esophageal mass/stricture, reflux esophagitis LA B‑D, current immunosuppressants or opioids.
4.2 Intervention & Assessments
  • Prednisone 20 mg PO daily for 14 days.
  • Baseline and post‑treatment: allergy/atopy questionnaire, Eckardt score, BEDQ (Brief Esophageal Dysphagia Questionnaire).
  • Repeat HRM on day 14 (all within ≤18 mo of baseline HRM).
4.3 Outcome Measures
  • Primary symptomatic: change in Eckardt and BEDQ scores.
  • Manometric: supine & upright integrated relaxation pressure (IRP) pre‑ vs post‑prednisone.
  • Ancillary: atopic history, presence of eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE), FLIP distensibility index, esophagram tablet/liquid retention.
5. Patient Cohort & Baseline Characteristics
  • Enrolled 10 patients (mean 58.2 y; 5 M/5 F).
  • EGJOO confirmation: 4 by FLIP only, 2 by esophagram only, 4 by both modalities.
5.1 Atopy & EoE Prevalence
  • 9/10 had atopic history (asthma, eczema, food or seasonal allergy).
  • 2 patients (#3, #10) diagnosed with EoE (biopsy eosinophils ≥15/hpf) without structural rings/strictures.
5.2 Baseline Testing Summary (Table 1)
  • Esophagram liquid retention: present in pts 1, 3, 5, 7, 8, 9.
  • Tablet retention: present in pts 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9.
  • FLIP DI (60 mL) ranged 0.5–3.5; Max diameter 5.1‑15 mm.
  • Peak eosinophil counts: 0/hpf in pts 1,2,5‑9; 4/hpf (baseline biopsy) in pt 3; 30/hpf distal only in pt 4; 15/hpf in pt 10.
6. Results – Symptom Scores
  • Eckardt improvement: observed in 6 patients post‑prednisone.
  • BEDQ improvement: observed in 8 patients.
  • Mean scores (not statistically significant):
  • Eckardt ↓ from 3.5 → 3.2 (p=0.315).
  • BEDQ ↓ from 9.8 → 8.2 (p=0.385).
  • Greatest symptom gain: pt 10 (atopy + EoE).
  • Least improvement: pt 6 (no atopic history).
7. Manometric Outcomes
7.1 IRP Changes (All Patients with Repeat HRM)
  • Supine IRP ↓ 31.4 ± 8.9 → 22.9 ± 7.7 mmHg (p=0.02).
  • Upright IRP ↓ 26.1 ± 8.5 → 21.7 ± 8.5 mmHg (p=0.03).
7.2 Individual IRP Responses (Table 2)
  • Supine IRP decreased in 5 pts; 4 of these also ↓ upright IRP.
  • Normalization achieved only in pt 2 (upright IRP 9.2 mmHg).
  • HRM unsuccessful in pts 1 & 9 (attempted but no data).
7.3 Correlation with Atopy/EoE
  • Patients with atopic history tended to show greater symptom/IRP improvement.
  • Pt 10 (atopy + EoE) had most pronounced symptomatic drop (Eckardt 5→2, BEDQ 20→4).
  • Pt 6 (no atopy) showed worst symptom increase (Eckardt 2→7, BEDQ 5→21).
8. Discussion & Interpretation
  • Prednisone (2 wk) ↓ IRP significantly; Δ not always to normal.
  • Findings imply EGJOO subset is anti‑inflammatory responsive → potentially reversible.
  • Invasive therapies (pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, POEM) destroy muscle, cause GERD, lose efficacy over time – medical reversal attractive.
9. Study Limitations & Future Directions
  • No control group → cannot rule out spontaneous improvement.
  • Spontaneous resolution rates historically 15‑74 % within 6 mo (pre‑CCv4.0); unlikely with stricter CCv4.0 criteria used here.
  • Prednisone dose/duration may be insufficient for full reversal; IRP often ↓ but rarely normalized.
  • Inflammatory substrate likely variable among EGJOO patients.
10. Conclusions & Clinical Implications
  • Two‑week prednisone significantly lowered supine/upright IRP (p < 0.05).
  • Majority showed symptomatic score trends toward improvement despite non‑significant mean changes.
  • Supports hypothesis: EGJOO may represent early, allergy‑mediated achalasia reversible with anti‑inflammatory therapy.
  • Calls for larger, controlled trials to confirm causality and optimal steroid regimen.
