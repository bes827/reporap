A Pilot Study of Corticosteroid Treatment for Achalasia‑Variant Esophagogastric Junction Outflow Obstruction

2. Study Design & Ethical Oversight
  • Design: Prospective pilot trial evaluating prednisone (20 mg daily × 14 days) in EGJOO patients.
  • Eligibility: Adult dysphagia and/or non‑cardiac chest pain; conclusive EGJOO per HRM (CC v4.0), confirmed by esophagram and/or EndoFLIP.
  • Exclusions: Prior foregut surgery, botulinum toxin ≤6 mo, hiatal hernia >2 cm, esophageal mass/stricture, reflux esophagitis LA B‑D, current immunosuppressants or opioids.
  • IRB Approval: Baylor Research Institute Institutional Review Board #022‑001.
  • Trial Registration: ClinicalTrials.gov NCT06588348 (STROBE reporting).
3. Patient Cohort & Baseline Characteristics
  • Total enrolled: 10 patients (mean age 58.2 y; 5 M/5 F).
  • Diagnostic confirmation: 4 by FLIP only, 2 by esophagram only, 4 by both modalities.
  • Atopy prevalence: 9/10 with asthma, eczema, food or seasonal allergy; 2 patients (#3, #10) had EoE (no structural rings/strictures).
3.1 Baseline Testing Summary (Table 1)
  • Peak eosinophil count: 0–30 /hpf across subjects; patient 4 showed distal‑only elevation (30 /hpf).
  • Esophagram findings: Liquid retention in patients 1, 5, 7, 8, 9; tablet retention in patients 1, 3, 6, 7, 8, 9.
  • FLIP metrics: Distensibility Index (DI) 0.5–3.5 mm²/mmHg·s; Max diameter 5.1–15 mm.
3.2 Symptom Scores & Manometry (Table 2)
  • Eckardt scores pre‑Rx: 2–6 (median 4).
  • BEDQ scores pre‑Rx: 1–26 (median 9.8).
  • Supine IRP mean: 31.4 ± 8.9 mmHg; Upright IRP mean: 26.1 ± 8.5 mmHg.
4. Intervention & Outcome Measures
  • Drug regimen: Prednisone 20 mg PO daily × 14 days.
  • Assessments: Allergy/atopy questionnaire, Eckardt score, BEDQ, repeat HRM on day 14; all within ≤18 months of baseline HRM.
4.1 Symptom Change Post‑Prednisone
  • Eckardt improvement: 6/10 patients ↓; mean change 3.5 → 3.2 (p=0.315, NS).
  • BEDQ improvement: 8/10 patients ↓; mean change 9.8 → 8.2 (p=0.385, NS).
  • Best responder: Patient 10 (atopy + EoE) – greatest symptom drop.
  • Worst responder: Patient 6 (no atopic history) – minimal improvement.
4.2 Manometric Change Post‑Prednisone
  • Supine IRP: ↓ to 22.9 ± 7.7 mmHg (p=0.02).
  • Upright IRP: ↓ to 21.7 ± 8.5 mmHg (p=0.03).
  • IRP normalization: Achieved in only patient 2; others remained above normal despite reductions.
5. Pathophysiological Rationale
  • Hypothesis: Subset of achalasia/EGJOO driven by type 2 (allergic) inflammation in esophageal muscle.
  • Mechanism proposed: Eosinophils/mast cells infiltrate LES muscle → release cytotoxins → neuronal injury → impaired LES relaxation.
5.1 Supporting Evidence from Prior Work
  • Eosinophil infiltrates reported in esophagectomy & LES biopsies of achalasia patients (Spechler 2018).
  • Mast cell degranulation: Profound in LES muscle of achalasia (Nelson 2021).
  • Th2 cytokine effect: IL‑4Rα stimulation ↑ eotaxin‑3 secretion & smooth‑muscle tension (Nelson 2024).
5.2 Epidemiologic Link
  • Population study: Achalasia strongly associated with EoE and other allergic disorders (Reddy 2024).
  • Current cohort: 90 % EGJOO patients had atopy, supporting allergy‑mediated hypothesis.
6. Clinical Implications & Comparison to Standard Therapies
  • Standard treatments: Pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, POEM – invasive, irreversible, may cause GERD, and efficacy wanes over time.
  • Potential advantage of steroids: Anti‑inflammatory therapy could reverse early allergic inflammation before neuronal loss, offering a reversible medical option.
7. Limitations & Future Directions
  • No control arm: Cannot exclude spontaneous EGJOO resolution; historic spontaneous remission rates 15‑74 % within 6 mo (pre‑CCv4.0 studies).
  • Sample size: n=10 limits statistical power; IRP normalized in only one patient.
  • Prednisone regimen: 20 mg × 14 days may be insufficient dose/duration for full reversal.
  • Inflammatory substrate variability: Not all EGJOO patients exhibited eosinophilic elevation; suggests heterogeneous pathogenesis.
7.1 Recommendations for Further Research
  • Conduct randomized, placebo‑controlled trials with larger cohorts.
  • Explore higher prednisone doses or longer tapering schedules.
  • Investigate biomarkers (e.g., tissue eosinophil count, mast cell markers) to identify steroid‑responsive EGJOO phenotypes.
8. Conclusions
  • Two‑week prednisone course significantly lowered supine and upright IRP in a pilot EGJOO cohort.
  • Majority showed symptom score improvement, though mean changes were not statistically significant.
  • Findings support (but do not prove) that a subset of EGJOO may be allergy‑mediated, anti‑inflammatory responsive, and potentially reversible.
9. Discussion of Inflammatory Mechanisms
  • Type 2 inflammation posited as muscle‑predominant EoE form affecting LES.
  • Activated eosinophils & mast cells release mediators altering smooth‑muscle tone and destroying neurons.
9.1 Mast Cell Findings (Nelson 2021)
  • Profound degranulation observed in LES of achalasia patients.
9.2 Cytokine Effects (Nelson 2024)
  • Th2 cytokine signaling via IL‑4Rα ↑ eotaxin‑3 secretion → increased muscle tension.

11. Limitations of the Pilot Study (Expanded)
  • Control absence: No placebo group → cannot rule out spontaneous improvement.
  • Spontaneous resolution rates: Historical 15‑74 % within 6 mo (pre‑CCv4.0).
  • IRP trends: Significant ↓ but rarely normalized; dose/duration may be suboptimal.
12. Recommendations for Clinical Practice
  • Consider short‑course prednisone for EGJOO patients with documented atopy/EoE.
  • Use as bridge before invasive therapies (pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, POEM).
