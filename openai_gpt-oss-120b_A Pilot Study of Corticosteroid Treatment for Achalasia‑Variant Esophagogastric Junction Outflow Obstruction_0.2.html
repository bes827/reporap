A Pilot Study of Corticosteroid Treatment for Achalasia‑Variant Esophagogastric Junction Outflow Obstruction
  • Source: Gastro Hep Advances (pre‑proof) 2026
  • Authors: Chanakyaram A Reddy, MD; Ashton Ellison, MBchB; Vani J A Konda, MD; Anh D Nguyen, MD; Rhonda F Souza, MD; Stuart J Spechler, MD
1. Background & Rationale
  • EGJOO = impaired LES relaxation with some preserved peristalsis.
  • Hypothesized subset caused by type‑2 (allergic) inflammation → eosinophil/mast cell infiltration → neuronal damage → achalasia‑like dysfunction.
  • Prednisone (anti‑inflammatory) proposed to test reversibility of this allergic component.
2. Study Design & Population
  • Prospective pilot, 10 adult patients with dysphagia ± non‑cardiac chest pain.
  • Inclusion: conclusive EGJOO per HRM (CC v4.0), corroborated by esophagram and/or EndoFLIP.
  • Exclusion: prior foregut surgery, botulinum toxin ≤6 mo, hiatal hernia > 2 cm, mass/stricture, reflux esophagitis LA B‑D, immunosuppressants/opioids.
  • Intervention: Prednisone 20 mg PO daily ×14 days.
3. Baseline Characteristics & Atopic Profile
  • Mean age 58.2 y; gender 5 M / 5 F.
  • EGJOO confirmation: FLIP only (4), esophagram only (2), both (4).
  • 9/10 had atopy (asthma, eczema, food or seasonal allergy).
  • 2 patients (#3, #10) diagnosed with EoE (no rings/strictures).
4. Outcome Measures & Data Collection
  • Symptom scores: Eckardt and BEDQ, recorded at baseline & day 14.
  • Manometry: repeat HRM on day 14 (≤18 mo from baseline).
  • Integrated Relaxation Pressure (IRP) measured supine & upright.

6. Manometric Changes (IRP)
  • Supine IRP: ↓ from 31.4 ± 8.9 to 22.9 ± 7.7 mmHg, p = 0.02.
  • Upright IRP: ↓ from 26.1 ± 8.5 to 21.7 ± 8.5 mmHg, p = 0.03.
  • Supine IRP decreased in 5 patients; 4 of these also showed upright IRP reduction.
  • Normalization achieved in 1 patient (#2).
7. Safety & Feasibility
  • Prednisone course well‑tolerated; no serious adverse events reported within the 14‑day window.

7. Interpretation of Findings
  • Two‑week prednisone significantly lowered IRP (both supine & upright).
  • Symptom scores improved in most individuals despite non‑significant group means.
  • Suggests allergic/inflammatory subset of EGJOO is steroid‑responsive and potentially reversible.
8. Mechanistic Insights Supporting the Allergy Hypothesis
  • Prior work: eosinophil infiltrates in esophagectomy/LES biopsies of achalasia patients.
  • Published data: mast cell degranulation in LES muscle (Achalasia).
  • Th2 cytokine (IL‑4Rα) stimulation → ↑ eotaxin‑3 secretion & smooth‑muscle tension.
9. Relation to Prior Epidemiologic Evidence
  • Population study: achalasia strongly linked with EoE and other allergic disorders.
  • Current cohort: 90 % EGJOO patients had atopy, reinforcing possible allergy‑driven pathogenesis.
10. Comparison With Standard Definitive Therapies
  • Conventional EGJOO/achalasia treatments (pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, POEM) = invasive, irreversible LES destruction → symptom relief only, risk GERD, efficacy wanes over time.
  • Anti‑inflammatory medical therapy offers potential reversal before neuronal loss becomes permanent.
11. Study Limitations
  • No control/placebo arm → cannot exclude spontaneous EGJOO resolution.
  • Historical spontaneous remission rates (pre‑CCv4.0) = 15 %–74 % within 6 mo; likely lower with stricter CC v4.0 criteria used here.
  • Prednisone dose/duration may be insufficient for full reversal; IRP normalized in only one patient.
  • Inflammatory substrate appears heterogeneous across EGJOO cohort.
12. Conclusions & Future Directions
  • Pilot data strongly suggest a steroid‑responsive subset of EGJOO, supporting the type‑2 inflammation hypothesis.
  • Findings justify larger, controlled trials to:
  • Confirm causality between allergic inflammation and EGJOO.
  • Optimize prednisone dosing/duration or explore alternative anti‑inflammatory agents.
  • Identify biomarkers (e.g., eosinophil count, atopic history) predicting response.

13. Implications for Clinical Practice
  • Short‑course prednisone may delay or avoid invasive dilation/myotomy in allergic EGJOO subset.
  • Potential to reduce procedure‑related GERD risk and preserve LES function.
