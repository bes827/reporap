Introduction & Definitions
  • - Neuroendocrine Neoplasms (NENs) are a broad category of tumors originating from neuroendocrine cells.
  • - They are subdivided into Neuroendocrine Tumors (NETs) and Neuroendocrine Carcinomas (NECs).
NETs vs. NECs
  • - NETs: More common, typically more indolent.
  • - NECs: Usually demonstrate aggressive behavior.

Gastroenteropancreatic NETs (GEP-NETs)
  • - The most common subtype of NETs.
  • - Occur in the stomach, pancreas, small intestine, appendix, and colorectum.
  • - Can be 'functional' (hormone-producing with symptoms) or 'nonfunctional'.
Key Characteristics
  • - Typically subepithelial, making complete endoscopic resection challenging.
  • - Management often dictated by size (except for aggressive small intestinal NETs).
  • - Most GEP-NETs overexpress somatostatin receptors (SSTR), enabling novel imaging and treatment.



Epidemiology
  • - Incidence has risen worldwide, with a more than 6-fold increase in the US between 1973 and 2012.
  • - Rising incidence is likely due to increased incidental detection from endoscopy and imaging.
Incidence & Prevalence
  • - Highest NET incidence is within the GI tract, with a rate of 3.56 cases per 100,000.
  • - 20-year limited-duration prevalence increased 8-fold between 1993 and 2012.
  • - Prevalence rate in 2012 was nearly 0.05% (1 in 2000) in the US.

Distribution & Demographics
  • - Highest prevalence GEP-NETs: Rectal NETs (rNETs) (approx. 1 in 7000) and Small Intestinal NETs (siNETs) (approx. 1 in 11,000).
  • - Race: Black individuals have a higher incidence compared to non-Hispanic Whites and Hispanics in the US.
Geographic Differences
  • - North America: rNETs and siNETs are most common.
  • - Europe: pNETs and siNETs are most common.
  • - Asia: rNETs and pNETs are most common.


Survival
  • - Overall survival (OS) depends on grade, stage, and location.
  • - OS has been improving across all NETs, especially metastatic GEP-NETs.
Highest Median OS
  • - Appendiceal NETs: over 30 years.
  • - Rectal NETs: 24.6 years.



Pathology and Grading
  • - Pathology is the gold standard for diagnosis.
  • - 2022 WHO classification focuses on tumor differentiation and grade.
Key Concepts
  • - Differentiation: How closely cells resemble their endocrine counterpart (well or poorly differentiated).
  • - Grade: Biological aggressiveness, measured by Ki-67 index or mitotic count. Higher values mean more aggressive behavior.

Immunohistochemistry
  • - Positive staining for INSM1, synaptophysin, and chromogranin-A (CgA) supports diagnosis.
Marker Specifics
  • - CgA: Highly specific but less sensitive; may be absent in rectal NETs.
  • - INSM1: Both highly sensitive and specific.


Well-Differentiated Grade 3 NETs
  • - A newer category with survival rates between well-differentiated NETs and poorly differentiated NECs.
  • - Distinguishing from NEC can be challenging.
Biomarkers for Differentiation
  • - Suggests Grade 3 NET: Loss of ATRX, DAXX, menin, or p27.
  • - Suggests NEC: Loss of retinoblastoma protein, abnormal p53 expression, and/or loss of SSTR expression.



Diagnostic Considerations
  • - GEP-NETs can have vague symptoms, leading to substantial diagnostic delays.
Clinical Features
  • - Symptoms can relate to tumor growth or secretion of bioactive amines.
Carcinoid Syndrome (CS)
  • - Results from hypersecretion of vasoactive amines (serotonin, histamine).
  • - Occurs in about 30% of GI NETs, mainly from siNETs.
  • - Rare in localized GI NETs; typically requires liver metastases to bypass hepatic metabolism.
Classic Symptoms
  • - Flushing: 85%
  • - Diarrhea: 80%
  • - Abdominal cramps: 75%
  • - Bronchospasm: 20%
  • - Valvular heart disease: 40%


Functional Pancreatic NETs (pNETs)
  • - Produce syndromes related to the predominant hormone secreted.
Examples of Syndromes
  • - Insulinoma: Hypoglycemia
  • - Glucagonoma: Hyperglycemia
  • - Gastrinoma (ZES): Chronic diarrhea, GERD, recurrent peptic ulcers
  • - VIPoma (Verner-Morrison): Watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, achlorhydria



Laboratory Tests
  • - Evaluation of secreted hormone levels is helpful in functional pNETs.
Recommended Tests
  • - Preferred initial screening for CS: 24-hour urinary 5-HIAA test.
  • - Sensitivity and specificity for siNETs is >90%.
  • - Plasma 5-HIAA performs similarly but is not widely available.

Tests with Limited Value
  • - Serum serotonin and CgA are not recommended by guidelines for diagnosis.
  • - CgA can be falsely elevated by drugs (PPIs, H2 blockers), food, exercise, and comorbidities (atrophic gastritis, renal failure).


Imaging
  • - Essential for diagnosis and staging.
Anatomic Imaging
  • - Triple-phase abdominal CT: Recommended to detect small vascular liver metastases.
  • - MRI Pancreas: Optimal for assessing pNET resectability.
  • - MRI Liver: Best for detecting hepatic metastases (using hepatocyte-specific contrast).

Functional Imaging (SSTR PET)
  • - 68-Gallium or 64-Copper DOTA-TATE PET is the preferred modality.
  • - Has largely replaced octreotide scans due to superior image quality.
  • - Detects significantly more lesions than octreotide scans (95% vs 31%).
  • - A prospective comparison showed 64Cu-DOTA-TATE had a substantially better lesion detection rate.
Compelling Indications for SSTR PET
  • - Localizing an occult primary tumor.
  • - Staging, especially before surgery.
  • - Selecting patients for Radioligand Therapy (RLT).
  • - Monitoring patients whose disease is predominantly seen on SSTR PET.




Specific Subtypes of GEP-NETs
  • - Management strategies vary significantly by tumor location, type, size, and grade.
Gastric NETs (gNETs)
  • - Classified into three types, with Type 1 being the most common.
Classification
  • - Type 1: Most common. Arise from chronic atrophic gastritis, driven by hypergastrinemia. Typically multiple, small, and indolent.
  • - Type 2: Arise with gastrinomas (often in MEN1). Gastrin-driven, but with low gastric pH (no atrophy). Indolent.
  • - Type 3: Gastrin-independent. Typically a single tumor. Aggressive, with distant metastases in over half of patients at diagnosis.

Management of gNETs
  • - Endoscopic resection is contraindicated if: invasion beyond submucosa, lymph node involvement, distant metastases, or size >2 cm.
Type 1 gNETs (Low-Grade)
  • - ≥1 cm: Remove all tumors.
  • - 1-2 cm: Perform EUS, then endoscopic removal (EMR, ESD, EFTR).
  • - >2 cm: Surgical resection is preferred.
  • - <1 cm: Endoscopic surveillance is safe. Repeat endoscopy at 1 year, then every 1-2 years.

Type 1 gNETs (Grade 3)
  • - ≥1 cm: Staging (CT/MRI, SSTR PET) followed by surgery.
  • - <1 cm (superficial): EUS followed by endoscopic resection.

Type 2 & 3 gNETs
  • - Type 2: Management is similar to Type 1, but focus on resecting the primary gastrinoma.
  • - Type 3: Surgery is typically recommended. Endoscopic resection may be considered for select low-grade, superficial, subcentimeter lesions.



Duodenal & Ampullary NETs
  • - Nearly 90% of duodenal NETs (dNETs) are nonfunctioning.
  • - Multiple dNETs (~10% of cases) should raise concern for MEN1.
Management of dNETs
  • - <1 cm: Can be managed endoscopically (snare, EMR, ESD, EFTR).
  • - 1-2 cm (no invasion): Endoscopic resection is increasingly performed, though optimal approach (endoscopic vs. surgical) is unclear.
  • - >2 cm, higher grade, or invasion: Surgical resection is indicated.
  • - <5 mm (Grade 1, non-surgical candidate): Endoscopic surveillance may be appropriate.
Endoscopic Complications
  • - Bleeding: up to 20%
  • - Perforation after ESD: up to 13%-67% (most managed endoscopically).


Ampullary NETs
  • - Typically more aggressive with worse survival than dNETs.
  • - Recommended treatment: Pancreaticoduodenectomy.
  • - Endoscopic ampullectomy: May be reasonable for small, low-grade lesions in non-surgical candidates.


Pancreatic NETs (pNETs)
  • - The majority (65%-90%) are nonfunctional.
  • - Multiple pNETs suggest genetic syndromes (MEN1, Von Hippel Lindau, etc.).
  • - EUS is crucial, increasing pNET detection by >25% over CT/MRI.
Diagnosis
  • - EUS-FNA/FNB provides pathologic diagnosis.
  • - FNB is favored over FNA for histology and cellularity.

Management of pNETs
  • - Functional pNETs (localized/resectable mets): Surgery is recommended, regardless of size.
  • - Nonfunctional pNETs >2 cm: Surgery is the treatment of choice.
  • - Nonfunctional pNETs <1 cm: Surveillance is recommended.
  • - Nonfunctional pNETs 1-2 cm: Controversial, but the trend is swinging toward surveillance.
EUS-guided Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA)
  • - An attractive minimally invasive option for non-surgical candidates.
  • - Compared to surgery, EUS-RFA is safer but has a higher relapse rate (16.9% vs 0%).



Small Intestine NETs (siNETs)
  • - Often present with metastatic disease and are most commonly associated with Carcinoid Syndrome.
  • - Known to cause a strong desmoplastic reaction in the mesentery.
Diagnosis & Management
  • - SSTR PET is the key imaging modality.
  • - Surgical resection is the mainstay of therapy for loco-regional disease.
  • - Surgery includes segmental small-bowel resection with resection of regional lymph nodes.
  • - Prophylactic cholecystectomy should be considered if SSAs are likely to be needed.

Recurrence & Surveillance
  • - Rate of metastatic recurrence was 31% after a median follow-up of 7.5 years.
  • - Recurrence risk is highest between years 3 and 8.
  • - Surveillance: Radiographic/clinical every 6-12 months for year 1, then every 1-2 years for up to 10 years.


Appendiceal NETs
  • - Most are found incidentally after appendectomy.
  • - The majority (70%) are localized in the tip of the appendix.
Staging & Risk Factors
  • - Staging (CT/MRI, SSTR PET) is recommended for tumors ≥2 cm or with incomplete resection/positive nodes.
  • - Key pathologic features: size, lymphovascular invasion, depth of mesoappendix invasion, grade.
Lymph Node Metastasis Risk by Size
  • - <1 cm: 2.5%
  • - 1-2 cm: 31%
  • - >2 cm: 64%


Management
  • - Primarily surgical resection.
Treatment by Size
  • - >2 cm: Right hemicolectomy is warranted.
  • - <1 cm (with negative margins): Appendectomy alone is sufficient.
  • - 1-2 cm: Controversial. Consider right hemicolectomy for high-risk features (positive margin, mesoappendiceal invasion >3 mm, lymphovascular invasion).


Surveillance
  • - Recommended for high-risk cases.
  • - Anatomic imaging and clinical surveillance every 6-12 months for year 1, then every 1-2 years for up to 10 years.


Colorectal NETs
  • - Subdivided into colonic and rectal NETs (rNETs) due to distinct clinical features and management.
  • - Colonic NETs are more aggressive than rNETs.
Survival Comparison
  • - 5-year Overall Survival (OS):
  • - Colonic NETs: 69%
  • - Rectal NETs: 92%

Colonic NETs Management
  • - Classically managed with surgery due to aggressiveness.
  • - Recent ENETS guidelines allow for endoscopic resection of select grade 1 colonic NETs that are ≤1 cm.
  • - However, the C-NET study showed lymphovascular involvement in 26.4% of lesions <5 mm.

Rectal NETs (rNETs) Management
  • - Management depends on size, grade, and depth of invasion.
By Size
  • - <1 cm: Endoscopic removal (EMR, ESD, EFTR). No further follow-up needed for completely resected grade 1 tumors.
  • - 1-2 cm: Perform EUS and/or pelvic MRI. Endoscopic resection can be considered for localized tumors.
  • - >2 cm: Surgical excision is preferred.

Incomplete Resection
  • - Repeat endoscopic removal of residual tumor should be considered.
  • - EUS has 94% sensitivity for detecting residual rNET.
  • - Some studies suggest positive resection margins after ESD may not impact survival.




Therapy for Advanced GEP-NETs
  • - Sequencing of therapy is challenging and requires shared decision-making.
  • - Watchful waiting is a widely accepted strategy for asymptomatic patients with low-volume, low-grade disease.
Management of Hormonal Symptoms
  • - Acute CS symptoms: Short-acting octreotide.
  • - Chronic CS symptoms: Octreotide LAR and lanreotide.
  • - Octreotide-refractory CS diarrhea: Telotristat ethyl, which reduces peripheral serotonin.
Carcinoid Crisis
  • - A life-threatening condition from massive release of vasoactive compounds.
  • - Prophylactic octreotide before procedures is debated.
  • - Treatment: IV fluids, vasopressors, and IV octreotide.


Locoregional Options (Liver-Dominant Disease)
  • - Resectable disease: Surgical debulking can improve survival and control symptoms.
  • - Unresectable disease: Transarterial therapies (bland, chemo-, or radio-embolization).
  • - Objective response rate for transarterial therapy is >50% decrease in size.

Somatostatin Analogues (SSAs)
  • - First-line therapy for metastatic, well-differentiated GEP-NETs with progression or high-risk features.
Key Trials
  • - PROMID (Octreotide LAR): Improved median PFS to 14.3 months vs 6 months with placebo.
  • - CLARINET (Lanreotide): Increased 2-year PFS rates to 65% vs 33% with placebo.


Radioligand Therapy (RLT)
  • - 177Lu-DOTA-TATE delivers targeted radioisotope to SSTR-expressing tumors.
Key Trials
  • - NETTER-1: Reduced risk of progression or death by 79% compared to high-dose octreotide. Median PFS was 28.4 months vs 8.4 months.
  • - NETTER-2 (High-risk G2-G3): Improved PFS to 23 months vs 9 months with SSAs.

Toxicities
  • - Renal insufficiency
  • - Leukemia/myelodysplastic syndromes (<3% risk)
  • - Small-bowel obstruction and abdominal pain


Everolimus (mTOR Inhibitor)
  • - Approved for well-differentiated NETs regardless of primary site.
Key Trials
  • - RADIANT-3 (pNETs): Improved median PFS to 11 months vs 4.6 months with placebo.
  • - RADIANT-4 (Nonfunctional GI/Lung NETs): Improved median PFS to 11 months vs 3.9 months with placebo.


Antiangiogenic Drugs
  • - Target tumor blood supply.
Key Trials
  • - Sunitinib (pNETs): Improved PFS to 11 months vs 5.5 months with placebo.
  • - CABINET (Cabozantinib): Extended PFS for pNETs (11 vs 3 months) and extrapancreatic NETs (8 vs 3 months).


Chemotherapy
  • - Alkylating agents have shown activity, primarily in pNETs.
  • - Cytotoxic chemotherapy has negligible activity in midgut NETs.
Key Trial
  • - E2211 (pNETs): Temozolomide with capecitabine improved PFS compared to temozolomide alone (22.7 vs 14.4 months).



Conclusions
  • - GEP-NETs present diverse challenges due to variable behavior and often indolent course.
  • - Gastroenterologists are central to diagnosis, often finding GEP-NETs incidentally.
  • - Endoscopy plays an increasingly important role in management, with advanced resection techniques offering definitive therapy for select localized tumors.
  • - Outcomes for advanced disease have improved with targeted therapies, but optimal sequencing needs refinement.
