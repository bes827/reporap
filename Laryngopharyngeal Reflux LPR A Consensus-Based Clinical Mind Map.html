Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR): A Consensus-Based Clinical Mind Map
  • - This mind map synthesizes and compares key findings from two major international consensus statements:
  • - The San Diego Consensus for Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (Am J Gastroenterol 2025)
  • - The Dubai Definition and Diagnostic Criteria of Laryngopharyngeal Reflux: The IFOS Consensus (Laryngoscope 2023)
Knowledge Test
  • - 1. What is the minimum number of hypopharyngeal reflux events in 24h on HEMII-pH testing that suggests a diagnosis of LPR according to the Dubai Consensus?
  • - 2. The San Diego Consensus recommends a trial of twice-daily PPI for how long to assess symptomatic response in LPS with esophageal symptoms?
  • - 3. According to the San Diego Consensus, what percentage of esophageal acid exposure time (AET) on all 4 days of a 96-hr wireless pH study predicts a low likelihood of response to antisecretory therapy?
  • - 4. The Dubai Consensus states that erosive esophagitis is found in what percentage range of LPR patients?
  • - 5. The San Diego Consensus highlights a tool called LCAT for identifying what specific psychological processes in patients with LPS?

I. Terminology & Definitions: A Tale of Two Paradigms
  • - A fundamental difference exists between the two consensus groups on how to define and label the condition, which impacts the entire clinical approach.
San Diego Consensus: Differentiating Symptoms (LPS) from Disease (LPRD)
  • - Proposes a major terminology shift to avoid assuming reflux is the cause based on symptoms alone.
  • - CRITICAL POINT: The presence of LPS does not equate to LPRD.
Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS)
  • - Refers to the chronic and frequent throat/airway symptoms themselves.
  • - This term is descriptive and does not implicate reflux as the etiology.
  • - Criteria: Symptoms must be frequent (at least twice weekly) and chronic (present for more than 8 weeks).

Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD)
  • - A specific diagnosis requiring two components:
  • - 1. Presence of chronic, troublesome LPS.
  • - 2. Objective evidence supporting a relationship between symptoms and gastroesophageal reflux.


Dubai Consensus: A Unified, Broader Definition of LPR
  • - Maintains the traditional term 'Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR)' but provides a formal definition.
  • - Defines LPR as a disease of the upper aerodigestive tract resulting from direct and/or indirect effects of gastroduodenal content reflux.
  • - This reflux induces morphological and/or neurological changes in the upper aerodigestive tract.
Alternative Names
  • - Acknowledges many alternative names used in practice:
  • - Silent reflux, pharyngolaryngeal reflux, extraesophageal reflux, atypical GERD, reflux laryngitis.


Areas of Consensus in Pathophysiology
  • - Both groups agree that LPR/LPS involves more than just acid and has a complex pathophysiology.
Shared Mechanisms
  • - LPR and GERD share common mechanisms (e.g., LES dysfunction) but can present with different clinical pictures.
  • - Dubai: Highlights the role of pepsin and bile salts in causing mucosal inflammation and tissue changes.

Contributing Factors
  • - Dubai Consensus identifies factors that may impair gastroesophageal function:
  • - Tobacco use, obesity, primary esophageal dysmotility.
  • - Diets high in fat, salt, sugar, mint, or acidic content.



II. Clinical Presentation: Symptoms & Signs
  • - There is broad agreement on the common symptoms and the limitations of physical signs.
Symptoms (Broad Consensus)
  • - Both panels agree on a core set of non-specific symptoms.
Core Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms
  • - Cough (chronic, troublesome)
  • - Voice change / hoarseness
  • - Throat clearing
  • - Excess throat phlegm / sticky mucus
  • - Throat pain / sore throat
  • - Globus sensation

Associated Esophageal Symptoms
  • - Both panels recognize that typical GERD symptoms can be present.
  • - Heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain.
  • - San Diego: Emphasizes that assessing for these is a critical first step in patient stratification.


Signs on Laryngoscopy (Strong Consensus on Limitations)
  • - CRITICAL CONSENSUS: Laryngoscopic findings are neither specific nor pathognomonic for LPR/LPRD.
  • - A diagnosis CANNOT be made based on laryngoscopy findings alone.
Primary Role of Laryngoscopy
  • - The main purpose is to evaluate for other non-reflux related causes of symptoms.
  • - Most importantly: to rule out malignancy.
  • - San Diego: Recommends videostroboscopy for hoarseness if white-light exam is unrevealing.

Common (but Non-Specific) Signs
  • - Arytenoid erythema and edema
  • - Posterior commissure hypertrophy (pachydermia)
  • - Diffuse laryngeal erythema
  • - Vocal fold edema
  • - Subglottic edema ('pseudosulcus')
  • - Endolaryngeal mucus

Scoring Systems (Consensus: Not for Diagnosis)
  • - Both panels agree that scoring systems based on laryngeal findings are not diagnostic.
  • - San Diego: Specifically states the Reflux Finding Score (RFS) and Reflux Sign Assessment (RSA) are not diagnostic of LPRD.

Vocal Process Granuloma
  • - Dubai: Can be a sign of LPR and should prompt evaluation or treatment.
  • - San Diego: Notes uncertainty, citing a survey where laryngologists considered phonotrauma (88%) a more likely cause than reflux (67%).



III. Diagnostic Pathway & Objective Testing
  • - This is an area with significant differences in recommended approaches, particularly regarding initial strategy and choice of reflux monitoring.
Initial Patient Stratification (San Diego Emphasis)
  • - A key unique contribution of the San Diego consensus is the initial branching of the care pathway.
Step 1: Assess for Esophageal Symptoms
  • - Crucial First Step: Categorize patients into two groups:
  • - 1. LPS with concurrent esophageal symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation).
  • - 2. Isolated LPS (no esophageal symptoms).


Empiric Therapeutic Trial (Divergent Recommendations)
  • - The role and timing of a medication trial differ between the two guidelines.
San Diego Recommendation
  • - An empiric trial is appropriate only for patients with LPS + concurrent esophageal symptoms.
  • - For isolated LPS, objective reflux testing is recommended before empiric trials to personalize management.
  • - Trial Regimen: Twice-daily PPI for 3 months, with or without alginate therapy.

Dubai Recommendation
  • - An empiric trial is an option if HEMII-pH testing is unavailable.
  • - The trial should cover acid, weakly acid, and non-acid reflux.
  • - Success is based on symptom improvement at 3 months.


Objective Reflux Monitoring (The Reference Standard)
  • - CONSENSUS: Objective testing is the reference standard for diagnosis and is required before considering invasive anti-reflux management (e.g., surgery).
  • - Testing should be performed OFF acid suppression therapy (washout period of at least 7 days).
pH-Impedance Monitoring
  • - Measures both acid (pH drop) and non-acid (impedance change) reflux events, providing mechanistic detail.
Dubai Consensus: HEMII-pH as the Preferred Tool
  • - Hypopharyngeal-esophageal multichannel intraluminal impedance-pH (HEMII-pH) is highlighted as the key objective tool.
  • - Diagnostic Criterion: >1 hypopharyngeal reflux event (acid, weakly acid, or non-acid) in 24 hours.
  • - Emphasizes that standard esophageal-only or dual pH probes are inadequate for diagnosing LPR.

San Diego Consensus: Role of pH-Impedance
  • - Acknowledged as a diagnostic option, particularly useful in isolated LPS to understand mechanisms.
  • - Notes it has a lower diagnostic yield for GERD compared to prolonged wireless pH monitoring.


Prolonged Wireless pH Monitoring (96-hour)
  • - A key focus of the San Diego Consensus, not emphasized by the Dubai group.
San Diego Recommendation
  • - Preferred test for unproven GERD, especially in patients with concomitant esophageal symptoms and before escalating management.
  • - Major Advantage: Captures day-to-day variability in acid exposure.
Key Diagnostic Thresholds (AET)
  • - Proven GERD: Distal esophageal Acid Exposure Time (AET) >6.0%.
  • - Physiologic Reflux: AET <4.0% on all 4 days of the study predicts a low likelihood of symptom response to antisecretory therapy.



Other Testing Modalities (Consensus: Limited/No Role)
  • - Oropharyngeal pH monitoring (e.g., Restech): Both panels agree this has poor specificity and is not recommended as a standalone diagnostic tool.
  • - Pepsin Saliva Detection: Dubai consensus notes it's a non-invasive adjunctive test but cannot confirm LPR and has many limitations (diet, timing, low sensitivity). Not discussed by San Diego.


Upper GI Endoscopy (Broad Consensus)
  • - CONSENSUS: Endoscopy may be normal in many patients with LPR/LPS.
  • - Its findings can provide conclusive or supportive evidence of underlying GERD.
Conclusive Evidence of GERD
  • - San Diego: Los Angeles (LA) grades B, C, or D erosive esophagitis and/or biopsy-proven Barrett's esophagus.
  • - Dubai: Notes erosive esophagitis is found in only 10%-30% of LPR patients.

Indications for Endoscopy
  • - CONSENSUS: Indicated for alarm symptoms (severe dysphagia, weight loss, hematemesis, family history of upper GI cancer).
  • - Also indicated for patients who do not respond to an empiric therapeutic trial.


Esophageal Manometry (Niche Role)
  • - Dubai Consensus: Indicated to evaluate for esophageal dysmotility or to assist in reflux probe placement in non-responders.
  • - Not recommended as a primary diagnostic tool for LPR.


IV. Beyond Reflux: The Brain-Larynx Axis (San Diego Emphasis)
  • - A major focus of the San Diego Consensus, highlighting that symptoms are often driven by factors other than or in addition to reflux.
Laryngeal Hyperresponse & Hypervigilance
  • - These behavioral and psychological processes can be the primary etiology of LPS or contribute to symptoms in confirmed LPRD.
Definitions
  • - Laryngeal Hyperresponse: Repetitive behaviors like throat clearing, coughing, or biomechanical strain.
  • - Laryngeal Hypervigilance: Increased threat-induced attention to the larynx, often co-existing with symptom-specific anxiety.

Identification Tool: LCAT
  • - The Laryngeal Cognitive-Affective Tool (LCAT) is a validated patient-reported outcome instrument.
  • - Helps identify individuals with elevated levels of hypervigilance and anxiety related to their LPS who may benefit from behavioral intervention.


Treatment for Brain-Larynx Dysfunction
  • - Recommended for:
  • - Patients with isolated LPS and negative reflux testing.
  • - Patients with confirmed LPRD who have a partial or poor response to anti-reflux therapy.
Laryngeal Recalibration Therapy (LRT)
  • - A structured behavioral therapy provided by a voice-specialized Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP).
  • - Integrates traditional voice therapy techniques with autonomic regulation (heart rate variability biofeedback) and cognitive reframing (acceptance and commitment therapy).

Pharmacologic Neuromodulation
  • - Aims to improve laryngeal hypersensitivity.
Gabapentin / Pregabalin
  • - May be an effective adjunct to LRT, especially for cough.

Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
  • - May help LPS symptoms, but must monitor for anticholinergic side effects like xerostomia (dry mouth).

SSRIs / SNRIs
  • - Not specifically studied in LPS, but may benefit individuals with concomitant clinical anxiety and/or depression.




V. Patient-Reported Outcome (PRO) Instruments
  • - STRONG CONSENSUS: PROs are valuable for tracking symptom severity and treatment response over time, but they are NOT for diagnosis due to their non-specificity.
LPR-Specific PROs
  • - Instruments like the Reflux Symptom Index (RSI) and Reflux Symptom Score (RSS) are non-specific for diagnosing LPR/LPRD.
  • - San Diego: A clinically significant RSI cutoff of ≥13 could not distinguish patients with increased proximal reflux on objective testing.

GERD-Specific PROs
  • - Instruments like GERDQ and GERD-HRQL are useful for assessing esophageal symptoms but are not specific for LPS and LPRD.
