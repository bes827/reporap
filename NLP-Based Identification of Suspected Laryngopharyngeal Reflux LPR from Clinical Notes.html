NLP-Based Identification of Suspected Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR) from Clinical Notes
  • A summary of design ideas for using Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Large Language Models (LLMs) to identify suspected LPR from unstructured GI and ENT clinical notes, even without a gold-standard diagnosis.

NLP Design Ideas for LPR Identification
  • Four distinct approaches to identify suspected LPR from clinical notes using NLP, focusing on different methods of knowledge extraction and model training.
1. Expert-Guided Term Extraction
  • A rule-based approach using manually curated clinical terms to flag potential LPR cases.
Methodology
  • - A panel of GI & ENT specialists manually reviews guidelines & notes.
  • - They list key symptoms, signs, & management phrases for suspected LPR.
  • - NLP is used to scan notes for these specific terms.
Prediction
  • - The model assesses how combinations of these terms predict a suspected diagnosis.
  • - Prediction is based on documented clinical impressions.
Example Terms
  • - Symptoms: “globus sensation,” “chronic throat clearing,” “hoarseness”.
  • - Management: “empiric PPI trial”.
2. Unsupervised Pattern Detection
  • Utilizes machine learning to discover naturally occurring language patterns associated with LPR without pre-defined rules.
Methodology
  • - Apply unsupervised ML techniques (e.g., clustering, topic modeling) to the note corpus.
  • - The model identifies recurring patterns of language that co-occur when LPR is mentioned as a suspected diagnosis.
Clinical Validation
  • - Physicians review the identified language clusters.
  • - These clusters are mapped to clinical phenotypes, creating “language signatures”.
  • - These signatures are then used to flag suspected LPR in new, unseen notes.
3. Consensus-Based Annotation
  • A supervised learning approach that trains a model on cases labeled by multiple clinicians, bypassing the need for a gold-standard diagnosis.
Dataset Creation
  • - A set of suspected LPR cases is developed through consensus annotation.
  • - Multiple clinicians independently review notes & tag suspected LPR.
Model Training
  • - A machine learning classifier or NLP model is trained on the consensus-labeled data.
  • - The model learns from both structured & unstructured data.
Data Features
  • - Structured Data: Demographics, treatments.
  • - Unstructured Data: Free text from clinical notes.
Evaluation
  • - The trained model's output is evaluated on new, unannotated records.
4. Differential Diagnosis Simulation
  • An advanced NLP approach that mimics a clinician's reasoning process to assess the likelihood of LPR among other potential diagnoses.
Methodology
  • - Uses a prompt-based or “Chain-of-Thought” (CoT) approach.
  • - The model is given clinical context from GI/ENT notes.
Reasoning Process
  • - The model is prompted to reason through the differential diagnosis stepwise.
  • - It weighs evidence for (+) and against (-) LPR among other contributors (e.g., GERD, allergy).
  • - Produces an intermediate ranking of suspicion for LPR, reflecting natural diagnostic reasoning.
Using Open-Weight LLMs for LPR Identification
  • Considerations and design ideas for implementing open-weight large language models (LLMs), highlighting advantages in privacy and customization.
Key Advantages of Open-Weight LLMs
  • - Feasibility: Practical to implement locally.
  • - Privacy: Data can remain within the institution.
  • - Customization: Can be fine-tuned for specific needs.
Additional Benefits
  • - Transparency: Model architecture is known.
  • - Flexibility: Adaptable to local data privacy protocols.
1. Prompt Engineering for Diagnosis
  • Leveraging the LLM’s reasoning capabilities through carefully structured prompts to identify and justify suspected LPR.
Process
  • - Feed clinical narratives into the LLM.
  • - Ask the model to flag suspected LPR, citing key evidence.
Cited Evidence
  • - Key symptoms.
  • - Clinical findings.
  • - Management decisions.
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Reasoning
  • - Use CoT style for transparent outputs.
  • - Allows clinicians to verify the model's reasoning process.
2. Fine-Tuning with Consensus Labels
  • Adapting the LLM to local clinical nuances by training it on a custom-labeled dataset.
Process
  • - Collect a consensus-labeled dataset where clinicians tag suspected LPR cases.
  • - Gold-standard confirmation is not required.
  • - Fine-tune the open-weight LLM on these specific cases.
Benefits
  • - ↑ Specificity for LPR identification.
  • - Adaptation to local language & abbreviations.
  • - Accounts for local guidelines & diagnostic uncertainty unique to LPR.
3. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
  • Enhancing the LLM's diagnostic output by integrating it with a knowledge base of medical texts or guidelines.
Process
  • - Integrate the LLM with a retrieval system over a corpus of medical texts or institutional guidelines.
  • - When parsing notes, the model first retrieves relevant references.
  • - It then uses this retrieved information to generate its diagnostic suspicion output.
Benefits
  • - ↑ Interpretability: The model's reasoning is linked to specific sources.
  • - ↑ Evidence Linkage: Directly connects output to established medical knowledge.
4. Benchmark Against Rule-Based Systems
  • Establishing a performance baseline by comparing the LLM against simpler, expert-defined systems.
Process
  • - Compare LLM outputs with simple rule-based systems (e.g., from Expert-Guided Term Extraction).
  • - Evaluate both methods on the same note corpus.
Evaluation Metrics
  • - Sensitivity.
  • - Interpretability.
  • - Reproducibility.
Goal
  • - Iteratively refine prompts and model training based on benchmark results.
Conclusion & Refinement
  • Final considerations for implementing these NLP approaches for LPR identification.
Iterative Refinement
  • - Any of these approaches can be iteratively refined.
  • - Physician feedback is crucial for improvement.
Overarching Goal
  • - To bring greater objectivity to how suspected LPR is identified in large clinical note datasets.
LLM Caveat
  • - Open-weight LLMs still require careful evaluation.
  • - Important to manage diagnostic uncertainty and ambiguous cases effectively.
