Device-assisted Enteroscopy: National Irish Data Support a Centralized High-Volume Model to Achieve Performance Targets
  • Source: Endoscopy International Open 2026; 14: a27779128
  • Topic: Device-Assisted Enteroscopy (DAE) Services in Ireland — Performance Evaluation Against ESGE KPIs
  • Author(s): Costigan C, Connaughton R, Sihag S, Leung E, Butler T, O'Hara FJ, Hall B, McNamara D
1. Introduction and Background
  • Device-assisted enteroscopy (DAE) provides effective diagnosis/treatment for small bowel disorders since its introduction
  • DAE often serves as therapeutic arm following less-invasive small bowel capsule endoscopy (SBCE)
  • European Society of Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ESGE) published DAE guidance in 2015, quality-assurance guidelines with KPIs in 2019, and updated performance measures in 2025
  • Study aim: review national DAE services in Ireland against international KPIs and determine practice elements for future development
2. Study Methods and Design
  • Dual-center, retrospective analysis of both Irish enteroscopy centers (Tallaght University Hospital and Connolly Hospital Blanchardstown)
  • Study period: January 2015 to May 2024 (Tallaght from 2015; Connolly from 2021)
  • Data collected via GI Reporting Tool (Unisoft) or EndoRAAD systems
  • Conscious sedation procedures performed in endoscopy suites with endoscopist-administered sedation
  • General anesthetic cases performed in surgical theater of adjacent day hospital (unstable/multimorbid patients excluded)
  • No access to deep sedation in Irish endoscopy suites or regular surgical theater space for enteroscopy
  • Decision for prolonged/repeat procedures sedation type at endoscopist discretion
  • Demographic, procedure, and follow-up data anonymized and combined; analyzed using chi-square, Fisher exact test, or logistical regression
  • Study approved as service evaluation in both hospitals
3. Patient Demographics and Cohort Characteristics
  • Total studies reviewed (ITT): 1,014 DAE studies
  • Excluded from per-protocol analysis (n=73):
  • Inadequate bowel preparation: 29
  • Ileal intubation failure due to looping: 16
  • Technical failure impeding overtube advancement: 5
  • Anatomical stenosis (esophageal/anal): 5
  • Poor patient tolerance: 8
  • Food bolus in stomach: 5
  • RDBE stopped after bleeding malignant colon lesion: 1
  • Panic attack with withdrawal consent: 1
  • Incomplete EPRs: 3
  • Final cohort (PP analysis): 941 studies on 798 individual patients
  • Gender distribution: 548 (58%) male
  • Mean age: 61 ± 17 years (range 17–91)
  • Procedure distribution:
  • Anterograde DBE (ADBE): 745 (79%)
  • Retrograde DBE (RDBE): 196 (21%)

5. Procedure and Technical Success
  • Enteroscope types: DBE used in 864 (92%), SBE in 77 (8%); no spiral enteroscopy performed in Ireland during study period
  • Pre-procedure imaging: SBCE performed in advance in 643 patients (68%), cross-sectional imaging in 191 (20%)
  • Depth of insertion: documented in 909 procedures (97%); mean depth ADBE: 201 cm ± 101 cm; mean depth RDBE: 101 cm ± 67 cm
  • Complete enteroscopy: only three patients achieved complete enteroscopy as single procedure — all had altered anatomy; one further patient had laparoscopic-assisted complete bidirectional enteroscopy
5.1 Tattooing and Lesion Marking
  • Maximal depth of insertion marked with tattoo in 645 (69%)
  • Lesions requiring further treatment/assessment: 68; tattooed: 62 of 68 (91%)
  • Anterograde enteroscopy placed significantly more tattoos than retrograde: 564/745 (76%) vs 80/196 (41%), P=0.0001
  • No difference in estimated depth or number of cycles between conscious sedation and general anesthetic groups (P=0.8942, P=0.7261)
  • No gender differences in depth or cycles (P=0.2328, P=0.1718)
5.2 Sedation Methods and Dosing
  • Conscious sedation: 868 (92%); General anesthetic: 73 (8%)
  • Xylocaine spray 50 mg used as topical anesthetic on oropharynx for all anterograde studies
  • Median midazolam dose: 5 mg (IQR 3–7 mg); median fentanyl dose: 75 µg (IQR 50–100 µg)
  • Male gender associated with higher sedation doses: P=0.0245 for midazolam, P=0.0287 for fentanyl
  • Age showed inverse correlation with midazolam dose by logistical regression (P=0.0003); no correlation for fentanyl (P=0.7487)
  • Antispasmodic use: hyoscine n-butyl bromide in 254 patients (27%) — mean dose 15.4 mg; glucagon 1 mg used as alternative in 11 patients with contraindications
5.3 Comfort Scores and Patient Tolerance
  • Comfort score recorded in 751 conscious sedation cases (87%)
  • Overall mean comfort score: 1.48 ± 0.745
  • Adequate comfort (Modified Gloucester Comfort Scale ≤3): 716 of 751 (95%)
  • Weak negative correlation between comfort and cycles/depth (Spearman R=0.13/R=0.14)
  • No correlation between comfort and medication dose (R=-0.05 for midazolam, R=0.02 for fentanyl)
  • Poor patient tolerance limited duration and extent in 8 of 868 procedures (0.92%) under conscious sedation
5.4 Technical Success and Failure Analysis
  • Overall technical success rate (ITT): 941 of 1014 (93%)
  • Failed procedures more likely to be retrograde than antegrade: 46/242 (19%) vs 27/772 (3.5%), P<0.0001 — primarily due to poor bowel preparation or excess looping in colon
  • Adequate preparation rate significantly lower for RDBEs: 213/242 (88%) vs ADBEs: 767/772 (99%), P<0.0001
  • Ileal intubation rate: 197 of 242 (81%) — no significant difference between sites
  • Significant difference in ileal intubation rate between sedation groups: 175/220 (80%) vs 22/22 (100%), P=0.0177
6. Diagnostic and Therapeutic Yield
  • Overall diagnostic yield: 592 of 941 (63%)
  • No difference in overall diagnostic yield between centers (P=0.855) or between DBE and SBE use (P=0.801)
  • ADBE more likely to identify pathology than RDBE: P<0.0001

7. Adverse Events
  • Minor adverse events: 24 cases (2.5%)
  • Major adverse events: 9 of 1014 cases (0.88%)
7.1 Minor Adverse Events Details
  • Mucosal tears/trauma related to overtube advancement: 19 (2%) — only one case required endotherapy; none required repeat endoscopy, surgical intervention, or hospital admission
  • Post-polypectomy bleeding: 4 cases — all managed endoscopically; hemostasis achieved in all cases
  • No patients documented significant drop in serum hemoglobin, presented with complications after procedure, or required repeat endoscopic assessment/intervention
  • One patient developed symptomatic hypoglycemia in recovery room
7.2 Major Adverse Events Details
7.2.1 Bowel Perforations Related to Stricture Dilatation (n=2)
  • Both patients had known history of structuring Crohn's disease
  • Both attended for dilatation due to symptoms; both underwent balloon dilatation of short stricture (identified on MRI) after MDT discussion
  • Both patients well interprocedurally with satisfactory endoscopic result

Case 1: Patient had persistent bloating in recovery room; CT revealed small amount of free air without evidence of bowel perforation — diagnosed as iatrogenic microperforation; managed conservatively, observed, discharged after short admission when interval imaging demonstrated resolution of pneumoperitoneum without surgical intervention

Case 2: Patient had multiple distal small bowel strictures identified on imaging of various severity; due to previous surgeries, MDT decided to proceed with endoscopic dilatation — patient well post-procedure; endoscopic clip placed at dilated stricture site for CT mapping and rediscussion at MDT regarding remaining strictures; discharged from endoscopy department, attended radiology later same day for CT, still feeling well, then contacted to return due to presence of free air — diagnosed with iatrogenic microperforation; managed conservatively, observed, discharged after short admission

7.2.2 Other Major Adverse Events
  • Intraprocedure hypotension: 2 cases — managed by reversal agent, no subsequent inpatient stay required
  • Intraprocedure hypoxia: 2 cases — managed by reversal agent, no subsequent inpatient stay required
  • Massive epistaxis: 1 case — treated appropriately, discharged same day
  • Hypothermia and acidemia: 1 case — due to malfunction with heating unit for water pump; received supportive care, later discharged well
  • Massive uncontrollable intraprocedural bleeding: 1 case — patient undergoing DAE for bleeding later died of hemorrhagic shock, deemed unrelated to procedure
7.3 Mortality
  • No direct procedure-related mortalities
  • Overall 30-day mortality rate: 1 of 941 (0.1%)

8.4 KPI Improvements Summary
  • Five of eight major KPIs met on both ITT and per-protocol basis
  • All minor performance measures met over study duration
  • KPIs consistent between both Irish referral centers
  • Both centers perform high volume (>50 procedures/year) as defined by UK-DEEP paper
9. Discussion
9.1 Overall Performance and KPI Compliance
  • National Irish DAE study demonstrates good compliance with ESGE KPIs, improving over time
  • Five of eight major KPIs and all minor performance measures met — consistent between both referral centers
  • Both Irish high-volume centers comparable to three high-volume UK centers from DEEP-UK study; all performed better than low-volume UK centers
  • Supports centralization of DAE services in high-volume referral centers where human resources and infrastructure can be optimized
  • High-volume model advantages include access to case volume/mix for training and research/innovation
9.2 Conscious Sedation vs General Anesthetic
  • Conscious sedation used frequently in Ireland: 92% (868); general anesthetic: 8% (73)
  • Mean comfort score adequate at 1.4; only 8 of 941 (0.85%) procedures limited due to intolerance
  • Overall performance not affected by sedation type except ileal intubation rates in retrograde procedures — higher with general anesthesia
  • Potential bias: local strict anesthetic protocols for day case surgery limiting GA access in elderly/comorbid patients (would bias results in favor of GA)
  • Study suggests conscious sedation can be used in majority of cases without impacting performance — differs from recent publications calling for deep sedation or GA for all DAEs
  • Caveat: majority of patients had prior SBCE (68%), suggesting possible bias toward selecting patients with targets requiring intervention in first/third tertile, thereby requiring shorter depth of insertion and shorter procedure
  • However, mean depth of insertion exceeds recently published DEEP-UK study: ADBE 201cm ± 101 cm, RDBE 101 cm ± 67 cm
