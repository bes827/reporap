Modern Advanced Therapies for Inflammatory Bowel Diseases: Practical Considerations and Positioning
  • Authors: David Fudman, Ryan A. McConnell, Christina Ha, Siddharth Singh
  • Journal: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology
  • Publication Date: Accepted June 7, 2024
  • Focus: Practical use of newer advanced IBD therapies, real-world effectiveness & safety, dosing & monitoring, special situations (pregnancy, comorbidities, ASUC, perioperative).
  • Positioning: Integration of medication effectiveness & safety w/ individual patient risk & preferences.
Knowledge Test

  • 2. Which JAK inhibitor is approved for both UC and CD?

  • 4. Which IL-23 antagonist has shown superiority to ustekinumab in clinical trials for CD?

JAK Inhibitors (JAKi)
  • Oral small molecule drugs inhibiting transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines.
  • Tofacitinib: Primarily inhibits JAK1 & JAK3, approved for UC in 2018.
  • Upadacitinib: Primarily inhibits JAK1, approved for UC in 2022 & CD in 2023.
Real-World Effectiveness
  • Tofacitinib: ≈ efficacy in real-world cohorts similar to clinical trials.
  • Upadacitinib: Limited real-world data, but high rates of clinical response & remission in UC.
  • Upadacitinib may be more effective than tofacitinib in UC.
Tofacitinib Details
  • Systematic review: 2/3 patients achieved response, 1/3 remission within 8 weeks.
  • Higher rates in biologic-naïve patients.
  • May be more effective than vedolizumab in UC w/ prior TNF antagonist failure.
Upadacitinib Details
  • >80% clinical response in some UC cohorts.
  • 64% CD patients achieved clinical response, 27% remission @ 3 months (TNF antagonist, vedolizumab, ustekinumab failure).
  • May be more effective in colon-dominant CD & perianal disease.
Dosing and Onset of Action
  • Efficacy & safety are dose-dependent → lowest effective dose is optimal.
  • Rapid onset of action: clinical improvement w/in 1-3 days of starting therapy.
  • Upadacitinib can reduce abdominal pain & stool frequency w/in 1 week.
Tofacitinib Dosing
  • Induction: 10mg twice daily (or 22mg ER daily) for 8 weeks.
  • Maintenance: 10mg twice daily or 5mg twice daily (or 11mg ER daily).
  • Dose de-escalation: Up to 50% lose response on de-escalation to 5mg twice daily.
Upadacitinib Dosing
  • Induction: 45mg daily for 8 weeks (UC) or 12 weeks (CD).
  • Maintenance: 15mg or 30mg daily (UC & CD).
  • 30mg daily may be more effective, esp. w/ prior TNF antagonist failure.
Safety
  • FDA black-box warnings: ↑ risk of mortality, MACE, thrombosis, malignancy, serious infections.
  • ORAL Surveillance trial (RA patients): ↑ rates of MACE, malignancy, VTE, serious infection, herpes zoster w/ tofacitinib.
  • No ↑ risk of MACE, VTE, or malignancy observed in IBD patients treated w/ tofacitinib or upadacitinib in long-term follow-up.
Herpes Zoster Risk
  • ↑ risk of herpes zoster reactivation in dose-dependent manner, esp. w/ corticosteroids.
  • All patients receiving JAKi should receive 2-dose recombinant herpes zoster vaccine.
  • Avoid live vaccines while taking JAKi.
Acne
  • Common adverse effect, particularly w/ upadacitinib @ higher doses (≈20%).
  • Upadacitinib associated w/ 4.8x higher odds of acne vs. placebo.
  • Topical therapies (clindamycin + benzoyl peroxide) or systemic antibiotics may be required.
Special Situations
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring: Not warranted due to strong correlation between dose & exposure.
  • Pregnancy & lactation: Avoid in pregnancy (discontinue 4 weeks prior to conception) & breastfeeding.
  • Acute severe UC: Rapid onset of action makes JAKis potential therapies for hospitalized patients.
Perioperative Management
  • Perioperative JAKi use not associated w/ ↑ risk of adverse outcomes in UC patients undergoing colectomy.
  • ACR/AAHKS guideline: withhold JAKis for 3 days prior to joint surgery; may be reasonable to extrapolate to elective surgery in IBD.
Extraintestinal Manifestations
  • JAKis approved for rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, atopic dermatitis.
  • Useful in patients w/ comorbid inflammatory peripheral arthritis or axial spondyloarthropathies.
Interleukin-23 Antagonists
  • Monoclonal antibodies targeting p19 subunit of IL-23.
  • Risankizumab: Approved for moderate-severe CD.
  • Mirikizumab: Approved for moderate-severe UC.
Effectiveness
  • Risankizumab: Real-world effectiveness confirmed in cohort studies.
  • Mirikizumab: Significantly higher proportion achieved clinical response, remission, & endoscopic remission vs. placebo.
  • Guselkumab: Phase 3 data awaited, but phase 2 trial showed higher rates of clinical & endoscopic outcomes vs. placebo.
Risankizumab Details
  • SEQUENCE trial: Risankizumab superior to ustekinumab in achieving clinical remission @ week 24 (59% vs. 40%) & endoscopic remission @ week 48 (32% vs. 16%) in CD.
  • INSPIRE & COMMAND trials: Efficacious for biologic-naïve & biologic-exposed UC patients.
Mirikizumab Details
  • LUCENT-1 & LUCENT-2 trials: ≈40% patients failed prior biologic or tofacitinib.
  • Week 12: clinical response (64% vs. 42%), clinical remission (24% vs. 15%), endoscopic remission (36% vs. 21%).
  • VIVID-1 trial (CD): Mirikizumab superior to placebo, non-inferior to ustekinumab.
Dosing and Onset of Action
  • Risankizumab: 600mg IV @ weeks 0, 4, 8, then 360mg or 180mg SC every 8 weeks.
  • Mirikizumab: 300mg IV @ weeks 0, 4, 8, then 200mg SC every 4 weeks.
  • Relatively rapid onset of action: ≈13% achieve symptomatic remission, 33% experience significant clinical improvement w/in 2 weeks.
Safety
  • Low rates of serious infection, MACE, & malignancy in clinical trials.
  • Most common adverse events: upper respiratory tract infections, joint pain, injection site reactions, rash, headache, UTI.
  • Ustekinumab appears safer than TNF antagonists & vedolizumab in CD.
Special Situations
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring: Risk of immunogenicity very low.
  • Pregnancy & lactation: Reasonable to continue IL-23 antagonists during pregnancy.
  • Acute severe UC: Unlikely to be effective agents for inpatient use due to slower onset of action.
Perioperative Management
  • Limited data on safety in patients undergoing IBD-related surgery.
  • Rheumatology/orthopedic guideline: Delay elective joint surgery until next biologic dose is due, withhold next dose until wound healing.
Extraintestinal Manifestations
  • Risankizumab & guselkumab approved for psoriasis & psoriatic arthritis.
  • Useful in patients w/ comorbid psoriasis or psoriatic arthritis w/ IBD.
Sphingosine-1 Phosphate Receptor Modulators (S1PR Modulators)
  • Etrasimod & ozanimod: Oral S1P receptor modulators approved for moderate-severe UC.
  • Work by binding S1P receptor on immune cell surfaces, preventing immune cell trafficking.
  • Leads to fewer immune cells being transported to peripheral circulation & target sites of inflammation.
Effectiveness
  • Ozanimod: Superior to placebo in achieving clinical remission, response, endoscopic improvement, & mucosal healing.
  • Etrasimod: Higher rates of clinical remission, response, endoscopic improvement, & endoscopic improvement-histologic remission vs. placebo.
Ozanimod Details
  • TRUE NORTH program: S1PR modulator targeting S1P1 & S1P5.
  • Higher rates in biologic-naïve patients.
  • Referral center study: 53% achieved clinical remission by week 10, 25% @ week 52.
Etrasimod Details
  • ELEVATE 12 & ELEVATE 52 studies: S1PR modulator targeting S1P1, S1P4 & S1P5.
  • Etrasimod more efficacious than placebo in achieving clinical remission & endoscopic improvement.
Dosing and Onset of Action
  • Ozanimod: "Ramp up" dose titration during first week, then 0.92mg daily.
  • Etrasimod: 2mg daily throughout induction & maintenance, w/o dose titration.
  • Relatively slower acting due to mechanism focusing on lymphocyte sequestration.
Safety
  • ALC decreases by ≈50% by week 10 w/ ozanimod, & by week 2 w/ etrasimod.
  • Increased risk of infections, including shingles (VZV vaccination recommended).
  • Contraindicated in patients w/ recent history of MI, unstable angina, stroke, advanced heart failure, etc.
Special Situations
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring: Not warranted given strong correlation between dose & exposure.
  • Pregnancy & lactation: Avoid during pregnancy & breastfeeding.
  • Acute severe UC: Probably not suited for management of ASUC due to slower action.
Perioperative Management
  • Very limited data on safety in patients undergoing colectomy or other extra-intestinal surgery.
  • Ozanimod washout period: ≈55 days; etrasimod washout period: ≈1 week.
Extraintestinal Manifestations
  • Ozanimod approved for multiple sclerosis.
  • May be preferred option for patients w/ UC & comorbid multiple sclerosis.
Positioning Modern Therapies in the Management of IBD
  • Approach relies on integration of medication effectiveness & safety in context of individual patient's risk & preferences.
  • Effective disease control w/ avoidance of corticosteroids is primary aim.
  • Goal of maintaining sustained remission & avoiding disease complications.
Crohn's Disease
  • Infliximab & adalimumab: Most effective for induction in biologic-naïve patients, esp. w/ complicated phenotypes.
  • Ustekinumab & risankizumab: Reasonable alternatives w/ superior safety profile in moderate luminal CD.
  • Risankizumab & upadacitinib: Likely most effective therapies in patients w/ prior TNFα antagonist failure.
Ulcerative Colitis
  • Upadacitinib: Most efficacious agent for most patients w/ moderate-severe UC.
  • Infliximab & vedolizumab: Probably most efficacious therapies for induction in biologic-naïve patients.
  • S1PR modulators: Efficacious & attractive first-line therapies as oral, small molecule drugs.
Cost and Access Considerations
  • Cost is major determinant of access to IBD therapies.
  • Patients w/ commercial insurance often eligible for manufacturer co-pay assistance programs.
  • Medicare prescription drug reform legislation caps patient out-of-pocket costs at $2000 annually in 2025.
