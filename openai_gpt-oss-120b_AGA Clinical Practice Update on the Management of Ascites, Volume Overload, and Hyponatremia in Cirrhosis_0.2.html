AGA Clinical Practice Update on the Management of Ascites, Volume Overload, and Hyponatremia in Cirrhosis
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2025;169:1547‑1557 (doi 10.1053/j.gastro.2025.08.029)
  • Topic: Evidence‑based best practice advice for ascites, hepatic hydrothorax, volume overload, and hyponatremia in cirrhosis
  • Author(s): Eric S. Orman, MD; Brett E. Fortune, MD; Binu V. John, MD MPH; Sumeet K. Asrani, MD
1. Pathophysiology & Clinical Impact
  • Portal hypertension → mesenteric/systemic vasodilation → RAAS activation → renal Na⁺/water reabsorption → intravascular‑interstitial volume expansion.
  • Ascites forms when hepatic lymphatic capacity is exceeded; hypoalbuminemia, ↑ hydrostatic pressure, and ↑ capillary permeability exacerbate fluid leak.
  • Hepatic hydrothorax (HH) arises via diaphragmatic pores allowing ascitic fluid to enter pleural space; associated with > 4‑fold mortality vs refractory ascites.
  • Hypervolemic hyponatremia results from vasopressin‑mediated water retention secondary to effective arterial hypovolemia; serum Na⁺ < 130 mEq/L worsens hepatic encephalopathy (HE) and predicts mortality.
2. Core Management Principles (Best Practice Advice 1‑3)
  • Dietary sodium restriction: target < 2000 mg/d; provide dietitian education, label reading, cooking tips; avoid caloric/protein malnutrition (≥ 35 kcal/kg/d; protein 1.2‑1.5 g/kg).
  • Diuretic regimen: start spironolactone 100 mg q.d. (range 50‑400 mg) + furosemide 40 mg q.d. (range 20‑160 mg) in a 40:100 ratio; titrate to lowest effective dose guided by weight loss (0.5 kg/d without edema, 1 kg/d with edema), urine output, symptoms, electrolytes, renal function.
  • Diagnostic paracentesis for new‑onset ascites or hospitalized decompensation: perform within 12‑24 h; obtain SAAG, cell count, Gram stain, culture (bedside inoculation). SAAG ≥ 1.1 g/dL → portal hypertension; neutrophils > 250/mm³ → SBP.
  • Therapeutic thoracentesis for HH with dyspnea/hypoxemia: relieve symptoms and expand lung; also obtain pleural fluid for SBE work‑up (neutrophils > 250/mm³).
3. Advanced Therapeutics & Referral Strategies (Best Practice Advice 4‑7)
  • Transplant evaluation: all patients with refractory ascites or HH considered irrespective of MELD; low MELD still confers high mortality (ascites → 20%‑year mortality, HH → 20‑50%).
  • Therapeutic paracentesis/thoracentesis frequency guided by recurrence; large‑volume removal (> 5 L) mandates IV albumin 6‑8 g/L removed (≈ 20‑25% of volume). Albumin also considered for smaller volumes in hypotension, renal insufficiency, or electrolyte derangements.
  • TIPS referral: indicated for well‑selected refractory ascites, HH, volume overload, or hyponatremia; contraindications include EF < 50%, severe pulmonary hypertension, uncontrolled HE, sepsis. Covered stents preferred; risk of post‑TIPS HE up to 50% (higher in age > 70 y, MELD > 18).
  • Alternative/bridge therapies: indwelling peritoneal catheters, alfapump system, or peritoneovenous shunts for TIPS‑ineligible patients; consider ultrafiltration in refractory anasarca awaiting transplant.
4. Hyponatremia Evaluation & Management (Best Practice Advice 8‑11)
  • Diagnostic work‑up: review diet/meds (diuretics, laxatives), electrolytes, renal function, GI bleeding, infections (including paracentesis cultures), thyroid/adrenal labs; differentiate hypervolemic vs euvolemic/hypovolemic causes.
  • Outpatient asymptomatic hypervolemic hyponatremia: restrict fluid to 1‑1.5 L/d; limit sodium intake; adjust/hold diuretics and laxatives; monitor electrolytes frequently.
  • Inpatient severe/symptomatic hyponatremia (Na⁺ < 120 mEq/L or neuro‑symptoms): discontinue offending agents, give IV albumin based on volume status, consider oral vasoconstrictors (midodrine) or IV vasopressors; hypertonic saline 3% for rapid correction only if life‑threatening, avoiding > 8‑10 mEq/L rise/24 h.
  • Refractory hyponatremia: multidisciplinary approach; options include IV vasoconstrictor therapy (terlipressin, norepinephrine), short‑course vaptans (tolvaptan, satavaptan) for transplant candidates, or renal replacement therapy in AKI/ESRD.

5. Inpatient Volume‑Overload Management (Best Practice Advice 12‑13)
  • IV loop diuretics: furosemide or bumetanide bolus 2‑3×/day; consider continuous infusion if needed.
  • Escalation schedule: increase dose every 2‑3 days; monitor weight, urine output, renal labs, symptoms.
  • Refractory anasarca strategies (nephrology co‑management): add acetazolamide for contraction alkalosis; introduce thiazide (metolazone) as second agent; consider ultrafiltration in ICU.
6. Definitions & Classification of Volume‑Overload Conditions (Table 1 summary)
  • Refractory ascites: cannot be mobilized or recurs early despite optimal sodium restriction and diuretics. Subtypes – diuretic‑resistant vs diuretic‑intractable.
  • Refractory hepatic hydrothorax: unresponsive to salt restriction/diuretics; requires therapeutic thoracentesis.
  • Refractory hyponatremia: persistent Na⁺ < 130 mEq/L despite maximal medical therapy.
  • Refractory anasarca: no improvement in volume status despite maximal diuretics and low urine sodium.
7. Conclusions, Future Directions & Implementation Gaps
  • Liver transplantation remains sole curative option for decompensated cirrhosis with fluid overload.
  • Bridging modalities (alfapump, indwelling catheters) improve quality of life but lack extensive real‑world data.
  • Need refined disease definitions to enable earlier recognition and standardized MELD‑Na exceptions for HH.
  • Ongoing trials required for advanced steatotic liver disease; multidisciplinary pathways essential to reduce readmissions and mortality.
8. Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS) – Efficacy, Risks & Timing
  • Covered stents reduce post‑procedure dysfunction; improve ascites control.
  • Absolute contraindications: EF < 50%, severe PH, uncontrolled HE, sepsis.
  • Early TIPS after ≤ 3 large‑volume paracenteses improves survival without excess HE.
  • Post‑TIPS HE occurs in up to 55% of older patients; monitor closely.
9. Albumin Use in Large‑Volume Paracentesis (Best Practice Advice 6)
  • > 5 L ascites removal → IV albumin 20‑25% of volume (6‑8 g/L).
  • Smaller‑volume paracentesis may also benefit from albumin; data pending.
10. Hyponatremia – Algorithmic Approach (Figure 2 summary)
  • Fluid restriction: aim 1‑1.5 L/d if Na⁺ > 125 mEq/L; stricter < 120 mEq/L.
  • Midodrine + albumin for hypotensive patients to augment renal perfusion (MAP > 70 mmHg).
  • Vaptans: short‑course 3‑day tolvatpan/tolvaptan for transplant candidates; limited by hepatic toxicity.
11. Clinical Outcomes & Quality‑of‑Life Considerations
  • Refractory ascites → higher HE, electrolyte disturbances, AKI risk vs medical therapy alone.
  • Indwelling pleural catheters and alfapump show low complication rates but require transplant bridge planning.
  • Albumin infusion after > 5 L paracentesis reduces circulatory dysfunction, improves mortality (NNT ≈ 8).
