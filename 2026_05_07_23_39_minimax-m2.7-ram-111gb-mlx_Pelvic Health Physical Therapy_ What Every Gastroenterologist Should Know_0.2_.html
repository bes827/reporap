Pelvic Health Physical Therapy: What Every Gastroenterologist Should Know
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, November 17, 2025
  • Topic: Pelvic floor rehabilitation for gastroenterology conditions
  • Author(s): M. Markowski Cucchiara, PT, DPT, WCS, BCB-PMD
What Is Pelvic Health Physical Therapy?
  • PHPT = specialized PT for pelvic floor disorders
  • Provided by licensed physical therapist with postdoctorate training
  • Assessment uses internal vaginal and/or anorectal examinations
  • Addresses underlying neuromusculoskeletal causes of pelvic floor disorders
Core Treatment Elements
  • Thorough patient education
  • Behavioral changes implementation
  • Neuromuscular re-education or biofeedback
  • Manual therapy techniques
  • Therapeutic exercises
  • Modalities as indicated
Key Features of PHPT
  • Conducted with consent and trauma-informed care approach
  • Patient-centered treatment model
  • Assesses patient's voluntary abdominal and PFM control
  • Tests ability to sense muscle movement
Biofeedback Definition
  • Converts unknown physiological information → visual, auditory, or tactile cues
  • Goal: make responses conscious --> enable modification
  • Purpose: re-establish neuromuscular coordination
Pelvic Health Physical Therapy vs Biofeedback
PHPT Is Broader Than "Biofeedback"
  • PHPT = comprehensive evaluation + treatment approach
  • Not limited to sEMG biofeedback only
  • Tailored to individual patient presentation
Biofeedback Methods
Noninstrumented Approaches
  • Digital rectal exam (DRE) with skilled examiner
  • Verbal and tactile cues from provider
  • Patient's own hands on abdomen/anus
  • Mirror to visualize PFM movement
Instrumented Approaches
  • sEMG sensors (internal or external)
  • Manometry equipment
  • Rectal balloon catheters for sensation/expulsion training
Key Insight About DRE as Biofeedback
  • DRE = cornerstone of pelvic health PT practice
  • Coupled with simultaneous verbal + tactile feedback
  • Efficient and effective biofeedback technique
  • Skilled DRE often more trusted than equipment
Best Practice Standard
  • 2023 consensus from American Society of Colon and Rectal Surgeons
  • Biofeedback with trained physical therapist = best practice
  • Choice of instrumented vs noninstrumented --> based on clinical presentation
Important Caveats
  • Level of care --> NOT defined by type of biofeedback used
  • Ultimate goal: consistent independent performance without external confirmation
  • Prolonged external biofeedback --> can inhibit internal learning
Evidence for PHPT in GI Conditions
Strong Support
  • Level 1, Grade A evidence for biofeedback in dyssynergic defecation
  • PHPT as first-line intervention for:
  • Urological symptoms
  • Gynecological symptoms
  • GI symptoms
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Chronic pelvic pain
Demonstrated Efficacies
  • Constipation / dyssynergic defecation
  • Fecal incontinence
  • Fecal urgency
  • Incomplete evacuation
  • Rectocele
  • Anorectal pain
Treatment Approach
  • Combines multiple elements
  • Not limited to biofeedback alone
  • Tailored to specific presentation of individual patient
Supporting Guidelines
  • Major guidelines --> support multimodal approach
  • Multimodal treatment --> more effective than sEMG only historically
Diagnostic Tests Before Referral
Key Finding
  • No specific tests mandatory before PHPT referral
  • Patient undergoes comprehensive assessment in PHPT
Current Reality Check
  • Guidelines suggest anorectal physiological testing before referral
  • But 98% of chronic constipation patients --> never get anorectal function testing
Limitations of Pre-Testing
  • Testing has limited sensitivity and specificity
  • Limited availability
  • Patient comfort concerns
  • Therefore: testing --> not essential before referral
Multidisciplinary Care Integration
  • Two-thirds of dyssynergic defecation patients --> also have slow transit motility
  • Need prescribed bowel regimen from referring physician
  • Addresses both:
  • Transit component
  • Motor dysfunction
Patient Selection Factors
  • Assess cognitive state (PHPT requires processing)
  • Evaluate psychological state
  • Check patient motivation to participate
  • Trauma history --> may need concurrent behavioral health support
Referral Process
When to Refer
  • Suspect musculoskeletal reason for GI complaint
  • Diagnosis involves pelvic floor related symptoms
Referral Steps

1. Provider identifies patient with bowel/pelvic floor symptoms

2. Determine appropriate cognition and psychological state

3. Refer to PHPT

Access Note
  • All 50 states allow direct access for PT without physician referral
  • Patients can seek care --> independently

PHPT Treatment Components

  • Manual/soft tissue therapies

  • Behavioral and habit retraining
  • Therapeutic exercise
  • Stretching
  • Modalities as indicated
Why PHPT Works
  • Restores neuromuscular coordination
  • Addresses underlying neuromusculoskeletal causes
  • Tailored to individual presentation
  • Multidisciplinary approach --> best outcomes
Referral Checklist
  • Suspect musculoskeletal component in bowel symptoms
  • Patient has adequate cognitive/psychological capacity

PHPT Evaluation Components (Detailed)
History Taking
  • Primary complaint and symptoms
  • Aggravating and relieving factors
  • Previous treatment history
  • Specific questions: bladder, bowel, sexual function
Examination Elements
  • Consent and trauma-informed approach
  • External lumbopelvic musculoskeletal exam
  • External pelvic examination
  • Internal vaginal or rectal (with consent)
  • Internal exam includes:
  • Measuring strength
  • Coordination assessment
  • Voluntary control testing
  • Resting tone/tension of PFM
  • Sensation evaluation
  • Position of pelvic organs
  • Perineal skin assessment
Plan Development
  • Individualized based on examination findings
  • Thorough patient education of findings
  • Correlation to patient's symptoms
  • Detailed rehabilitation plan
Biofeedback as Treatment Tool
Goals of Biofeedback Therapy
  • Create effective coordination of:
  • Abdomen
  • Anorectum
  • Pelvic floor muscles
  • Enhance anorectal sensory perception
  • Promote defecation or continence
  • Reduce pain
Biofeedback vs Neuromuscular Re-Education
  • These terms --> often used interchangeably
  • Both aim to address muscle incoordination
No Standard Protocol
  • Currently no standard device for biofeedback
  • No standard treatment protocol
  • For clinic or home use
Home Device Limitations
  • Most available home devices --> evaluated for vaginal strength training
  • For urinary incontinence primarily
  • Not designed for coordination/relaxation training needed for defecatory dysfunction
Multidisciplinary Care Team
Key Providers to Coordinate With
  • Referring physician or APP
  • Gastroenterologist
  • Colorectal surgeon
  • Registered dietician
  • Gynecologist
  • Urogynecologist or urologist
  • Behavioral health provider
  • Pain management physician/physiatrist
  • Primary care physician
Collaboration Benefits
  • Addresses multiple aspects of complex cases
  • Transit issues + motor dysfunction simultaneously
  • Improved patient outcomes
