Vonoprazan–Tetracycline Dual Regimen as Rescue Therapy for Helicobacter pylori Infection: Randomized Controlled Trial
  • Source: Gastroenterology, Accepted 6 January 2026
  • Topic: Helicobacter pylori rescue therapy (vonoprazan-tetracycline dual regimen)
  • Author(s): Gao W, Li J, Yang G, et al.
1. Study Background and Rationale
  • H. pylori is a Gram-negative bacterium colonizing the human stomach, affecting nearly half of the global population
  • Antimicrobial treatment remains primary approach to limit bacterial transmission, promote gastric lesion healing, and reduce gastric cancer risk
  • Simplified dual regimens using a single sensitive antibiotic have emerged as feasible alternative with growing challenge of antibiotic resistance
  • In Asia, dual therapy with vonoprazan and amoxicillin has demonstrated high eradication rates and good tolerability
  • Tetracycline serves as compelling alternative due to persistently low resistance rate (typically <3%) and established role in bismuth quadruple therapy
  • Prior retrospective study showed VT dual regimen achieved eradication rate of 90.9% (40/44) as rescue therapy in patients with prior treatment failure
2. Study Design and Methods
2.1 Trial Overview
  • Prospective, open-label, randomized controlled non-inferiority trial conducted from February 2024 to July 2025
  • Conducted at Peking University First Hospital, a tertiary medical center in Beijing, China
  • Approved by Ethics Committee of Peking University First Hospital; conducted in accordance with Declaration of Helsinki
  • Registered at chictr.org.cn (ChiCTR2400080705); followed CONSORT guidelines
2.2 Participants
  • Eligibility: Adults aged 18-80 years with confirmed H. pylori infection who experienced at least one prior eradication failure
  • Exclusion criteria:
  • Prior use of regimen containing PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole in combination
  • Use of antibiotics, bismuth, or PPI within 4 weeks prior to enrollment
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Severe comorbidities (advanced hepatic, cardiac, or renal disease; malignancy; alcohol dependence)
  • Known hypersensitivity to study medications
  • Participation in another clinical drug trial within 3 months
  • Inability to communicate effectively due to psychiatric conditions or severe neurosis
  • Confirmation of infection: Positive 13C-urea breath test (UBT; 75 mg 13C-urea)
2.3 Randomization and Interventions
  • Allocation: 1:1 ratio to VT dual therapy or standard bismuth quadruple therapy (BQT)
  • VT regimen: Vonoprazan 20 mg b.i.d. + tetracycline 500 mg t.i.d. for 14 days
  • BQT regimen: Lansoprazole 30 mg b.i.d. + colloidal bismuth 150 mg t.i.d. + tetracycline 500 mg t.i.d. + metronidazole 400 mg t.i.d. for 14 days
  • Dosing instructions: Vonoprazan, lansoprazole, and bismuth taken ~30 minutes before meals; tetracycline and metronidazole taken immediately after meals
  • Randomization: Computer-generated random number sequence with block size of 4; assignments concealed in sequentially numbered, opaque, sealed envelopes
2.4 Outcomes
  • Primary outcome: Rate of successful H. pylori eradication determined via 13C-urea breath testing at 8-week follow-up (6 weeks after treatment completion)
  • Secondary outcomes: Adverse event frequency and severity, medication adherence
  • Adverse events graded: None; mild (transient, easily tolerated); moderate (noticeable interference with routine activities); severe (significant impact on daily functioning, hospitalization, or death)
  • Adherence definition: Inadequate if <80% of prescribed doses taken
2.5 Sample Size and Statistical Analysis
  • One-sided significance level α = 0.025 (Bonferroni correction: α = 0.0125 per comparison)
  • Statistical power: 80% (β = 0.20); non-inferiority margin predefined at 10%
  • Expected eradication rate for BQT as rescue therapy: 88%
  • Required sample size: 166 patients per group
  • Planned total sample size: 350 participants (175 per arm) to account for ~5% dropout
  • Analysis populations:
  • ITT: all enrolled patients with at least 2 months follow-up (loss to follow-up = treatment failure)
  • mITT: patients who received ≥1 dose and completed 13C-UBT ≥6 weeks after treatment
  • PP: patients who completed follow-up and took ≥80% of prescribed medications

4. Eradication Results (Primary Efficacy Outcomes)

  • Difference: 1.7% (95% CI: –5.9% to 9.3%)
  • P value for difference: 0.75
  • Non-inferiority P value: 0.0005

6. Discussion and Clinical Implications
6.1 Efficacy Summary
  • VT dual therapy was shown to be non-inferior to BQT as rescue treatment for H. pylori infection in patients with prior eradication failure
  • High eradication rates achieved across ITT, mITT, and PP analyses, comparable to standard BQT
  • First prospective evaluation of vonoprazan combined with tetracycline as simplified, penicillin-free rescue regimen
6.2 Mechanistic Rationale
  • Success of H. pylori eradication depends heavily on intensity, consistency, and duration of acid suppression
  • Dual therapies became consistently favorable only when combined with double-dose PPIs or standard-dose P-CABs
  • Potent acid suppression broadens applicability of dual therapy, allowing single sensitive antibiotic to achieve reliable eradication when gastric pH maintained ≥6 with P-CABs
  • Pharmacologic rationale explains more favorable outcomes with VT dual therapy compared to omeprazole-tetracycline dual therapy in earlier studies
6.3 Antibiotic Resistance Considerations
  • Major obstacle to successful eradication, particularly in rescue treatment
  • Asia-Pacific surveillance data (1990-2022) showed resistance rates: metronidazole 52%, levofloxacin 26%, clarithromycin 22%
  • Resistance to tetracycline and amoxicillin remained low at approximately 4%
  • Empiric regimens built on low-resistance antibiotics more likely to remain effective in real-world settings
  • Tetracycline resistance profile remains stable even after prior treatment failures
6.4 Comparison with Previous Studies
  • Prior randomized trial (treatment-naïve, penicillin-allergic patients): VT dual therapy achieved 94.5% eradication rate (mITT), comparable to BQT with significantly fewer adverse events (14.0% vs 48.0%)
  • Prior real-world study: VT dual regimen achieved 90.9% eradication rate (40/44) as rescue therapy
  • Present RCT results consistent: 90.6% eradication rate (VT, mITT) vs 90.9% in real-world study
  • VT dual therapy demonstrated similar eradication rate to BQT (90.6% vs 89.3%), supporting its potential as optimized yet effective alternative for rescue treatment
6.5 Treatment Optimization Strategy
  • Evolving trend in H. pylori treatment seeks to optimize regimens by focusing on effective acid suppression combined with antibiotics with low resistance rates
  • Strategy involves optimizing key components to achieve high eradication rates while reducing treatment complexity
  • VT dual therapy fits well within this approach, offering simpler yet effective option reflecting advances in understanding bacterial susceptibility and treatment optimization
  • VT regimen achieves simplification by removing one antibiotic without increasing dose of remaining antibiotic (tetracycline at standard 500 mg t.i.d. = 1.5 g/day vs conventional 2 g in BQT)
6.6 Safety and Adherence Advantages
  • Incidence of TEAEs significantly lower in VT group than BQT group (10.9% vs 45.7%, P < 0.001)
  • Fewer moderate or severe adverse events reported with VT therapy
  • No treatment discontinuations due to adverse effects in VT group vs 15/16 patients in BQT group
  • Higher adherence with VT dual therapy (96.0% vs 87.4%, P = 0.006)
  • Favorable tolerability attributed to simplified dual regimen avoiding adverse effects commonly associated with multi-drug therapies
6.7 Microbiota Considerations
  • Adverse events related to eradication therapy include not only short-term symptoms but also potential long-term effects on gut microbiota
  • VT dual therapy maintains tetracycline at standard dose without increasing overall antibiotic burden while omitting both bismuth and additional antibiotic used in BQT
  • Streamlined composition may lead to less disruption of intestinal microbiota, representing potential therapeutic advantage
6.8 Clinical Positioning
  • VT dual therapy and high-dose amoxicillin dual therapy are complementary options (no cross-resistance between tetracycline and amoxicillin)
  • VT may serve as effective alternative for patients who have failed amoxicillin-based regimens
