Vonoprazan–Tetracycline Dual Regimen as Rescue Therapy for Helicobacter pylori Infection: Randomized Controlled Trial
  • Source: Gastroenterology, Accepted 6 January 2026
  • Topic: Helicobacter pylori Rescue Therapy
  • Author(s): Gao W, Li J, Yang G, et al.
1. Study Design and Methodology
Trial Overview
  • Prospective, open-label, randomized controlled non-inferiority trial conducted at Peking University First Hospital (Beijing, China) from February 2024 to July 2025
  • Registered at chictr.org.cn (ChiCTR2400080705)
  • Compared vonoprazan-tetracycline (VT) dual therapy vs bismuth quadruple therapy (BQT) as rescue treatment
  • 350 patients randomized 1:1 (175 per group)
Eligibility Criteria

Inclusion: Adults aged 18-80 years with confirmed H. pylori infection and ≥1 prior eradication failure

Exclusion:

  • Prior use of PPI+bismuth+tetracycline+metronidazole combination
  • Antibiotics, bismuth, or PPI use within 4 weeks prior to enrollment
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding
  • Severe comorbidities (advanced hepatic, cardiac, renal disease, malignancy, alcohol dependence)
  • Known hypersensitivity to study medications
  • Psychiatric conditions precluding cooperation
Treatment Regimens
  • VT Dual Therapy: Vonoprazan 20 mg b.i.d. + Tetracycline 500 mg t.i.d. for 14 days
  • BQT: Lansoprazole 30 mg b.i.d. + Colloidal bismuth 150 mg t.i.d. + Tetracycline 500 mg t.i.d. + Metronidazole 400 mg t.i.d. for 14 days
Sample Size Calculation
  • Non-inferiority margin: 10%
  • Expected BQT eradication rate: 88% (based on prior rescue therapy data)
  • Required sample: 166 per group → adjusted for 5% dropout to 175 per group
  • Power: 80%, α = 0.025 (one-sided)

3. Primary Efficacy Outcomes — Eradication Rates
Intention-to-Treat (ITT) Analysis
  • VT: 88.0% (154/175; 95% CI: 82.0–92.2%)
  • BQT: 86.3% (151/175; 95% CI: 80.1–90.8%)
  • Difference: +1.7% (95% CI: –5.9% to 9.3%)
  • P for difference = 0.75
  • P for non-inferiority = 0.0005 ✓
Modified Intention-to-Treat (mITT) Analysis
  • VT: 90.6% (154/170; 95% CI: 84.9–94.4%)
  • BQT: 89.3% (151/169; 95% CI: 83.5–93.4%)
  • Difference: +1.2% (95% CI: –5.7% to 8.2%)
  • P for difference = 0.84
  • P for non-inferiority = 0.0003 ✓
Per-Protocol (PP) Analysis
  • VT: 91.1% (153/168; 95% CI: 85.4–94.7%)
  • BQT: 92.2% (141/153; 95% CI: 86.4–95.7%)
  • Difference: –1.1% (95% CI: –7.8% to 5.6%)
  • P for difference = 0.88
  • P for non-inferiority = 0.002 ✓
Subgroup Analysis by Penicillin Allergy (mITT)
  • VT without penicillin allergy: 91.8% (101/110)
  • VT with penicillin allergy: 88.3% (53/60)
  • BQT without penicillin allergy: 89.4% (101/113)
  • BQT with penicillin allergy: 89.3% (50/56)
  • No significant difference between allergy subgroups in either group

6. Discussion — Key Findings and Clinical Implications
Primary Efficacy Finding
  • VT dual therapy achieved non-inferior eradication rates compared to BQT across all analysis populations (ITT, mITT, PP)
  • Eradication rates: 90.6% VT vs 89.3% BQT (mITT)
  • Non-inferiority P = 0.0003 (threshold: P < 0.01)
  • First prospective evaluation of vonoprazan-tetracycline as penicillin-free rescue regimen
Mechanistic Rationale for VT Dual Therapy

Acid Suppression:

  • Success of H. pylori eradication depends on intensity, consistency, and duration of acid suppression
  • Vonoprazan (P-CAB) provides potent acid suppression maintaining gastric pH ≥6
  • Superior acid suppression vs. PPIs enables single antibiotic to achieve reliable eradication

Antibiotic Selection:

  • Tetracycline resistance rate: typically <3% globally
  • Low primary resistance maintained even after prior treatment failures
  • Stable resistance profile in Asia-Pacific region (1990-2022): ~4% tetracycline resistance
  • Compares favorably to high metronidazole (52%), levofloxacin (26%), and clarithromycin (22%) resistance rates
Comparison with Prior Studies

First-line Setting:

  • Prior RCT in penicillin-allergic patients: VT dual therapy achieved 94.5% eradication (mITT)
  • Comparable to BQT with significantly fewer AEs (14.0% vs 48.0%)

Rescue Setting:

  • Prior retrospective study: VT achieved 90.9% (40/44) eradication rate
  • Current RCT confirms similar efficacy: 90.6% (mITT)
Advantages of VT Dual Therapy Over BQT

Efficacy:

  • Non-inferior eradication rates

Safety:

  • Significantly lower TEAE incidence: 10.9% vs 45.7% (P < 0.001)
  • Fewer severe AEs: 1.1% vs 9.1% (P = 0.001)
  • No treatment discontinuations due to AEs vs 8.6% in BQT

Adherence:

  • Higher completion rates: 96.0% vs 87.4% (P = 0.006)
  • Simplified regimen (2 drugs vs 4 drugs)

Microbiome Impact:

  • Standard-dose tetracycline (1.5 g/day) vs higher doses in some regimens
  • Avoids bismuth and additional antibiotics in BQT
  • Potential for less gut microbiota disruption
Clinical Positioning of VT Dual Therapy

Suitable Patient Populations:

  • Patients with prior eradication failure (rescue setting)
  • Patients with penicillin allergy or amoxicillin ineligibility
  • Patients who failed amoxicillin-containing regimens (~70% of enrolled patients)
  • Patients requiring simplified treatment regimens

Complementary to High-Dose Amoxicillin Dual Therapy:

  • No cross-resistance between tetracycline and amoxicillin
  • VT serves as alternative when amoxicillin-based regimens fail
Treatment Optimization Strategy
  • Reflects trend toward "subtraction" in H. pylori therapy
  • Focus on effective acid suppression + low-resistance antibiotic
  • Achieves high eradication with simplified regimen (2 drugs vs 4)
  • Maintains therapeutic effectiveness without increasing antibiotic doses
7. Study Limitations
Generalizability
  • Initially designed as multicenter trial but completed at single center (Peking University First Hospital)
  • All participants from one tertiary hospital in China
  • Not yet evaluated outside China
  • Regional differences may affect results:
  • Acid-inhibitor potency variations
  • Body weight distribution differences
  • Antibiotic pharmacokinetics
Antibiotic Resistance Testing
  • Not performed at individual patient level
  • Relied on existing surveillance data showing tetracycline resistance <4% in China
  • Cannot directly correlate eradication outcomes with resistance profiles
Open-Label Design
  • Potential reporting bias in adverse event assessment
  • Unequal pill burden (VT: fewer pills vs BQT) may contribute to bias
  • Patients taking fewer medications might perceive treatment as easier and underreport mild AEs
Non-Inferiority Margin
  • 10% margin may be considered relatively wide
  • Future trials with narrower margins and larger samples needed
Subgroup Analysis Limitations
  • Sample size not planned for stratified analysis by number of prior failures
  • Cannot provide reliable eradication estimates for single vs. multiple (≥2) prior failures
  • Small number of patients with ≥2 prior failures
  • Heterogeneous previous regimens in heavily pretreated patients
Microbiome Assessment
  • No gut microbiota profiling before/after treatment
  • Cannot assess ecological impact of simplified dual regimen
Future Research Needs
  • Multicenter, multinational trials
  • Blinded designs with matched placebo tablets
  • Antibiotic resistance assessment at individual level
  • Microbiome profiling studies
  • Evaluation in diverse populations with varying body weight distributions
8. Conclusion
Main Findings
  • Fourteen-day vonoprazan-tetracycline dual therapy demonstrated non-inferior eradication rates compared to standard bismuth quadruple therapy as rescue treatment for H. pylori infection
  • VT achieved 90.6% eradication rate (mITT) vs 89.3% for BQT
  • Non-inferiority established with P = 0.0003 (threshold: P < 0.01)
Efficacy Summary
  • ITT analysis: 88.0% VT vs 86.3% BQT (P for non-inferiority = 0.0005)
  • mITT analysis: 90.6% VT vs 89.3% BQT (P for non-inferiority = 0.0003)
  • PP analysis: 91.1% VT vs 92.2% BQT (P for non-inferiority = 0.002)
  • No significant difference between groups in any analysis population (P > 0.05)
Safety and Tolerability
  • Significantly lower adverse event rate: 10.9% VT vs 45.7% BQT (P < 0.001)
  • Fewer severe adverse events: 1.1% VT vs 9.1% BQT (P = 0.001)
  • No treatment discontinuations due to AEs in VT group vs 8.6% in BQT (P < 0.001)
  • All adverse events resolved without intervention after treatment completion
Adherence
  • Higher treatment adherence: 96.0% VT vs 87.4% BQT (P = 0.006)
  • Simplified dual regimen improved patient compliance
Clinical Value
  • First prospective evidence for VT as effective rescue therapy
  • Penicillin-free composition suitable for patients with penicillin allergy
  • Viable alternative for patients who failed amoxicillin-containing regimens (~70% of study population)
  • Simplified regimen (2 drugs) offers practical advantage over quadruple therapy (4 drugs)
Population-Specific Findings
  • Effective in patients with and without penicillin allergy
  • Consistent efficacy regardless of number of prior treatment failures
  • Comparable eradication rates to BQT across all subgroups
Final Statement

This randomized controlled trial provides evidence that vonoprazan-tetracycline dual therapy is an effective and well-tolerated rescue regimen for H. pylori infection in Chinese patients with prior eradication failure. The simplified regimen achieves eradication rates non-inferior to standard bismuth quadruple therapy while substantially reducing adverse events and improving adherence. By omitting bismuth and one additional antibiotic without compromising efficacy, this optimized dual therapy offers a practical alternative for rescue treatment, particularly for patients with penicillin allergy or prior failure of amoxicillin-containing regimens.

9. Clinical Take-Home Points — What You Need to Know
Background and Context
  • Rescue therapy for H. pylori typically relies on multi-drug quadruple regimens (PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole)
  • Quadruple regimens can be difficult for patients to tolerate and complete due to high pill burden and adverse events
  • The authors previously demonstrated that simplified vonoprazan-tetracycline (VT) dual therapy was effective as first-line treatment
  • This study examines whether VT remains effective after previous eradication failure
New Findings from This Trial
  • First prospective evaluation of VT dual therapy as rescue option
  • Non-inferior eradication rates compared to bismuth quadruple therapy:
  • mITT: 90.6% VT vs 89.3% BQT (P for non-inferiority = 0.0003)
  • Markedly fewer adverse events: 10.9% VT vs 45.7% BQT (P < 0.001)
  • Better overall tolerability and adherence: 96.0% VT vs 87.4% BQT (P = 0.006)
  • No treatment discontinuations due to AEs in VT group
Limitations to Consider
  • Open-label, single-center design conducted in China
  • Did not include antibiotic resistance testing
  • Generalizability to other regions or clinical settings may be limited
  • 10% non-inferiority margin may be considered relatively wide
Clinical Research Relevance
  • VT dual therapy represents practical alternative to complex quadruple regimens
  • Simpler dosing (2 drugs vs 4 drugs) improves patient compliance
  • Particularly useful for:
  • Patients who struggle with multiple medications
  • Patients who cannot receive amoxicillin due to penicillin allergy
  • Patients who failed previous amoxicillin-containing regimens
Basic Research Implications
  • Reinforces concept that effective acid suppression + single low-resistance antibiotic achieves reliable eradication
  • Provides mechanistic support for simplified dual therapy strategies
  • Tetracycline maintains low resistance rates (<3%) globally, supporting its use in empiric regimens
10. Patient-Facing Summary — Lay Summary
What This Study Was About
  • H. pylori is a bacterial infection that can cause stomach problems and increase gastric cancer risk
  • When standard treatments fail (rescue therapy), doctors need simpler, better-tolerated options
  • This study tested a simplified treatment combining only two medications: vonoprazan and tetracycline
Who Participated
  • 350 adult patients who had failed at least one previous H. pylori treatment
  • Divided into two groups: 175 received VT dual therapy, 175 received standard four-drug therapy
What Happened
  • Both treatment groups took medications for 14 days
  • VT group: vonoprazan (20 mg twice daily) + tetracycline (500 mg three times daily)
  • BQT group: lansoprazole + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole (four drugs)
Key Results
  • Eradication success: VT achieved 90.6% eradication vs 89.3% with standard therapy — equally effective
  • Side effects: VT caused far fewer adverse events (10.9% vs 45.7%)
  • Treatment completion: More patients completed VT therapy (96.0% vs 87.4%)
  • No patients stopped VT due to side effects, but 8.6% stopped standard therapy
What This Means for Patients
  • A simpler two-drug treatment works as well as the standard four-drug regimen
  • Fewer side effects makes treatment more comfortable
  • Better completion rates mean more patients achieve successful eradication
  • Particularly helpful for patients allergic to penicillin or who cannot take amoxicillin
Bottom Line

A simplified treatment combining vonoprazan and tetracycline successfully cleared H. pylori in patients whose previous therapies had failed. It worked as well as standard four-drug regimens but caused fewer side effects, offering a more comfortable option for patients.
