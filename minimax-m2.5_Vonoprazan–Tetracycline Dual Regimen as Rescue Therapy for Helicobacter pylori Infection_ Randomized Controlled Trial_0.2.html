Vonoprazan–Tetracycline Dual Regimen as Rescue Therapy for Helicobacter pylori Infection: Randomized Controlled Trial
  • Source: Gastroenterology, Accepted 6 January 2026
  • Topic: Helicobacter pylori rescue therapy (second-line treatment)
  • Authors: Gao W, Li J, Yang G, et al.
1. Background and Rationale
  • H. pylori is a Gram-negative bacterium colonizing the human stomach, affecting nearly half of the global population
  • Antimicrobial treatment remains primary approach to limit bacterial transmission, promote gastric lesion healing, and reduce gastric cancer risk
  • Simplified dual regimens using a single sensitive antibiotic have emerged as feasible alternative with growing challenge of antibiotic resistance
  • In Asia, dual therapy with vonoprazan and amoxicillin has demonstrated high eradication rates and good tolerability
  • Tetracycline serves as compelling alternative due to persistently low resistance rate (typically <3%) and established role in bismuth quadruple therapy
  • Prior retrospective study by authors showed VT dual regimen achieved eradication rate of 90.9% (40/44) as rescue therapy
2. Study Design and Methods
2.1 Trial Overview
  • Prospective, open-label, randomized controlled non-inferiority trial
  • Conducted from February 2024 to July 2025 at Peking University First Hospital, Beijing, China
  • Approved by Ethics Committee of Peking University First Hospital
  • Conducted in accordance with Declaration of Helsinki
  • Registered at chictr.org.cn (ChiCTR2400080705)
  • Followed CONSORT guidelines for randomized controlled studies
2.2 Eligibility Criteria

Inclusion criteria:

  • Adults aged 18-80 years with confirmed H. pylori infection (positive 13C-urea breath test)
  • At least one prior eradication failure, irrespective of number of previous treatment attempts

Exclusion criteria:

  • Prior use of regimen containing PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole in combination
  • Use of antibiotics, bismuth, or PPI within 4 weeks prior to enrollment
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Presence of severe comorbidities (advanced hepatic, cardiac, or renal disease; malignancy; alcohol dependence)
  • Known hypersensitivity to study medications or components
  • Participation in another clinical drug trial within 3 months prior
  • Inability to communicate effectively due to psychiatric conditions or severe neurosis
  • Any other condition deemed by investigator unsuitable for participation
2.3 Randomization and Interventions
  • Patients randomly assigned in 1:1 ratio to VT dual therapy or standard BQT
  • Computer-generated random number sequence with block size of 4
  • Assignments concealed in sequentially numbered, opaque, sealed envelopes
  • Open-label study (both investigators and participants aware of treatment allocation)
  • Technicians conducting 13C-urea breath tests remained blinded to group allocation

VT Dual Therapy (n=175):

  • Vonoprazan 20 mg twice daily (Takeda Pharmaceutical Co.)
  • Tetracycline 500 mg three times daily (Guilin Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.)

BQT (Bismuth Quadruple Therapy, n=175):

  • Lansoprazole 30 mg twice daily (Takeda Pharmaceutical Co.)
  • Colloidal bismuth 150 mg three times daily (North China Pharmaceutical Co.)
  • Tetracycline 500 mg three times daily (Guilin Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.)
  • Metronidazole 400 mg three times daily (Sichuan Kelun Pharmaceutical Research Institute Co., Ltd.)
  • Treatment duration: 14 consecutive days for both regimens
  • PPI, vonoprazan, and bismuth taken ~30 minutes before meals
  • Tetracycline and metronidazole taken immediately after meals
2.4 Outcome Measures

Primary outcome:

  • Rate of successful H. pylori eradication, determined via 13C-urea breath testing at 8-week follow-up (6 weeks after treatment completion)

Secondary outcomes:

  • Adverse event frequency and severity
  • Medication adherence
2.5 Sample Size Calculation
  • Randomized, active-control, parallel-group study designed to test non-inferiority
  • One-sided significance level (α) of 0.025, with Bonferroni correction allocating α = 0.0125 to each individual comparison
  • Statistical power: 80% (β = 0.20)
  • Non-inferiority margin: predefined at 10%
  • Expected eradication rate for BQT as rescue therapy: 88% [11]
  • Required sample size: 166 patients per group
  • Anticipated dropout rate: ~5%
  • Planned total sample size: 350 participants (175 per arm)
2.6 Statistical Analysis Populations
  • Intention-to-treat (ITT): All enrolled patients with at least 2 months of follow-up; any loss to follow-up counted as treatment failure
  • Modified intention-to-treat (mITT): Patients who received at least one dose of study medication and completed 13C-UBT at least 6 weeks after treatment, regardless of adherence
  • Per-protocol (PP): Patients who completed follow-up and took at least 80% of prescribed medications

3.3 Eradication of H. pylori Infection

Intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis:

  • VT dual therapy: 88.0% (154/175; 95% CI: 82.0%–92.2%)
  • BQT: 86.3% (151/175; 95% CI: 80.1%–90.8%)
  • Difference: 1.7% (95% CI: –5.9% to 9.3%)
  • P value for difference: 0.75
  • Non-inferiority P: 0.0005

Modified intention-to-treat (mITT) analysis:

  • VT dual therapy: 90.6% (154/170; 95% CI: 84.9%–94.4%)
  • BQT: 89.3% (151/169; 95% CI: 83.5%–93.4%)
  • Difference: 1.2% (95% CI: –5.7% to 8.2%)
  • P value for difference: 0.84
  • Non-inferiority P: 0.0003

Per-protocol (PP) analysis:

  • VT dual therapy: 91.1% (153/168; 95% CI: 85.4%–94.7%)
  • BQT: 92.2% (141/153; 95% CI: 86.4%–95.7%)
  • Difference: –1.1% (95% CI: –7.8% to 5.6%)
  • P value for difference: 0.88
  • Non-inferiority P: 0.002

No statistically significant difference in eradication efficacy was observed between the two treatment groups across ITT, mITT, and PP analyses. VT dual therapy achieved predefined non-inferiority endpoint compared with BQT as rescue treatment.

Eradication rates by penicillin allergy status (mITT analysis):

VT group:

  • Patients without penicillin allergy: 91.8% (101/110; 95% CI: 86.4%–95.2%)
  • Patients with penicillin allergy: 88.3% (53/60; 95% CI: 79.8%–93.5%)
  • Absolute difference: +3.5% (95% CI: –6.1% to 13.1%)
  • P value: 0.48

BQT group:

  • Patients without penicillin allergy: 89.4% (101/113; 95% CI: 83.6%–93.2%)
  • Patients with penicillin allergy: 89.3% (50/56; 95% CI: 80.6%–94.3%)
  • Absolute difference: +0.1% (95% CI: –9.8% to 10.0%)
  • P value: 0.98

No significant difference in eradication rates between patients with or without penicillin allergy in either group.

4. Discussion
4.1 Main Findings

This randomized clinical trial demonstrated that vonoprazan-tetracycline (VT) dual therapy is non-inferior to bismuth quadruple therapy (BQT) as rescue treatment for H. pylori infection in patients with prior eradication failure. Among 350 enrolled participants, VT regimen achieved high eradication rates across ITT, mITT, and PP analyses, comparable to standard BQT. This is the first prospective evaluation of vonoprazan combined with tetracycline as a simplified, penicillin-free rescue regimen.

4.2 Mechanistic Rationale

Success of H. pylori eradication depends heavily on intensity, consistency, and duration of acid suppression. Although amoxicillin-based dual therapies were proposed decades ago, eradication rates became consistently favorable only when combined with double-dose PPIs or standard-dose P-CABs. Potent acid suppression broadens applicability of dual therapy, allowing a single sensitive antibiotic to achieve reliable eradication when gastric pH is maintained ≥6 with P-CABs. This pharmacologic rationale explains favorable outcomes observed with VT dual therapy compared with omeprazole-tetracycline dual therapy in earlier studies.

4.3 Antibiotic Resistance Considerations

Antibiotic resistance is a major obstacle to successful eradication of H. pylori, particularly in rescue treatment. Surveillance data from Asia-Pacific region (1990–2022) showed persistently high resistance rates to metronidazole (52%), levofloxacin (26%), and clarithromycin (22%), while resistance to tetracycline and amoxicillin remained low at approximately 4%. These findings emphasize value of empiric regimens built on low-resistance antibiotics. Tetracycline stands out not only due to low primary resistance but also because its resistance profile remains stable even after prior treatment failures.
