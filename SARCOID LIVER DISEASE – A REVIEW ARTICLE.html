Introduction & Core Concepts
  • - Sarcoidosis is a multisystemic inflammatory disease characterized by non-necrotizing granulomas.
  • - The liver is a commonly affected organ, but significant liver disease is less common.
  • - Diagnosis requires a multidisciplinary approach and exclusion of other pathologies.
Hallmark Pathology
  • - The cornerstone of diagnosis is a liver biopsy confirming a pattern of non-necrotizing granulomas.


Epidemiology
  • - The worldwide burden is unknown due to diagnostic challenges and mimicking other diseases like tuberculosis (TB).
  • - Reported incidence varies widely from 0.02 to 1.6 per thousand adults.
Geographic Variation
  • - Lowest incidence: Eastern Asian countries (1-5 per 100,000).
  • - Highest incidence: Scandinavian countries (140-160 per 100,000).
  • - North America: Prevalence ranges from 60 to 160 per 100,000.

Demographics: Ethnicity, Age, and Gender
  • - Race: Strongly associated, with African Americans having the highest incidence in the U.S. (17.8 per 100,000).
  • - Age: Peak onset is 30–55 years old, with a possible bimodal distribution (second peak in women 50-65 years).
  • - Mortality: Age-adjusted mortality rate is higher for Black Americans.

Epidemiology of Hepatic Sarcoidosis
  • - Data is scarce compared to pulmonary sarcoid.
  • - Prevalence varies significantly, documented between 3.6% to 20% in clinical cohorts, with a predominance in African American patients.
Clinical vs. Autopsy Findings
  • - Autopsy studies show a much higher prevalence, with nearly 80% of patients with sarcoidosis having hepatic granulomas.
  • - Over 50% have hepatic granulomas on biopsy, but only 10–30% have elevated liver-associated enzymes (LAE).
  • - Clinically significant disease with symptoms is reported in less than 20% of patients.



Natural History
  • - The clinical course is highly variable, ranging from spontaneous regression to progressive fibrotic disease.
Systemic Sarcoidosis
  • - Generally has a good prognosis with low mortality.
  • - Spontaneous resolution occurs in 50% of cases within the first 2 years.
  • - 5-year survival is estimated at 93% to 95%.
  • - Associated with a 1.8-fold increased risk of serious infections, especially in the first 2 years after diagnosis.

Hepatic Sarcoidosis
  • - Natural history is poorly understood due to its varied clinical course.
  • - Extrapulmonary manifestations can precede or occur without thoracic involvement.
  • - Long-standing disease may lead to liver transplantation.
Phenotypes
  • - A study by Schupp et al. divided sarcoidosis into 5 phenotypes.
  • - Hepatic sarcoidosis is most observed in the 'abdominal phenotype', often with spleen involvement (36.7%).



Morbidity & Mortality
  • - Morbidity is linked to the specific organ system affected (pulmonary, cardiac, hepatic, neurologic, ocular being most severe).
  • - Global mortality from sarcoidosis is around 10 cases per 1,000 person-years.
  • - The most common cause of sarcoidosis-related death is often undiagnosed cardiac involvement.

Risk Factors
  • - Sarcoidosis is likely triggered by antigen exposures in genetically susceptible individuals.
Antigenic & Infectious Triggers
  • - Mycobacterial catalase-peroxidase protein (mKatG) has been identified to induce granulomatous inflammation.
  • - Propionibacterium acnes is also associated.

Environmental & Lifestyle Factors
  • - Inorganic aerosols: metal dust, photocopier toners (copper, iron, silica).
  • - Organic bioaerosols: associated with flares in the spring season.
  • - Obesity: In one cohort, obese women had a 3.59-fold increased risk of developing sarcoidosis.


Pathogenesis
  • - Pathological hallmark is the presence of compact, epithelioid, non-necrotizing granulomas.
  • - Involves a multifactorial interplay of environmental, genetic, and immunological factors.
Immune Response Cascade
  • - Initial step: A dysregulated and exaggerated T helper 1 (TH1) immune response to an antigen.
  • - This activates alveolar macrophages, which then initiate a downstream adaptive immune response.
  • - Pro-inflammatory factors like tumor necrosis factor α (TNF-α) and interleukin-17 create an environment for granuloma formation.

Granuloma Composition
  • - Comprised primarily of macrophages that fuse to form multinucleated giant cells.
  • - CD4+ T helper cells are interspersed within the granuloma.
  • - The periphery is surrounded by CD8+ T cells, regulatory T cells (Tregs), fibroblasts, and B cells.

Genetic Susceptibility
  • - Human leukocyte surface antigen (HLA) polymorphisms play a significant role.
  • - Associated with development: HLA-DRB1*03/07/15.
  • - Protective role: HLA-DRB1*04.
  • - Associated with acute disease: HLA-DQB1*0201.


Clinical Presentation
  • - Sarcoidosis can mimic various diseases, making differential diagnosis crucial.
  • - General symptoms include fatigue (50-70%), painless peripheral lymphadenopathy (~20%), low-grade fever, weight loss, and night sweats.
Non-Hepatic Sarcoidosis
  • - Lung involvement and lymph node enlargement are seen in > 90% of cases.
  • - Eye, skin, and liver involvement: 15%–30%.
  • - Cardiac and nervous system involvement: 2% to 10%.
Specific Organ Manifestations
  • - Cardiac: Often asymptomatic, but can present with atrioventricular block, arrhythmias, and heart failure.
  • - Ocular: Uveitis (frequently bilateral) is seen in 60-80% of cases.
  • - Skin: Erythema nodosum, macules, nodules, papules, or plaques in up to one-third of patients.
  • - Gastrointestinal: Rare, accounting for 0.1 to 1.6% of cases.


Hepatic Sarcoidosis
  • - Isolated hepatic sarcoidosis is rare, but can occur without intra-thoracic disease in 13–50% of cases.
  • - Most patients are asymptomatic with a mild course.
Symptoms & Signs
  • - Symptomatic patients (~15%) may report fatigue, right upper quadrant pain, pruritus, fever, jaundice, and weight loss.
  • - Hepatomegaly is present in up to 30% of patients and carries a worse prognosis.
  • - Liver hilum adenopathy can cause cholestasis via external compression.

Biochemical Pattern
  • - Often presents as an asymptomatic elevation of liver associated enzymes (LAE).
  • - Most common pattern is cholestatic, with elevated Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) and GGT.
  • - A common threshold for treatment consideration is LAE elevation > three times the upper limit of normal (ULN).
  • - Synthetic function is rarely affected.

Cholestatic Syndromes
  • - Can mimic other cholestatic diseases, delaying diagnosis.
  • - Pathogenesis involves progressive destruction of bile ducts by portal granulomas.
  • - Can resemble primary biliary cirrhosis (PBC) or, less commonly, primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC).

Advanced Disease & Complications
  • - Fibrosis and cirrhosis may be observed in 20–29% of biopsies.
  • - Can cause noncirrhotic portal hypertension (NCPH) in up to half of patients with portal hypertension.
  • - Budd-Chiari syndrome is a rare but reported association.
  • - Progression to cirrhosis occurs in 6–24% of cases.



Diagnosis & Workup
  • - Diagnosis is challenging and requires excluding other causes of granulomatous liver disease (e.g., infections, drugs, autoimmune diseases).
  • - A diagnosis of hepatic sarcoidosis requires histopathologic confirmation of noncaseating granuloma in the liver.
Laboratory Tests
  • - Serum angiotensin-converting enzyme (sACE): Increased in > 50% of patients with active disease.
  • - Limitation: Low sensitivity and specificity, with variability among ethnic groups.

Imaging
  • - Ultrasound/CT: May show hypodense nodules in 5–35% of cases and hepatomegaly in 8–18%.
  • - MRI and 18F-FDG PET CT are the most sensitive modalities for detecting focal liver nodules.

Liver Biopsy & Histopathology
  • - Remains the only way to make a definitive diagnosis of hepatic involvement.
  • - Key feature: Well-differentiated, non-caseating granulomas.
  • - Essential step: Specimens must be stained for acid-fast bacilli, fungi, and spirochetes to rule out infectious causes.
Granuloma Distribution & Features
  • - Granulomas are often multifocally distributed.
  • - Lobular involvement: 70% of cases.
  • - Portal or periportal localization: 60% of cases.
  • - Bile duct association: 40% of cases.
  • - Ductopenia: Present in 15% of cases.

Necrotizing Sarcoid Granulomatosis
  • - A rare form characterized by necrosis within granulomas, often with vasculitis.
  • - Hepatic involvement is uncommon.



Treatment
  • - Main goals: Prevent progression to fibrosis, relieve symptoms, and decrease organ damage.
  • - No prospective randomized controlled trials exist for universal management.
General Principles & Indications
  • - Observation: Asymptomatic disease with no organ dysfunction may not require treatment, as spontaneous remission occurs in > one-third of patients.
  • - Indications for treatment: Organ dysfunction, progressive damage, risk of death, or significantly compromised quality of life (See Table 2).
  • - A trial of treatment withdrawal every 1-2 years is suggested for stable patients.

Treatment of Hepatic Sarcoidosis
  • - Approach is personalized and relies on expert opinion due to a lack of evidence-based recommendations.
  • - Treatment is often guided by extrahepatic involvement, especially pulmonary disease.
Stepwise Approach (See Figure 3)
  • - First-line: Corticosteroids or Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA).
  • - Second-line: Azathioprine or Methotrexate for partial response or steroid-sparing.
  • - Third-line (Salvage): Anti-TNF alpha agents.

Corticosteroids
  • - Remain the first-line therapy to reduce inflammation and granulomas.
  • - Dose: Prednisone 20 to 40 mg daily for up to 2–3 months, then tapered.
  • - Efficacy: Can reduce granuloma size/number in 77.8% of patients and improve symptoms.
  • - Limitation: May decrease LAE levels without significant histological regression.

Ursodeoxycholic Acid (UDCA)
  • - A potential first-line option, especially for predominant cholestasis or pruritus.
  • - Dose: Typically 10 mg/kg/day.
  • - Benefits: Improves fatigue, pruritus, and liver biochemistries. Favorable safety profile for long-term use.
  • - Limitation: Has not been shown to reverse histological damage or prevent complications.

Immunomodulators (Steroid-Sparing)
  • - Used as second-line agents for relapsing/resistant cases or to reduce steroid exposure.
Methotrexate (MTX)
  • - A common second-line agent.
  • - Dose: Starting at 10 mg/week with a maximum of 15 mg/week.
  • - Caution: Potential for drug-induced liver injury; requires monitoring.

Azathioprine (AZA)
  • - Can be considered when tapering corticosteroids.
  • - Dose: 50 to 150 mg/d.
  • - Prerequisite: Check thiopurine S-methyltransferase (TPMT) activity to assess risk of myelosuppression and hepatotoxicity.

Other Agents
  • - Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) and hydroxychloroquine have been used, but data is scarce.


Anti-Tumor Necrosis Factor (TNF) Inhibitors
  • - Includes infliximab and adalimumab.
  • - Used for refractory disease or when steroids are contraindicated/intolerable.
  • - Can effectively induce remission of hepatic granulomas.
Infliximab Efficacy
  • - A study showed clinical and biochemical improvement in 9 out of 10 patients.
  • - Dose: 5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, and 6, followed by every 6-week intervals.



Management of Complications
  • - Management of portal hypertension and its sequelae (e.g., varices) is crucial.
Portal Hypertension
  • - Hepatic sarcoidosis causes pre-sinusoidal portal hypertension.
  • - Management includes non-selective beta-blockers, endoscopic variceal screening, and consideration of TIPS.

Liver Transplantation
  • - A rare intervention, accounting for about 0.012% of all liver transplants in the US.
  • - Candidacy requires careful assessment of other organ involvement.
  • - Survival rates: 78% at 1-year and 61% at 5-years.
  • - Recurrence in the graft is rare and accounted for 7% of total deaths in one analysis.



Future Directions
  • - Current treatment strategies focus on interrupting granuloma formation, but clinical trials for hepatic sarcoidosis are lacking.
  • - A deeper understanding of pathogenesis may lead to new treatment approaches.
  • - Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning may help in prognostication and interpreting radiologic studies.
Unmet Needs (See Table 3)
  • - Diagnosis: Need for non-invasive biomarkers to replace or supplement liver biopsy.
  • - Therapeutics: Need for steroid-sparing therapies and targeted treatments for granuloma resolution.
  • - Prognosis: Need for markers to predict disease progression and complications.
