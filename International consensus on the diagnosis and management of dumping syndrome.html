International consensus on the diagnosis and management of dumping syndrome
  • - Nature Reviews Endocrinology | August 2020 | volume 16
  • - https://doi.org/10.1038/s41574-020-0357-5
  • - A Delphi consensus process with international multidisciplinary experts to develop uniform guidance on the definition, diagnosis, and management of dumping syndrome.

Definition & Symptoms
  • - Dumping syndrome is a frequent complication of oesophageal, gastric, or bariatric surgery.
  • - It comprises a constellation of symptoms categorized as early or late dumping syndrome.
Prevalence
  • - Prevalence depends on the type of surgery and diagnostic criteria used.
  • - Bariatric surgery has become the main cause of postoperative dumping syndrome.
Prevalence by Surgery Type
  • - Vagotomy with pyloroplasty: ~20%
  • - Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB) or sleeve gastrectomy: up to 40%
  • - Oesophagectomy: up to 50%
  • - Also reported after Nissen fundoplication.


Classification
  • - Symptoms can occur jointly or separately.
Early Dumping Syndrome
  • - Occurs within the first hour after a meal.
  • - It is the typical and most frequent manifestation.
Symptoms
  • - A combination of gastrointestinal and vasomotor symptoms.
Gastrointestinal Symptoms
  • - Abdominal pain & cramps
  • - Bloating
  • - Borborygmi (rumbling sounds)
  • - Nausea
  • - Diarrhoea

Vasomotor Symptoms
  • - Fatigue & desire to lie down
  • - Flushing & perspiration
  • - Palpitations & tachycardia
  • - Hypotension
  • - Rarely, syncope



Late Dumping Syndrome
  • - Occurs 1–3 hours after a meal.
  • - Characterized by (reactive) hypoglycaemia.
  • - Also referred to as 'postbariatric hypoglycaemia'.
Symptoms
  • - Manifestations of hypoglycaemia due to neuroglycopenia and autonomic activation.
Neuroglycopenic Symptoms
  • - Fatigue & weakness
  • - Confusion
  • - Hunger
  • - Syncope

Autonomic/Adrenergic Symptoms
  • - Perspiration
  • - Palpitations
  • - Tremor
  • - Irritability




Clinical Impact
  • - Symptoms are often debilitating and emotionally distressing.
Quality of Life
  • - In severe cases, associated with a substantial reduction in quality of life.

Weight Loss
  • - In severe cases, can lead to considerable weight loss due to food avoidance.



Pathophysiology
  • - Caused by changes in gastric anatomy and innervation, allowing undigested food to reach the small intestine too rapidly.
Early Dumping Syndrome Mechanisms
  • - A cascade of events activated by the rapid passage of nutrients to the small intestine.
Mechanical Factors
  • - Reduced gastric volume capacity (e.g., sleeve gastrectomy).
  • - Removal of pyloric barrier function (e.g., gastrectomy).
  • - These lead to rapid delivery of undigested food.

Fluid Shift
  • - Hyperosmolar contents in the small intestine trigger a fluid shift from the intravascular space to the intestinal lumen.
  • - This leads to decreased circulating blood volume, causing tachycardia and hypotension.
  • - Intestinal distention contributes to cramps, bloating, and diarrhoea.

Hormonal Release
  • - Rapid nutrient delivery triggers the release of several GI hormones.
  • - These contribute to both gastrointestinal and cardiovascular effects.
Key Hormones Involved
  • - Vasoactive agents: neurotensin, vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP)
  • - Incretins: glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP1), glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP)
  • - Glucose modulators: insulin, glucagon



Late Dumping Syndrome Mechanisms
  • - Primarily driven by a hyperinsulinaemic response to carbohydrate ingestion.
Hyperinsulinaemic Hypoglycaemia
  • - Rapid delivery of carbohydrates to the small intestine leads to high glucose concentrations.
  • - This triggers a hyperinsulinaemic response, resulting in subsequent hypoglycaemia.

Role of GLP1
  • - An exaggerated GLP1 response is the key mediator.
  • - Rising GLP1 levels are correlated with insulin release.
  • - Blockade of the GLP1 receptor with exendin (9–39) amide was shown to correct reactive hypoglycaemia after gastric bypass.


Role in Bariatric Surgery Weight Loss
  • - Dumping syndrome after bariatric surgery is considered a complication, not a desired effect.
  • - No trial has demonstrated that patients with dumping syndrome lose more weight.


Diagnosis
  • - In clinical practice, dumping syndrome is often missed, and diagnosis is delayed.
  • - A key clinical clue is profound fatigue after meals with the need to lie down.
Symptom-Based Questionnaires
  • - The consensus panel did not endorse the use of questionnaires like Sigstad’s score or the Arts dumping questionnaire for diagnosis.
  • - Their diagnostic accuracy and sensitivity to therapy are not well-established, especially after bariatric surgery.

Diagnostic Testing
  • - A structured approach to confirm the diagnosis is crucial.
Spontaneous Hypoglycaemia
  • - Spontaneous plasma glucose levels <2.8 mmol/l (50mg/dl) are indicative of late dumping syndrome.
  • - A cutoff of <3.3 mmol/l (60mg/dl) was not endorsed.

Modified Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)
  • - The preferred diagnostic test for dumping syndrome.
Procedure
  • - Patient ingests 75g of glucose in solution after an overnight fast.
  • - Measure glucose, haematocrit, pulse rate, and blood pressure before and at 30-min intervals for up to 180 minutes.

Criteria for Early Dumping Syndrome
  • - Test is considered positive if at 30 minutes there is:
  • - An increase in haematocrit >3%
  • - OR an increase in pulse rate >10 bpm (this is the most sensitive sign).

Criteria for Late Dumping Syndrome
  • - Test is considered positive based on the development of late hypoglycaemia.
  • - Glucose nadir <50 mg/dl between 60–180 minutes after ingestion.


Tests with No Established Value
  • - The consensus panel did not find sufficient evidence to recommend these tests for routine diagnosis.
Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)
  • - The diagnostic value of CGM has not been established.

Mixed Meal Tolerance Test
  • - Not considered superior to the modified OGTT.

Gastric Emptying Test
  • - Has low sensitivity and specificity for dumping syndrome.
  • - Its use to confirm a diagnosis is not recommended.




Treatment
  • - A stepwise approach is recommended, starting with conservative measures.
First-Line: Dietary Modification
  • - The initial approach and is usually beneficial for the majority of patients.
Key Dietary Advice
  • - Reduce meal size: Eat smaller, more frequent meals.
  • - Delay fluid intake: Wait at least 30 minutes after meals to drink fluids.
  • - Eliminate simple carbohydrates: Avoid sweet foods and drinks.
  • - Increase complex nutrients: Eat a diet high in fibre and rich in protein.

Lying Down Post-Meal
  • - Advising patients to lie down for 30 minutes after meals was not endorsed due to a lack of evidence.


Second-Line: Pharmacotherapy
  • - To be considered in patients who do not respond to dietary modification.
Acarbose
  • - An alpha-glycosidase inhibitor that slows carbohydrate absorption.
  • - Particularly effective for symptoms of late dumping syndrome.
  • - Effect on early dumping syndrome is unclear.
Dosing & Side Effects
  • - Usual dose: 50–100 mg three times a day with meals.
  • - Main adverse effect is flatulence and related GI symptoms.


Somatostatin Analogues (e.g., Octreotide)
  • - The preferred option for patients unresponsive to diet and/or acarbose.
  • - Effective for symptoms of both early and late dumping syndrome.
Mechanism of Action
  • - Slows gastric emptying and small bowel transit.
  • - Inhibits the release of various GI hormones.
  • - Inhibits insulin secretion.

Formulations
  • - Short-acting formulations are considered more effective than long-acting ones for symptom control.
  • - However, the need for repeated daily injections is a major limitation of short-acting forms.


Therapies Not Endorsed
  • - The panel did not support the use of the following agents due to insufficient evidence or poor tolerability.
Dietary Supplements (Viscosity Agents)
  • - Agents like guar gum, pectin, and glucomannan are not recommended.
  • - Rationale is to slow nutrient release, but palatability and tolerability are poor.

Diazoxide
  • - A potassium channel activator that inhibits insulin release.
  • - Its use is not established, with only anecdotal evidence available.



Third-Line: Refractory Cases
  • - For severe cases unresponsive to diet and pharmacotherapy, options are limited and evidence is scarce.
Enteral Nutrition
  • - Constant enteral nutrition via a feeding jejunostomy or gastrostomy has no established value.

Surgical Re-intervention
  • - A conservative, non-surgical approach is recommended as patients may improve over time.
  • - Surgical interventions (e.g., bypass reversal, pouch restriction, pancreatic resection) have uncertain outcomes and are not endorsed.


Summary of Recommendations (Table 5)
  • - A high-level overview of the consensus recommendations.
Diagnosis
  • - Suspect based on clinical history.
  • - Use modified OGTT for confirmation (Pulse >10bpm or Hct >3% for early; Glucose <50mg/dl for late).
  • - Questionnaires and gastric emptying tests have no proven diagnostic value.

Treatment
  • - 1st Line: Dietary intervention (eliminate simple carbs, small meals).
  • - 2nd Line: Acarbose (especially for late dumping) or Somatostatin analogues (for early and late).
  • - 3rd Line: Surgical re-interventions have uncertain outcomes and are not recommended.
