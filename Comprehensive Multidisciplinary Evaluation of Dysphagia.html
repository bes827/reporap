The Clinical Problem
  • Dysphagia affects ≈ 1 in 25 US adults annually.
  • Swallowing involves complex sensorimotor control: 2 brainstem central pattern generators, widespread cortical control, 6 cranial nerves, >25 muscle pairs.
  • Etiologies are vast, can involve multiple disorders, & may have upstream/downstream effects.
Multidisciplinary Perspective
  • Traditional division into specialties (ENT, GI) → myopic view.
  • Primary care referral pathways are unclear → time-consuming, costly, inefficient care.
  • Multidisciplinary approach provides comprehensive assessment, reconstructing the entire swallow.
Multidisciplinary Swallow Clinic
  • Clinic model: videoﬂuoroscopic & endoscopic (laryngoscopy & esophagoscopy) evaluation + simultaneous consultation w/ 3 specialists.
  • Goal: Shared & complete view of dysphagia evaluation.
Clinical Evaluation of Dysphagia
  • Begins w/ detailed clinical history (Figure 1).
  • Patients often mislocalize swallowing impairment → importance of diagnostic studies.
  • Physical exam has limited role, but can detect etiologic signs.
Physical Examination Components
  • Oral examination: dentition, xerostomia, oral lesions (lichen planus, tumors).
  • Tongue assessment: protrusion, movement, deviation, atrophy, fasciculation (neurologic process).
  • Neck examination: lymphadenopathy, palpable masses.
Additional Examination Considerations
  • Dermatologic examination: systemic inflammatory disorders (mucous membrane pemphigoid), rheumatologic illness (systemic sclerosis).
  • Neurologic examination: focal weakness, rigidity, other neurologic signs.
Role of Speech Language Pathologist (SLP)
  • SLPs are trained in oropharyngeal anatomy/physiology & esophageal phase of deglutition.
  • SLPs conduct independent clinical swallow evaluation (cranial nerve & oral mechanism exam).
  • SLP evaluation incorporates fluoroscopy for oropharyngeal & esophageal analysis.
Robust Esophageal Screening Tool (REST)
  • Standardized assessment protocol.
  • Uses liquid barium, solid barium, & 12.7 mm barium tablet.
  • Evaluates from upper esophageal sphincter to lower esophageal sphincter.
REST Diagnostic Performance
  • High sensitivity: 83.7%.
  • High specificity: 73.7%.
  • Compared w/ comprehensive esophageal evaluation (endoscopy, manometry, esophagography).
ENT Evaluation
  • Transnasal flexible laryngoscopy is routinely incorporated.
  • Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR) diagnosis based on endoscopic findings (pharyngeal erythema, cobblestoning).
  • LPR findings are prevalent in asymptomatic controls → not sufficiently specific diagnostic criteria.
A Systematic Approach to Categorizing Causes for Dysphagia
  • Traditional dichotomy: oropharyngeal vs esophageal causes.
  • Growing evidence of interplay between phases of swallow.
  • Proposed mechanistic classification: structural, disease, functional causes.
Structural Causes
  • Anatomical abnormalities obstructing bolus passage: diverticula (Zenker’s, Killian-Jamieson), rings, strictures, webs.
  • Inflammatory processes: GERD, eosinophilic esophagitis, lichen planus.
  • Iatrogenic insults: trauma, surgery, radiation, medications, artificial airways.
More Structural Causes
  • Tumors of head/neck/chest.
  • Cervical osteophytes, cervical spine abnormalities.
  • Anomalous vascular structures: dysphagia lusoria/aortica.
Clinical Presentation of Structural Causes
  • Dysphagia particularly for solids (bread/meat) & pills.
  • Intermittent dysphagia, improves w/ careful mastication & small bite size.
  • Regurgitation of unaltered food, halitosis → diverticulum.
Disease Causes
  • Localized & systemic conditions impacting swallowing physiology.
  • Clinical history corresponds to underlying disease features.
  • Neurologic disorders: cerebrovascular disease, traumatic brain injury, neurodegenerative conditions (ALS, Parkinson’s).
Neurologic Disorder Presentation
  • Dysphagia occurs regularly w/ all consistencies.
  • Worsens w/ use.
  • Accompanied by coughing, choking, nasal regurgitation.
Rheumatologic/Autoimmune Disease Causes
  • Scleroderma, sarcoidosis, inflammatory myopathy.
  • Variable clinical presentation.
  • Typically presents w/ other systemic signs of illness.
Esophageal Dysmotility Conditions
  • Achalasia, esophagogastric junction outflow obstruction, distal esophageal spasm.
  • Dysphagia for both solids & liquids.
  • Accompanied by chest pain & regurgitation.
Respiratory Disorders
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).
  • Dysphagia during disease exacerbations.
  • Interference w/ airway closure during swallowing.
Iatrogenic Causes
  • Surgery, medication, artificial airway placement.
  • Must be considered & elicited in history.
Functional Causes
  • Difficulty swallowing w/o structural or physiologic findings (Rome IV criteria).
  • Diagnosis of exclusion.
  • Hypersensitivity: symptoms inconsistent w/ examination.
Etiology of Functional Causes
  • Abnormalities in gut-brain axis, cortical changes, biopsychosocial disturbances.
  • Subtle muscle tension/discoordination (muscle tension dysphagia).
Muscle Tension Dysphagia
  • Abnormal sensation experienced as dysphagia.
  • Considered a subtype of functional dysphagia.
Clinical Presentation of Functional Causes
  • Sudden onset of symptoms, often associated w/ inciting event (trauma, stress, illness, surgery).
  • Concomitant anxiety is common.
  • Inconsistent symptoms: normal swallowing at times, severe impairment at others.
Behavioral Changes
  • Food avoidance behavior is typical.
  • Can lead to weight loss & malnutrition.
Evaluation and Management Approach
  • Decision regarding next steps depends on suspected etiology.
  • Figure 2 outlines comprehensive evaluation approach.
  • Suspected structural cause → ENT consultation & upper endoscopy.
Endoscopic Evaluation
  • Can underestimate subtle stenosis correlating w/ symptomatic dysphagia.
  • Empiric dilation is safe & may detect/manage subtle stenoses.
Empiric Dilation
  • Use wire-guided bougie dilators.
  • Sequentially dilate esophagus to maximal caliber (eg, 17–18 mm).
Esophageal Dysmotility Assessment
  • Comprehensive assessment is beyond scope of this review.
  • Refer to previously published reviews for testing strategies.
Functional Cause Evaluation
  • Still requires screening to exclude structural & functional abnormalities.
  • Fluoroscopy provides a “road map” to guide next steps.
Iterative Evaluation
  • If initial strategy fails, re-evaluate & choose different subsequent strategy.
  • Continue until etiology is clearly identified.
The Impact of Interconnected Systems
  • Protective reflexes showcase swallow continuum interconnectivity.
  • Explains proximal sensations/discomfort in response to distal abnormalities.
  • Cricopharyngeus response mechanisms to distal esophageal stimulation.
Cricopharyngeus Reflexes
  • Air entry (eructation) → cricopharyngeus relaxation.
  • Liquid entry (reflux) → cricopharyngeus contraction.
Esophageal Distension Reflexes
  • Can cause airway reactivity responses: cough, laryngospasm.
Clinical Implications
  • Awareness facilitates accurate diagnosis & effective treatment.
  • Gastroenterologists aware of GERD complications (Barrett’s, strictures, dysmotility).
  • May not be aware of upstream consequences (cricopharyngeal dysfunction, muscle tension, timing changes).
Upstream Adaptations
  • Patients w/ esophageal dysfunction alter swallowing behaviors upstream.
  • Swallow initiation hesitation, piecemeal deglutition.
  • Failure to recognize → suboptimal symptom management & patient dissatisfaction.
Simultaneous Oropharyngeal and Esophageal Dysfunction
  • Conditions disturb both oropharynx & esophagus.
  • Related: primary esophageal motility disturbance w/ secondary cricopharyngeal dysfunction & Zenker diverticulum.
  • Unrelated: oropharyngeal impairment from radiation & GERD-induced Schatzki ring.
Take-home Message
  • Swallowing involves complex overlapping physiologic functions.
  • Interconnected systems contribute to dysphagia development.
  • Mechanistic approach needed to understand varying etiologies.
  • Collaboration between GI, ENT, & SLP is critical for accurate & comprehensive evaluation & treatment.
