Review Article: Individualised Management of Reflux-Like Symptoms—Strategies Beyond Acid Suppression
  • Authors: Peter J. Kahrilas, Laurie Keefer, Rena Yadlapati, et al.
  • Journal: Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics
  • Publication Date: 2025
  • DOI: https://doi.org/10.1111/apt.70115
  • Focus: Pathophysiology & management of reflux-like symptoms, emphasizing strategies beyond acid suppression.

  • 2. What is the adjusted positive predictive value for identifying cancer in patients undergoing gastroscopy for reflux-like symptoms?
  • 3. What is the primary focus of brain-gut behavior therapies (BGBT) in managing reflux-like symptoms?
  • 4. What score range does the Oesophageal Hypervigilance and Anxiety Scale (EHAS) provide?
  • 5. What dietary practice from Japanese culture promotes heightened awareness of satiety cues?
Introduction
  • Reflux-like symptoms are frequently treated with PPIs, often long-term & at high doses.
  • These symptoms are often conflated w/ reflux oesophagitis, assuming similar responsiveness to acid suppression.
  • However, many patients report persistent symptoms despite optimized PPI therapy, questioning this approach.
Methods
  • A panel of 10 experts (primary care, gastroenterology, psychology) held online meetings.
  • They shared perspectives on pathophysiology & management of reflux-like symptoms when PPIs are ineffective/partially effective.
  • The goal was to enhance care via personalized strategies from a primary care/generalist perspective.
Pathophysiology
  • Focuses on understanding the underlying mechanisms of reflux oesophagitis & reflux-like symptoms.
  • Highlights the role of acid reflux, psychological factors, & visceral hypersensitivity.
  • Emphasizes the importance of differentiating between reflux oesophagitis & NERD.
Reflux Oesophagitis
  • Characterized by endoscopically visible mucosal breaks in the distal oesophagus.
  • Graded by the Los Angeles Classification (A-D) based on severity.
  • Pathophysiology centers on anti-reflux barrier incompetence → ↑ gastro-oesophageal acid reflux & poor oesophageal clearance.
Key Factors
  • Hypotensive lower oesophageal sphincter.
  • Dilatation of the diaphragmatic hiatus.
  • Hiatal hernia.
Treatment & Management
  • Healed w/ potent antisecretory therapy (PPIs or PCABs).
  • Physiological defects often require open-ended maintenance therapy.
  • Alternative strategies: intensive weight loss or surgery.
'Typical' Reflux-Like Symptoms
  • Defined by heartburn, chest pain, & regurgitation (Montreal Consensus).
  • Most individuals w/ typical symptoms do not have reflux oesophagitis → NERD.
  • Further classified by pH-metry/pH-impedance testing: true NERD, reflux hypersensitivity, functional heartburn.
Classifications
  • True NERD: Abnormal oesophageal acid exposure.
  • Reflux hypersensitivity: Normal acid exposure, significant symptom-reflux correlation.
  • Functional heartburn: Normal acid exposure, no symptom-reflux correlation.
Poor Correlation
  • Poor correlation between symptoms & reflux events.
  • Disconnect between symptom severity & physiological abnormality.
  • Psychological stressors, cognitive-affective factors, & coping behaviors modulate symptom experience.
Oesophageal Hypervigilance and Anxiety Scale (EHAS)
  • Quantifies oesophagus-focused anxiety & symptom vigilance.
  • 15-item questionnaire, scores 0-60.
  • EHAS score correlates w/ symptom severity, regardless of NERD subtype.
EHAS-7
  • Shorter 7-item version.
  • Performs as well as original.
  • Easier to implement in clinical practice.
Psychosocial Factors
  • Study of 393 'PPI-refractory' patients: psychological variables predict symptom severity better than reflux-related variables.
  • Machine-learning model: psychological factors accounted for 88% of variance in symptom severity scores.
  • Reflux-related variables had lower predictive worth.
Key Psychological Variables
  • Depressive symptoms (worth = 0.210).
  • Illness behavior (worth = 0.165).
  • PTSD symptoms (worth = 0.128).
  • GI-specific anxiety (worth = 0.117).
  • Pain catastrophizing (worth = 0.114).
Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction (DGBIs)
  • Psychological comorbidities & visceral hypersensitivity play a dominant role in symptom perception.
  • DGBIs (functional heartburn, dyspepsia, IBS) commonly overlap.
  • Adaptation responses influence symptom experience; rationalization improves coping.
Oesophageal Hypervigilance
  • Behavioral reaction: increased attention to oesophageal symptoms & settings.
  • Leads to 'expecting' symptoms & maladaptive coping behaviors.
'Atypical' Reflux Symptoms
  • Include laryngitis, pharyngitis, post-nasal drip, otitis, asthma.
  • Establishing reflux as a cause is problematic; affected patients rarely have reflux oesophagitis.
  • pH-metry/pH-impedance metry are used, but interpretation lacks consensus.
Key Principles
  • Reflux is rarely the dominant cause of extra-oesophageal syndromes w/o typical oesophageal symptoms.
  • Even when involved, reflux is usually one of multiple contributors.
  • Diagnostics are more helpful in ruling out GERD than ruling it in.
  • Strong tendency to over-treat w/ PPIs.
Laryngeal Symptoms
  • Elevated hypervigilance & anxiety (EHAS) in patients w/ & w/o abnormal oesophageal pH-metry.
  • Highlights need for approaches beyond acid reduction.
  • Heterogeneity exists: some phenotypes driven by abnormal reflux, others by hypersensitivity & behavioral factors.
Overlapping Gastrointestinal Symptoms
  • Dyspeptic symptoms frequently overlap w/ reflux-like symptoms in PPI 'non-responders'.
  • Functional heartburn has more in common w/ functional dyspepsia than reflux disease.
  • IBS overlaps more frequently w/ functional heartburn than true NERD/reflux hypersensitivity.
Clinical Implications
  • Differentiate between dyspeptic & reflux-like symptoms.
  • Avoid medical jargon; use easy-to-understand tools (pictograms).
  • H. pylori testing is important in dyspepsia, not indicated in typical reflux-like symptoms.
Potential Lifestyle Contributors to Reflux-Like Symptoms
  • Identifies dietary habits, weight gain, & obesity as factors that can exacerbate reflux-like symptoms.
  • Highlights the importance of lifestyle modifications in managing these symptoms.
  • Focuses on practical strategies to address these contributors.
Dietary Habits
  • Poor habits (eating past satiety, close to bedtime) are associated w/ gastro-oesophageal reflux.
  • Large meals cause gastric distention, ↑ acid secretion, & trigger transient lower oesophageal sphincter relaxations.
  • Dietary triggers vary widely; common ones include coffee, alcohol, chocolate, fatty/fried foods, processed foods.
Carbonated Beverages
  • Shown to increase the risk of heartburn during sleep.
Weight Gain, Obesity and Hiatal Hernia
  • Reflux-like symptoms increase w/ BMI, even among normal weight individuals.
  • Obesity is a risk factor for reflux complications (oesophagitis, Barrett's, adenocarcinoma).
  • Hiatal hernia & ↑ intra-abdominal pressure are important physiological risk factors linked to obesity.
Hiatal Hernia
  • Spectrum of disruption of the diaphragmatic hiatus is age-related.
  • Excessive abdominal straining can also cause hiatal hernia in normal weight individuals.
Individualised Management Strategies
  • Emphasizes patient-centered care w/ personalized strategies.
  • Focuses on addressing physiological, behavioral, & dietary factors.
  • Highlights the importance of expectation setting & counseling.
Expectation Setting and Counselling
  • Reflux-like symptoms have a multifactorial pathophysiology & wax/wane.
  • PPI continuation is individualized based on benefit & preference.
  • 'PPI failure' may be due to unrealistic expectations rather than PPI efficacy.
Key Components
  • Understand patient's circumstances, perceptions, symptom type, severity, comorbidities, & alarm features.
  • Counsel on multifactorial aetiology, including the gut-brain connection.
  • Explain the gut-brain axis in patient-friendly language; reassure patients symptoms are not 'all in the mind'.
Doctor-Patient Relationship
  • Key to holistic management based on patient experience.
  • Use active listening, show compassion, & build rapport.
  • Use PRO tools (EHAS, GERDQ, GIS, QOLRAD) to make consultations interactive & effective.
Reassurance
  • Reassure patients that the risk of oesophageal cancer is extremely low.
  • Gastroscopy has low diagnostic yield for symptom evaluation (0.2% positive predictive value for reflux-like symptoms).
Diet and Lifestyle Changes
  • Key element of care for all patients w/ reflux-like symptoms.
  • Dietary & lifestyle contributors are highly individualized.
  • Expert consensus: avoid high-fat foods, dietary triggers, overeating, & recumbency after meals.
Practical Strategies
  • Use smaller plates.
  • Set a cut-off time for the last meal (3-h interval before bedtime).
  • Raise the head of the bed.
Mindful Eating
  • Promote awareness of satiety cues by paying attention to flavors, textures, & chewing.
  • Pre-meal mantra: 'hara hachi bu' (eat until 80% full).
Weight Management
  • Recommended for overweight/obese patients & those who have recently gained weight.
  • Weight loss reduces symptoms dose-dependently & increases treatment success.
  • PPI + tailored diet/exercise program → greater symptom improvement & PPI discontinuation in >50% of patients.
Interventions
  • Specific intervention depends on local services.
  • GLP-1RA may support weight loss as an adjunct to diet & exercise.
  • Some short-acting GLP-1RAs may ↑ risk of oesophagitis & Barrett's in type 2 diabetes.
Atypical (Laryngopharyngeal) Symptoms
  • PPI treatment has little benefit over placebo.
  • Disconnect between objective findings & symptom severity.
  • Paradigm shift: phenotype-driven, personalized approach acknowledging psychosocial factors.
Key Factors
  • Chronicity is laryngeal hypersensitivity amplified by anxiety, hypervigilance, & detrimental behaviors.
  • Laryngeal Cognitive-Affective Tool (LCAT) measures laryngeal hypervigilance & symptom-specific anxiety.
Psychological Interventions
  • Brain-gut behavior therapies (BGBT) may be beneficial for patients w/ stress-related symptoms, coping difficulties, & avoidance behaviors.
  • BGBTs reduce symptom severity & psychological symptoms, improving QoL.
  • EHAS tool gauges symptom-associated anxiety & hypervigilance.
Good Candidates
  • Accept & show interest in the gut-brain connection.
  • Acknowledge coping could be improved.
  • Willing to invest time in behavior change.
BGBT Techniques
  • Nurse-led GI self-management skills training.
  • GI-focused CBT.
  • Gut-directed hypnotherapy.
  • Mindfulness-based interventions.
Hypnotherapy Example
  • Oesophageal-directed hypnotherapy improved heartburn impact (QOLRAD, p=0.01), visceral anxiety (p=0.01), & emotional QoL (p=0.05).
Implementation
  • Combination of techniques can individualize therapy.
  • Primary care can implement nurse-led self-management skills training.
  • Stress management & relaxation techniques are key components.
Atypical Symptoms
  • BGBT may be relevant for patients w/ chronic atypical symptoms & laryngeal-specific anxiety/hypervigilance.
  • Laryngeal recalibration therapy (cognitive behavioral element) had 85% symptomatic response.
Diaphragmatic Breathing
  • May be effective for reducing reflux-like symptoms.
  • Activates parasympathetic nervous system, increases lower oesophageal sphincter pressure & crural diaphragm tension.
  • Useful for patients w/ regurgitation; helps relaxation by increasing vagal tone.
On-Demand Symptom Control Measures
  • Alternative to continuous PPI therapy.
  • Antacid/alginate-antacid products are useful for postprandial acid reflux & regurgitation, especially w/ hiatal hernia.
  • Alginate-antacid forms a viscous pH-neutral raft, reducing acid reflux & displacing the acid pocket.
Efficacy
  • Meta-analyses show efficacy for symptom control vs. placebo & non-inferiority to some PPIs.
  • Bioadhesive treatments (hyaluronic acid-chondroitin sulphate) may reduce symptoms by protecting the mucosa.
Adjunct Therapy
  • On-demand therapy can be useful as an alternative to dose escalation.
  • Alginate or bioadhesive agents added to PPIs can reduce residual symptoms.
Summary
  • PPIs are effective for healing reflux oesophagitis, but less so for persistent/atypical reflux symptoms.
  • This is due to differences in pathophysiology: anatomical/physiological degradation vs. cognitive-affective/behavioral processes.
  • Hypersensitivity, hypervigilance, & anxiety correlate w/ symptom severity & persistence.
Management Approach
  • Individualized for patients, starting w/ expectation setting & counseling.
  • Utilize behavior modification (diet/lifestyle, weight control), education on brain-gut interactions, psychological interventions, & on-demand symptom control.
  • Acid suppression is just one of several interventions in an individualized approach.
