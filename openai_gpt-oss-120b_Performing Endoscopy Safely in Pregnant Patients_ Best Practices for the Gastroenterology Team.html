Performing Endoscopy Safely in Pregnant Patients: Best Practices for the Gastroenterology Team
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, Jan 2026
  • Topic: Endoscopic care during pregnancy
1. Introduction & Epidemiology
  • ~0.4% of GI endoscopies occur during gestation.
  • Goal: safe, effective GI endoscopy while minimizing maternal‑fetal risks.
  • Review synthesizes guidelines, literature, author experience → best‑practice table (Table 1).
2. Maternal & Fetal Risks Overview
  • Absolute risk quantification limited; data sparse.
  • Large cohort study: ↑ preterm birth, ↓ fetal growth; no ↑ stillbirth or congenital malformations.
  • Residual confounding – disease vs procedure unclear.
2.1 Specific Maternal Risks (Table 2)
  • Oversedation → hypotension, hypoventilation, hypoxia.
  • Aspiration risk ↑ due to reduced LES tone & increased intra‑abdominal pressure.
  • Bronchospasm, hypoxemia, hemodynamic instability.
2.2 Specific Fetal Risks (Table 2)
  • Hypoxia/hypoperfusion → fetal acidosis.
  • Radiation exposure: <50 mGy typical ERCP; threshold for concern ≈100 mGy.
  • Supine/IVC compression → maternal hypotension → placental hypoperfusion.
  • Teratogenic meds, contrast‑induced hypothyroidism, amniotic fluid conduction of electrocautery current.
3. Preprocedural Considerations
  • Multidisciplinary triage mandatory (GI, obstetrics/MFM, anesthesia).
3.1 Indication & Timing
  • Elective cases → postpone until postpartum.
  • Urgent/ emergent indications (e.g., cholangitis, GI bleed) → proceed after risk‑benefit analysis.
  • Align decisions with patient values/preferences.
3.2 Contraindications
  • Imminent delivery, ruptured membranes.
  • Obstetric complications: placental abruption, eclampsia.
3.3 Informed Consent Modifications
  • Discuss disease‑related vs procedure‑related risks (both often “difficult to quantify”).
  • Emphasize trust‑building, transparent risk communication.
3.4 Preoperative Consultations
  • Obstetrician: assess maternal/fetal status; plan fetal monitoring pre/post.
  • Anesthesiologist: airway edema, ↓ functional residual capacity, cephalad stomach displacement → adjust ventilation, positioning, drug choice.
3.4.1 Sedative & Analgesic Safety (Table 3)
  • Short‑acting agents in low doses generally safe.
  • Avoid high‑dose benzodiazepines, propofol without obstetric input.
3.5 Periprocedural Medications
  • Bowel prep: polyethylene glycol first choice.
  • Antibiotics: penicillin, cephalexin, metronidazole, ciprofloxacin acceptable.
  • NSAIDs: may use in 1st trimester (↑ miscarriage risk) → avoid after 20 wk (risk of ductus arteriosus closure, oligohydramnios).
  • Indomethacin (rectal) – case‑by‑case for ERCP; consider prophylactic pancreatic duct stent & hydration.
4. Procedural Considerations
  • Experienced endoscopist + dedicated team essential.
4.1 General Technical Goals
  • Minimize procedure time, cautery use, fluoroscopy duration.
  • For ERCP: prefer standard cannulation with brief fluoroscopic pulses; avoid experimental “fluoro‑free” techniques until validated.
4.2 Patient Positioning
  • Left lateral decubitus or left pelvic tilt preferred for all GI endoscopies after 20 wk to prevent IVC/aortic compression.
  • Colonoscopy: underwater insertion, avoid abdominal pressure; if needed, apply away from uterus.
4.3 Electrosurgery Safety
  • Use bipolar cautery over monopolar when possible.
  • Grounding pad placement: right thorax/right shoulder/right arm → keep uterus out of current path.
  • Limit voltage & duration of electrocautery (amniotic fluid conducts electricity).
4.4 Intra‑operative Injections
  • Methylene blue & epinephrine contraindicated (teratogenicity) – may consider epinephrine if benefit outweighs risk.
  • No evidence to avoid contrast in ERCP despite theoretical thyroid risk.
4.5 Radiation Management (ERCP/Fluoroscopy)
  • Typical fetal dose: 0.001–1.8 mGy (mean fluoroscopy time 0.14 min).
  • Threshold for concern ≈100 mGy; modern ERCP usually <10–15 mGy.
  • Provide total radiation dose in post‑procedure report.
4.5.1 Fluoroscopy Best Practices (Table 4)
  • Discontinue routine lead shielding – may increase scatter & degrade image quality.
  • Use pulsed (“short taps”) fluoroscopy, low‑dose settings, tight collimation.
  • Keep image intensifier close; avoid magnification unless essential.
  • Prefer wire‑guided cannulation; confirm biliary access by bile aspiration.
5. Postprocedural Considerations
  • Obstetrician present for fetal monitoring & interventions.
5.1 Monitoring Timeline
  • <24 wk: Doppler fetal heart rate check post‑procedure.
  • ≥24 wk: electronic fetal heart and uterine contraction monitoring after procedure.
5.2 Complication Surveillance
  • Early detection of maternal hypoxia, bleeding, perforation, pancreatitis (ERCP ↑ risk OR ≈ 2.8).
  • Prompt obstetric evaluation if premature labor signs or fetal distress arise.
6. Summary & Clinical Recommendations
  • Endoscopy in pregnancy feasible with structured multidisciplinary protocol.
  • Core principle: risk‑benefit balance → prioritize maternal indication, fetal safety.
6.1 Key Take‑aways (Table 1 synthesis)
  • Provide non‑endoscopic alternatives when available.
  • Classify cases: elective → postpone; urgent/emergent → proceed after consent.
  • Involve obstetrics & anesthesia early; ensure fetal monitoring capability.
  • Use experienced endoscopy team; adopt left lateral/pelvic tilt positioning.
  • Minimize procedure time, cautery, and fluoroscopy exposure.
6.2 Post‑procedure Follow‑up Algorithm

1. Immediate maternal vitals check → address hypoxia, hypotension.

2. Fetal heart rate assessment per gestational age (Doppler <24 wk; electronic >24 wk).

3. Observe for procedure‑specific complications: bleeding, perforation, pancreatitis.

4. Schedule obstetric follow‑up within 24–48 h for any concerning signs.

8. Radiation Safety & Fluoroscopy Optimization (Table 4 Details)
8.1 General Principles
  • Aim for ≤10 mGy fetal dose; document exact exposure.
  • Discontinue routine abdominal lead shielding – may increase scatter and degrade image quality.
8.2 Technical Settings Checklist
  • Pulsed fluoroscopy (“short taps”) → lower cumulative dose.
  • Low‑dose rate mode whenever available.
  • Tight collimation to region of interest; avoid unnecessary field size.
  • Image intensifier proximity – keep as close as possible to patient.
  • Manual exposure control: use 2–4 mA current, 75–80 kV voltage for dose reduction.
8.3 Image Acquisition Strategies
  • Utilize “last image hold” or digital storage; limit hard‑shot spot images to essential documentation.
  • Avoid boost/magnification unless clinically indispensable.
8.4 Procedural Technique Adjustments
  • Prefer wire‑guided biliary cannulation over contrast injection.
  • Confirm ductal access by bile aspiration or direct visualization rather than repeated fluoroscopic shots.
  • Consider staged ERCP: temporary stent placement now, definitive therapy postpartum if procedure predicted to be prolonged/complex.
8.5 Alternative Imaging Pathways
  • Perform endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) first when feasible; can clarify stone burden/anatomy and potentially obviate need for fluoroscopy‑based ERCP.
9. Positioning Specifics by Trimester & Procedure Type
  • Trimester 1: Supine acceptable for brief ERCP to optimize cholangiogram, but still monitor maternal hemodynamics.
  • Trimester 2: Left pelvic tilt recommended; avoid full supine to reduce IVC compression.
  • >20 wk (Trimester 3): Strict left lateral decubitus or left pelvic tilt for all endoscopies; supine contraindicated due to aortic/IVC compression risk.
9.1 Colonoscopy Adjustments
  • Underwater insertion technique reduces need for abdominal pressure.
  • If external pressure required, apply away from uterus.
9.2 ERCP Specifics
  • Position patient with left lateral tilt; ensure fluoroscopic C‑arm angled to minimize scatter toward fetus.
  • Use bipolar cautery if electrosurgical hemostasis needed; place grounding pad on right thorax/shoulder/arm.
10. Post‑procedure Monitoring Protocols (Expanded)
10.1 Immediate Maternal Assessment
  • Vital signs, oxygen saturation, airway patency.
  • Assess for aspiration, bronchospasm, bleeding.
10.2 Fetal Surveillance Timeline
Gestational Age Monitoring Modality Timing
<24 wk Doppler fetal heart rate Within 30 min post‑procedure
≥24 wk Continuous electronic fetal monitoring + uterine activity Initiate within 15 min, continue ≥30 min
10.3 Complication Management Pathway
  • Hypoxia → maternal oxygen supplementation, consider emergent obstetric evaluation.
  • Bleeding → endoscopic hemostasis (bipolar cautery/hemoclips) → monitor hemoglobin & fetal status.
  • Pancreatitis post‑ERCP → aggressive IV hydration, analgesia (avoid NSAIDs after 20 wk), consider prophylactic pancreatic duct stent intra‑procedurally.
10.4 Discharge Criteria
  • Stable maternal vitals ≥2 h, no ongoing bleeding or pain.
  • Fetal heart rate reassuring for at least 30 min post‑monitoring.
  • Clear instructions for emergency contact if uterine contractions or vaginal bleeding occur.

14. Patient Education Handout (Key Points)
  • Endoscopy can be performed safely when medically indicated.
  • Risks are low but include preterm labor, radiation exposure (generally <10 mGy).
  • You will be monitored before and after the procedure; fetal heart rate checks are routine.
  • Discuss any concerns with your GI and obstetric team; alternatives will be offered when possible.
