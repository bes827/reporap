Key Concepts & Definitions
  • Firm grasp of core terminology is essential for epidemiological research.
  • These concepts are fundamental building blocks for understanding study strengths/limitations.
  • Mastering these terms enables evaluation of validity & appropriate design selection.
Cross-Sectional Study
  • All measurements (predictors + outcomes) made @ single occasion/short period.
  • No follow-up involved.
  • Provides snapshot of prevalence.
Examples
  • NHANES: US population health/habits @ specific time.
  • Study associating childhood obesity → hours spent watching television.
  • Study on movie smoking exposure → smoking initiation: prevalence of ever trying smoking was 22% (highest quartile) vs 2% (lowest).
Cohort Study
  • Group of participants (cohort) assembled @ outset, followed longitudinally.
  • Estimates incidence of an outcome among exposed/unexposed.
  • Tracks disease development over time.
Examples
  • Nurses' Health Studies (NHS): examining diseases in women since 1976.
  • Study on menstrual cycle regularity → risk of death (NHS-II).
  • Study on 'door-to-needle time' for stroke treatment → 1-year mortality in Medicare patients.
  • Study on effect of postmenopausal hormone therapy → heart disease.
Case-Control Study
  • Investigator starts w/ sample of people w/ outcome (cases) & w/o outcome (controls).
  • Looks backward to measure/compare previous exposures in both groups.
  • Efficient for studying rare outcomes.
Examples
  • Comparing previous exposure to arc welding in ocular melanoma cases vs. healthy controls.
  • Study of Hepatitis A outbreak: comparing consumption of frozen pomegranate arils between cases/controls.
  • Study linking maternal diethylstilbestrol use → vaginal cancer in daughters.
  • Study linking prone sleeping position → SIDS.
Prevalence
  • Proportion of people who have a disease/condition @ one point in time.
  • Snapshot of existing cases.
  • Calculated as: # people w/ disease / # people @ risk.
Examples
  • Prevalence of smoking in various demographic groups (NHANES).
  • In a study of children and movie smoking exposure, prevalence of ever having tried smoking was the outcome.
Incidence
  • Proportion of people who get a disease/condition over a given period of time.
  • Measures new cases.
  • Two main types: Cumulative Incidence & Incidence Rate.
Cumulative Incidence
  • # who get disease / # @ risk.
  • Represents the average risk of developing the disease over a specified period.
Incidence Rate
  • # who get disease / person-time @ risk.
  • Accounts for varying follow-up times among individuals.
Examples
  • Incidence of death before age 70 in the Nurses’ Health Study II.
  • Incidence of 1-year mortality after stroke (Man et al. study).
Odds Ratio (OR)
  • Measure of association: compares odds of exposure in cases to odds of exposure in controls.
  • Approximates Risk Ratio (RR) when outcome is rare (≈ 10% or less).
  • Commonly used in case-control studies.
Examples
  • Hepatitis A outbreak study: OR for consumption of frozen pomegranate arils was 45.0.
  • Opiate overdose deaths: opiate agonist treatment associated w/ OR of 0.09.
  • Case-crossover study (mobile phone use/car crashes): OR was 4.5 (calculated as 27 discordant pairs w/ phone before crash vs. 6 w/o).
Risk Ratio (RR)
  • Quotient of risks in two groups.
  • Risk in exposed group / Risk in unexposed group.
  • Also known as Relative Risk.
Examples
  • Nurses' Health Study: RR of major coronary heart disease for women taking estrogen w/ progestin was reported as 0.39.
Selection Bias
  • Bias arising in case-control studies from separate sampling of cases/controls.
  • Occurs when selected cases ≠ all cases (wrt exposure) OR selected controls ≠ population that produced cases.
  • Threatens internal validity.
Examples
  • Study of vasectomy and prostate cancer: cases may over-represent men who received more medical care (e.g., those who had a vasectomy).
  • Hepatitis A study: using controls w/ other infectious diseases could be problematic if exposure of interest related to general food/water safety.
Recall Bias (Differential Misclassification)
  • Type of measurement bias.
  • Being diagnosed w/ disease → cases remember/report past exposures differently from controls.
  • Differential reporting based on outcome status.
Examples
  • Study of sun exposure and melanoma: cases might recall sun exposure history differently due to publicity about the link.
  • A twin study found evidence of this when the twin w/ melanoma was asked which twin sunbathed more as a child.
Nested Case-Control Study
  • Case-control study 'nested' within a defined cohort.
  • Cases are incident cases that occur within the cohort.
  • Controls sampled from remaining members of the same cohort.
Advantages
  • Reduces recall bias by using prospectively collected exposure data from the cohort.
  • More efficient than full cohort analysis for rare outcomes.
Examples
  • Study of malignant melanoma nested within a cohort that had collected sun exposure data years earlier.
  • Used to directly test for recall bias.
Nested Case-Cohort Study
  • Design similar to a nested case-control study.
  • Control group: random sample of all cohort members @ baseline.
  • Controls selected regardless of whether they later develop the outcome.
Advantages
  • Single random 'subcohort' can serve as the control group for multiple outcomes.
  • More flexible for studying multiple outcomes than nested case-control.
Examples
  • Plasma ACE2 study used the PURE study cohort to see if ACE2 levels predicted death/cardiometabolic diseases.
  • A single random 'subcohort' served as the control group for multiple outcomes (death, MI, stroke, etc.).
Incidence-Density Design
  • Nested case-control design.
  • Controls sampled from 'risk sets' for each case as it occurs.
  • Risk set: cohort members followed for same length of time as case, but not yet a case.
Purpose
  • Accounts for changes over time in exposure status or risk factors.
  • Ensures controls are truly 'at risk' at the time the case developed the outcome.
Examples
  • Italian study of opiate overdose deaths.
  • Sampled controls who were still alive on the date of each case's death, matching on follow-up time.
  • Accounts for variations in treatment status over time.
Case-Crossover Study
  • Each case serves as their own control.
  • Compares exposures @/just before outcome occurred vs. exposures of same cases @ other points in time.
  • Used for studying short-term effects of intermittent exposures.
Examples
  • Study on mobile phone use and car crashes.
  • Compared phone usage in 10 minutes before a crash to usage at other times (e.g., 24 hours or 7 days before) for the same individual.
  • The OR was 4.5 (calculated as 27 discordant pairs where driver used phone before crash vs. 6 where they did not).
Immortal Time Bias
  • Bias in cohort studies of treatments.
  • Can occur when exposure time is misclassified.
  • Results from study design making it impossible for an event to occur during a period classified as 'unexposed' but actually 'exposed'.
Mechanism
  • A period of 'immortal time' exists where an outcome cannot occur because the person must survive to initiate treatment or be classified as exposed.
  • This artificially inflates the protective effect of the exposure.
Examples
  • Postmenopausal hormone therapy studies.
  • A woman starting hormones in 1981 but not reporting it until a 1982 questionnaire would be counted as a 'non-user' in 1981.
  • If she had a heart attack in that first year, it would be misattributed to the 'non-user' group, biasing results.
