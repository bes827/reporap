Management of Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): A Synthesized Guideline
  • - This mind map synthesizes and compares recommendations from two major clinical guidelines:
  • - BSG (British Society of Gastroenterology): Vasant DH, et al. Gut 2021;0:1–27.
  • - ACG (American College of Gastroenterology): Lacy BE, et al. Am J Gastroenterol 2021;116:17–44.
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the NNT (Number Needed to Treat) with soluble fiber (ispaghula) for IBS symptoms, according to the BSG-cited meta-analysis? 7
  • - The ACG guideline contraindicates eluxadoline in patients without a gallbladder due to the risk of what specific adverse event? Sphincter of Oddi spasm
  • - According to the BSG guideline, what is the maximum recommended daily dose for amitriptyline when used as a gut-brain neuromodulator for IBS? 30–50 mg
  • - The ACG guideline notes that what percentage of patients with IBS-D may have Bile Acid Malabsorption (BAM) based on SeHCAT testing? 28.1%
  • - The BSG guideline states that moving from Rome III to Rome IV criteria for IBS diagnosis reduces the worldwide prevalence from approximately 9.2% to what percentage? 3.8%

IBS Definition & Diagnostic Criteria
  • - IBS is re-classified as a Disorder of Gut-Brain Interaction, moving away from the term 'functional disorder'.
  • - The diagnosis is based on a characteristic pattern of symptoms, primarily abdominal pain associated with changes in bowel habits.
Rome IV Criteria
  • - Recurrent abdominal pain, on average, at least 1 day per week in the last 3 months, associated with ≥2 of the following:
  • - Related to defecation.
  • - Associated with a change in stool frequency.
  • - Associated with a change in stool form (appearance).
  • - Criteria must be fulfilled for the last 3 months with symptom onset at least 6 months prior to diagnosis.
IBS Subtypes (based on Bristol Stool Form Scale)
  • - IBS-C (Constipation): >25% of BMs are type 1 or 2, and <25% are type 6 or 7.
  • - IBS-D (Diarrhoea): >25% of BMs are type 6 or 7, and <25% are type 1 or 2.
  • - IBS-M (Mixed): >25% of BMs are type 1 or 2, AND >25% are type 6 or 7.
  • - IBS-U (Unclassified): Patients who meet criteria for IBS but do not fit into the other subgroups.


Clinical Application of Criteria: Consensus & Discrepancy
  • - Both guidelines agree on using symptom-based criteria for diagnosis.
  • - However, they differ on which criteria are most practical in clinical settings.
BSG Guideline View
  • - Recommends the NICE guideline definition as more pragmatic for primary care.
  • - Definition: Abdominal pain/discomfort associated with altered bowel habit for at least 6 months, in the absence of alarm symptoms.
  • - Argues that Rome IV criteria may be too restrictive for clinical practice, potentially leaving many symptomatic patients without a clear diagnosis.

ACG Guideline View
  • - Endorses the use of Rome IV criteria for diagnosis in both clinical and research settings.
  • - Emphasizes that accurate subtyping based on Rome IV is critical for selecting appropriate therapy.



Diagnostic Strategy: Strong Consensus
  • - Both guidelines strongly advocate for a positive diagnostic strategy rather than a diagnosis of exclusion.
Rationale
  • - BSG: Make a positive diagnosis based on symptoms, absence of alarm features, and normal simple blood/stool tests.
  • - ACG: A positive strategy improves time to initiating therapy and is more cost-effective.
  • - An 'exclusion' strategy leads to unnecessary testing, increased costs (ACG notes it consumes $400 more per patient), and does not improve patient reassurance or outcomes.

Alarm Features (Red Flags)
  • - Presence of these features warrants further investigation (e.g., colonoscopy) to rule out organic disease.
  • - Key Features: Unexplained weight loss, rectal bleeding, family history of IBD or colorectal cancer, iron deficiency anaemia, onset of symptoms at age >50 years (BSG uses >60 for some criteria), abdominal/rectal mass.


Investigations
  • - Both guidelines agree on a limited, targeted approach to investigations in patients with typical IBS symptoms and no alarm features.
Initial Diagnostic Tests: Strong Consensus
  • - A minimal set of tests is recommended to rule out key organic diseases.
BSG Recommendations (Primary Care)
  • - All patients: Full Blood Count (FBC), C-Reactive Protein (CRP) or ESR, and Coeliac serology.
  • - Patients <45 years with diarrhoea: Add Faecal Calprotectin to exclude IBD.

ACG Recommendations
  • - Patients with IBS-D: Coeliac serology (Strong recommendation).
  • - Patients with IBS-D: Faecal Calprotectin and/or CRP to rule out IBD (Strong recommendation).


Tests NOT Routinely Recommended: Strong Consensus
  • - Both guidelines advise against broad, untargeted testing.
BSG Guideline
  • - No role for:
  • - Hydrogen breath testing (for SIBO or carbohydrate intolerance).
  • - Testing for exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (unless steatorrhoea is present).
  • - Food elimination diets based on IgG antibodies.

ACG Guideline
  • - Recommend against:
  • - Routine stool testing for enteric pathogens (unless high-risk, e.g., for Giardia).
  • - Routine testing for food allergies/sensitivities (unless reproducible symptoms suggest a true allergy).


Role of Colonoscopy: Strong Consensus
  • - Routine colonoscopy is NOT recommended in younger patients with typical IBS symptoms and no alarm features.
  • - ACG specifies age: <45 years.
  • - BSG notes: The yield is extremely low and it does not provide reassurance.
When to Consider Colonoscopy
  • - Presence of alarm features.
  • - To screen for colorectal cancer per national guidelines (e.g., age >45/50).
  • - BSG specific: In IBS-D patients with risk factors for microscopic colitis (female, age ≥50, autoimmune disease, watery diarrhoea, use of NSAIDs/PPIs).


Further Specialised Investigations
  • - Considered in secondary care for refractory symptoms or diagnostic uncertainty.
Bile Acid Diarrhoea (BAD)
  • - BSG: Consider SeHCAT scan or serum 7α-hydroxy-4-cholesten-3-one in IBS-D with atypical features (e.g., nocturnal diarrhoea) or prior cholecystectomy.

Anorectal Physiology
  • - Consensus: Consider anorectal manometry (ARM) and balloon expulsion test (BET) in patients with refractory constipation or symptoms of a pelvic floor disorder (e.g., straining, sensation of incomplete evacuation).



General Management & First-Line Therapies
  • - Management should be holistic, starting with lifestyle, diet, and simple pharmacological agents.
Lifestyle
  • - Doctor-Patient Relationship: Both guidelines strongly emphasize establishing an effective, empathic relationship as key to successful management.
  • - Exercise: BSG strongly recommends regular exercise for all patients with IBS.

Dietary Therapies
Fibre: Strong Consensus
  • - Soluble fibre (e.g., ispaghula/psyllium) is effective for global symptoms.
  • - Insoluble fibre (e.g., wheat bran) should be avoided as it can worsen symptoms.
  • - BSG: Start at a low dose (3–4 g/day) and titrate up gradually to avoid bloating.

Low FODMAP Diet: Strong Consensus
  • - Both recommend a trial of a low FODMAP diet for global symptoms.
  • - Positioned as a second-line dietary therapy (BSG) or a 'limited trial' (ACG).
  • - Crucial point: Must be supervised by a trained dietitian to ensure nutritional adequacy and proper reintroduction of foods.

Other Diets
  • - Gluten-Free Diet: BSG weakly recommends against it due to insufficient evidence.
  • - IgG-based Elimination Diets: Both strongly recommend against this approach.


Probiotics
  • - Nuanced Discrepancy: Both guidelines are cautious due to heterogeneity of evidence.
BSG Guideline
  • - Weak recommendation FOR trying probiotics.
  • - States they 'may be an effective treatment' but it's not possible to recommend a specific species or strain.
  • - Suggests a trial for up to 12 weeks and stopping if no benefit.

ACG Guideline
  • - Conditional recommendation AGAINST probiotics for global symptoms.
  • - Cites inconsistent benefits, small study sizes, and lack of rigorous endpoints in trials.


First-Line Pharmacotherapies
Antispasmodics & Peppermint Oil: Key Discrepancy
  • - The guidelines differ significantly on the role of antispasmodics, largely due to the availability of agents and quality of evidence.
BSG Guideline
  • - Weak recommendation FOR certain antispasmodics (e.g., hyoscine butylbromide) and peppermint oil for global symptoms and pain.
  • - Notes that alverine and mebeverine did not show benefit over placebo in their meta-analysis.

ACG Guideline
  • - Conditional recommendation AGAINST antispasmodics available in the US (dicyclomine, hyoscyamine) due to poor quality, decades-old data.
  • - Conditional recommendation FOR peppermint oil to provide relief of global symptoms.


Loperamide (for IBS-D)
  • - Consensus on use for diarrhoea, but not for global symptoms.
  • - BSG: Strong recommendation, but notes common side effects (pain, bloating, constipation) may limit tolerability.
  • - ACG: Notes it improves diarrhoea but not global IBS symptoms, so not recommended as a primary therapy for IBS-D.

Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) (for IBS-C)
  • - Discrepancy in Recommendation: Both agree it does not treat pain, but frame their recommendation differently.
BSG Guideline
  • - Weak recommendation FOR PEG as an effective treatment for constipation in IBS.
  • - Acknowledges abdominal pain is a common side effect.

ACG Guideline
  • - Conditional recommendation AGAINST PEG for relieving global IBS symptoms.
  • - Rationale: While it improves bowel movements, RCTs failed to show it improves abdominal pain, a core component of IBS.




Second-Line Therapies
  • - For patients whose symptoms are refractory to first-line treatments. Many are initiated in secondary care.
Gut-Brain Neuromodulators: Strong Consensus
  • - Both guidelines strongly endorse the use of neuromodulators, particularly TCAs.
Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
  • - Strong recommendation FOR TCAs (e.g., amitriptyline, desipramine) as a second-line drug for global symptoms and abdominal pain.
  • - Mechanism: Act on central pain pathways and have peripheral effects on motility.
  • - BSG Dosing: Start low (e.g., 10 mg amitriptyline at night) and titrate slowly up to a maximum of 30-50 mg daily.

Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)
  • - Weaker evidence than TCAs.
  • - BSG: Weak recommendation for global symptoms.
  • - ACG: Notes their meta-analysis showed TCAs improved pain, but SSRIs did not.


Treatments for IBS-D
Rifaximin: Strong Consensus
  • - A non-absorbable antibiotic.
  • - Both guidelines give a strong recommendation FOR rifaximin to treat global IBS-D symptoms.
  • - BSG: Notes its effect on abdominal pain is limited.
  • - ACG: Cites trials showing efficacy for both pain and stool consistency, and allows for up to two retreatments.

5-HT3 Receptor Antagonists: Strong Consensus
  • - Includes alosetron and ondansetron.
  • - BSG: Weak recommendation. Notes alosetron is unavailable in many countries and suggests ondansetron as a reasonable alternative, titrated from 4 mg once daily up to 8 mg TID.
  • - ACG: Conditional recommendation for alosetron in women with severe symptoms who have failed conventional therapy, under the REMS program.

Eluxadoline: Strong Consensus
  • - A mixed opioid receptor agonist.
  • - Both guidelines recommend it for IBS-D.
  • - Crucial Contraindications: Prior cholecystectomy, history of pancreatitis, alcohol dependence, or severe liver impairment.

Bile Acid Sequestrants: Discrepancy in Approach
  • - BSG: Recommends testing for Bile Acid Diarrhoea (BAD) and treating if positive, implying use of sequestrants.
  • - ACG: Conditional recommendation AGAINST their use for global IBS-D symptoms, citing a lack of controlled trials. Suggests empiric therapy is a reasonable clinical option if BAD is suspected.


Treatments for IBS-C
Secretagogues: Strong Consensus
  • - Both guidelines give strong recommendations FOR secretagogues.
Guanylate Cyclase-C (GC-C) Agonists
  • - Linaclotide and Plecanatide.
  • - BSG: Linaclotide is likely the most efficacious secretagogue available.
  • - Common side effect: Diarrhoea.

Chloride Channel Activators
  • - Lubiprostone.
  • - BSG: Less likely to cause diarrhoea than other secretagogues.
  • - Common side effect: Nausea.

Sodium-Hydrogen Exchange Inhibitors
  • - Tenapanor.
  • - Common side effect: Diarrhoea.


5-HT4 Receptor Agonists: Consensus
  • - Tegaserod.
  • - Both guidelines note its efficacy but highlight its restricted availability (US only) and use (women <65 with ≤1 CV risk factor) due to cardiovascular safety concerns.



Psychological Therapies: Strong Consensus
  • - Both guidelines strongly recommend gut-directed psychotherapies for global IBS symptoms, especially for patients with refractory symptoms.
Recommended Modalities
  • - Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): Strong evidence for efficacy, can be delivered face-to-face, by telephone, or web-based.
  • - Gut-Directed Hypnotherapy (GDH): Strong evidence for efficacy, modulates the gut-brain axis.

Timing of Referral
  • - BSG: Consider when symptoms have not improved after 12 months of drug treatment, but can be offered earlier based on patient preference and local availability.
  • - ACG: Presented as a general treatment option without a specific timeline.


Management of Severe or Refractory IBS
  • - Consensus: This population requires a careful, integrated, and multidisciplinary approach.
Key Principles
  • - Review the diagnosis and consider further targeted investigation.
  • - Manage with an integrated multidisciplinary team (gastroenterologist, dietitian, psychologist).
  • - Primum non nocere (First, do no harm): Avoid iatrogenic harms from opioid prescribing, unnecessary surgery (e.g., cholecystectomy, hysterectomy), and unproven therapies.

Advanced Pharmacotherapy (BSG)
  • - Augmentation: Consider using combination gut-brain neuromodulators (e.g., TCA + SNRI).
  • - Requires vigilance for risks of serotonin syndrome.


Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT)
  • - Strong Consensus: Both guidelines recommend AGAINST the use of FMT for IBS outside of a research setting.
Rationale
  • - Meta-analyses of RCTs have shown no significant benefit of FMT compared to placebo.
  • - Some trials even showed placebo capsules were superior to donor stool capsules.
