Lecture 2: Legislative Developments
Speaker Background
  • Partner at leading government relations firm
  • Heads healthcare lobbying practice with 20+ years experience
  • Executive Director of AI Healthcare Coalition
  • Teaches health law at American University
  • Previously lobbyist/attorney at AMA and legislative counsel on Capitol Hill
Current Congress Status
  • Congress is in "education mode" on AI—members recognize it but lack deep understanding
  • Basic building blocks being discussed:
  • What is an algorithm?
  • What is an LLM?
  • How does a chatbot differ from a diagnostic tool?
  • Few staff members do both tech AND health—expertise melding is "work in progress"
Key Legislative Actions
1. AI Moratorium (Rejected)
  • Ten-year moratorium proposed in reconciliation bill debate ("One Big Beautiful Bill Act")
  • Would have precluded state laws that inhibit AI innovation while waiting for federal system
  • Passed out of House but was beaten back—did not make into final bill
  • Many in larger tech organizations still want to see it proceed before year-end
2. Algorithmic Based Healthcare Service (ABHS) Bill
  • Creates new classification for AI medical services in OUTPATIENT PAYMENT SYSTEM
  • Currently, AI services reimbursed under new technology ambulatory payment classifications (no specific pathway, time-limited)
  • This bill would give minimum 5-year certainty for reimbursement
  • Defines "algorithmic-based healthcare service" (ABHS) for first time
  • ABHS definition could be duplicated across:
  • Physician fee schedule
  • Inpatient payment system
  • Private payers
  • Medicare Advantage
  • Leads: Senators Rounds and Heinrich (leaders of AI Senate caucus)
3. Sandbox Act (Senator Cruz)
  • Concept: Regulatory "sandbox" for experimentation when legacy regulations encumber innovation
  • Would give FDA flexibility to permit experimentation, innovation, development in controlled environment
  • Applies to AI tools under FDA jurisdiction
  • Example of executive-level AI action plan duplicated in Congress
  • Speaker uncertain if it will pass but considers it "interesting bill to watch"
4. Health Technology Act (Mr. Schweikert, House)
  • Would clarify that AI/ML technologies can qualify as "practitioner eligible to prescribe drugs"
  • Speaker guesses it probably won't pass but shows willingness to break through AI barriers
  • Connects to workforce debate—what does AI healthcare service mean for employment?
  • Note: Autonomous AI already has CPT code being paid in physician fee schedule (outpatient)
State vs. Federal Tension
  • Significant state-level activity (50+ slides worth of bills)
  • California chatbot bill mentioned as recent example
  • State laws create friction with need for federal preemption
  • Expect more state activity, which will continue to prompt federal moratorium discussions
Timeline Outlook
  • Current 119th Congress: ~4 AI-related bills
  • Next session: Expect ~30 bills instead of 4
  • "Drips and drabs" currently but will accelerate
  • AI is "fun issue" for members—easy to talk about risks AND American innovation vs. China
Key Quotes/Phrases
  • "Congress is in education mode" on AI basics
  • "White House down to Congress"—legislation flows top-down, not bottom-up
  • ABHS definition "much more important than what the bill actually does" because it will be replicated
  • Regulatory sandbox for "experimentation, innovation, development"
Lecture 3: Medicare & Reimbursement
Speaker Background
  • Senior Vice President of Payment and Healthcare Delivery Policy, head of research at AdvaMed
  • Previously led healthcare policy teams at MITRE supporting CMS on quality/payment strategy
  • JD from American University Washington College of Law
  • MPH from Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health
Core Premise
  • Medicare is the "bellwether"—how Medicare handles AI drives private payer behavior
  • One Blues program varies by insurer, but Medicare sets tone
Three Key Questions CMS Asks
1. Benefit Categories (Statutory—set by Congress)
  • Hasn't been meaningfully updated since 1970s
  • Social Security Act mentions "iron lungs" (no longer in use)—doesn't contemplate modern technology
  • Many AI services not contemplated by existing benefit categories
2. Is It "Reasonable and Necessary" for Medicare Population?
  • Ages 65+, certain disabilities, end-stage renal disease
  • Standard Medicare criteria
3. Coding and Payment
  • How do we explain the service? (coding)
  • How much will we pay? (fee schedule)
Current Payment Landscape by Setting
Inpatient Hospital (IPPS)
  • Coverage exists for AI technologies but is "very technology specific"
  • New Technology Add-on Payments pathway available for innovative tech
  • Example: Stroke AI predictor received pass-through payment, now part of IPPS
  • Once added to system, must "fight with every other specialty" about payment rates
Outpatient (OPPS + ASCs)
  • Clear pathway: New Technology APC (Ambulatory Payment Classification)
  • CMS acknowledges significant upfront costs for new tech adoption
  • Will pay for period of years based on technology cost (if certain tests met)
  • ABHS bill would expand New Technology APC timeline beyond current 3 years
  • After new tech APC expires, must go back to groupings based on clinical/cost coherence
  • CMS uses "dartboard approach"—difficult, involves proprietary pricing information
  • CMS often pays 30% of requested price—no one happy with outcome
Physician Fee Schedule
  • "Yes, but a very big yes, but" (complicated)
  • CMS generally does NOT pay for efficiency gains—will "pay less as you become more efficient" with idea that physicians can do infinitely more care
  • EXCEPTION: One fully autonomous AI code covered (Digital Diagnostics—eye imaging for diabetic retinopathy)
  • Problem: Crosswalked to contact lens correction code (not cataract), creating complex dynamics
  • Every time price drops for crosswalked code, everything linked to it also drops
  • Constant tension: "I'm getting paid but my rates are dropping because of something beyond my control"
Physician Services (Non-AI Adjacent)
  • Active remote monitoring covered but limited to specific conditions
  • Physiologic monitoring: GI NOT included (limited set of conditions)
  • Therapeutic monitoring: Mental health conditions, physical therapy tracking covered
  • Digital therapeutics: Cognitive behavioral therapy technologies covered
  • Efficiency improvements outside SAMD (Software as Medical Device) NOT reimbursable
Current CMS Activity
  • Request for Information (RFI) in proposed rules this year around payment for SaaS
  • CMS has specific SaaS definition (from years ago) that doesn't quite fit
  • AdvaMed pushed to focus on SAMD (Software as a Medical Device) instead:
  • Regulated, FDA-evaluated technologies
  • Greater certainty around accuracy and endpoint achievement
Practice Expense Pathway
  • Not perfect answer (physicians don't want to "buy and bill")
  • Two options:

1. Directly into cost of procedure (fight over code costs)

2. As supply—goes into direct practice expense component

  • "Not beautiful or perfect system" but only discrete pathways available
Durable Medical Equipment (DMEPOS)
  • Acronym means: "things used by patient at home"
  • Currently NO coverage for digital technologies used by patients at home under conventional care
  • Example: If AI solution tracks patient at home outside PFS coverage—nothing happens, can't get paid
  • Industry workaround: Reverse-engineer software into physical components to meet DMEPOS requirements
  • Example: Abbott sensor technology—package includes handheld (even though patient has phone)
  • Handheld legally required under DMEPOS statute even if redundant
  • Companies build "little plastic box" to satisfy legal requirements
Medicare Advantage (MA)
  • Extremely important to watch—now 52% of Medicare patients in MA plans
  • Early interest: Patients transitioning into system
  • Current concern: Plans using AI for prior authorizations (inappropriately limiting care)
  • Example: United Healthcare issue with SNF stays
  • Plans closely evaluating what is/isn't covered to ensure nothing uncovered is offered
  • Troubling for AI space—no level of certainty on payment rates
  • Monitoring MA closely as larger transition occurs and margins prove variable
Key Insight: Perpetual Tension
  • "How do we ensure payment rates can be appropriately supported by specialists who use technologies BUT also want to ensure rates for other procedures aren't inappropriately lowered just to pay for different things?"
  • "Impossible tension"—hard to explain rebalancing when representing 600 members
CMMI Innovation Center Opportunity
  • Center for Medicare and Medicaid Innovation (CMMI) can pay for expanding into NEW care pathways facilitated by AI/digital health
  • Recent model: WISER (specifics not detailed)
  • Proposed model: Ambulatory surgical pathway
  • Key message being communicated to CMMI:
  • Focus on EXPANDING access and facilitating efficient/effective care
  • Efficiency should NOT mean "making you work harder"
  • Should allow AI/digital tech to carry load WHILE ensuring providers get paid for adoption costs
Summary: Payment for AI in Medicare
  • Yes, but approached on technology-by-technology basis
  • No high enough certainty for manufacturers going through FDA process
  • Creates disincentive: Do studies for FDA, then CMS says "what does this look like for Medicare patients?"
  • Payment rate significantly lower than expected because fitting into system built by people who "did not contemplate this world"
  • Not negative message—actively working to address issues
Key Quotes/Phrases
  • "Benefit categories haven't been meaningfully updated since the 1970s"
  • "Iron lungs"—statute mentions obsolete technology, not modern AI
  • "Efficiency trap": CMS doesn't pay for pure efficiency gains
  • "Crosswalking" problems—AI diagnostics pigeonholed into wrong code categories
  • "Dartboard approach" to pricing—CMS uses imprecise methods
  • "Perpetual dance with Medicare"—uncertainty cycle for manufacturers
