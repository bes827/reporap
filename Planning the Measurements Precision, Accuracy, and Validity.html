Planning the Measurements: Precision, Accuracy, and Validity
  • Focus: Designing measurements that accurately represent phenomena of interest for research questions.
  • Key Concepts: Measurement scales, precision (random error), accuracy (precision + bias), validity.
  • Application: Applies to predictor, outcome variables, and covariates in studies.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What type of measurement scale offers the highest statistical power and allows for the most detailed distribution patterns?
  • 2. When assessing the accuracy of a measurement, what is the primary reference point used for comparison?
  • 3. Name three main sources of random error that can reduce the precision of a measurement.
  • 4. What is the key difference between precision and accuracy in measurement, particularly regarding systematic error?
  • 5. Beyond standardizing methods and training observers, what is a crucial strategy for reducing systematic error in mechanical or electrical instruments?
Measurement Scales
  • Classification of variables based on information content.
  • Influences statistical power, sample size, and distribution patterns.
  • Prefer continuous over categorical variables for ↑ statistical efficiency.
Categorical Variables
  • Phenomena classified into categories; not suitable for quantification.
  • Information content < numerical variables.
Dichotomous
  • Two possible values/categories.
  • Example: Vital status (alive/dead).
  • Descriptive Statistics: Counts, proportions.
  • Statistical Power: Low.
Nominal
  • More than two categories; unordered.
  • Example: Gender identity, blood type.
  • Descriptive Statistics: Counts, proportions.
  • Statistical Power: Low.
Ordinal
  • More than two categories; ordered, but intervals not quantifiable.
  • Example: Degree of pain (none, mild, moderate, severe); social class.
  • Descriptive Statistics: Counts, proportions, medians.
  • Statistical Power: Intermediate.
Designing Ordinal Scales
  • Useful to provide ~6 categories (e.g., 'strongly agree' to 'strongly disagree').
  • Allows later collapse into dichotomy (e.g., agree/disagree); reverse not possible.
Numerical Variables
  • Quantified with a number ('how much' or 'how many').
  • Offer ↑ information, ↑ statistical power, ↓ sample size requirements.
Continuous
  • Theoretically infinite number of values on a scale.
  • Example: Weight, hemoglobin level.
  • Descriptive Statistics: Means, standard deviations.
  • Statistical Power: High.
Advantages of Continuous Variables
  • ↑ Statistical efficiency; allows observation of magnitude of change.
  • Flexibility in fitting data to variable nature/association shape.
  • Example: BMI vs. mortality → U-shaped pattern, threshold values.
  • Example: Birth weight → record actual weight, allows flexible cutoffs (<1500, 1500-1999, etc.).
Discrete or Count
  • Limited, countable number of values (typically integers).
  • Example: Number of pregnancies, number of sexual partners.
  • Descriptive Statistics: Same as ordinal (few values); same as continuous (many values).
  • Statistical Power: High if many possible values, especially if average near middle.
Discrete vs. Continuous in Analysis
  • Well-distributed discrete variables (≥6 possible values) ≈ continuous variables in statistical analyses.
  • Equivalent for measurement design purposes.
Quantifying Subjective Phenomena
  • Essential for research questions on pain, quality of life.
  • Example: SF-36 questionnaire → discrete numerical ratings for 8 dimensions (physical, social, emotional function, general well-being).
  • Process: ↑ objectivity, ↓ bias, ↑ detailed communication.
Precision
  • Degree to which a variable is reproducible; nearly same value each time measured.
  • Also called reproducibility, reliability, consistency.
  • ↑ Precision → ↑ statistical power for given sample size.
Mechanism: Random Error
  • Precision is a function of random error (chance variability).
  • ↑ Error → ↓ Precision.
Sources of Random Error
  • 1. Observer variability: Choice of words, skill w/ instrument.
  • 2. Instrument variability: Changing environmental factors (temp), reagent differences.
  • 3. Participant variability: Intrinsic variability unrelated to study variables (e.g., strength due to time of day, food intake, effort).
Assessing Precision
  • Evaluated by reproducibility of repeated measurements.
  • Comparison: Same person (within-observer) / different people (between-observer).
  • Also: Within/between instruments.
For Continuous Variables
  • Expressed as: Within-participant standard deviation / coefficient of variation (within-participant SD ÷ mean).
For Categorical Variables
  • Expressed as: Percent agreement, interclass correlation coefficient, kappa statistic.
Strategies for Enhancing Precision
  • Aim: Minimize random error; ↑ reproducibility.
  • Decision based on: Variable importance, magnitude of precision problem, feasibility/cost.
1. Standardizing Measurement Methods
  • All study protocols must include detailed instructions.
  • Example: Specify cuff deflation rate @ 2 mm Hg/s for BP.
  • Example: Participant sit in quiet room for 5 min before BP measurement.
  • Materials: Written directions, pictures, video for environment prep, participant prep, measurement execution, recording, instrument calibration.
  • Benefit: Ensures uniform performance, basis for method description in publications.
2. Training & Certifying Observers
  • Improves consistency of measurement techniques, esp. w/ multiple observers.
  • Test mastery of techniques; certify prescribed performance level.
  • Measure & optimize interrater reliability (e.g., using Kappa) when multiple observers.
3. Refining Instruments
  • Mechanical/electronic instruments: Engineer to ↓ variability.
  • Questionnaires/interviews: Multiple iterations to ↑ clarity, avoid ambiguities.
4. Automating Instruments
  • Eliminates human observer variation.
  • Examples: Automatic BP devices, self-response questionnaires.
5. Repetition
  • Influence of random error ↓ by repeating measurement; use mean of ≥2 readings.
  • Example: Average of two leg strength measurements ↑ precision.
  • Benefit: Substantially ↑ precision.
  • Limitations: Added cost, practical difficulties; potential for systematic changes (e.g., tiring, improved test-taking).
Accuracy
  • Degree to which a variable represents the true value.
  • Depends on: Maximizing precision + minimizing bias.
  • Many strategies for ↑ precision also ↑ accuracy.
Mechanism: Bias (Systematic Error)
  • Accuracy is affected by bias.
  • ↑ Bias → ↓ Accuracy.
  • Example: Miscalibrated instruments → precise but biased results.
Sources of Bias
  • Systematic errors corresponding to random error sources.
Observer Bias
  • Conscious/unconscious distortion in perception/reporting by observer.
  • Example: Tendency to round BP measurements down to nearest zero.
  • Example: Using leading questions in interviews.
Instrument Bias
  • Faulty function of mechanical instrument.
  • Example: Uncalibrated scale drifts downward → consistently low body weights.
Participant Bias
  • Distortion by study participant (respondent/recall bias).
  • Example: Breast cancer patients exaggerating alcohol intake if they believe it's a cause.
Assessing Accuracy
  • Best assessed by comparing to a 'gold standard'.
  • Gold Standard: Reference measurement by technique believed to best represent true value.
  • Designating gold standard: Difficult judgment, draws on prior work & expert consensus.
For Continuous Scales
  • Expressed as: Mean difference between measurement under investigation and gold standard.
For Dichotomous Scales
  • Described by: Sensitivity and specificity.
For Categorical Scales (>2 options)
  • Calculated as: Percent correct on each option.
Strategies for Enhancing Accuracy
  • Aim: Minimize systematic error.
  • Decision based on: Potential impact of inaccuracy on conclusions, feasibility/cost.
  • Always use: Standardizing, training.
  • Essential when feasible: Calibration (for instruments changing over time), blinding (for comparisons).
1. Standardizing Measurement Methods
  • Example: Define diastolic BP as point where sounds cease to be heard (vs. muffled).
  • Example: Participant sit in quiet room for 5 min before measurement.
2. Training & Certifying Observers
  • Example: Trainer checks observer's BP reading accuracy by repeating measurement.
3. Refining Instruments
  • Example: Use extra-wide BP cuff for obese/muscular patients to avoid consistently high readings.
4. Automating Instruments
  • Example: Use automatic BP device to eliminate observer tendency to read BP lower in active drug group.
  • Example: Use automatic BP device to mitigate participant's emotional reaction to observer.
5. Making Unobtrusive Measurements
  • Participants unaware of measurement → eliminates conscious bias.
  • Example: Observers blending in to evaluate hand hygiene in cafeteria.
6. Calibrating Instruments
  • Periodic calibration w/ gold standard ↑ accuracy of mechanical/electrical instruments.
  • Example: Calibrate BP manometer monthly.
7. Blinding
  • Does not improve overall accuracy, but eliminates differential bias between study groups.
  • Double-blind trial: Participants/observers unaware of active medicine/placebo assignment.
  • Result: Inaccuracy in outcome measurement is same in both groups.
  • Example: Double-blind placebo conceals study group assignment to prevent observer reading BP lower in active group.
  • Example: Prevents participant overreporting side effects if they know they're on active drug.
Validity
  • Qualitative dimension of how well a measurement represents the phenomena of interest.
  • Example: Cystatin C more valid for kidney function than creatinine (creatinine influenced by muscle mass).
  • Often not amenable to gold standard assessment, esp. for subjective/abstract phenomena (pain, quality of life).
Types of Validity
  • Constructs used to address validity when no gold standard.
Content Validity
  • Examines how well measurement represents all aspects of phenomena.
  • Example: SF-36 questionnaire includes social, physical, emotional function, general health, mental health, bodily pain to assess quality of life.
Face Validity
  • Describes whether measurement seems inherently reasonable.
  • Example: Assessing frailty by muscle weakness, exhaustion, weight loss, slow walking speed.
Construct Validity
  • Degree to which measurement matches theoretical expectations.
  • Example: Social isolation measure should differentiate individuals w/ few vs. many friendships/social activities.
Predictive Validity
  • Ability of measurement to predict an outcome.
  • Example: How well frailty measurement predicts admission to long-term care.
Criterion-Related Validity
  • Degree to which new measurement correlates w/ established measurements.
  • Example: New biochemical test of muscle mass correlates w/ muscle strength measurements.
Approach to Measuring Subjective/Abstract Phenomena
  • 1. Search literature/consult experts: Find suitable, validated instrument.
  • Advantages: Results comparable to prior work, simplifies grant/publication process.
  • Disadvantages: Validation suboptimal, instrument may be outmoded/not optimal for research question.
  • 2. Develop new measurement approach: Can be challenging, time/effort intensive.
  • May lead to worthwhile contribution, but validation process often less conclusive.
Other Features of Measurement Approaches
  • Considerations for designing effective and efficient measurements.
Sensitivity
  • Ability to detect differences in a characteristic important to investigator.
  • Required sensitivity depends on research question.
  • Example: 6-minute walk distance more sensitive to Δ cardiac performance than self-reported shortness of breath after walking one block.
Specificity
  • Represents only the characteristic of interest.
  • Example: Echocardiographic measurement of heart contractile function more specific to heart failure treatment effect than 6-minute walk distance (also influenced by lung function, leg muscle fatigability).
Appropriateness
  • Measurements must align w/ study objectives.
  • Example: Stress study for myocardial infarction → define type of stress (psychological/physical, acute/chronic) before operational definitions.
Adequate Distribution of Responses
  • Measurement most useful if values range from high to low in participants.
  • Pretesting ensures responses don't cluster at one end ('floor and ceiling effects').
Objectivity
  • Minimize subjective judgments.
  • Achieved by: ↓ observer/participant involvement, using automated instruments.
  • Danger: Tunnel vision, limits scope of observations, ability to discover unanticipated phenomena.
  • Mitigation: Include open-ended questions, opportunity for subjective/qualitative data.
Efficiency & Parsimony
  • Avoid adding items not central to research question.
  • Collect useful data @ affordable cost (time, money) w/ acceptable participant burden.
  • Common error: Collecting too much info → participant fatigue, overwhelmed team, cluttered data management/analysis.
  • Result: More expensive study, paradoxically less successful in answering main questions.
Measurements on Stored Materials
  • Many measurements can be done later on banked biologic specimens or electronically filed images.
  • Examples: Medical history (EMR, NLP), biochemical measures (serum, plasma, urine, stool), genetic/molecular tests (whole blood, tissue), imaging (X-rays, CT).
Advantages of Storage
  • 1. Cost reduction: Measure only on individuals w/ outcome of interest + sample of others (e.g., nested case-control study).
  • 2. Scientific advances: New ideas/measurement techniques can be employed later, funded by new grants.
  • 3. Sophisticated analyses: Allows application of proteomics, gene expression in individual cells.
Practical Considerations
  • Specimen Collection: Carefully collected, quickly frozen.
  • Archiving: Very low temperatures (-70 °C or -190 °C) for preservation.
  • Types of Specimens: Consider archiving plasma, urine, white blood cells, stool samples to anticipate wide array of future assays.
  • Informed Consent: Obtain broad consent covering wide scope of future potential uses.
