Writing the Methods and Results Sections
  • This document provides guidelines for writing the Methods and Results sections of scientific articles.
  • It covers purpose, content, structure, verb tense, use of figures/tables, and highlighting findings.
  • Target audience: physicians.

  • 2. What is the primary purpose of the Methods section in a scientific article?
  • 3. What key elements should be included in the Methods section of a prospective clinical study, according to the CONSORT statement?

Methods Section
  • Explains precisely what was done to answer the research question.
  • Provides enough detail for replication.
  • Common problem: lack of sufficient detail.
Purpose and Content
  • Explain what you did to answer your research question.
  • Provide enough detail that a reader in your field could repeat your study.
  • Explain why each experiment, intervention, or analysis was performed.
Suggested Structure
  • Use subheadings to organize material.
  • Basic science: Materials, Cell Lines/Cultures, Human Subjects, Animals, Experiments, Statistical Tests.
  • Retrospective clinical: Subjects, Selection criteria, Data reviewed, Subgroups, # of patients, Approval, Evaluations/interventions, Outcome measures, Statistical methods.
  • Prospective clinical: Participants, Interventions, Outcome measures, Sample size, Randomization, Blinding, Statistical methods.
Basic Science Studies
  • Materials: Generic drug names, concentrations, doses, reagent grades, supplier locations.
  • Cell Lines/Cultures: Supplier info, preparation methods.
  • Human Subjects: Approval, consent, source, inclusion/exclusion, sample collection, # samples.
  • Animals: Standards of care, supplier, # animals, procedures, method of killing.
  • Experiments: Description, rationale, procedure. Chronological order usually best.
  • Statistical Tests: Identify tests, what they evaluate, significance values (e.g., P < 0.05).
Retrospective Clinical Studies
  • Subjects: Source (chart review, database).
  • Selection criteria: Inclusion/exclusion, dates.
  • Data reviewed: Types of data extracted.
  • Subgroups: How defined.
  • # Patients: Total and per subgroup (may be in Results).
  • Approval: IRB and consent (or waiver).
  • Evaluations/Interventions: Pretreatment, treatments, follow-up. Report numbers receiving each if variations affect outcome.
  • Outcome Measures: Measures and clinically important differences.
  • Statistical Methods: Detailed for replication.
Prospective Clinical Studies (CONSORT)
  • Participants: Eligibility criteria, settings, locations.
  • Interventions: Precise details, administration.
  • Outcome measures: Primary/secondary, quality enhancement methods.
  • Sample size: Determination, interim analyses, stopping rules.
  • Randomization: Sequence generation (blocking, stratification), allocation concealment (numbered containers, central telephone), implementation (who generated/enrolled/assigned).
  • Blinding (Masking): Participants, administrators, assessors. How success evaluated.
  • Statistical methods: Group comparisons, subgroup/adjusted analyses.
  • Ethical conduct: IRB approval, informed consent.
Tailoring to Journal
  • Location: Varies (after Intro, before Results, or last). Check journal.
  • Detail: Broad readership → more detail. Specialized → less detail for known methods.
  • Cite previously published techniques; briefly describe + modifications.
Verb Tense
  • Use past tense. E.g., 'Tissue specimens were examined...'
  • Past perfect for events before the study. E.g., '...interferon-alfa therapy had failed.'
  • Avoid present tense.
Results Section
  • Provides a straightforward account of findings.
  • Avoids methods details (already in Methods).
  • Avoids in-depth interpretation (belongs in Discussion).
Purpose and Content
  • Present findings clearly and concisely.
  • Methods: Mention type of experiment, intervention, or analysis performed.
  • Interpretation: Limited, can include phrases or sentences.
Interpretation Examples
  • Example 1: Use interpretive phrases (e.g., contrasting finding, conflicting result, interesting finding) to guide the reader.
  • Example 2: Brief speculation about specific findings is acceptable.
Suggested Structure
  • Order: Same as experiments in Methods.
  • Subheadings: Useful for organization; can mirror Methods subheadings.
  • Basic science: Remind readers of experiment, describe findings (refer to figures/tables), state most important aspect of data.
  • Clinical: Similar, but omit opening reminder sentence.
Example Structure
  • 1. Remind readers of the experiment/analysis performed.
  • 2. Describe the findings, referencing figures and tables.
  • 3. Conclude with the most important aspect of the data.
Verb Tense
  • Use past tense. E.g., 'Serum levels correlated...'
  • Avoid present tense (reserved for established facts and general conclusions).
Figures and Tables
  • Present most data in figures/tables.
  • First mention belongs in Results.
  • Forces data organization/analysis.
  • See 'Effective Figures and Tables' chapter.
Text vs. Figure vs. Table
  • 1-2 sentences in text is best.
  • More than 2 sentences → table/figure.
  • Tables of raw data may obscure trends; summarize data for clarity.
  • Graphs effectively show trends/relationships.
Limiting Numbers
  • Adhere to journal limits.
  • Too many figures reduce impact.
  • Multiple panels per figure should be closely related.
  • Combine tables if possible.
  • Show representative results + note similarity in text/legend.
Minimizing Repetition
  • Avoid repeating table/figure data in text.
  • Summarize instead of repeating.
Consistency and Accuracy
  • Double-check all numbers and percentages.
  • Ensure data match across Abstract, Results, Discussion, tables, figures.
  • Follow journal guidelines for figures/tables.
Use of 'Data Not Shown'
  • Indicates absence of data was not an oversight.
  • Use sparingly.
  • Limit to: unimportant numbers, findings similar to presented data, routine assays.
  • ≠ 'unpublished results' (from other studies).
Highlighting Important Findings
  • Summary statement before/after detailed results.
  • Subheadings (if permitted) can highlight importance.
Strategy for Writing
  • 1. Construct figures/tables.
  • 2. Outline Results with subheadings.
  • 3. List figures/tables under each subheading.
  • 4. Write text for each subheading, referring to figures/tables.
  • 5. Ensure a result for each method in Methods.
  • 6. Verify data consistency between text, figures, tables.
Worksheets
  • Methods and Results worksheets for prospective, retrospective, and basic science studies.
Prospective Studies Worksheet
  • Methods: Patient recruitment (eligibility, setting), sample size, ethical considerations (IRB, consent), interventions, outcome measures, randomization, blinding, treatment complications, statistical methods.
  • Results: Number of patients per subgroup, past tense, no data duplication, summarize data, no repeated methods, limit speculation, highlight findings.
Basic Science Studies Worksheet
  • Methods: Materials, cell lines/cultures, human subjects (IRB, consent, source, criteria, collection), animals (care, supplier, #, treatment), experiments (header, what/why), statistical tests.
  • Results: Past tense, no data duplication, summarize findings, no repeated methods, limit speculation, highlight findings.
Retrospective Studies Worksheet
  • Methods: Study subjects (source, criteria, setting), ethical considerations (IRB, consent), study design (data types, subgroups, # patients), evaluations/interventions, outcome measures, statistical methods.
  • Results: Past tense, no data duplication, summarize findings, no repeated methods, limit speculation, highlight findings.
