Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP) Management: Small Bowel and Gastric Polyp Guidelines
  • This document summarizes current recommendations for evaluating and managing small bowel polyps and fundic gland polyps (FGPs) in patients with Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP).
  • Focuses on capsule endoscopy indications, surveillance strategies, and management of dysplastic FGPs.
  • Aims to provide clear, actionable guidance for physicians.

Small Bowel Evaluation in FAP
  • Capsule endoscopy (CE) is recommended for specific FAP patient groups.
  • Not indicated for routine screening in all FAP patients.
  • Small bowel visualization (CE / CT/MR enterography) considered for advanced duodenal polyposis.
Capsule Endoscopy (CE) Indications
  • Advanced duodenal polyposis: Spigelman stage III or IV.
  • Consideration for duodenal surgery.
  • History of duodenal/ampullary cancer.
Rationale for CE
  • Higher prevalence of jejunal/ileal polyps in advanced duodenal disease.
  • Impacts surgical planning or management decisions.
Guidelines & Recommendations
  • National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) and American Society for Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ASGE) advise consideration.
  • CE most strongly considered in Spigelman stage III/IV disease or prior to duodenectomy.
Surveillance Intervals
  • Higher-risk groups: 2–4 years.
Clinical Relevance of Distal Small Bowel Polyps
  • Uncertain: most are small, rarely progress to malignancy.
  • Routine surveillance in lower-risk FAP patients: not supported by current evidence.
Emerging Evidence: Genotype-Based Surveillance
  • Certain APC gene variants (codons 278, 1062–1504) → associated w/ ↑ small bowel polyp burden.
  • Genotype-based surveillance: not yet standard practice.
Jejunal and Ileal Polyps in FAP
  • Common, esp. in patients w/ advanced duodenal polyposis.
  • Malignant potential is low.
  • Routine surveillance not recommended for all FAP patients.
Prevalence
  • Increases w/ advanced duodenal disease (Spigelman stage III/IV).
  • Jejunal adenomas: up to 75% prevalence.
  • Ileal adenomas: up to 20% prevalence.
Malignant Potential & Characteristics
  • Most polyps: small (<1 cm), lack dysplasia, rarely progress to malignancy.
  • Advanced lesions (>1 cm or w/ high-grade dysplasia): uncommon, typically found in proximal jejunum of patients w/ advanced duodenal disease.
  • Lifetime risk of small bowel cancer in FAP: ≈5%, most cancers arise in the duodenum.
Management
  • Endoscopic resection: polyps >1 cm, typically using double-balloon or single-balloon enteroscopy.
  • Surgical intervention: rarely required, endoscopic management usually feasible.
Surveillance
  • High-risk patients: ongoing surveillance every 2–4 years.
  • Low-risk patients: do not require routine small bowel surveillance.
Impact of Intervention
  • Current evidence: does not show that ↓ jejunal/ileal polyp burden improves cancer outcomes.
  • Most polyps: clinically insignificant.
  • Impact of surveillance/intervention on long-term cancer risk: unproven; further prospective studies needed.
Fundic Gland Polyps (FGPs) in FAP
  • Highly prevalent in FAP patients (almost 90%).
  • Dysplasia is common (≈50% of FGPs may be dysplastic).
  • Low rate of progression to high-grade dysplasia or adenocarcinoma.
Malignancy Risk
  • Overall lifetime risk of gastric cancer in FAP patients: <1%.
Recommended Management (Low-Grade Dysplasia)
  • Endoscopic resection: polyps ≥10 mm.
  • Representative sampling: smaller polyps to confirm histology.
  • Routine surveillance: advised for low-grade dysplasia w/o high-risk features.
  • General approach: observe and biopsy gastric polyps to monitor for adenomatous changes or high-grade dysplasia.
Surveillance Intervals (Low-Grade Dysplasia)
  • FGPs w/ low-grade dysplasia <1 cm: every 3 years.
  • FGPs w/ low-grade dysplasia ≥1 cm OR if not all large polyps removed in a single procedure: annually.
Surgical Indications
  • Reserved for: multifocal high-grade dysplasia or invasive cancer.
  • Not for: isolated low-grade dysplasia.
  • Referral to expert center: recommended if lesions cannot be managed endoscopically.
High-Risk Endoscopic Features
  • Low-grade dysplasia alone does not require specialized surveillance/surgical intervention unless accompanied by:
  • Carpeting
  • Mounds ≥20 mm
  • Solitary polyps >10 mm
  • White mucosal patches.
Upper GI Surveillance (General FAP Context)
  • Routine upper endoscopy: every 1 to 3 years, starting @ age 18 to 25 years.
  • Primary surveillance target: duodenal/periampullary region.
  • Duodenal/periampullary region: carries much higher lifetime cancer risk (4–12%), major cause of mortality after colectomy.
