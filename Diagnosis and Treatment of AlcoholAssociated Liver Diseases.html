Diagnosis and Treatment of Alcohol‐Associated Liver Diseases

Prevalence & Burden of ALD
  • - ALD is a major cause of liver disease worldwide, both alone and as a co-factor.
  • - It is a leading indication for liver transplantation (LT) in the US, competing with chronic HCV.
  • - Mortality from alcohol-associated cirrhosis (AC) is increasing, especially in younger patients aged 25-34 years.
Statistics
  • - US Prevalence: Cirrhotic and noncirrhotic ALD is ~2% in the general population.
  • - Global Impact: AC deaths account for ~10% of all alcohol-attributable deaths, causing the loss of 22.2 million disability-adjusted life years annually.

Challenges in Assessment
  • - Prevalence is often underestimated due to stigma and difficulty identifying early, asymptomatic stages.
  • - Studies often exclude patients with concomitant liver diseases (e.g., NASH, HCV), where ALD rates can be as high as 61%.
  • - This may lead to a 2-fold underestimate of ALD-related mortality.


Diagnosis of Alcohol Use Disorders (AUD)
  • - The term "Alcohol Use Disorder" has replaced "alcohol abuse" and "dependence".
  • - It is categorized as mild, moderate, or severe based on symptom count (See Table 1).
DSM-5 Criteria for AUD
  • - A diagnosis of AUD requires at least 2 of 11 symptoms in the past year.
  • - Mild: 2-3 symptoms
  • - Moderate: 4-5 symptoms
  • - Severe: 6 or more symptoms

Screening, Brief Intervention, & Referral (SBIRT)
  • - A nonjudgmental, open interview style is crucial to limit underreporting.
  • - Guidance: All patients in primary care, GI/hepatology clinics, EDs, and inpatient settings should be screened for alcohol use with validated questionnaires.
Screening Tools
  • - NIAAA One-Question Screen: "How many times in the past year have you had 5 or more drinks in a day (men) or 4 or more (women)?"
  • - A single positive response ---> perform full AUDIT.
AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Inventory Test)
  • - 10-question tool assessing consumption, dependence, and problems.
  • - Score > 8 ---> predictive of harmful/hazardous use.
  • - Score > 20 ---> suggestive of moderate/severe AUD (dependence).

AUDIT-C
  • - A brief, 3-question version focusing on consumption.
  • - Performs better than CAGE questionnaire.
  • - Score of at least 4-5 may indicate harmful alcohol use.




Biomarkers of Alcohol Use
  • - Used to aid diagnosis and support recovery, not to "catch" patients.
  • - Guidance: Urine/hair EtG, urine EtS, and PEth are preferred as they are not affected by liver disease.
Traditional Markers (Limited Specificity)
  • - GGT: Sensitive but not specific for alcohol use.
  • - CDT (%CDT): Half-life of 2-3 weeks. Limited by low sensitivity (25-50%) and false positives in severe liver disease.

Direct Metabolites (Preferred)
  • - These are more specific for recent alcohol consumption.
Ethyl Glucuronide (EtG) & Ethyl Sulfate (EtS)
  • - Measured in urine, blood, or hair.
  • - Urine Detection Time: Up to 3 days.
  • - Urine EtG Performance: Sensitivity 76-89%, Specificity 93-99%.
  • - Hair EtG: Detects use over months but is costly and has limited availability.
  • - Caution: Detection time is prolonged in renal failure.

Phosphatidylethanol (PEth)
  • - Measured in blood (erythrocyte membrane).
  • - Half-life: 10-14 days, longer with chronic heavy use.
  • - Not influenced by age, BMI, sex, or kidney/liver disease.
  • - Performance (cutoff >20 ng/mL): Sensitivity 100%, Specificity 96% in pre/post-LT patients.



Treatment of Alcohol Use Disorders (AUD)
  • - Abstinence is the single most important factor for improving survival in ALD.
  • - Guidance: Multidisciplinary, integrated management of ALD and AUD is recommended and improves abstinence rates.
Psychosocial & Behavioral Approaches
  • - Includes CBT, motivational interviewing (MET), 12-step facilitation, etc.
  • - Integrating AUD treatment providers with medical providers in the liver clinic produces better abstinence rates than referral to an outside provider.

Relapse Prevention Medications
  • - Guidance: Based on limited data, acamprosate or baclofen can be considered for AUD treatment in patients with ALD.
FDA-Approved Medications
  • - Naltrexone: Opioid antagonist. Hepatotoxicity concerns; not studied in ALD.
  • - Acamprosate: NMDA receptor antagonist. No hepatic metabolism, appears safe but not studied in ALD.
  • - Disulfiram: Not recommended for use in patients with ALD.

Non-FDA Approved / Off-Label Use
  • - Baclofen: GABA-B agonist. The only AUD pharmacotherapy tested in an RCT in patients with AC.
  • - A 12-week course (10 mg TID) improved abstinence and decreased relapse.
  • - Caution: May impair mentation; patients with hepatic encephalopathy were excluded from the trial.
  • - Gabapentin, Topiramate: Not studied in ALD; require close monitoring.



Pathophysiology & Risk Factors
  • - Only a minority of heavy drinkers develop significant liver disease.
  • - Pathophysiology involves fat accumulation, increased gut permeability ---> endotoxin release ---> innate immune activation, inflammation, and fibrosis.
Alcohol Consumption Thresholds
  • - Safe Limits (General Population): No more than 1 standard drink/day for women, 2 for men.
  • - Binge Drinking Definition (NIAAA): A pattern that brings BAC to 0.08 g/dL. Typically after 4 drinks for women and 5 for men in about 2 hours.
  • - Guidance: Patients with ALD or other liver diseases should be counseled that there is no safe level of drinking and they should abstain.

Key Risk Factors (See Table 5)
  • - Dose & Pattern: Daily drinking and binge drinking confer greater risk.
  • - Sex: Women have a greater risk of liver injury than men for any level of drinking.
  • - Smoking: Independently increases cirrhosis risk.
  • - Obesity/Metabolic Syndrome: Important risk factors for alcohol-associated liver injury.
  • - Comorbidities: Greatly accelerates fibrosis in HCV, NAFLD, and hemochromatosis.
Genetic Susceptibility
  • - Heritability of AUD is about 50%.
  • - Risk Alleles: Polymorphisms in PNPLA3, TM6SF2, and MBOAT7 are associated with increased risk of AC and AH.
  • - Protective Allele: A polymorphism in HSD17B13 confers protection against progression from steatosis to steatohepatitis.


Protective Factors
  • - Coffee consumption protects against cirrhosis of many causes, including ALD.


Diagnosis of ALD Subtypes
  • - ALD presentation is often nonspecific and can be clinically indistinguishable from other liver diseases.
  • - A high index of suspicion for AUD is required.
Alcohol-Associated Steatosis
  • - Usually asymptomatic, may have a palpably enlarged liver.
  • - Readily identified on imaging (US, CT, MRI).
  • - Reversible with cessation of alcohol use.

Alcohol-Associated Cirrhosis (AC)
  • - Often diagnosed at time of decompensation.
  • - Prognosis assessed with CPT and MELD/MELD-Na scores.
  • - Outcome is crucially influenced by the patient's ability to abstain.

Alcoholic Hepatitis (AH)
  • - A clinical syndrome with a histological correlate (alcoholic steatohepatitis, ASH).
  • - Spectrum ranges from asymptomatic to acute-on-chronic liver failure.
Clinical Diagnosis
  • - Guidance: Diagnosis should use the 2016 consensus criteria (See Fig. 2).
  • - Definite AH: Biopsy-proven.
  • - Probable AH: Clinical criteria met (heavy alcohol use, recent onset jaundice, AST>ALT but <400, bilirubin >3) with no confounding factors.
  • - Possible AH: Atypical features; requires biopsy for confirmation.

Role of Liver Biopsy
  • - Used to resolve diagnostic dilemmas and for clinical trial enrollment.
  • - Cannot distinguish ASH from NASH histologically.

Assessing Prognosis in AH
  • - Several validated scoring systems predict short-term mortality and guide treatment.
Prognostic Scores (at admission)
  • - Guidance: Use lab-based prognostic scores to determine prognosis in AH.
Maddrey Discriminant Function (MDF)
  • - Identifies patients with high short-term mortality risk.
  • - MDF ≥ 32 ---> Severe AH, high mortality (30-50% at 28 days), consider corticosteroids.

MELD Score
  • - Emphasizes impaired renal function.
  • - MELD ≥ 20 ---> Also prompts consideration of steroid treatment.

Glasgow Alcoholic Hepatitis Score (GAHS)
  • - Refines identification of patients who will benefit from steroids.
  • - GAHS ≥ 9 (in a patient with MDF ≥ 32) ---> Supports corticosteroid use.


Dynamic Score (Response to Therapy)
  • - Guidance: The Lille score should be used to reassess prognosis and guide treatment after 7 days of corticosteroids.
Lille Score
  • - Calculated at day 7 of steroid therapy, incorporates change in bilirubin.
  • - Lille score > 0.45 ---> Predicts non-response and poor prognosis.
  • - Action: Supports cessation of corticosteroids and consideration of other options (clinical trial, early LT).


Other Prognostic Factors
  • - Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): A major negative prognostic factor.
  • - Infection: Common (12-26% at admission) and associated with high mortality.
  • - Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS): Predicts multiorgan failure and early death.




Treatment of Alcoholic Hepatitis (AH)
  • - Management depends on severity, assessed by prognostic scores.
General Management
  • - Abstinence: Key to long-term survival.
  • - Nutritional Therapy: Patients are typically malnourished. Enteral nutrition is recommended.
  • - A daily intake of < 21.5 kcal/kg/day was associated with increased infection and mortality.

Treatment for Severe AH (MDF ≥ 32)
  • - Focuses on reducing short-term mortality from severe liver inflammation.
Corticosteroids
  • - Guidance: Prednisolone 40 mg/day orally should be considered to improve 28-day mortality.
  • - Meta-analysis shows a 36% risk reduction in 28-day mortality.
  • - The STOPAH trial showed a non-significant trend toward benefit at 28 days, but post-hoc analysis was positive.
Contraindications
  • - Active uncontrolled infection
  • - Uncontrolled GI bleeding (can be given after bleeding is controlled)
  • - Severe acute kidney injury (if resolved, can reconsider steroids)
  • - See Figure 3 for full assessment pathway.


N-Acetylcysteine (NAC)
  • - Guidance: The addition of intravenous NAC to prednisolone may improve 30-day survival.
  • - One RCT showed co-administration with steroids reduced infection, HRS, and improved 1-month mortality (8% vs 24%).

Treatments of Unlikely Benefit
  • - Guidance: Pentoxifylline is no longer recommended.
  • - Multiple trials, including the large STOPAH trial, failed to show a survival benefit.
  • - TNF-α inhibitors (infliximab, etanercept) were terminated early due to infection-related mortality.



Liver Transplantation (LT) for ALD
  • - ALD is now a leading indication for LT in the United States.
Referral and Candidate Selection
  • - Guidance: Refer for LT evaluation with new onset decompensation, SBP, HCC, or MELD-Na ≥ 21.
  • - Guidance: Candidate selection should not be based solely on a fixed interval of abstinence.
The "6-Month Rule"
  • - A traditional requirement of 6 months of abstinence before listing.
  • - Rationale was to allow for liver recovery and assess commitment to abstinence.
  • - Now considered an inadequate predictor of post-LT relapse and penalizes patients who may not survive 6 months.

Psychosocial Assessment
  • - Crucial for predicting post-LT abstinence.
  • - Favorable factors: social support, stable home/work, insight into AUD.
  • - Negative factors: failed rehab attempts, comorbid psychiatric disorders.


Outcomes of LT for ALD
  • - Liver allograft and recipient survival are among the highest of all indications for LT.
  • - Return to any drinking occurs in 20-25% of recipients in the first 5 years.
  • - Severe relapse can lead to recurrent fibrosis and cirrhosis in the allograft in as little as 5 years.

Early LT for Severe AH
  • - An emerging indication for patients with severe AH not responding to medical therapy, who have a 70% 6-month mortality.
  • - Guidance: LT may be considered in carefully selected patients with favorable psychosocial profiles.
Evidence & Selection
  • - Seminal French/Belgian study showed dramatically improved survival (77% vs 23% at 6 months) compared to controls.
  • - Key Selection Criterion: Severe AH as the first liver-decompensating event.
  • - Patients undergo rigorous psychosocial screening to identify low relapse risk.
SALT Score
  • - A predictive model to identify patients at low risk for sustained alcohol use post-LT.
  • - Variables: >10 drinks/day, multiple rehab attempts, legal issues, illicit substance use.
  • - A score of < 5 had a 95% negative predictive value for sustained alcohol use.


Outcomes
  • - A large US multicenter study confirmed high survival rates (94% at 1 year, 84% at 3 years).
  • - Cumulative incidence of sustained alcohol use was 10% at 1 year and 17% at 3 years.



Suggestions for Future Research
  • - Improve prevalence assessment for early-stage ALD using noninvasive tools.
  • - Study efficacy of AUD treatments specifically in the ALD population.
  • - Develop and test new medical agents for AH targeting inflammation, gut microbiota, and fibrosis.
  • - Conduct prospective studies on early LT for severe AH, focusing on patient selection and post-LT AUD management.
