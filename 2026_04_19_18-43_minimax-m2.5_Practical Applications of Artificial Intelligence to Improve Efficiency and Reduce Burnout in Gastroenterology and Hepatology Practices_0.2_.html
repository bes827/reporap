Practical Applications of Artificial Intelligence to Improve Efficiency and Reduce Burnout in Gastroenterology and Hepatology Practices
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology, Vol. 121 (2026), pp. 818–821
  • Topic: AI applications in gastroenterology/hepatology for improving practice efficiency and reducing provider burnout
  • Author(s): Hersh Shroff, MD, MPA; Oren K. Fix, MD, MSc (University of North Carolina)
1. Introduction and Background
1.1 AI Adoption in Healthcare
  • Physician use of AI nearly doubled from 2023 to 2024 (AMA data)
  • Growing majority of physicians are enthusiastic about AI's potential
  • Top clinician priorities: ↑ work efficiency, ↓ stress and burnout
1.2 GI/Hepatology Specific Challenges
  • Gastroenterologists experience high rates of burnout
  • Providers spend an additional 45–50 minutes of EHR time per hour of scheduled patient care
  • AI tools (including LLMs) now available through EHR systems or HIPAA-compliant servers
2. Outpatient Clinic Applications
2.1 Ambient Listening Technology for Documentation
  • Uses AI-powered voice recognition to transcribe and summarize patient-provider interactions
  • Creates structured clinical notes from encounters
  • Companies offering ambient AI scribes: Microsoft Dragon Ambient Experience Copilot, Abridge, Oracle
  • Available through EHR vendor partnerships
2.1.1 Study Results on Documentation Efficiency
  • Prospective pre-post quality improvement study showed:
  • ↓ time writing notes by 20.4% (from 10.3 to 8.2 minutes, p<0.001)
  • ↓ after-hours work by 30.0% (from 50.6 to 35.4 minutes, p=0.02)
2.2 AI for Previsit Preparation
  • Record review and history taking automation
  • Pilot study in otolaryngology used GPT-4 (HIPAA-secure) to review new patient records
  • LLM answered 10 prewritten questions about patient's history
  • 95% agreement with human reviewers
2.2.1 Hepatology Previsit Chatbot
  • University of California San Diego used LLM-based chatbot in academic hepatology practice
  • Chatbot reviewed existing patient records and engaged in real-time question-asking
  • Generated structured encounter note for provider use
  • Physician ratings (5-point Likert scale):
  • Documentation quality: 5.0
  • Assessment accuracy: 4.7
  • Management recommendations: 4.6
3. Endoscopy Applications
3.1 AI for Procedure Documentation
  • Typical endoscopy day involves numerous tasks where AI can ↑ efficiency:
  • Procedure documentation
  • Patient discharge
  • Charting equipment
  • Recording quality metrics
3.2 Image and Video Recognition Tools
  • Convolutional neural networks can identify tools used during endoscopy
  • Can track withdrawal times automatically
3.2.1 FUJIFILM AR-C1 Tool Study
  • Uses machine learning and convolutional neural networks
  • Automatically identifies: instruments used, insertion time, withdrawal time, cecal intubation, total examination times
  • Proceduralists still manually indicate lesion location and size
  • ↓ median report creation time from 245.5 to 164.0 seconds (p<0.05)
  • Study did not assess report accuracy
3.3 Future Endoscopy Integration
  • Epic's Lumens planning to integrate ambient listening for endoscopy note generation
  • Likely future: complete procedure note generated by AI → simple review and verification by endoscopist
4. Administrative and Clerical Tasks
4.1 Patient Messages and Test Results
  • Prominent current use of generative AI in healthcare
  • Embedded LLMs in EHR vendors (e.g., Epic's partnership with OpenAI)
  • AI generates draft responses to patient messages
  • Can ↑ physician task load, ↓ work exhaustion, improve message communication style
4.1.1 Usability Variability
  • Provider-rated usability as low as 20% in one study
4.2 LLM for Patient Question Answering
  • General purpose LLMs (e.g., ChatGPT) investigated for answering common patient questions
  • Tested for GI conditions: metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD)
  • Contextualized LLMs provided with clinical guideline documents for additional training
  • Piloted for determining appropriate colonoscopy surveillance intervals
4.2.1 OpenEvidence (OE) Platform
  • Available to all HCPs
  • HIPAA-compliant LLM trained on leading medical literature
  • Use cases:
  • Developing evidence-based answers to clinical questions
  • Drafting letters to patients
  • Generating patient education handouts
4.3 Quality Improvement and Metrics
  • Many quality measures can be reported to CMS for national benchmarking
  • Used for performance-based payment adjustments for Medicare patients
  • Current metrics center around endoscopy:
  • Adenoma detection rate (ADR)
  • Colonoscope withdrawal time
  • Adherence to recommended surveillance intervals
4.3.1 NLP for Quality Metrics Extraction
  • Natural language processing algorithms extract quality metrics from procedure and pathology reports:
  • ADR
  • Bowel preparation adequacy
  • Cecal intubation rates
  • Withdrawal times
  • Recommended surveillance intervals
5. Future Directions and Limitations
5.1 Patient Perspectives
  • Patients are crucial stakeholders → patient perspectives on generative AI must not be overlooked
  • Blinded patients preferred AI-generated responses significantly more than human responses
  • Preference decreased only slightly after AI use was disclosed
  • Conclusion: Disclosure can maintain patient autonomy without significantly sacrificing benefits
5.2 Practical Considerations for Adoption
  • Data privacy and security laws vary across regions and countries
  • Complex web of overlapping requirements with nuanced differences
  • Model generalizability impacted by context and data used for development
5.2.1 Mitigating Generalizability Issues
  • Data transparency
  • Federated learning
  • Local fine-tuning under institutional governance
5.3 Provider Engagement
  • Ground-level providers must remain actively engaged
  • Individual GI/hepatology providers best positioned to:
  • Apply intimate understanding of daily workflows and pain points
  • Generate novel use cases
  • Adapt general AI tools through LLM fine-tuning and retrieval-augmented generation
5.4 Current Availability Status
  • Only limited use cases readily available to practicing gastroenterologists/hepatologists (see Table 1)
  • Most widely available:
  • EHR-based ambient AI scribes
  • AI-assisted responses to patient messages
  • OpenEvidence available to all practitioners → rapidly changing physician access to medical knowledge
  • Some EHRs: ADR automatically detected from pathology reports
  • Remainder limited to pilot or experimental use cases
6. Risks and Ethical Concerns
6.1 Security and Privacy Risks
  • Risk: Using protected health information (PHI) with AI
  • Mitigation strategies:
  • Appropriate staff training for data protection compliance
  • Clear institutional policies on appropriate AI tool use
  • Development of HIPAA-secure versions of AI tools
6.2 Model-Generated Inaccuracies (Hallucinations)
  • Mitigation strategies:
  • Ensure human-in-the-loop oversight and verification
  • Conduct noninferiority evaluations comparing AI to humans
  • Use prompt libraries
  • Implement LLM guardrails
6.3 Algorithmic Bias
  • Risk: Under-represented groups in training data
  • Mitigation strategies:
  • Use diverse data representation across disease states, demographics, and healthcare settings
  • Conduct fairness audits
  • Continuously monitor models for performance and safety
6.4 Automation Bias and Over-Reliance
  • Mitigation strategies:
  • Train providers to critically appraise AI
  • Understand legal and ethical risks of using AI for patient care
  • Embed AI as decision support (not decision making)
  • Ensure regulatory compliance and clear protocols for handling patient safety concerns
6.5 Nondisclosure Concerns
  • Risk: Compromising patient autonomy and self-determination
  • Mitigation strategies:
  • Consider transparent disclosures of AI use
  • Disclosures should emphasize AI's role to support rather than replace providers
6.6 Sustainability and Model Drift
  • Mitigation strategies:
  • Regularly retrain models with updated data
  • Sunset or revise outdated models
7. Conclusion
  • AI has transformative potential in healthcare with many applications in GI/hepatology
  • Field characterized by: high patient complexity, heavy documentation requirements, concerning provider burnout rates
  • AI tools positioned to dramatically ↑ efficiency of daily workflows → tangible improvements in provider satisfaction and patient care
  • Ongoing exploration of novel use cases within GI/hepatology → anticipate ongoing adoption of specialty-specific tools on larger scales
