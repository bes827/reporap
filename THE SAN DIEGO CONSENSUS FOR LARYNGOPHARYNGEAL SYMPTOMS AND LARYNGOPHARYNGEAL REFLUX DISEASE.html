DEFINITIONS & TERMINOLOGY
  • - A modern paradigm to differentiate presenting symptoms from a confirmed diagnosis.
Laryngopharyngeal Symptoms (LPS)
  • - Refers to chronic and frequent throat and upper airway symptoms with the potential to be induced by reflux.
  • - The term LPS does not implicate reflux as the etiology.
Criteria
  • - Frequent: Occurring at least twice weekly.
  • - Chronic: Present for more than 8 weeks.

Core Symptoms
  • - Cough (89% agreement)
  • - Regurgitation (89% agreement)
  • - Throat pain/sore throat (89% agreement)
  • - Throat clearing (86% agreement)
  • - Excess phlegm (82% agreement)
  • - Hoarseness/voice change (80% agreement)

Associated Symptoms (Not LPS)
  • - Globus: Did not reach agreement for inclusion as an LPS, but is an important symptom warranting evaluation.
  • - Often a negative predictor of reflux physiology.

Key Distinction
  • - The presence of LPS does not equate to LPRD.
  • - LPS can occur with or without esophageal reflux symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain).


Laryngopharyngeal Reflux Disease (LPRD)
  • - A diagnosis of LPRD requires BOTH:
  • - 1. Chronic troublesome LPS
  • - 2. Objective evidence supporting a relationship between symptoms and gastroesophageal reflux.
Prevalence
  • - While LPS is common, true LPRD is much less common.
  • - The proportion of individuals with LPS found to have objective LPRD is low, approximately 35%.

Objective Evidence Sources
  • - Reflux metrics on esophageal testing (ambulatory reflux monitoring).
  • - Positive response to a pharmacologic trial (only in the setting of concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms).
  • - Endoscopic findings (e.g., erosive esophagitis).

Clinical Impact
  • - Health care costs of LPR are reported to be 5.4 times higher than for GERD.
  • - Significant negative impact on quality of life, with higher rates of anxiety and depression.



DIAGNOSTIC EVALUATION OF LPS
  • - A structured approach to differentiate LPRD from other causes of LPS.
1. Symptom Characterization
  • - First step is to determine if LPS is isolated or occurs with concurrent esophageal reflux symptoms (heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain).
  • - This distinction dictates the subsequent diagnostic and management algorithm.

2. Laryngoscopy
  • - A necessary step in evaluating LPS.
Primary Role
  • - To rule out alternate laryngeal pathologies, including neoplasia.
  • - To assess for other non-reflux related otolaryngologic processes.

Limitations
  • - LPRD cannot be diagnosed based on laryngoscopy findings alone.
  • - Findings like edema, mucosal hypertrophy, and erythema are non-specific and can be found in healthy individuals.

Videostroboscopy
  • - Should be performed if hoarseness is not explained by white light laryngoscopy to evaluate for other etiologies.

Scoring Instruments
  • - Instruments like the Reflux Finding Score (RFS) and Reflux Sign Assessment (RSA) should not be used to diagnose LPRD due to suboptimal specificity.


3. Medical Anti-Reflux Trial
  • - Role depends on symptom presentation.
LPS with Esophageal Symptoms
  • - A trial of medical anti-reflux management is appropriate.
  • - Should be combined with dietary and lifestyle modification.
Regimen
  • - PPI: Twice daily (e.g., omeprazole 20 mg BID) for 3 months is considered adequate.
  • - Alginates: Can be used in conjunction with PPIs (e.g., four times daily).


Isolated LPS
  • - Empiric medical trials are not recommended as first-line therapy.
  • - Esophageal evaluation (reflux testing) is recommended to personalize management.


4. Esophageal Evaluation
  • - Performed for isolated LPS or when empiric trials fail in patients with concurrent esophageal symptoms.
Upper GI Endoscopy
  • - Can establish or raise suspicion of GERD's contribution to LPS. Should be performed off acid suppression.
Conclusive Evidence of GERD
  • - Los Angeles (LA) grades B, C, or D esophagitis.
  • - Biopsy-proven Barrettâ€™s esophagus.

Supportive Evidence
  • - Disrupted esophagogastric junction (e.g., hiatal hernia).

Transnasal Esophagoscopy (TNE)
  • - A convenient, office-based screening tool for esophageal signs of reflux.
  • - Requires providers with specialized training.

Cervical Inlet Patch
  • - Area of heterotopic gastric mucosa in the proximal esophagus.
  • - Association with LPS and globus is noted, but evidence for treatment (ablation) is limited.


Ambulatory Reflux Monitoring
  • - The reference standard for diagnosing LPRD in the absence of conclusive endoscopic evidence.
  • - Required prior to any invasive anti-reflux management (endoscopic or surgical).
  • - Should be performed off acid suppression in patients with unproven GERD.
Key Cutoffs (Distal Esophagus)
  • - Proven GERD: Acid Exposure Time (AET) > 6.0%.
  • - Physiologic Reflux: AET < 4.0%, especially if present on all 4 days of a 96-hour study, predicts a low likelihood of response to anti-secretory therapy.

pH-Impedance Monitoring (24h)
  • - Best for: Investigating mechanisms, especially in isolated LPS.
  • - Detects proximal, weakly-acidic, and non-acidic reflux events.
  • - Hypopharyngeal-esophageal (HEMII-pH) catheters can identify pharyngeal reflux events missed by standard probes.

Prolonged Wireless pH Monitoring (96h)
  • - Best for: Guiding anti-reflux therapy, especially with concomitant esophageal symptoms and prior to escalating management.
  • - Captures day-to-day variability, increasing diagnostic accuracy.
  • - Higher Diagnostic Yield for LPS: 50% on prolonged monitoring vs. 27% on 24-h pH impedance.

Oropharyngeal pH Monitoring (e.g., Restech)
  • - Not recommended as a stand-alone tool.
  • - Poor specificity for LPRD and poor concordance with traditional monitoring.




LARYNGEAL HYPERRESPONSE & HYPERVIGILANCE
  • - A critical component of LPS, which may contribute to or be the primary etiology, with or without true LPRD.
Behavioral & Psychological Factors
  • - The larynx can be a site for manifesting emotional distress.
Laryngeal Hyperresponse
  • - Repetitive behaviors like throat clearing or coughing.
  • - Biomechanical strain at the vocal folds causing voice change or globus.

Laryngeal Hypervigilance
  • - Increased threat-induced attention to the larynx.
  • - Often co-exists with symptom-specific anxiety (fear/worry about LPS).

Assessment Tool
  • - The Laryngeal Cognitive Affective Tool (LCAT) is a validated instrument to identify patients with elevated hypervigilance and anxiety who may benefit from behavioral intervention.


Behavioral & Neuromodulatory Therapies
  • - Target the brain-larynx interaction and hypersensitivity.
Laryngeal Behavioral Therapy
  • - Aims to modify dysregulated laryngeal patterns.
Laryngeal Recalibration Therapy (LRT)
  • - Provided by a voice-specialized speech-language pathologist (SLP).
  • - Combines voice therapy, autonomic regulation (biofeedback), and cognitive reframing.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • - Can be used for patients with incomplete relief after LRT.
  • - Targets specific cognitive, emotional, and behavioral processes exacerbating symptoms.


Pharmacologic Neuromodulation
  • - May attenuate laryngeal hypersensitivity.
  • - Considered in LPRD with suboptimal response to anti-reflux therapy or in isolated LPS with negative testing.
Key Agents
  • - Gabapentin and Pregabalin: Particularly studied for chronic cough.
  • - Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): Use with caution due to anticholinergic side effects (e.g., dry mouth) which can aggravate LPS.
  • - SSRIs/SNRIs: Can be considered for individuals with concomitant anxiety/depression.




PATIENT REPORTED OUTCOMES (PROs) & RISK TOOLS
  • - Current tools have limitations for diagnosing LPRD.
PRO Instruments
  • - Should be used to follow symptoms over time, not for diagnosis.
GERD-Specific (GERDQ, GERD-HRQL)
  • - Useful for assessing esophageal symptoms, but not specific for LPS/LPRD.

LPRD-Specific (RSI, RSS)
  • - Not specific enough for diagnosing LPRD.

General Voice/Cough (VHI, CSI, LCQ)
  • - Have unclear value in diagnosing LPRD.


Risk Stratification Tools
  • - Tools like HAS-BEER and COuGH RefluX have been developed to help direct management.
  • - The panel agreed that more data are needed before they can be fully adopted.


SD-LPRD CLINICAL PARADIGM (SUMMARY)
  • - A modern, inter-disciplinary care model distinguishing symptoms (LPS) from objective disease (LPRD).
Core Principle
  • - LPS (symptoms) â‰  LPRD (disease).
  • - Only LPRD stands to benefit from management of gastroesophageal reflux.

Management Algorithm (See Figure 1)
  • - The approach is personalized based on the presence or absence of esophageal symptoms.
LPS + Concurrent Esophageal Symptoms
  • - Step 1: Lifestyle modification + Empiric trial of acid suppression (PPI BID x 3 months Â± alginates).
  • - Step 2 (Response): If both LPS and esophageal symptoms respond, likely LPRD. Continue management.
  • - Step 3 (No/Partial Response): Proceed to objective esophageal testing and consider a disordered laryngeal behavioral response.

Isolated LPS
  • - Step 1: Critically consider a disordered laryngeal behavioral response (hyperresponsiveness/hypervigilance).
  • - Step 2: Proceed with objective esophageal testing (reflux monitoring) prior to any anti-reflux management to personalize therapy.


Multifactorial Mechanisms
  • - LPS can arise from several pathways, not just direct reflux injury:
  • - Neurogenic: Vagally mediated reflexes.
  • - Inflammatory: Direct tissue exposure to acid, pepsin, bile.
  • - Behavioral: Laryngeal hyperresponsiveness and hypervigilance.
  • - Motility-related: Esophageal dysfunction.
