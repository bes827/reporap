AGA Clinical Practice Guideline on the Prevention and Treatment of Hepatitis B Virus Reactivation in At-Risk Individuals
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2025;168:267–284 | https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2024.11.008
  • Topic: Hepatitis B virus reactivation (HBVr) risk stratification and antiviral prophylaxis
  • Author(s): Faisal S. Ali, Mindie H. Nguyen, Ruben Hernaez, Daniel Q. Huang, Julius Wilder, Alejandro Piscoya, Tracey G. Simon, Yngve Falck-Ytter
1. Definitions and Risk Stratification Framework
  • HBVr defined as: de novo HBV-DNA detection OR ≥10-fold rise from baseline; permissible surrogates: new HBsAg or HBeAg
  • Hepatitis flare due to HBVr = ALT ≥3× baseline (minimum above reference range)
  • Baseline risk of HBVr = reactivation rate in exposed individuals not on prophylaxis
  • Risk categories: low (<1%), moderate (1%–10%), high (>10%)
Key serologic definitions
  • HBsAg-positive: current infection
  • anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative: resolved infection (occult HBV)
  • anti-HBs–positive status does not mitigate reactivation risk
2. Evidence on Antiviral Prophylaxis Efficacy
  • Pooled RR for HBVr with prophylaxis vs monitoring: 0.18 (95% CI, 0.11–0.30) → 82% relative reduction
  • Pooled RR for hepatitis flare: 0.22 (95% CI, 0.10–0.50) → 72% relative reduction
  • Absolute risk reduction scales with baseline risk:
  • High-risk (e.g., 50% baseline): 410 fewer HBVr cases / 1000
  • Moderate-risk (e.g., 5% baseline): 41 fewer HBVr cases / 1000
  • Low-risk (e.g., 0.1% baseline): 1 fewer HBVr case / 1000
Evidence certainty
  • High for HBVr prevention: moderate certainty (⨁⨁⨁/)
  • Hepatitis flare: moderate certainty
  • Chemotherapy interruption & adverse events: very low / low certainty
3. Exposure-Specific Risk Categorization
High-risk exposures (baseline risk >10%)
  • HBsAg-positive patients receiving:
  • Anthracyclines (e.g., doxorubicin)
  • B cell–depleting agents (rituximab, ofatumumab)
  • Anti–TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab)
  • TKIs (e.g., imatinib, sunitinib)
  • Cytokine/integrin inhibitors (ustekinumab, secukinumab)
  • CAR-T therapy
  • Anti-IL6 agents (tocilizumab)
  • JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, baricitinib)
  • TACE for hepatocellular carcinoma
  • HCV co-infection + DAA therapy
Moderate-risk exposures (baseline risk 1%–10%)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive patients receiving:
  • Anthracyclines
  • ICIs (nivolumab, pembrolizumab, ipilimumab)
  • Anti–T-cell therapy (abatacept)
  • TKIs
  • Cytokine/integrin inhibitors
  • CAR-T therapy
  • Anti-IL6 agents
  • JAK inhibitors
  • TACE
  • Corticosteroids: moderate/high dose (≥10–20 mg prednisone eq) for ≥4 weeks
  • HBsAg-positive patients receiving:
  • ICIs, anti–T-cell therapy, JAK inhibitors, TACE → high risk
  • Corticosteroids: low dose (<10 mg) for ≥4 weeks
Low-risk exposures (baseline risk <1%)
  • All serologies receiving:
  • Methotrexate, 6-MP, azathioprine (monotherapy)
  • Intra-articular steroid injection
  • Corticosteroids: low/moderate/high dose for <1 week
  • HBsAg-negative receiving:
  • Anti–TNF agents
  • ICIs
  • HCV co-infection + DAA therapy
4. Recommendations and Implementation Guidance
Recommendation 1: High-risk individuals
  • Strong recommendation for antiviral prophylaxis over monitoring
  • RR reduction: 82%; NNT ≈ 3 at 50% baseline risk
  • Implementation:
  • Use antivirals with high barrier to resistance (e.g., entecavir, tenofovir)
  • Start before immunosuppression; continue ≥6 months post-therapy
  • ≥12 months for B cell–depleting agents (risk of delayed reactivation)
Recommendation 2: Moderate-risk individuals
  • Conditional recommendation for antiviral prophylaxis over monitoring
  • RR reduction: 82%; absolute benefit depends on baseline risk (e.g., 41 fewer / 1000 at 5% baseline)
  • Monitoring protocol if chosen:
  • 1- to 3-month intervals
  • Must include HBV-DNA + ALT
  • Shared decision-making encouraged: prioritize patient values on cost, adherence, long-term drug use
Recommendation 3: Low-risk individuals
  • Conditional recommendation for monitoring alone over prophylaxis
  • Absolute benefit minimal (e.g., 1 fewer / 1000 at 0.1% baseline)
  • Exceptions: consider prophylaxis if:
  • Multiple low-risk agents used concurrently
  • High patient risk aversion despite small benefit
Recommendation 4: Universal HBV Screening
  • Strong recommendation for testing all at-risk individuals
  • Aligns with CDC universal screening: HBsAg, anti-HBs, total anti-HBc for all ≥18 years
  • Stratified screening by HBVr risk no longer needed
  • Testing algorithm:
  • Initial: HBsAg + anti-HBc (minimum)
  • If positive: add anti-HBs and HBV-DNA as indicated
  • Note: Antiviral therapy indicated for active HBV regardless of reactivation risk context

5. Duration of Antiviral Prophylaxis
  • Pooled analysis of 3 RCTs comparing longer vs shorter prophylaxis duration:
  • RR for HBVr: 0.99 (95% CI, 0.76–1.28)
  • RR for hepatitis flare: 1.24 (95% CI, 0.46–3.39)
  • Certainty downgraded to low due to serious imprecision (wide CIs crossing null)
  • Subgroup analysis: 12 vs 6 months post-exposure cessation:
  • RR for HBVr: 1.07 (95% CI, 0.68–1.68)
  • RR for hepatitis flare: 1.35 (95% CI, 0.45–3.99)
  • Panel conclusion:
  • Minimum duration: ≥6 months after stopping risk-imposing exposure
  • Extend to 12 months for high-risk exposures (e.g., B cell–depleting agents, CAR-T)
  • Special case: HCV/DAA co-infection → extend prophylaxis 6–12 months after DAA completion, despite early HBVr clearance during therapy (cumulative incidence >90% post-cessation at 12 weeks)
6. Risk-Specific Baseline Estimates by Exposure Class
Corticosteroid Therapy
  • Highest risk: high-dose (≥20 mg prednisone eq/day) for ≥4 weeks in HBsAg-positive
  • Baseline risk: >10% (high-risk)
  • Moderate-dose (10–20 mg) for ≥4 weeks in HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive:
  • Baseline risk: 1%–10% (moderate-risk)
  • Low-dose (<10 mg) for ≥4 weeks in HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive:
  • Baseline risk: <1% (low-risk)
  • Short-course (<1 week) at any dose: low risk across serologies
  • Intra-articular injection: low risk regardless of serology
  • Evidence limitations: confounding by concomitant immunomodulators (e.g., methotrexate); most data indirect
Anti–TNF Agents
  • Baseline risk in HBsAg-positive: 332 per 1000 (high-risk)
  • Baseline risk in HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: 2 per 1000 (low-risk)
  • Sensitivity analyses: annualized risk ~5–11 per 1000, but nonannualized values reflect worst-case (used for guidance)
  • No reclassification needed: low-risk category retained for HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive
Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICIs)
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk 70 per 1000 (moderate-risk)
  • Upper 95% CI: 160 per 1000 → crosses high-risk threshold (>100/1000)
  • Certainty downgraded to low due to imprecision
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk <0.1% (low-risk)
  • Upper 95% CI: not specified, but panel retained low-risk classification
  • No stratification by ICI type (nivolumab, pembrolizumab, ipilimumab) due to lack of data
Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)/HBV Co-infection + DAA Therapy
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk 2 per 1000 (low-risk)
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk 240 per 1000 (high-risk)
  • One RCT (entecavir vs monitoring):
  • No HBVr during DAA therapy
  • >90% cumulative incidence after entecavir cessation at 12 weeks
  • Panel recommendation: extend prophylaxis 6–12 months post-DAA completion
Cytokine/Integrin Inhibitors (e.g., ustekinumab, secukinumab)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk 13 per 1000 (moderate-risk)
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk 260 per 1000 (high-risk)
  • Studies: psoriasis (4), axial spondyloarthritis (1); follow-up 9–24 months
  • No RCTs for HBsAg-positive on prophylaxis-free arms (clinical practice precludes it)
Anti–IL6 Therapy (Tocilizumab)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk <0.1% (low-risk)
  • Upper 95% CI: 13 per 1000 → crosses moderate-risk threshold
  • Certainty downgraded to low due to imprecision
  • HBsAg-positive: no empirical data (all on prophylaxis in clinical practice)
  • Panel classified as high-risk by biological plausibility
Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors (TKIs)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk <0.1% (low-risk)
  • Upper 95% CI: 22 per 1000 → suggests true risk ~moderate
  • Multiple case reports of HBVr in TKI-treated patients
  • Panel classified as moderate-risk (22/1000) despite point estimate <0.1%
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk 110 per 1000 (high-risk)
Anti–T-Cell Therapy (Abatacept)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk 46 per 1000 (moderate-risk)
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk <0.1% (low-risk)
  • Upper 95% CI: 44 per 1000 → suggests true risk ~moderate
  • Single study (n=34), no events → serious imprecision
  • Panel classified as moderate-risk by biological plausibility
CAR-T Cell Therapy
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk 21 per 1000 (moderate-risk)
  • Confounders: prior B cell–depleting agents (e.g., rituximab), co-interventions
  • Panel acknowledges additive risk from temporally proximate high-risk exposures
  • HBsAg-positive: no prophylaxis-free data; classified high-risk by biological plausibility
Transarterial Chemoembolization (TACE)
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk 180 per 1000 (high-risk)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: no empirical data; classified moderate-risk by biological plausibility
Antimetabolites (Methotrexate, 6-MP, Azathioprine)
  • Baseline risk: <1% (low-risk) for monotherapy
  • No studies reporting HBVr events
  • Biological plausibility: minimal impact on antibody responses
  • Classification is conditional until prospective validation
Janus Kinase (JAK) Inhibitors
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive: baseline risk 18 per 1000 (moderate-risk)
  • HBsAg-positive: baseline risk 333 per 1000 (high-risk)
  • Certainty downgraded to low for both due to imprecision (small samples, all from RA trials)
Gut-Specific Anti–T-Cell Therapy (Vedolizumab)
  • Insufficient evidence: 1 case reported in HBV carrier on vedolizumab + corticosteroids + azathioprine
  • Risk categorization not possible pending further data

  • Willingness-to-pay threshold: US$45,662/QALY

8. Antiviral Prophylaxis Safety Profile and Harms
  • Overall undesirable consequences considered small or trivial in benefit–harm balance
  • Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF):
  • Concerns: renal dysfunction, ↓ bone mineral density
  • Overall effect: small to trivial
  • Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF):
  • No adverse impact on renal function or bone density vs TDF
  • Insurance coverage: generally covered, but out-of-pocket costs may act as barrier to adherence
9. Role of Hepatitis B Surface Antibody (anti-HBs) Status
  • Pooled proportion of anti-HBs–positive patients in control arms of RCTs: 65% (95% CI, 58%–72%)
  • Meta-analysis of RCTs + nonrandomized studies (rituximab cohort):
  • Baseline risk for anti-HBs–positive individuals: 6.6%
  • Upper 95% CI: 14.4%
  • Panel conclusion: anti-HBs positivity does not mitigate HBVr risk
  • Therefore, anti-HBs status should not guide prophylaxis decisions
10. Knowledge Gaps and Future Research Priorities
  • Critical gaps:
  • Need for prospective registries with standardized HBV serology, clear HBVr definition, and large nonselective cohorts
  • Uncertainty in risk categorization for newer agents (e.g., gut-specific anti–T cell agents, novel JAK inhibitors) due to lack of data
  • Long-term reactivation risk beyond 12 months post-exposure cessation (e.g., CAR-T, B cell–depleting agents)
  • Technological opportunities:
  • Online repository updated periodically with baseline risk estimates by exposure class
  • Integration into clinical decision support tools (e.g., EHR-based alerts)
  • Need for formal cost-effectiveness analyses stratified by risk category (low, moderate, high)
  • Priority areas for future RCTs:
  • Duration optimization (e.g., 6 vs 12 months) in specific high-risk groups
  • Head-to-head comparisons of antivirals (e.g., TAF vs entecavir) in prophylaxis
  • Real-world adherence and outcomes with monitoring-only strategies
11. Limitations of Current Evidence
  • Serious imprecision in baseline risk estimates for several exposures:
  • ICIs (HBsAg-positive): upper 95% CI crosses high-risk threshold
  • Anti-IL6 (HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive): upper 95% CI crosses moderate-risk threshold
  • TKI (HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive): no events, upper CI suggests moderate risk
  • Abatacept (HBsAg-positive): very small sample (n=34), CI crosses risk thresholds
  • Indirect evidence for corticosteroids: confounding by concomitant immunomodulators
  • Lack of RCTs for many exposures (e.g., vedolizumab, CAR-T in HBsAg-positive without prophylaxis)
  • Publication bias: potential underreporting of low-event-rate HBVr cases
  • Heterogeneity in HBVr definition:
  • Some RCTs use absolute HBV-DNA cutoff (e.g., ≥2000 IU/mL)
  • Some define hepatitis flare as ALT >100 IU/L (vs guideline’s ≥3× baseline)
  • Panel accepted deviations if clinically reasonable
12. Implementation Considerations and Clinical Decision Support
  • Clinical decision support tool referenced in guideline (Figure 2):
  • Integrates serologic status + exposure type → risk category → recommendation
  • Key implementation principles:
  • Universal HBV screening (per CDC) eliminates need for risk-stratified screening
  • Antiviral choice: high barrier to resistance (entecavir, tenofovir)
  • Monitoring protocol: mandatory HBV-DNA + ALT at 1–3 month intervals if prophylaxis deferred
  • Shared decision-making triggers:
  • Moderate-risk patients: discuss tradeoffs between prophylaxis burden vs reactivation risk
  • Low-risk patients: emphasize minimal absolute benefit but acknowledge individual risk aversion
  • Special populations:
  • HCV/HBV co-infection: extend prophylaxis beyond DAA course (6–12 months post-DAA)
  • CAR-T or B cell–depleting therapy: extend prophylaxis to 12 months
  • Corticosteroid bursts (<1 week): low risk regardless of serology; no prophylaxis needed
  • Follow-up triggers:
  • If HBV-DNA rises ≥10-fold or new HBsAg: initiate antivirals immediately
  • If ALT ≥3× baseline + detectable HBV-DNA: treat as hepatitis flare due to HBVr
  • Documentation requirements:
  • Baseline serology (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs) before immunosuppression
  • Baseline HBV-DNA if anti-HBc–positive or HBsAg-positive
  • Plan for monitoring schedule or prophylaxis duration documented at initiation
13. Guideline Development Methodology and Evidence Grading
  • GRADE framework applied throughout:
  • Risk of bias assessed via Cochrane RoB 2.0 for RCTs
  • Certainty of evidence rated across domains: bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias
  • Evidence profiles generated for: HBVr, hepatitis flare, chemotherapy interruption, adverse events
  • Pooled relative risks computed using inverse-variance random-effects model (STATA)
  • Baseline risk estimates from control arms of RCTs or observational studies meeting criteria:
  • Consecutive at-risk cohort not on prophylaxis
  • Defined HBVr/hepatitis flare criteria aligned with panel consensus
  • Reported follow-up duration
  • Meta-analysis of proportions: Freeman-Tukey double arcsine transformation for variance stabilization (fixed-effects model)
  • Class effect assumption: risk estimates applied to entire drug class, not just studied agents
  • Panel consensus process: Evidence-to-Decision framework used to weigh:
  • Certainty of evidence
  • Balance of desirable/undesirable effects
  • Patient values and preferences
  • Feasibility, acceptability, cost-effectiveness, equity
