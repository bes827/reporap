AGA Clinical Practice Guideline on the Prevention and Treatment of Hepatitis B Virus Reactivation in At-Risk Individuals
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2025;168:267–284
  • Topic: Hepatitis B virus reactivation (HBVr) risk stratification and antiviral prophylaxis
  • Author(s): Faisal S. Ali, Mindie H. Nguyen, Ruben Hernaez, Daniel Q. Huang, Julius Wilder, Alejandro Piscoya, Tracey G. Simon, Yngve Falck-Ytter
1. Definitions and Risk Stratification Framework
  • HBVr defined as: de novo HBV-DNA detection OR ≥10-fold ↑ in HBV-DNA vs baseline
  • Permissible surrogates: new HBsAg or HBeAg detection
  • Hepatitis flare due to HBVr = ALT ≥3× baseline (minimum above reference range)
  • Baseline risk of HBVr = reactivation rate in exposed individuals not on prophylaxis
  • Risk categories:
  • Low risk: <1% baseline HBVr
  • Moderate risk: 1%–10% baseline HBVr
  • High risk: >10% baseline HBVr
  • Risk categorization based on:
  • Serologic status (HBsAg ± anti-HBc)
  • Immunosuppressive exposure type, dose, duration
  • Risk estimates derived from: RCT control arms (for high-certainty exposures) or observational studies (with strict inclusion criteria)
2. Evidence on Antiviral Prophylaxis Efficacy
  • Pooled analysis of 12 RCTs (n = 879):
  • Antiviral prophylaxis → RR 0.18 `[0.11–0.30]` for HBVr vs monitoring
  • Absolute risk ↓: 410 fewer HBVr cases / 1000 at 50% baseline risk
  • Pooled analysis of 9 RCTs (n = 624):
  • Antiviral prophylaxis → RR 0.22 `[0.10–0.50]` for hepatitis flare
  • Absolute risk ↓: 390 fewer flares / 1000 at 50% baseline risk
  • Chemotherapy interruption: RR 0.31 `[0.05–1.84]` (very low certainty)
  • Adverse events: RR 1.08 `[0.47–2.49]` (low certainty)
  • High barrier-to-resistance antivirals assumed for all recommendations
  • Duration: ≥6 months after stopping most immunosuppressants; ≥12 months for B-cell–depleting agents
3. Exposure-Specific Risk Categorization and Recommendations
High-Risk Exposures (>10% baseline HBVr)
  • HBsAg-positive individuals:
  • Anthracyclines (e.g., doxorubicin)
  • B-cell–depleting agents (rituximab, ofatumumab)
  • Anti–TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab)
  • CAR-T therapy
  • Cytokine/integrin inhibitors (ustekinumab, secukinumab)
  • Anti-IL6 agents (tocilizumab)
  • JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, baricitinib)
  • TACE
  • HCV co-infection + DAA therapy
  • Recommendation: Strong recommendation for antiviral prophylaxis
Moderate-Risk Exposures (1%–10% baseline HBVr)
  • HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive individuals:
  • Anthracyclines
  • ICIs (nivolumab, pembrolizumab, ipilimumab)
  • Anti–T-cell therapy (abatacept)
  • TKIs (imatinib, sunitinib)
  • Cytokine/integrin inhibitors
  • CAR-T therapy
  • Anti-IL6 agents
  • JAK inhibitors
  • TACE
  • Corticosteroids: moderate/high dose × ≥4 weeks
  • Recommendation: Conditional recommendation for antiviral prophylaxis; monitoring acceptable if patient values prioritize avoiding long-term therapy
Low-Risk Exposures (<1% baseline HBVr)
  • All serologic groups:
  • Methotrexate, 6-MP, azathioprine (monotherapy)
  • Intra-articular steroid injection
  • Corticosteroids: low dose (<10 mg prednisone eq) × ≥4 weeks (HBsAg-negative only)
  • Corticosteroids: low/moderate/high dose × <1 week (all groups)
  • Anti–TNF agents (HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive)
  • ICIs (HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive)
  • HCV co-infection + DAA (HBsAg-negative/anti-HBc–positive)
  • Recommendation: Conditional recommendation for monitoring alone; antiviral prophylaxis acceptable if patient values prioritize avoiding small reactivation risk
4. Screening, Testing, and Implementation
  • Universal HBV screening recommended for all adults ≥18 y (CDC 2023):
  • Test for HBsAg, anti-HBs, total anti-HBc
  • Stratified screening by HBVr risk no longer needed due to universal CDC guidance
  • Testing algorithm:
  • Initial: HBsAg + anti-HBc (minimum)
  • If positive: add anti-HBs and HBV-DNA
  • Monitoring protocol for those on antiviral prophylaxis omission:
  • Every 1–3 months
  • Must include HBV-DNA and ALT
  • Special considerations:
  • Anti-HBs positivity does not mitigate HBVr risk (pooled proportion anti-HBs+ in controls = 65% `[58%–72%]`)
  • Vedolizumab: insufficient evidence for risk categorization (1 case reported w/ concomitant steroids + azathioprine)
  • DAA therapy in HBsAg-positive/HCV co-infection: extend antiviral prophylaxis to 6–12 months after DAA completion (per 1 RCT showing >90% HBVr post-entecavir cessation at 12 weeks)
5. Duration of Antiviral Prophylaxis
  • Pooled analysis of 3 RCTs comparing longer vs shorter prophylaxis duration:
  • RR for HBVr = 0.99 `[0.76–1.28]`
  • RR for hepatitis flare = 1.24 `[0.46–3.39]`
  • Certainty downgraded to low due to very serious imprecision
  • Subgroup analysis: 12 months vs 6 months continuation post-exposure cessation:
  • RR for HBVr = 1.07 `[0.68–1.68]`
  • RR for hepatitis flare = 1.35 `[0.45–3.99]`
  • Panel conclusion:
  • Minimum duration = ≥6 months after stopping most immunosuppressants
  • Extension to 12 months reasonable for high-risk exposures
  • ≥12 months mandatory for B-cell–depleting agents (e.g., rituximab) due to delayed HBVr cases beyond 12 months
6. Cost-Effectiveness and Equity Considerations
Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)
  • No direct CEAs for low/moderate/high risk categories identified
  • Fujita et al CEA (moderate-risk exposure baseline risk = 6%):
  • Incremental cost-effectiveness ratio (ICER) = US$132,048 / QALY
  • Willingness-to-pay threshold = US$45,662 (converted from Japanese yen)
  • Model limitation: assumed HBVr risk with prophylaxis = 2.1%
  • Corrected estimate: 2.1% × (1 – 0.82) = 0.378%
  • Lower probability → lower ICER likely falling within $50,000–100,000 / QALY acceptable range
  • Extrapolation:
  • Antiviral prophylaxis likely cost-effective for high-risk cases
  • May be cost-effective for moderate-risk after model correction
  • Likely not cost-effective for low-risk cases (trivial benefit, ongoing drug costs)
  • Panel conclusion: Formal CEAs stratified by risk category needed
Equity Considerations
  • Additional Health Disparities and Minority Health Search Strategy conducted
  • No potential equity issues identified from guideline recommendations
  • Antivirals covered by most insurance plans, but out-of-pocket costs may act as barrier
  • Tenofovir alafenamide preferred over tenofovir disoproxil fumarate due to:
  • No adverse impact on renal function
  • No adverse impact on bone mineral density
7. Serologic Modifiers of HBVr Risk
Anti-HBs Status and Risk Mitigation
  • 4 RCTs reported anti-HBs status in control arms: pooled proportion anti-HBs+ = 65% `[58%–72%]`
  • Combined meta-analysis (RCTs + nonrandomized studies) of rituximab-exposed patients:
  • Baseline HBVr risk for anti-HBs+ individuals = 6.6%
  • Upper bound of 95% CI = 14.4%
  • Panel conclusion: anti-HBs positivity does not mitigate HBVr risk
  • Anti-HBs status should not be used to guide prophylaxis decisions
Serologic Risk Modulation by Exposure Type
  • HBsAg-positive vs anti-HBc–positive/HBsAg-negative:
  • Baseline risk consistently higher in HBsAg+ across all exposures (e.g., anti–TNF: 332/1000 vs 2/1000)
  • Key exceptions with uncertainty:
  • Abatacept in HBsAg+ individuals: observed risk <0.1% but upper 95% CI = 44/1000 → panel classified as moderate-risk based on biological plausibility
  • CAR-T in HBsAg+ individuals: no direct data → panel classified as high-risk based on background immunosuppression and co-therapies
  • Vedolizumab: insufficient evidence (1 case w/ concomitant immunomodulators)
8. Knowledge Gaps and Future Research Priorities
Evidence Deficiencies Identified
  • High uncertainty risk categories:
  • Anti-HBs status not used for risk stratification despite high prevalence in controls
  • Vedolizumab: single HBVr case w/ confounding immunomodulators
  • Anti-IL6 in HBsAg+ individuals: no unprophylaxed cohort data
  • TKI in anti-HBc+ individuals: observed risk <0.1% but upper 95% CI = 22/1000
  • Duration uncertainty:
  • No RCTs directly comparing optimal prophylaxis duration across exposure classes
  • Current recommendations extrapolated from limited RCTs (3 total)
Recommended Research Priorities
  • Maintain national registries with:
  • Baseline HBV serologies (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs)
  • Clear HBVr definition (viral load + ALT criteria)
  • Nonselective cohort enrollment
  • Develop online repository updated periodically with:
  • Exposure-specific baseline HBVr estimates
  • Risk modification by serology, dose, duration
  • Prioritize studies on:
  • Long-term antiviral prophylaxis duration (e.g., >12 months for high-risk)
  • Cost-effectiveness stratified by risk category
  • Equity-focused implementation research (e.g., access barriers to antivirals)
  • Address gaps in:
  • Gut-specific anti–T cell agents (vedolizumab)
  • Novel immunotherapies (e.g., bispecific antibodies, novel CAR-T constructs)
  • Pediatric populations (current evidence adult-only)
9. Clinical Decision Support and Implementation Tools
Recommendation Summary Table (Adapted from Table 4)
  • Recommendation 1: For individuals at high risk of HBVr, the AGA recommends antiviral prophylaxis over monitoring alone
  • Strong recommendation, moderate certainty evidence
  • Antivirals must have high barrier to resistance (e.g., entecavir, tenofovir alafenamide)
  • Initiate before immunosuppression; continue ≥6 months post-therapy (≥12 months for B-cell–depleting agents)
  • Recommendation 2: For individuals at moderate risk of HBVr, the AGA suggests antiviral prophylaxis over monitoring alone
  • Conditional recommendation, moderate certainty evidence
  • Monitoring acceptable if patient values prioritize avoiding long-term antiviral use and cost, provided adherence to 1–3 monthly monitoring (HBV-DNA + ALT)
  • Recommendation 3: For individuals at low risk of HBVr, the AGA suggests monitoring alone over antiviral prophylaxis
  • Conditional recommendation, moderate certainty evidence
  • Antiviral prophylaxis acceptable if patient values prioritize avoiding small reactivation risk (e.g., multiple low-risk immunosuppressants)
  • Recommendation 4: For individuals at potential risk of HBVr, the AGA recommends testing for hepatitis B
  • Strong recommendation, moderate certainty evidence
  • Universal CDC screening (HBsAg, anti-HBs, total anti-HBc) for all adults ≥18 y obviates risk-stratified screening
  • Initial test: HBsAg + anti-HBc (minimum); if positive, add anti-HBs and HBV-DNA
Clinical Decision Support Tool (Figure 2 Summary)
  • Algorithm flow:
  • Step 1: Confirm HBV serologic status (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs)
  • Step 2: Identify immunosuppressive exposure (see Tables 5–6)
  • Step 3: Assign risk category based on exposure × serology matrix (Table 6)
  • Step 4: Apply recommendation (1–3) with shared decision-making where conditional
  • Risk matrix highlights:
  • High-risk combinations: HBsAg+ × any immunosuppressant (except low-dose steroids <1 week)
  • Moderate-risk combinations: HBsAg–/anti-HBc+ × moderate/high-dose steroids ≥4 weeks, ICIs, TKIs, CAR-T
  • Low-risk combinations: anti-HBc– × any exposure; low-dose steroids <1 week or ≥4 weeks (HBsAg– only)
  • Special cases:
  • Corticosteroid dose/duration interplay:
  • High-dose (>20 mg prednisone eq) × ≥4 weeks → high-risk if HBsAg+, moderate-risk if HBsAg–/anti-HBc+
  • Moderate-dose (10–20 mg) × ≥4 weeks → moderate-risk for both serologies
  • Low-dose (<10 mg) × ≥4 weeks → moderate-risk if HBsAg+/anti-HBc–, low-risk if HBsAg–
  • Any dose × <1 week → low-risk for all serologies
  • Combination immunosuppression: Risk may exceed single-agent estimates (e.g., rituximab + corticosteroids)
  • HCV co-infection: DAA therapy unmasked HBVr in occult infection → risk depends on baseline HBsAg status
10. Grading of Evidence and Certainty Assessment
GRADE Framework Application
  • Certainty assessment domains:
  • Risk of bias (Cochrane RoB 2.0 for RCTs)
  • Inconsistency (I² statistic + visual forest plots)
  • Indirectness (population, intervention, outcome mismatches)
  • Imprecision (95% CI crossing clinically important thresholds: 1% and 10%)
  • Publication bias (funnel plots + Egger test not feasible due to few studies)
  • Large effect estimate (e.g., RR <0.5 for benefits)
  • Evidence profiles generated per outcome (Tables 2–3):
  • HBVr: moderate certainty (⨁⨁⨁/)
  • Downgraded for imprecision in high-risk estimates (e.g., ICIs: upper 95% CI crossed 10% threshold)
  • Hepatitis flare: moderate certainty (⨁⨁⨁/)
  • Fragility Index = 5 (indicates fragile result)
  • Chemotherapy interruption: very low certainty (⨁///)
  • CI crossed clinically important threshold (RR 0.31 `[0.05–1.84]`)
  • Adverse events: low certainty (⨁⨁//)
  • Wide CI including both harm and benefit (RR 1.08 `[0.47–2.49]`)
Baseline Risk Estimation Methodology
  • RCT-derived baseline risks:
  • Pooled using inverse-variance random-effects model (STATA)
  • Applied to varying baseline risks to compute absolute risk differences (Table 5)
  • Observational study-derived baseline risks:
  • Inclusion criteria:
  • Consecutive at-risk cohort not on prophylaxis
  • Appropriate baseline serology (HBsAg, anti-HBc)
  • HBVr definition aligned with panel criteria
  • Follow-up duration covering exposure period or 1 year
  • Excluded: case series without denominator, case reports, case-control studies
  • Analysis: inverse-variance fixed-effects model + Freeman-Tukey double arcsine transformation
  • Class effect assumption: Risk estimates applied to entire drug class (e.g., TKI class risk used for all TKIs)
  • Uncertainty handling:
  • When 95% CI crosses risk thresholds → downgraded certainty (Figure 1)
  • Biological plausibility used to override statistical imprecision when evidence insufficient (e.g., abatacept in HBsAg+)
11. Limitations and Future Updates
Documented Limitations
  • Evidence gaps:
  • No RCTs for most novel immunotherapies (e.g., bispecific antibodies, emerging CAR-T targets)
  • Pediatric populations excluded from all included studies
  • Limited data on HBVr with gut-specific anti–T cell agents (vedolizumab)
  • Methodological constraints:
  • Baseline risk estimates for moderate/low-risk categories often derived from observational data with residual confounding
  • Duration of prophylaxis recommendations extrapolated from limited RCTs (3 studies)
  • Cost-effectiveness analysis based on outdated assumptions (unadjusted HBVr risk in prophylaxis arm)
  • Clinical heterogeneity:
  • Variable HBVr definitions across studies (e.g., absolute HBV-DNA >2000 IU/mL vs 10-fold ↑)
  • Heterogeneous antiviral regimens (entecavir vs lamivudine vs tenofovir)
  • Differing follow-up durations (1–10 years in anti–TNF studies)
Guideline Update Protocol
  • Scheduled review: 5 years from publication (2030)
  • Trigger-based updates:
  • New high-quality RCTs for novel immunotherapies (e.g., JAK inhibitors in IBD)
  • Emergence of new risk-modifying factors (e.g., anti-HBs titer thresholds)
  • Changes in CDC screening guidelines or antiviral formulary (e.g., generic tenofovir alafenamide availability)
  • Continuous evidence monitoring:
  • Annual literature search using updated search strategy (Supplementary Table 1)
  • Health disparities and minority health search strategy integrated into annual update cycle
12. Implementation Considerations for Frontline Practitioners
Serologic Testing Workflow
  • Step 1: Universal screening (all adults ≥18 y):
  • Test HBsAg, anti-HBs, total anti-HBc (per CDC 2023)
  • Step 2: Reflex testing:
  • If HBsAg+ → order HBV-DNA and liver ultrasound
  • If anti-HBc+ (regardless of HBsAg) → order anti-HBs and HBV-DNA
  • If all negative → no further testing unless high-risk exposure (e.g., stem cell transplant)
  • Step 3: Risk categorization:
  • Cross-reference exposure (drug, procedure) with serology in Table 6
  • Adjust for dose/duration (e.g., corticosteroids: low/moderate/high × <1 week vs ≥4 weeks)
  • Consider combination immunosuppression (e.g., methotrexate + anti–TNF → ↑ risk beyond monotherapy estimates)
