Disorders of the Stomach and Duodenum
  • Source: MKSAP 19 - Gastroenterology and Hepatology
  • Topic: Disorders of the Stomach and Duodenum
  • Author(s): American College of Physicians
1. Dyspepsia
1.1. Clinical Features
  • Dyspepsia = term describing epigastric pain
  • Associated symptoms:
  • Early satiety
  • Epigastric burning
  • Nausea
  • Postprandial symptoms: pain, fullness, bloating, belching
  • Heartburn may be present → indicates coexisting GERD
  • Recurrent vomiting → raises concern for other conditions
1.2. Organic Causes of Dyspepsia
  • Most common: Peptic ulcer disease and GERD
  • 20% of patients with erosive esophagitis report dyspepsia
  • If no structural/organic/metabolic cause identified → functional dyspepsia
1.3. Functional Dyspepsia Categories
  • Postprandial distress syndrome: predominant symptoms are meal-induced
  • Epigastric pain syndrome: predominant symptoms are epigastric pain and/or burning
1.4. Evaluation and Management
  • Testing based on: patient age, symptom severity, risk for gastric cancer
  • Upper endoscopy:
  • Recommended for patients age ≥60 years (ACG/CAG guidelines)
  • NOT recommended for patients <60 years even with alarm features
  • Alarm features: weight loss, anemia, dysphagia, vomiting → poorly predict structural pathology
1.4.1. Special Considerations for Younger Patients
  • Consider endoscopy in younger patients with:
  • Multiple or severe alarm features
  • Emigration from high-prevalence areas (Asia, Russia, South America)
  • Positive family history of gastric malignancy
1.4.2. Testing for Patients <60 Years
  • Noninvasive testing for H. pylori recommended
  • Other testing (routine labs, celiac screening, abdominal imaging, gastric emptying) → individual basis
1.4.3. Treatment Approach
  • H. pylori positive → eradication therapy
  • H. pylori negative OR eradication fails → empiric trial of PPI
  • Functional dyspepsia → variety of interventions available
1.5. Key Points
  • Routine upper endoscopy for malignancy → reserved for age ≥60 years
  • Upper endoscopy NOT necessary in most patients <60 years even with alarm symptoms
  • Patients <60 years with dyspepsia → test and treat for H. pylori
2. Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)
2.1. Clinical Features
  • Most common symptom: epigastric pain
  • Worse during fasting
  • Improved with eating, antacid, or antisecretory therapy
  • Associated symptoms: early satiety, abdominal bloating, nausea, belching, heartburn
  • Minimal/absent pain in: elderly patients, immunosuppressed patients, long-term NSAID users
2.2. Causes of PUD
  • Most common: NSAIDs and H. pylori infection
  • Idiopathic PUD (NSAID-negative, H. pylori–negative) → increasingly common as H. pylori incidence falls
  • Rare causes:
  • Gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison syndrome)
  • Systemic mastocytosis
  • α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • COPD, chronic kidney disease
  • Gastric cancer, gastric lymphoma
  • Crohn disease, eosinophilic gastroenteritis
  • Herpes simplex virus, cytomegalovirus (immunocompromised)
2.3. Diagnosis and Complications
  • Most often diagnosed by upper endoscopy
  • Complications:
  • Bleeding: overt (melena, hematemesis, hematochezia) or obscure (iron deficiency anemia, occult blood positive)
  • Perforation: sudden severe epigastric pain → generalized + peritoneal findings
  • CT diagnostic: sensitivity 98%
  • Obstruction: vomiting, early satiety, abdominal distention, weight loss
2.4. Management of Uncomplicated PUD
  • Once-daily PPI initiation
  • Discontinue PUD-causing agent (e.g., NSAID)
  • Treat H. pylori if identified → confirm eradication after treatment
  • If acute testing negative → repeat after discharge
2.5. Management of Bleeding PUD
  • Upper endoscopy within 24 hours
  • Risk factors for recurrent bleeding/death:
  • Tachycardia, hypotension
  • Age >60 years
  • Hemoglobin <7 g/dL (70 g/L) or <8 g/dL (80 g/L) with cardiovascular disease
  • Comorbid illness
  • Interventions for modifiable risks before endoscopy
  • Nasogastric tube NOT required before endoscopy
  • PPI before endoscopy → improves endoscopic appearance, reduces need for therapy
2.5.1. Aspirin Management in Bleeding PUD
  • For secondary cardiovascular prevention → restart aspirin 1 to 7 days after PPI initiation + bleeding cessation
  • Can discontinue PPI after H. pylori eradication confirmed
2.5.2. NSAID Management in Bleeding PUD
  • Discontinue NSAIDs
  • If no alternative → selective COX-2 inhibitor + once-daily PPI
2.5.3. Idiopathic Ulcers
  • Once-daily PPI therapy indefinitely (substantial rebleeding risk)
2.6. Repeat Endoscopy
  • Reserved for patients who:
  • Have persistent symptoms after 8 to 12 weeks of therapy
  • Have ulcers of unknown cause
  • Did NOT undergo gastric ulcer biopsy during initial endoscopy
2.7. Perforated PUD
  • Surgical emergency
  • Mortality rate: up to 30%
  • Risk factors: older age, comorbidity, delayed surgery
  • Prompt identification + urgent surgical intervention + sepsis management essential
  • Address cause (H. pylori, NSAIDs)
  • Postoperative endoscopy to rule out gastric cancer
2.8. Gastric Outlet Obstruction
  • Biopsy at diagnosis to exclude malignancy
  • Mild symptoms → PPI therapy, H. pylori treatment, NSAID cessation
  • Severe/persistent symptoms → endoscopic dilation
  • Surgery if obstruction persists after repeated dilation attempts
2.9. Key Points
  • Most common causes: NSAIDs and H. pylori
  • Aspirin for secondary prevention → restart 1 to 7 days after PPI + bleeding cessation
  • NSAID-induced bleeding → discontinue NSAID or use selective COX-2 inhibitor + PPI
  • Idiopathic ulcers that bled → once-daily PPI indefinitely
  • Repeat endoscopy: persistent symptoms 8-12 weeks, unknown cause, or no initial biopsy
3. Helicobacter pylori Infection
3.1. Indications for Testing
  • Active PUD
  • History of PUD without documented H. pylori cure
  • Gastric MALT lymphoma
  • History of endoscopic resection of early gastric cancer
3.1.1. Consider Testing For
  • Dyspepsia
  • Before long-term low-dose aspirin or NSAID use
  • Unexplained iron deficiency anemia
  • Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura in adults
3.1.2. NOT Indicated For
  • Typical GERD symptoms
  • Lymphocytic gastritis
  • Hyperplastic gastric polyps
  • Hyperemesis gravidarum
  • Asymptomatic individuals with family history of gastric cancer
3.2. Diagnosis
  • Invasive: gastric biopsies during upper endoscopy
  • Noninvasive:
  • 13C-urea breath test → preferred (sensitivity/specificity >95%)
  • Stool antigen test (monoclonal) → preferred
  • Serologic testing (IgG): cheapest, convenient, but CANNOT distinguish active vs. previous infection
3.2.1. Serologic Testing Considerations
  • Negative serology → reasonably high negative predictive value
  • Positive serology in low-prevalence population (U.S.) → may require confirmation with breath test, stool antigen, or biopsy
  • Most useful as adjunct in bleeding PUD (decreased biopsy sensitivity in acute bleeding)
3.2.2. False-Negative Risk Factors
  • PPI use, recent antibiotic use
  • Atrophic gastritis, intestinal metaplasia
  • MALT lymphoma
3.3. Treatment
  • Any patient with positive test → requires treatment for eradication
  • First course = best chance of eradication → choose carefully
  • Duration: 14 days in most cases
3.3.1. Regimen Selection
  • Based on: resistance patterns, previous antibiotic use, antibiotic allergies
  • Treatment fails → second-line therapy minimum 14 days
  • Use different antibiotics from initial treatment
3.4. Eradication Testing
  • Timing: no sooner than 4 weeks after completion of therapy + PPI cessation (if possible)
  • Methods: 13C-urea breath test, fecal antigen test, or gastric biopsy
  • Serologic testing NOT used for confirmation (remains positive after eradication)
3.5. Key Points
  • Diagnosis: gastric biopsies OR noninvasive testing (13C-urea breath, stool antigen)
  • Treatment: ≥3 agents (2 antimicrobials + 1 antisecretory), 14 days
  • Eradication testing: 13C-urea breath, fecal antigen, or biopsy ≥4 weeks post-therapy
4. Miscellaneous Gastropathy
4.1. Erosive Gastropathy
  • Causes: NSAIDs, iron, alcohol, ischemia, portal hypertension
  • May cause GI bleeding → rarely clinically significant
4.2. Atrophic Gastritis
  • Environmental metaplastic atrophic gastritis (multifocal): result of H. pylori infection → lessens after eradication
  • Autoimmune atrophic gastritis: autoantibody to parietal cell antigens → associated with pernicious anemia
4.2.1. Complications of Autoimmune Atrophic Gastritis
  • Achlorhydria → leads to:
  • Iron deficiency
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth
  • Enteric infections
  • Hypergastrinemia → promotes gastric neuroendocrine tumors
4.2.2. Management
  • Prevention: vitamin B12 supplementation, iron replacement
  • Surveillance for gastric neoplasm
  • Screening upper endoscopy with gastric biopsy recommended for pernicious anemia
4.3. Gastric Intestinal Metaplasia
  • Preneoplastic gastropathy → from chronic inflammation (usually H. pylori)
  • Test for H. pylori → treat if positive
  • Other causes: other gastric infections, chemical agents, autoimmune disease
  • NOT promoted by long-term PPI use (no conclusive evidence)
  • Intermediary stage: chronic atrophic gastritis → gastric adenocarcinoma
4.3.1. Management
  • If secondary to H. pylori → eradication therapy may decrease progression to cancer
  • NOT recommended: routine surveillance endoscopy (U.S. rarity)
  • Consider surveillance for increased risk:
  • Complete or extensive metaplasia on endoscopy
  • Family history of gastric cancer
  • Certain racial/ethnic minorities
  • Emigration from East Asia, Russia, South America
4.4. Eosinophilic Gastritis
  • Rare: infiltration of eosinophils in gastric mucosa
  • Exclude secondary causes first:
  • Parasitic/bacterial stomach infections
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Hypereosinophilic syndrome
  • Myeloproliferative disorders
  • Polyarteritis nodosa, allergic vasculitis, scleroderma
  • Drug injury, drug hypersensitivity
  • Primary cause unknown → believed allergic
  • Treatment: food allergen avoidance, elemental diets, glucocorticoids
4.5. Lymphocytic Gastritis
  • Rare: nonspecific dyspeptic symptoms, normal endoscopy appearance
  • Sometimes: thickened folds with small nodules, aphthous ulceration
  • Most common cause: celiac disease
  • Other causes: HIV, Crohn disease, common variable immunodeficiency, H. pylori (rare)
4.6. Key Points
  • Autoimmune atrophic gastritis → pernicious anemia, iron deficiency, SIBO, gastric carcinoid
  • Management: vitamin B12 + iron replacement, neoplasm surveillance
  • H. pylori associated with: environmental metaplastic atrophic gastritis, gastric intestinal metaplasia, lymphocytic gastritis (rare)
5. Gastrointestinal Complications of NSAIDs
5.1. Epidemiology and Risk Factors
  • Dose-dependent complications (short or long-term)
  • Linear increase in incidence over time
  • Long-term NSAID use: 1-2% clinically significant upper GI event annually
  • Elderly users: 14% rate of upper GI adverse events
  • Greatest risk factor: history of gastrointestinal bleeding
5.2. Low-Dose Aspirin Complications
  • 2-4x increased risk for upper GI complications (bleeding ulcer, perforation, obstruction)
  • Enteric-coated aspirin → does NOT lower risk
  • ↑ Dosage → ↑ risk
5.3. Prevention of NSAID-Induced Injury
  • Avoid NSAIDs
  • Address modifiable risk factors
  • Coadminister gastroprotective agents: PPIs, misoprostol, or H2-blockers
  • Use selective COX-2 inhibitor
5.3.1. PPI as Preferred Agent
  • PPIs preferred for treatment and prophylaxis of NSAID-related (including aspirin-related) complications
  • Superior to H2-blockers for preventing PUD and bleeding from low-dose aspirin
5.3.2. Misoprostol
  • Alternative to PPIs if intolerant/unwilling to take PPIs
  • Side effects: diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, nausea (can be limiting)
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy
5.3.3. Selective COX-2 Inhibitors
  • Decreased GI toxicity vs. NSAIDs
  • NO advantage over NSAID when coadministered with aspirin
  • At-risk individuals need gastroprotective therapy
5.3.4. High-Risk Patients
  • Best protection: selective COX-2 inhibitor + PPI (vs. NSAID + PPI)
  • Lower risk for perforation, obstruction, bleeding
  • Lower risk for NSAID/COX-2 withdrawal due to GI adverse events
5.4. Post-NSAID Bleeding Management
  • Safest: avoid future NSAID use
  • If must use: selective COX-2 inhibitor + PPI = best GI protection to prevent rebleeding
5.5. Key Points
  • Upper GI complications common with NSAIDs (short and long-term) and low-dose aspirin
  • PPIs preferred for preventing/treating NSAID-related complications
  • High-risk patients: selective COX-2 inhibitor + PPI if NSAID avoidance not possible
6. Gastroparesis
6.1. Presentation
  • Syndrome: specific symptoms + no mechanical outlet obstruction + objective evidence of delayed gastric emptying
  • Symptoms (in order of prevalence):
  • Nausea (90%)
  • Vomiting (84%)
  • Upper abdominal pain (72%)
  • Early satiety (60%)
  • Other: abdominal fullness, bloating
  • May be chronic/persistent or intermittent
  • Severe cases: weight loss, malnutrition, dehydration
  • Viral prodrome (gastroenteritis/respiratory infection) → suggests postviral gastroparesis (may resolve over time)
6.2. Diagnostic Testing
  • First study: upper endoscopy → exclude gastric outlet obstruction
  • After structural cause excluded → objective test for gastric emptying:
  • Gastric scintigraphy (standard)
  • Wireless motility capsule
  • Gastric emptying carbon breath test (13C-labeled Spirulina platensis)
6.2.1. Gastric Scintigraphy
  • Standard test for gastroparesis
  • Assess solid meal emptying for ≥3 hours
  • Stop before testing (≥48 hours): opioids, cannabinoids, prokinetics, antiemetics, neuromodulators
6.3. Management
  • Symptom severity does NOT correlate with delayed emptying severity (especially bloating, epigastric pain)
  • Suggests: gastroparesis = motility disorder + altered sensation
6.3.1. Initial Management
  • Correct dehydration and electrolyte abnormalities
  • Nutritional support if needed
  • Dietary intervention: small, frequent meals, low in fat and soluble fiber
  • Referral to dietitian may help
6.3.2. Diabetic Gastroparesis
  • Poor glycemic control (blood glucose >200 mg/dL [11.1 mmol/L]) → worsens symptoms and gastric emptying
  • Tight glycemic control = most important treatment
6.3.3. Prokinetic Therapy
  • Metoclopramide: only FDA-approved prokinetic in U.S.
  • Use lowest effective dose (neurologic side effects risk)
  • Stop immediately if neurologic side effects develop
  • Dystonia, parkinsonian movements, tardive dyskinesia
6.3.4. Other Treatments
  • Erythromycin: improves gastric emptying
  • Limit to treatment of flares or short-term use (2-3 weeks)
  • Risk for tachyphylaxis
  • Antiemetics: relieve symptoms, no effect on gastric emptying
  • Acupuncture alone or with prokinetic → beneficial in diabetic gastroparesis
6.3.5. Pain Management
  • Treat without opioids → use central neuromodulators instead
6.3.6. Interventional Therapy
  • Consider if dietary and pharmacologic therapy fails:
  • Enteral feeding via jejunostomy
  • Gastric stimulator placement
  • Gastric peroral endoscopic myotomy
  • Pyloroplasty
  • Subtotal or total gastrectomy
6.4. Key Points
  • After gastric outlet obstruction excluded → objective test of gastric emptying (typically scintigraphy)
  • Initial management: correct dehydration/electrolytes, nutritional support, small frequent meals (low fat/fiber), glycemic control in diabetes
  • Metoclopramide: improves emptying but risk for dystonia, tardive dyskinesia, parkinsonism
7. Gastric Polyps and Subepithelial Lesions
7.1. Gastric Polyps
  • All polyps should be biopsied for histology
  • Types:
  • Fundic gland polyps: 70-90% of stomach polyps, NO malignant potential, common with PPI use
  • Multiple fundic gland polyps in familial adenomatous polyposis
  • Hyperplastic polyps: 5-19% harbor dysplasia/malignancy
  • Risk factors: size >1 cm, pedunculated morphology
  • Resect if >0.5-1.0 cm
  • Adenomas: sporadic or hereditary (familial adenomatous polyposis, Lynch syndrome)
  • Resect + surveillance: 1 year after resection, then every 3-5 years
7.2. Subepithelial Lesions
7.2.1. Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumors (GISTs)
  • Most common mesenchymal tumors of stomach
  • May present with bleeding, abdominal pain, or found incidentally
  • Best diagnostic modality: endoscopic ultrasonography
High-Risk Features on Endoscopic Ultrasonography
  • Size >2 cm
  • Lobulated or irregular borders
  • Invasion into adjacent structures
  • Heterogeneity
Management
  • Biopsy possible, but high-risk features better predictors of malignant potential
  • Treatment: surgical or endoscopic excision if symptomatic OR high-risk features present
  • No high-risk features → yearly endoscopic surveillance
7.2.2. Gastrointestinal Neuroendocrine Tumors (NETs)
  • Formerly carcinoid tumors
  • Can occur throughout GI tract including stomach
  • Usually sporadic, but seen in:
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome
  • Atrophic gastritis
  • Rare syndromes: multiple endocrine neoplasia type 1, neurofibromatosis type 1
Classification
  • By size, number, anatomic distribution
Management
  • Lesions <1 cm: endoscopic surveillance (especially multiple lesions)
  • High-risk features: solitary lesions NOT in setting of atrophic gastritis or Zollinger-Ellison syndrome → endoscopic or surgical excision
7.3. Key Points
  • Polyps: fundic gland (no malignancy risk), hyperplastic/adenomas (resect)
  • GISTs: evaluate with endoscopic ultrasonography, excise if symptomatic or high-risk features
  • NETs: usually sporadic, but associated with Zollinger-Ellison, atrophic gastritis, MEN1, NF1
8. Gastric Adenocarcinoma
8.1. Epidemiology
  • Incidence: 6.6 per 100,000
  • Mortality: 3.1 per 100,000 (U.S.)
  • Rates decreased since 1990s
8.2. Types
  • Intestinal-type (more common)
  • Diffuse-type
8.3. Risk Factors
  • H. pylori: primary nongenetic risk factor for both types
  • Other risk factors (primarily intestinal-type):
  • Male sex
  • Ethnicity: highest in Asian/Pacific Island descent
  • Geography: highest in Asia, Eastern Europe, Central/South America
  • Diet: high in smoked, salted, pickled foods, nitrates, nitrites
  • Smoking, obesity
  • Additional: previous stomach surgery, chronic atrophic gastritis
  • Hereditary syndromes:
  • Hereditary diffuse gastric cancer (diffuse type)
  • Lynch syndrome
  • Familial adenomatous polyposis
  • Gastric intestinal metaplasia and dysplasia
8.4. Screening and Surveillance
  • No population-based screening in low-incidence countries (U.S.)
  • Intestinal metaplasia with high-grade dysplasia → resect (25% progress to adenocarcinoma)
  • Indicated for: familial adenomatous polyposis, Lynch syndrome
8.5. Clinical Manifestations and Diagnosis
  • Symptoms: poor appetite, weight loss, abdominal pain, early satiety, nausea, vomiting
  • Signs: iron deficiency anemia
  • Diagnosis: upper endoscopy with biopsy
8.6. Key Points
  • Primary nongenetic risk factor: H. pylori
  • Low-incidence countries (U.S.): screening reserved for genetic cancer syndromes
  • Diagnostic test: upper endoscopy with biopsy
9. Gastric Surgery Complications
9.1. Post-Gastrectomy Considerations
  • Extent of resection, reconstruction type, disease nature → affect morbidity/mortality
  • Malignancy: require lifelong surveillance for recurrence in remaining stomach
  • Benign disease: increased cancer risk in gastric remnant 15-20 years after surgery (0.8-8.9%)
9.2. Dumping Syndrome
  • Results from rapid gastric emptying after gastric surgery
9.2.1. Early Dumping Syndrome
  • Occurs within 30 minutes of eating
  • Causes: gastrointestinal hormone hypersecretion, autonomic dysregulation, bowel distention
  • Symptoms:
  • Palpitations, flushing/pallor, diaphoresis
  • Light-headedness, hypotension, fatigue
  • Diarrhea, nausea, abdominal bloating, cramping, borborygmus
9.2.2. Late Dumping Syndrome
  • Occurs 1-3 hours after meals
  • Cause: reactive hypoglycemia
  • Symptoms: decreased concentration, faintness, altered consciousness
  • Severe cases: protein-wasting malnutrition
9.2.3. Prevalence
  • 25-50% of post-gastric surgery patients experience some symptoms
  • Severe, persistent: ~10%
9.2.4. Diagnosis
  • Oral glucose challenge testing
  • Sensitivity: ~100%
  • Specificity: 94%
9.2.5. Treatment
  • First-line: dietary modification
  • Smaller, more frequent meals
  • Ingest liquids AFTER meals
  • Decrease carbohydrate intake (especially simple)
  • Increase protein and fiber
Pharmacologic Therapy
  • Acarbose: α-glycosidase hydrolase inhibitor → helps late symptoms
  • Anticholinergics: slow gastric emptying
  • Antispasmodics
  • Octreotide: for severe cases (rare)
  • If subcutaneous effective → monthly long-acting intramuscular injections
9.3. Key Points
  • Partial gastrectomy for malignancy → lifelong surveillance
  • Partial gastrectomy for benign disease → increased cancer risk in remnant
  • Dumping syndrome: first-line treatment = smaller, more frequent meals + liquids after meals
