Psychology of Rare Events: Terrorism & Lotteries
  • - Human intuition struggles with rare events, often leading to irrational behavior.
  • - The actual probability of an event is often less important than the mere possibility of it.
Terrorism & the Availability Cascade
  • - Example: Suicide bombings in Israel (2001-2004).
  • - Statistically, the risk was tiny: 236 fatalities from 23 bombings, with 1.3 million daily bus riders.
  • - However, public fear led to bus avoidance.
Mechanism of Fear
  • - Terrorism works by inducing an availability cascade.
  • - A vivid image of death, reinforced by media, becomes highly accessible.
  • - This triggers an automatic, emotional System 1 impulse for protective action, even if System 2 knows the probability is low.

Insensitivity to Probability
  • - The emotional response is insensitive to the exact level of probability.
  • - A city warned of two bombers does not feel much less safe than a city warned of one, despite a 50% lower risk.


Lottery Psychology
  • - The psychology of high-prize lotteries is similar to that of terrorism.
  • - The thrilling possibility of winning is reinforced by community conversations.
  • - Buying a ticket is immediately rewarded by pleasant fantasies, making the actual low probability inconsequential.


Overestimation vs. Overweighting
  • - These are two distinct phenomena, but they share psychological mechanisms.
Core Concepts
  • - Overestimation: People judge the probability of unlikely events to be higher than they are.
  • - Overweighting: People give unlikely events more weight in their decisions than they deserve.

Shared Psychological Mechanisms
  • - Focused Attention: Concentrating on an event makes it more salient.
  • - Confirmation Bias: System 1 selectively retrieves evidence that makes a statement true.
  • - Cognitive Ease: Judgments are determined by the fluency with which a plausible scenario comes to mind.
Example: Third-Party President
  • - When asked about the probability of a third-party president winning, your mind seeks a plausible scenario for it to happen, not reasons why it won't.
  • - This confirmatory process can lead to overestimating the probability.


Focusing on the Unusual
  • - When an event is very likely (e.g., a baby being released from hospital in 3 days), our minds spontaneously focus on the alternative (what could go wrong).
  • - This makes the rare, unlikely event (complications) become the focal point, leading to its overestimation.

The Denominator Neglect Bias
  • - The probability of a rare event is most likely overestimated when the alternative is not fully specified.
NBA Playoff Study (Craig Fox)
  • - Basketball fans were asked to estimate the win probability for each of the 8 participating teams.
  • - The sum of probabilities for the 8 teams added up to 240%!
  • - Reason: Each team's victory is a specific, focal event, while its alternative ('one of the other 7 teams winning') is diffuse.
Betting Behavior
  • - Fans were also willing to bet, assigning cash equivalents to a $160 payoff for each team winning.
  • - The sum of cash equivalents was $287, guaranteeing an average loss of $127 if all bets were taken.
  • - This shows they not only overestimated probability but also overweighted it in decisions.


Urn Experiment
  • - Choice: Draw a marble from Urn A (1 red, 9 white) or Urn B (8 red, 92 white).
  • - Correct choice is Urn A (10% chance), but 30-40% of students choose Urn B (8% chance).
  • - This is denominator neglect: attention is drawn to the number of winning marbles (the numerator), not the total number (the denominator).



Impact of Vividness & Framing
  • - A rich, vivid representation of an outcome reduces the role of probability in evaluations.
Vivid Outcomes vs. Probability
  • - Prospect Theory: Decision weights are not linear with probabilities (e.g., 90% chance might have a weight of 71.2, not 90).
  • - Utility Theory: Decision weights and probabilities are the same.
Emotional Outcomes
  • - Valuations are less sensitive to probability for emotional outcomes (e.g., 'kissing a movie star', 'getting an electric shock') than for cash.
  • - The mere possibility of a shock can trigger a full fear response, regardless of the probability.

Rich Representations
  • - The key factor is not emotion, but a rich and vivid representation of the outcome.
  • - Adding irrelevant but vivid details (e.g., 'a large blue cardboard envelope containing $59') disrupts calculation and reduces sensitivity to probability compared to just '$59'.


Vivid Probabilities (Framing Effects)
  • - Different ways of communicating risks have vastly different effects due to denominator neglect.
Frequency vs. Abstract Terms
  • - Low-probability events are weighted more heavily when described in relative frequencies (how many) versus abstract terms (risk, chance).
  • - System 1 is better at dealing with individuals than categories.
Examples of Framing
  • - 'A disease that kills 1,286 people out of every 10,000' is judged more dangerous than 'a disease that kills 24.14% of the population', even though the first risk is about half the second.
  • - 'One of 100,000 vaccinated children will be disabled' is more evocative than 'a 0.001% risk of disability'.


Impact on Professionals
  • - Forensic psychologists evaluated a patient's risk of violence.
  • - When risk was framed as '10% probability', 21% denied discharge.
  • - When framed as 'Of every 100 patients... 10 are estimated to commit an act of violence', 41% denied discharge (almost twice as many).



Choice from Experience vs. Description
  • - The way we learn about probabilities—by description or by personal experience—dramatically changes how we weigh rare events.
Choice from Description
  • - Prospects are described explicitly (e.g., '99% chance to win $1,000, 1% chance to win nothing').
  • - This guarantees separate attention to the rare event.
  • - Leads to overweighting of rare events, consistent with Prospect Theory.

Choice from Experience
  • - We learn outcomes by observing them over many trials (e.g., pressing buttons to get rewards).
  • - This often leads to underweighting of rare events.
Primary Reason for Underweighting
  • - Many participants never experience the rare event during the learning phase.
  • - Examples: Most Californians have never experienced a major earthquake; no banker in 2007 had personally experienced a devastating financial crisis.

Global Impressions (System 1)
  • - Even if experienced, rare events may be absorbed into a 'global impression'.
  • - Example: Choosing between helpful colleague Adele (consistent) and colleague Brian (usually less helpful but rarely extremely generous).
  • - System 1 forms a general impression based on typical behavior, favoring Adele. The rare event is not explicitly considered and is therefore underweighted.



Risk Policies & Framing
  • - Humans are by nature narrow framers, making decisions as problems arise.
  • - A rational agent would use broad framing, considering decisions jointly.
Narrow vs. Broad Framing
  • - Narrow framing: A sequence of two simple decisions, considered separately.
  • - Broad framing: A single comprehensive decision, with all options combined.
The Concurrent Decisions Problem
  • - Decision (i): A) sure gain of $240 vs. B) 25% chance to gain $1000.
  • - Decision (ii): C) sure loss of $750 vs. D) 75% chance to lose $1000.
  • - Most people are risk-averse for gains (choose A) and risk-seeking for losses (choose D). 73% of respondents chose the combination A & D.
The Broad Frame Reveals the Flaw
  • - Combining the choices reveals the true outcomes:
  • - AD (the popular choice): 25% chance to win $240 and 75% chance to lose $760.
  • - BC (the unpopular choice): 25% chance to win $250 and 75% chance to lose $750.
  • - Option BC is clearly superior (dominates) AD, yet most people choose the inferior option due to narrow framing.



Samuelson's Problem & Aggregation
  • - A friend rejects a single coin toss bet (lose $100 or win $200) due to loss aversion.
  • - But he would accept an offer to make 100 such bets.
The Power of Aggregation
  • - Aggregating favorable gambles rapidly reduces the probability of losing.
  • - While one bet is unattractive to a loss-averse person, bundling two makes the combined prospect positive.
  • - The impact of loss aversion diminishes as more gambles are bundled.


Developing a Risk Policy (A Broad Frame)
  • - A risk policy is a broad frame routinely applied to relevant problems, avoiding case-by-case decisions.
The Mantra: 'You Win a Few, You Lose a Few'
  • - Main purpose is to control the emotional response to loss.
  • - Helps to see small gambles as part of a larger bundle.
When to Use the Mantra
  • - Gambles are genuinely independent.
  • - The possible loss does not cause worry about your total wealth.
  • - Does not apply to long shots (very low probability of winning).


Practical Applications
  • - Think like a trader: Blunts emotional reaction to losses and increases risk-taking.
  • - Reduce checking investments: Avoids the pain of frequent small losses. Checking once a quarter is enough for individual investors.
  • - Adopt policies: E.g., 'always take the highest possible deductible' or 'never buy extended warranties'.

Organizational View
  • - A CEO can adopt a broad frame for the company, encouraging division managers (who are naturally narrow-framing and loss-averse) to take on positive-expected-value risks.
  • - The goal is to combine an outside view (for planning) with a risk policy (for risk-taking).
