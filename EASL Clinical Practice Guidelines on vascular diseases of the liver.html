Aetiological Factors in Primary Budd-Chiari Syndrome (BCS) or Portal Vein Thrombosis (PVT) without Cirrhosis
  • - PVT is more common in males (median age 55 years), while BCS shows no sex difference (median age 35-40 years).
  • - Can be classified as provoked (local/systemic factors) or unprovoked.
  • - In 15% to 25% of patients, no predisposing factors are identified.
Risk Factor Work-up
  • - A thorough work-up for local and systemic risk factors should be initiated rapidly after diagnosis (See Table 3).
  • - A local precipitating factor is common in PVT, but not in BCS.
Local Risk Factors
  • - Solid abdominal cancer is present in up to 30% of patients with PVT, especially liver or pancreatic cancer.
  • - Intra-abdominal inflammation/infections are reported in 20-30% of PVT cases.
  • - Abdominal surgery accounts for about 10% of PVT cases.
  • - Local risk factors are infrequent in BCS (about 5%).

Systemic Risk Factors
  • - A comprehensive assessment for systemic risk factors is crucial, especially in the absence of local causes.
Myeloproliferative Neoplasms (MPNs)
  • - A major and permanent risk factor.
  • - Test for JAK2 V617F mutation routinely. If negative, consider next-generation sequencing and bone marrow biopsy.
  • - Polycythemia vera is the most common, followed by essential thrombocythemia.

Inherited Thrombophilia
  • - Routinely test for Factor V Leiden and prothrombin gene mutations.
  • - Diagnosis of protein C, protein S, and antithrombin deficiency can be hampered by liver dysfunction or acute thrombosis.

Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)
  • - A major and permanent risk factor.
  • - Test for lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, and anti-beta2 glycoprotein 1 antibodies.
  • - Testing must be repeated after 12 weeks for confirmation.

Paroxysmal Nocturnal Haemoglobinuria (PNH)
  • - Test routinely in BCS via flow cytometry.
  • - In PVT, test only if signs of intravascular haemolysis or unexplained cytopenia are present.

Other Systemic Factors
  • - Behçet’s disease: Suspect with history of thrombosis and mucocutaneous/ocular/gastrointestinal manifestations.
  • - Hormonal factors: Oestrogen-derived oral contraceptives, hormonal replacement therapy, and pregnancy are transient risk factors.
  • - Infections: SARS-CoV-2 and Cytomegalovirus are reported risk factors for PVT.



Influence of Risk Factors on Therapy
  • - Risk factors impact the choice of anticoagulant, need for concomitant treatments, and duration of therapy.
  • - In MPN, cytoreductive drugs may reduce recurrence risk.
  • - In Behçet’s disease, immunosuppressants are pivotal; anticoagulation alone is ineffective.
  • - In APS (double/triple positive), DOACs are not recommended.

Pediatric Considerations
  • - The same diagnostic work-up as in adults should be performed.
  • - Use age-specific cut-offs for inherited thrombophilia (protein C, S, antithrombin).
  • - Common risk factors for PVT in children include umbilical vein catheters, omphalitis, and abdominal infections/cancer.
  • - Unprovoked events are less common in children (10%) than in adults.


Primary Budd-Chiari Syndrome (BCS)
  • - Definition: Hepatic venous outflow obstruction from small hepatic venules to the junction of the inferior vena cava (IVC) with the right atrium.
  • - Epidemiology: Pooled annual incidence of 1 per million inhabitants and prevalence of 11 per million.
Clinical Presentation
  • - Highly heterogeneous, from asymptomatic to acute liver failure (ALF).
  • - Most common symptoms: ascites (83%), hepatomegaly (67%), and abdominal pain (61%).
  • - ALF is rare (6.1%) but has high inpatient mortality (35-58%).
  • - A distinguishing feature of ALF in BCS is higher AST than ALT levels in 95% of cases.

Diagnosis
  • - BCS should be systematically sought in patients with acute or chronic liver disease.
Imaging Modalities
  • - First-line: Doppler ultrasound. Good sensitivity (0.89) but reasonable specificity (0.68).
  • - Confirmatory: Contrast-enhanced CT or MRI to confirm diagnosis and assess treatment options.
  • - Hepatic venography is recommended if diagnosis remains uncertain.

Liver Biopsy
  • - Recommended for suspected small hepatic vein BCS when imaging shows patent hepatic veins.
  • - Not recommended for radiologically confirmed BCS.


Management Strategy
  • - A stepwise management strategy is recommended for non-fulminant BCS, leading to excellent long-term survival (5-year survival 74-89%).
  • - Patients should be managed in collaboration with expert centers with LT capabilities.
Step 1: Medical Management
  • - Anticoagulation: Initiate as soon as possible and continue indefinitely.
  • - Start with LMWH, followed by Vitamin K Antagonists (VKAs), targeting an INR of 2.0-3.0.
  • - Avoid unfractionated heparin due to high risk of heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (15-28%).
  • - DOACs may be considered in patients with preserved liver function.
  • - Ensure proper variceal prophylaxis to avoid bleeding.

Step 2: Percutaneous Angioplasty
  • - Consider for patients with short-segment stenoses in the IVC or hepatic veins.
  • - Technical success is high, but reinterventions are needed in two-thirds of patients.

Step 3: TIPS
  • - Recommended if medical management is insufficient and angioplasty is not an option or has failed.
  • - Technically challenging; should be performed in experienced centers.
  • - Covered stents are preferred due to lower dysfunction rates compared to bare stents.

Step 4: Liver Transplantation (LT)
  • - Considered for ALF, HCC, or progressive disease not responding to other therapies.
  • - Needed in 13-18% of patients.
  • - In ALF, attempt emergency TIPS while listing for LT.
  • - 10-year patient survival rates are 68-84%.


Prognostic Scores
  • - Currently available scores (Clichy, Rotterdam, MELD) are insufficient for individual patient management (best AUC <0.80).
  • - Can be used for research purposes.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) in BCS
  • - Benign regenerative nodules (FNH-like) are common (27-50%).
  • - Annual HCC incidence is 0.8-2.8%, justifying surveillance.
Surveillance & Diagnosis
  • - Surveillance: Ultrasound and AFP measurement every 6 months.
  • - An AFP level of >15 ng/ml should raise suspicion for HCC.
  • - MRI with hepatobiliary contrast is recommended to differentiate benign from malignant lesions.
  • - Histological confirmation should be obtained for lesions suspected of being HCC.
MRI Differentiating Features
  • - Benign lesions: Typically hyperintense on T1, hypo/isointense on T2, hyperintense on hepatobiliary phase.
  • - Malignant lesions (HCC): Typically hypointense on T1, hyperintense on T2, hypointense on hepatobiliary phase.


Treatment
  • - No evidence to recommend a different strategy from other chronic liver diseases.
  • - All options (resection, TACE, LT, etc.) should be considered on a case-by-case basis.


Pediatric BCS Management
  • - Endovascular management (e.g., angioplasty) is suggested as first-line therapy.
  • - Combine with post-procedure anticoagulation to prevent recurrence.
  • - LT can be considered if endovascular management fails or is not possible.


Portal Vein Thrombosis (PVT) - General
  • - Recent PVT: Thrombus present for <6 months.
  • - Chronic PVT: Thrombus present for >6 months.
  • - Portal Cavernoma: Porto-portal collaterals that develop following recent PVT.
Standardised Classification
  • - A standardised description of site, extent, percent occlusion, and time course is required (See Table 4).
  • - This allows for consistent assessment of evolution over time.

Work-up for Underlying Chronic Liver Disease
  • - Crucial as it impacts management (e.g., portal hypertension persistence, need for HCC screening).
  • - Perform a complete clinical evaluation, liver blood tests, imaging, and Liver Stiffness Measurement (LSM).
Role of Liver Biopsy
  • - Consider biopsy if there is suspicion of cirrhosis or PSVD.
  • - Indications: Persistent liver blood test abnormalities, abnormal liver morphology on imaging, or elevated LSM (>10 kPa).
  • - Biopsy has a major impact on management if it shows cirrhosis (indicates HCC screening) or PSVD (may favor TIPS with recanalization).



Recent PVT without Cirrhosis
  • - Main goals of treatment are to prevent bowel necrosis and achieve recanalization to prevent long-term portal hypertension.
Diagnosis
  • - Ultrasound is a good initial test.
  • - Contrast-enhanced CT or MRI should be used to characterize the extension of PVT.
  • - Contrast-enhanced CT is best to assess for signs of intestinal ischemia.

Management
  • - Anticoagulation should be initiated as soon as possible.
  • - Early initiation increases the probability of recanalization and may reduce ischemia risk.
  • - Continue for at least 6 months.
Endovascular Interventions
  • - Thrombolysis and/or other endovascular interventions are not first-line.
  • - Consider in expert centers for patients with signs of intestinal ischemia and no early improvement with anticoagulation.


Pediatric Recent PVT
  • - Management is similar to adults, with key exceptions.
  • - In the neonatal setting and in premature babies, an expectant management approach is preferred to avoid the risk of intracranial bleeding.


Chronic PVT / Portal Cavernoma without Cirrhosis
  • - Patients are at risk for complications of portal hypertension, such as variceal bleeding.
Anticoagulation
  • - The decision to use long-term anticoagulation depends on the underlying risk factors for thrombosis.
Indications
  • - Long-term anticoagulation is recommended for patients with a major and permanent prothrombotic risk factor (e.g., MPN, homozygous mutations, APS).
  • - In other patients, anticoagulation can be considered to prevent recurrence. High levels of Factor VIII or D-dimer (>500 ng/ml) may support this decision.


Endovascular Portal Vein Recanalization (+/- TIPS)
  • - Should be considered in expert centers for specific indications.
Indications
  • - Recommended for patients with refractory complications of portal hypertension (e.g., recurrent bleeding, refractory ascites) or symptomatic portal cavernoma cholangiopathy.
  • - Not generally recommended as a preventive measure in patients without such complications.


Portal Cavernoma Cholangiopathy
  • - Biliary abnormalities seen in >80% of patients, but only symptomatic in about 20%.
  • - For symptomatic patients (cholangitis, jaundice, pruritus), treatment options include ursodeoxycholic acid, endoscopic/radiological stenting, and/or portal vein recanalization.

Pediatric Chronic PVT
  • - Restoration of portal blood flow is the main objective.
  • - Meso-Rex bypass is the recommended procedure in children.
  • - If not feasible, manage complications of portal hypertension; surgical shunts may be considered to delay or avoid LT.


PVT with Cirrhosis
  • - PVT in cirrhosis is often a marker of disease progression.
Risk Factors & Surveillance
  • - MASLD may be associated with an increased risk of PVT.
  • - Work-up for specific thrombotic risk factors (e.g., thrombophilia) is not recommended.
  • - Surveillance for PVT (e.g., every 6 months) is recommended for potential LT candidates and suggested for others.

Prophylactic Anticoagulation (without PVT)
  • - In patients with Child-Pugh B and C cirrhosis, prophylactic anticoagulation may reduce morbidity and mortality, but data is limited and safety concerns exist.

Therapeutic Anticoagulation (with PVT)
  • - Anticoagulation improves recanalization rates and may reduce mortality.
Indications
  • - Potential LT candidates: Anticoagulation should be used regardless of occlusion degree to improve LT feasibility and outcomes.
  • - Non-LT candidates: May be used for PVT with >50% occlusion of the main portal vein. Consider for <50% occlusion if it progresses or extends to the SMV.

Choice of Anticoagulant
  • - DOACs can be used in patients with Child-Pugh A or B cirrhosis.
  • - DOACs are not recommended in Child-Pugh C cirrhosis.


TIPS for PVT in Cirrhosis
  • - TIPS can be considered over anticoagulation alone in specific scenarios.
Indications
  • - For patients with complications of portal hypertension (e.g., variceal bleeding, recurrent ascites).
  • - For patients whose PVT progresses despite anticoagulation.

Post-TIPS Management
  • - Routine anticoagulation after TIPS is not recommended to improve recanalization rates.


Post-LT Management
  • - For patients with pre-existing PVT at the time of LT:
  • - Anticoagulation after LT is not suggested for Yerdel grade 1 or 2 PVT.
  • - No recommendation can be made for Yerdel grade 3 or 4 PVT.


Porto-sinusoidal Vascular Disorder (PSVD)
  • - A group of vascular entities damaging intrahepatic portal venules and/or sinusoids, causing portal hypertension without cirrhosis.
Clinical Suspicion
  • - Suspect PSVD in patients with signs of portalhypertension, especially with LSM 10 kPa, HVPG <10 mmHg, and a smooth liver surface.
  • - Unexplained liver blood test abnormalities, particularly in the presence of associated disorders (Table 5), should also raise suspicion.

Diagnosis
  • - A liver biopsy is required to establish the diagnosis of PSVD.
  • - The biopsy should be of good quality (ideally >20 mm in length).
  • - Diagnosis is based on excluding cirrhosis and identifying characteristic histological lesions (See Table 7).
Diagnostic Criteria
  • - Diagnosis requires exclusion of cirrhosis plus one of the following:
  • - 1. At least one specific sign of portal hypertension (e.g., varices, collaterals).
  • - 2. At least one specific histological lesion.
  • - 3. At least one non-specific sign of portal hypertension AND at least one non-specific histological lesion.
Specific Histological Lesions
  • - Obliterative portal venopathy (portal vein stenosis).
  • - Nodular regenerative hyperplasia.
  • - Incomplete septal fibrosis/cirrhosis.

Non-Specific Histological Lesions
  • - Herniated portal vein branches.
  • - Hypervascularised portal tracts.
  • - Sinusoidal dilatation.
  • - Mild perisinusoidal fibrosis.



Work-up for Associated Conditions
  • - An extensive work-up is recommended to identify conditions associated with PSVD (See Table 5).
  • - Common associations include immune disorders (up to 32%), HIV infection (8%), prothrombotic conditions, and exposure to drugs like azathioprine and oxaliplatin.
  • - Routine genetic testing is not systematically recommended.

Surveillance
  • - The risk of developing PVT is substantial (16-33% at 5 years).
  • - Surveillance for PVT every 6 months is recommended.
  • - Surveillance for HCC is not recommended as it is exceptionally rare.

Management
  • - Management focuses on complications of portal hypertension and associated conditions.
Prophylactic Anticoagulation for PVT
  • - No recommendation can be made regarding prophylactic anticoagulation due to a lack of data.
  • - An RCT is ongoing (NCT04007289).

Severe/Refractory Complications
  • - TIPS and/or LT are recommended for severe or refractory portal hypertension-related complications.
  • - TIPS: 2-year survival rate is 80%. HE occurs in up to 31%.
  • - LT: 5-year survival rate is 69% (up to 85% without severe associated conditions).


Pediatric PSVD
  • - Diagnosis and management in children may follow the recommendations for adults.
  • - Associated with conditions like cystic fibrosis, Turner syndrome, and specific genetic mutations.


Non-obstructive Sinusoidal Dilatation (NO-SD) and Peliosis Hepatis
  • - A spectrum of vascular disorders affecting the liver sinusoids.
Non-obstructive Sinusoidal Dilatation (NO-SD)
  • - Definition: Enlargement of the sinusoidal lumen without sinusoidal or post-sinusoidal obstruction.
  • - Clinical presentation is variable and depends on associated conditions.
Diagnosis
  • - Suspected on imaging when a "mosaic enhancement pattern" is seen on CT/MRI in the absence of hepatic venous outflow obstruction.
  • - Liver biopsy is not systematically recommended but can be considered if findings persist after removing potential causes.

Associated Conditions & Management
  • - Work-up should screen for associated conditions like drug use (oxaliplatin, azathioprine, oral contraceptives), solid tumors, infections, and inflammatory disorders.
  • - Long-term outcomes are unknown; follow-up should focus on the associated condition.
  • - Discontinuation of platinum-based chemotherapy is not recommended.
  • - Discontinuation of oral contraceptives may be considered to reverse sinusoidal dilatation.


Peliosis Hepatis
  • - Definition: Rare disorder with blood-filled spaces randomly distributed in the liver parenchyma.
  • - Often asymptomatic but can lead to hepatic failure, portal hypertension, or liver rupture.
Diagnosis
  • - No specific imaging features; it is a great mimic of other lesions.
  • - Diagnosis requires a liver biopsy showing blood-filled spaces not entirely lined with endothelial cells.

Management
  • - Remove predisposing conditions when feasible (e.g., stop anabolic steroids, treat infections like Bartonella henselae).
  • - MRI is suggested to monitor disease progression.
  • - For complications like hemorrhage, management should be discussed in expert centers (surveillance, interventional radiology, or surgery).


Pediatric Considerations
  • - Management in children should follow adult recommendations.


Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome (SOS)
  • - Also known as veno-occlusive disease (VOD), a potentially life-threatening complication, typically after haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT).
  • - Severe forms with multiorgan failure have a mortality rate of >80% if untreated.
Prophylaxis in HSCT
  • - Focuses on reducing modifiable risk factors and pharmacological prevention.
Recommended
  • - Ursodeoxycholic acid is recommended in all patients.
  • - Suggested dose is 12 to 15 mg/kg divided into two doses, starting the day before conditioning and continuing for 3 months post-HSCT.

Not Recommended
  • - Defibrotide prophylaxis is generally not recommended based on recent phase III trial data (HARMONY trial).
  • - Heparins are not recommended due to lack of efficacy and potential side effects.


Diagnosis in HSCT
  • - Suspicion arises from clinical signs, elevated LSM, and ultrasound findings, typically within 21 days post-HSCT (classical SOS) but can be later (late-onset SOS).
Diagnostic Criteria
  • - Use criteria adjusted from EBMT 2023 (Table 9).
  • - Three degrees of certainty: probable, clinical, and histologically proven.
  • - A proven diagnosis should be restricted to histologically confirmed cases.


Treatment in HSCT
  • - Management depends on the severity of SOS.
Medical Therapy
  • - Defibrotide is recommended for severe SOS to improve survival.
  • - It can be considered for moderate SOS.
  • - Early initiation after diagnosis may be preferable.

Interventional Therapy
  • - TIPS may be considered in severe/very severe SOS with rapid clinical deterioration despite medical treatment.
  • - LT may be discussed in very severe SOS for patients with a favorable haematological prognosis.


Long-Term Follow-up after SOS
  • - Patients can develop intrahepatic non-cirrhotic portal hypertension (PSVD) in the long term.
  • - Follow-up with liver blood tests and imaging may be considered.

SOS Outside the HSCT Setting
  • - Most commonly associated with oxaliplatin-based chemotherapy.
  • - Other drugs are also implicated (Box 1).
  • - Diagnosis requires a liver biopsy as signs are non-specific.
  • - Management involves stopping the offending drug whenever possible. TIPS may be considered for severe portal hypertension complications.

Pediatric SOS
  • - Mortality rate is high (80%).
  • - In contrast to adults, defibrotide should be initiated as soon as SOS is suspected in children, regardless of severity.


Aneurysms of Splanchnic Arteries (SAAs)
  • - Rare vascular diseases affecting arteries of the celiac trunk and mesenteric systems.
  • - Must distinguish true aneurysms (all 3 vessel layers intact) from pseudoaneurysms (vessel wall injury).
Diagnosis & Work-up
  • - Contrast-enhanced CT is recommended over ultrasound for diagnosis and treatment planning.
  • - Work-up should identify risk factors: portal hypertension, arterial wall diseases, and causes of pseudoaneurysm (trauma, surgery, infection).

Management Strategy
  • - Treatment decisions are guided by aneurysm type, size, location, and symptoms (Fig. 5).
Conservative Treatment
  • - Recommended for small (<2-2.5 cm), asymptomatic, true SAAs.
  • - Follow-up with CT at 6-12 months, then every 3 years if stable.

Interventional Treatment
  • - Recommended for all pseudoaneurysms (high rupture risk).
  • - Recommended for true aneurysms that are symptomatic, large (>2-2.5 cm), or fast-growing.
  • - Endovascular treatment is preferred over open surgery.
Special Populations Requiring Intervention
  • - Splenic aneurysms of any size in women of child-bearing potential or LT candidates.
  • - Gastro/peripancreatic aneurysms related to celiac axis occlusion.



Pediatric SAAs
  • - Management should be the same as in adult patients.


Hepatic Arteriovenous Fistula (HAVF)
  • - Rare vascular communications between a hepatic artery branch and a portal or hepatic vein branch.
  • - Can be congenital (AVMs) or acquired (iatrogenic, trauma, tumor-related).
Diagnosis & Work-up
  • - Doppler ultrasound and contrast-enhanced CT are both useful and may be complementary.
  • - Work-up should search for a history of liver biopsy/trauma, features of HHT (multiple fistulae), malignancy, or chronic liver disease.

Management
  • - Conservative approach is recommended for asymptomatic patients (many post-biopsy fistulae resolve spontaneously).
  • - Closure at diagnosis is recommended for symptomatic patients.
  • - Endovascular closure is the method of choice.

Pediatric HAVF
  • - General approach is similar to adults, but two key differences:
  • - 1. Must rule out congenital hemangioma, which is managed medically with propranolol.
  • - 2. Use caution with embolic agent volume in infants due to risk of migration.


Issues Common to Vascular Liver Diseases
  • - This section covers management principles applicable across various vascular liver diseases.
Portal Hypertension & Varices
  • - Surveillance for varices is recommended when portal hypertension is suspected.
  • - If no varices, repeat endoscopy in 2 years. If small varices, repeat in 1 year.
Bleeding Prophylaxis
  • - Primary prophylaxis: NSBBs or endoscopic treatment are recommended for high-risk varices.
  • - Secondary prophylaxis: Both NSBBs and endoscopic treatment are recommended.
  • - Refractory bleeding: TIPS is recommended.


Anticoagulation
  • - In patients with PVT or BCS without cirrhosis:
DOACs vs. VKAs/LMWH
  • - Recent PVT: DOACs are suggested as an alternative to LMWH/VKAs.
  • - Chronic PVT: DOACs may be preferred over VKAs for adherence and safety.
  • - BCS: DOACs may be considered if liver function is preserved.


Contraception
  • - Oestrogen-containing oral contraceptives should be avoided due to increased thrombotic risk.
  • - Preferred methods: Intrauterine devices, mechanical contraception, and microprogestatives.

Drugs to Avoid
  • - Avoid or discontinue drugs known to be associated with specific vascular liver disorders (Table 11).
  • - Use drugs with high first-pass effect or liver metabolism with caution, especially with impaired liver function or shunting (Table 12).

Pregnancy Management
  • - Pregnancy should be planned when the liver disease is well-controlled.
Anticoagulation during Pregnancy
  • - Switch VKAs or DOACs to LMWH as soon as pregnancy is confirmed (before 4th week).
  • - Continue LMWH throughout the pregnancy.
  • - Prophylactic LMWH may be considered for 6 weeks post-partum in patients not on anticoagulants.

Delivery & Post-partum
  • - Vaginal delivery is preferred over caesarean section.
  • - Safe platelet counts: >20x10^9/L for vaginal, >50x10^9/L for caesarean, >75x10^9/L for epidural.
  • - Warfarin and beta-blockers are compatible with breastfeeding; other VKAs and DOACs are contraindicated.


Psychosocial Support & Expertise
  • - Systematically assess the psychological and social impact of the disease.
  • - Refer patients to dedicated professionals and patient associations.
  • - Management requires a multidisciplinary team in an expert referral center with expertise in TIPS, LT, and critical care.
