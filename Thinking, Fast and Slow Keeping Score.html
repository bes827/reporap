Thinking, Fast and Slow: Keeping Score
  • - Source: Book 'Thinking, Fast and Slow' by Daniel Kahneman.
  • - This section explores how we use mental accounting, regret, and framing to make decisions, often leading to irrational choices.
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the massive preference for selling winning stocks over losing ones called?
  • - In the context of regret, which outcome do people expect to have stronger emotional reactions to: one produced by action or one by inaction?
  • - What is the 'precautionary principle' often used in European regulations?
  • - What is the 'MPG Illusion'?
  • - What is the single best predictor of whether people will donate their organs?

Keeping Score: The Psychology of Money
  • - For most people, money is a proxy for points on a scale of self-regard and achievement.
  • - This emotional score-keeping shapes our preferences and motivates our actions.
Resulting Biases
  • - We refuse to cut losses when doing so would mean admitting failure.
  • - We are biased against actions that could lead to regret.
  • - We draw a sharp, illusory distinction between omission (not doing) and commission (doing).


Mental Accounts
  • - A concept by Richard Thaler.
  • - We organize our lives by putting money and decisions into separate mental 'accounts'.
  • - This is a form of narrow framing that helps a finite mind keep things manageable.
Types & Examples
  • - Physical/Mental Accounts: We have separate pots for spending money, general savings, or a child's education.
  • - Self-Control: We use accounts to create budgets or limit daily consumption.
  • - Score-Keeping: Professional golfers putt more successfully to avoid a bogey (preventing a loss) than to achieve a birdie (securing a gain).

The Sunk-Cost Fallacy
  • - The mistake of investing additional resources in a losing account because of the prior investment.
  • - It's an attempt to avoid the emotional pain of closing an account with a loss.
Example: The Blizzard Game
  • - Two fans have tickets for a game during a blizzard. One paid for the ticket; the other got it for free.
  • - The fan who paid is more likely to brave the blizzard to attend.
  • - Reason: For the paying fan, staying home closes their mental account with a more negative balance (lost money + missed game).

Example: Corporate Projects
  • - A company has spent $50 million on a failing project.
  • - It requires another $60 million to continue, even though a new project looks more profitable.
  • - Often, the company continues the failing project, 'throwing good money after bad' to avoid admitting a costly failure.

The Agency Problem
  • - This occurs when an executive's personal interests (e.g., avoiding a failure on their record) are misaligned with the company's objectives (e.g., cutting losses).
  • - Boards often replace a CEO to bring in someone who doesn't carry the same mental accounts and can ignore past sunk costs.


The Disposition Effect
  • - A massive, documented preference for selling winning stocks rather than losing stocks.
  • - This is an instance of narrow framing, where the investor wants to close each 'account' (each stock) as a gain.
Why it's a Costly Bias
  • - Tax Implications (US): Realizing losses reduces your taxes, while selling winners exposes you to taxes.
  • - Market Anomaly: Stocks that recently gained value are often likely to continue gaining for a short while.
  • - One study found the expected after-tax extra return of selling the loser instead of the winner was 3.4% over the next year.



Regret
  • - An emotion and a form of self-punishment.
  • - It is a counterfactual emotion, triggered by the ease with which we can imagine an alternative to reality ('if only...').
Regret & Normality
  • - Regret is felt more intensely for unusual events because the 'normal' alternative is easier to imagine.
Example: The Hitchhiker
  • - Mr. Brown (rarely picks up hitchhikers) and Mr. Smith (frequently does) both get robbed after giving someone a ride.
  • - Who feels more regret? ---> Mr. Brown (88% of respondents agree), because his action was abnormal for him.
  • - Who gets more blame from others? ---> Mr. Smith (77% of respondents agree), because he violated a social norm of reasonable behavior.


Action vs. Inaction
  • - People expect to have stronger emotional reactions, including regret, to an outcome produced by action than to the same outcome produced by inaction.
Example: The Stocks
  • - Paul considered switching from stock A to B but didn't (inaction).
  • - George owned stock B and switched to A (action).
  • - Both would have been $1,200 better off if they owned stock B.
  • - Who feels more regret? ---> George (92% of respondents agree), because his loss resulted from an action.


The Power of the Default Option
  • - The key distinction is not just action/inaction, but the difference between the default option and actions that deviate from it.
  • - Deviating from the default makes it easier to imagine the alternative, which is the source of painful emotions.
  • - This bias favors conventional and risk-averse choices.


Responsibility & Taboo Tradeoffs
  • - Loss aversion is much higher for important aspects of life, like health, than for money.
  • - We are extremely reluctant to 'sell' important endowments if it makes us responsible for a terrible outcome.
Example: The Vaccine vs. Volunteering
  • - Scenario 1 (Buying): What is the maximum you would pay for a vaccine to avoid a 1-in-1,000 chance of a fatal disease? ---> People offer a limited amount.
  • - Scenario 2 (Selling): What is the minimum you would ask to be paid to volunteer for research with a 1-in-1,000 chance of contracting the same disease? ---> People demand a far higher amount.
  • - The typical ratio of the 'selling' price to the 'buying' price is about 50:1.

Taboo Tradeoffs
  • - The idea of trading safety for money is often seen as morally wrong or 'taboo'.
  • - Example: Parents refusing to buy a slightly less safe insecticide for their child, even at a large discount.
  • - This attitude can be incoherent and damaging, as it prevents the efficient use of a limited 'safety budget'.

The Precautionary Principle
  • - A doctrine, especially strong in Europe, that prohibits any action that might cause harm.
  • - It places the entire burden of proving safety on anyone who undertakes a new action.
  • - When interpreted strictly, it can be paralyzing and would have blocked innovations like airplanes, antibiotics, and X-rays.


Preference Reversals
  • - Our judgments and choices can 'reverse' depending on the context of evaluation.
  • - This challenges the economic model of stable, reality-bound preferences.
Single vs. Joint Evaluation
  • - Single Evaluation: Judging one option in isolation. Often driven by System 1's emotional reactions (WYSIATI).
  • - Joint Evaluation: Comparing two or more options side-by-side. This involves more careful, comparative assessment by System 2.
Example: Victim Compensation
  • - A man is shot in a convenience store. Scenario A: his regular store. Scenario B: a store he rarely visited.
  • - In single evaluation, the victim in Scenario B gets much higher compensation due to poignancy ('if only...').
  • - In joint evaluation, compensation is the same for both, as location is correctly identified as an irrelevant factor.


Classic Reversal: The Bets
  • - An experiment by Lichtenstein and Slovic.
  • - Bet A: 11/36 chance to win $160, 25/36 chance to lose $15.
  • - Bet B: 35/36 chance to win $40, 1/36 chance to lose $10.
  • - When asked to choose, most people pick the safer Bet B.
  • - When asked to set a selling price for each bet, they set a higher price for Bet A, anchoring on the larger potential prize.
  • - This is a preference reversal, a violation of rational choice theory.


The Role of Categories
  • - Our judgments are coherent within categories but can be incoherent across different categories.
  • - System 1 automatically retrieves a norm for comparison based on the category an item belongs to.
Example: Dolphins vs. Farmworkers
  • - In single evaluation, people tend to offer larger contributions to save dolphins (a charismatic species within the 'endangered animals' category).
  • - In joint evaluation, people contribute substantially more to help farmworkers.
  • - The 'human vs. animal' feature, which is decisive, only becomes salient when the two are compared directly.

Evaluability Hypothesis
  • - By Christopher Hsee. Some attributes are difficult to evaluate on their own ('unevaluable').
  • - Example: Two dictionaries. A has 10,000 entries and is 'like new'. B has 20,000 entries and a torn cover.
  • - In single evaluation, Dictionary A is valued more highly because 'condition' is easy to evaluate.
  • - In joint evaluation, Dictionary B is valued more, as it's obvious that the number of entries is a far more important attribute.


Frames and Reality
  • - Logically equivalent statements can evoke different reactions because they trigger different associations in System 1.
  • - Our choices are often frame-bound rather than reality-bound.
Emotional Framing
  • - Using emotionally charged words to influence choice, even when the underlying reality is identical.
  • - Example: A bad outcome is more acceptable as the 'cost of a lottery ticket' than as 'losing a gamble'.
  • - Example: People will more readily forgo a 'cash discount' than pay a 'credit surcharge'.
Neuroeconomics Study: KEEP vs. LOSE
  • - A choice between a sure thing and a gamble was framed as either 'KEEP £20' or 'LOSE £30' (from an initial £50).
  • - The amygdala (emotion center) was more active when subjects' choices conformed to the frame.
  • - The anterior cingulate (conflict center) was more active when subjects resisted the frame's suggestion.

Medical Study: Survivalvs. Mortality
  • - Physicians were given statistics for two lung cancer treatments: surgery and radiation.
  • - Survival Frame: 'The one-month survival rate is 90%.' ---> 84% of physicians chose surgery.
  • - Mortality Frame: 'There is 10% mortality in the first month.' ---> Only 50% chose surgery.
  • - Conclusion: Medical training is no defense against the power of framing.


Empty Intuitions: The Asian Disease Problem
  • - This classic problem shows that risk preferences for lives saved/lost are not reality-bound.
Frame 1: Gains (Lives Saved)
  • - Program A: '200 people will be saved' (a sure thing).
  • - Program B: 'a 1/3 probability that 600 people will be saved, and a 2/3 probability that no people will be saved' (a gamble).
  • - Result: A substantial majority chooses Program A. People are risk-averse for gains.

Frame 2: Losses (Lives Lost)
  • - Program A': '400 people will die' (a sure thing).
  • - Program B': 'a 1/3 probability that nobody will die, and a 2/3 probability that 600 people will die' (a gamble).
  • - Result: A large majority chooses Program B'. People are risk-seeking for losses.

The Inconsistency
  • - The outcomes of A and A' are identical, as are B and B'.
  • - When confronted with their inconsistent choices, most people have no compelling moral intuition from System 2 to resolve the conflict.
  • - Conclusion: Our moral intuitions are attached to descriptions of reality, not reality itself.


Schelling's Tax Problem
  • - Illustrates how a simple moral rule ('favor the poor') can generate contradictory answers depending on the frame.
Frame 1: Child Exemption
  • - Question: 'Should the child exemption be larger for the rich than for the poor?'
  • - Intuitive Answer: No, this is unacceptable.

Frame 2: Childless Surcharge
  • - The law is reframed: the default is a family with two children, and childless families pay a surcharge.
  • - Question: 'Should the childless poor pay as large a surcharge as the childless rich?'
  • - Intuitive Answer: No, this is also unacceptable.

The Contradiction
  • - The two moral positions are logically inconsistent.
  • - If you want the poor to get the same or greater tax reduction for having children, you must want them to pay the same or greater tax increase for being childless.



Good Frames
  • - Not all frames are equal; some are clearly better because they lead to more rational decisions.
  • - Principle: Broader frames and inclusive accounts generally lead to better choices.
Example: Lost Tickets vs. Lost Cash
  • - Scenario 1 (Lost Tickets): A woman loses two $80 tickets. Most people predict she will not buy new ones.
  • - Reasoning: The loss is posted to the 'theater show' mental account, making the cost seem to be $320.
  • - Scenario 2 (Lost Cash): A woman loses $160 cash. Most people predict she will still buy the tickets.
  • - Reasoning: The loss is posted to a 'general revenue' account and is seen as separate from the ticket purchase.
  • - Better Frame: The lost cash frame is superior because the loss is a sunk cost and should be ignored.

Example: The MPG Illusion
  • - The 'miles per gallon' (MPG) frame is a poor guide for assessing fuel efficiency savings.
The Puzzle
  • - Adam switches from a 12 mpg car to a 14 mpg car.
  • - Beth switches from a 30 mpg car to a 40 mpg car.
  • - Who saves more gas over 10,000 miles?

The Answer
  • - Answer: Adam saves more gas.
  • - Adam's savings: 119 gallons (833 down to 714).
  • - Beth's savings: 83 gallons (333 down to 250).
  • - Better Frame: 'Gallons per mile' (or Liters per 100km) is a much more informative frame.


Example: Organ Donation
  • - The single best predictor of organ donation rates is the designation of the default option.
Opt-Out vs. Opt-In
  • - Opt-out countries (e.g., Austria, Sweden): You are a donor by default unless you check a box to opt out. ---> Donation rates are close to 100%.
  • - Opt-in countries (e.g., Germany, Denmark): You are not a donor unless you check a box to opt in. ---> Donation rates can be as low as 4%.
  • - Reason: The laziness of System 2. When unprepared, people stick with the default.
