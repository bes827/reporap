Core Concepts of Chronic Hepatitis B (CHB)
  • - CHB is a dynamic disease characterized by distinct phases, requiring regular monitoring to guide management changes.
Phases of CHB (See Table 1)
  • - Understanding the phase of infection is critical for treatment decisions.
Immune-tolerant
  • - HBeAg-positive
  • - HBV DNA: >10,000,000 IU/mL
  • - ALT: Normal (<35 U/L for males, <25 U/L for females)

HBeAg-positive Immune Active
  • - HBeAg-positive
  • - HBV DNA: >20,000 IU/mL
  • - ALT: Elevated ≥2x ULN

HBeAg-negative Immune Active
  • - HBeAg-negative
  • - HBV DNA: >2,000 IU/mL
  • - ALT: Elevated ≥2x ULN

Inactive
  • - HBeAg-negative
  • - HBV DNA: <2,000 IU/mL
  • - ALT: Normal

HBsAg-negative Immune Clearance
  • - HBV DNA: Undetectable
  • - May have antibody to HBsAg (anti-HBs).


Indeterminate Phases ('Grey Zone')
  • - Refers to HBsAg-positive persons whose ALT and HBV DNA levels do not meet criteria for immune active, tolerant, or inactive phases.
  • - Affects up to 40% of adults with CHB.
  • - More common in HBeAg-negative persons.

Liver Disease Severity Assessment
  • - Integral for making treatment and surveillance decisions.
Non-invasive Tests
  • - Hepatic transient elastography and serum markers (e.g., FIB-4) can be used for fibrosis staging.
  • - Elastography generally shows better performance than FIB-4.

Liver Biopsy
  • - Infrequently used but is the most accurate method for defining inflammation severity.


Approved Drug Classes
  • - Two classes are approved: Peginterferon and Nucleos(t)ide Analogues (NAs).
Peginterferon
  • - Rarely used due to limitations in applicability, safety, and tolerability compared to NAs.

Nucleos(t)ide Analogues (NAs)
  • - Preferred treatment for CHB.
Preferred NAs
  • - Entecavir (ETV)
  • - Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF)
  • - Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF)
  • - All offer high efficacy, low resistance rates, and are well-tolerated.

Selection Criteria
  • - Renal or bone disease: Avoid TDF.
  • - Pregnancy: Avoid ETV.
  • - HIV coinfection: Avoid ETV unless HIV RNA is suppressed on an existing regimen.
  • - Prior lamivudine exposure: Avoid ETV.

Renal Considerations
  • - TAF: Not recommended if CrCl is <15 ml/min and not on dialysis.
  • - ETV and TDF: Require dose adjustment for renal dysfunction or dialysis.



Goals of Antiviral Treatment
  • - Primary Goals: Prevention of progression to cirrhosis, HCC, and liver-related death.
  • - Other Goals: Reduce vertical and horizontal transmission, improve quality of life.
Surrogate Endpoints
  • - Used as measures of treatment efficacy due to the long-term nature of clinical endpoints.
Virological
  • - HBV DNA undetectable
  • - HBeAg loss/seroconversion
  • - HBsAg loss/seroconversion

Biochemical & Histological
  • - Biochemical: Normal serum ALT.
  • - Histological (infrequent): Improvement in necroinflammation by ≥2 points with no worsening of fibrosis.



Functional Cure
  • - Defined as HBsAg loss.
  • - Associated with a reduction in clinical outcomes and long-term durability.
  • - Infrequent with current therapies, but higher with peginterferon than NAs.

Shared Decision Making
  • - A collaborative process where provider and patient make informed decisions considering clinical evidence and the patient's values, preferences, and circumstances.


Introduction & Background
  • - CHB affects ~258 million globally and leads to an estimated 1.1 million deaths annually.
  • - In the U.S., the estimated burden may be as high as 1.8 million people, with half unaware of their diagnosis.
CDC Recommendations
  • - Screening: All adults ≥18 years at least once; all pregnant persons during each pregnancy.
  • - Vaccination: All infants, children <19, adults 19-59, and adults ≥60 with risk factors.

Treatment Prioritization
  • - Traditionally: Prioritized for those at greatest risk (immune active disease, significant fibrosis).
  • - WHO Recommendation: Simplification and expansion of treatment criteria to address high disease burden in low/middle-income countries.


PICO Question 1: Antiviral Strategy in Pregnancy
  • - Question: What is the optimal antiviral strategy for HBsAg-positive pregnant individuals with HBV DNA >200,000 IU/ml to prevent mother-to-child transmission (MTCT)?
Recommendation 1
  • - For pregnant persons with HBV DNA >200,000 IU/mL, AASLD recommends initiating TDF or TAF at gestational week 28 to prevent MTCT.
  • - Note: TDF has a more extensive safety record in pregnancy.
  • - Strength: Strong; Certainty: Moderate.
Implementation Considerations
  • - Continue therapy: If already on TDF or TAF.
  • - Switch therapy: If on entecavir or other antivirals, switch to TDF or TAF.
  • - Early initiation: Consider earlier start if invasive procedures (e.g., amniocentesis) are anticipated or if there's a high risk of preterm labor.
  • - Discontinuation: TDF/TAF can be stopped at delivery if the sole purpose was preventing MTCT.
Special Scenario: HBIG Unavailability
  • - If hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) for the infant is unavailable, initiate TDF or TAF earlier, at gestational week 16.

Post-Discontinuation Monitoring
  • - Monitor HBV DNA and ALT every 1-3 months for up to six months for withdrawal flares.
  • - Reinitiate treatment for a significant flare (ALT ≥5x ULN).

Breastfeeding
  • - Breastfeeding is safe for persons on TDF or TAF.


Evidence & Rationale
  • - Perinatal transmission occurs in ~8-10% of infants despite standard immunoprophylaxis if maternal HBV DNA is >200,000 IU/mL.
  • - TDF and TAF are equally effective and safe in reducing MTCT.
  • - TDF/TAF are preferred over lamivudine or telbivudine due to lower resistance risk.



PICO Question 2: Antivirals to Reduce Horizontal Transmission
  • - Question: Should antiviral therapy be provided to HBsAg-positive persons with viremia who do not meet disease-specific treatment indications to reduce transmission in high-risk scenarios?
Recommendation 2
  • - For persons in high-risk scenarios for transmission, AASLD suggests a shared decision-making approach regarding antiviral treatment.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.
Implementation Considerations (See Table 2)
  • - High-risk scenarios: Unprotected sex with multiple partners, injection drug use with inconsistent harm reduction, living with susceptible household members, certain healthcare procedures.
  • - Favoring treatment: If exposed persons are unvaccinated, have inadequate vaccine response, or are immunocompromised.
  • - Avoid stigmatizing practices.
Healthcare Workers
  • - Workers performing SHEA category III exposure-prone procedures are at higher risk.
  • - Antiviral treatment may be required to reduce transmission risk (SHEA suggests maintaining HBV DNA <1000 IU/mL).

Treatment Cessation
  • - Counsel on risks of cessation (e.g., ALT flare).
  • - Revisit the decision to continue therapy if transmission risks are no longer high.


Evidence & Rationale
  • - Evidence is indirect, from nonrandomized studies in HIV-positive individuals.
  • - No reported transmissions from healthcare workers with low/undetectable HBV DNA on therapy.
  • - Rationale is based on biological plausibility and high acceptability.



PICO Question 3: Treatment in Immune-Tolerant Phase
  • - Question: Should individuals in the immune-tolerant phase start antiviral therapy versus observation?
Recommendation 3
  • - For persons in the immune-tolerant phase, AASLD suggests antiviral therapy for those:
  • - Over age 40 years OR
  • - With significant liver inflammation (≥ grade 2) or fibrosis (≥ F2).
  • - For persons under age 40, a shared decision-making approach is suggested.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.
Implementation Considerations
  • - Accurate identification: Requires persistently normal ALT (35 U/L males, 25 U/L females) with HBV DNA >10^7 IU/mL on ≥2 measurements ≥6 months apart.
  • - Fibrosis Assessment: A VCTE cutoff of 7 kPa has 83% specificity for identifying ≥F2 fibrosis.
  • - Monitoring if untreated: Test HBV DNA and ALT at least every 6 months for adolescents and adults.

Evidence & Rationale
  • - High HBV DNA is strongly associated with HCC risk.
  • - Age over 40 is associated with a higher likelihood of significant histological disease.
  • - Treatment effectively suppresses HBV DNA but has limited effect on HBeAg/HBsAg loss in this phase.
  • - Only one retrospective study reported a potential reduction in HCC (HR 0.23) and mortality (HR 0.24).



PICO Question 4: Treatment in HBeAg-Negative Indeterminate Phase
  • - Question: Should HBsAg-positive, HBeAg-negative individuals without cirrhosis and in the indeterminate phase start antiviral therapy versus observation?
Recommendation 4
  • - AASLD suggests antiviral therapy using a shared decision-making approach, assessing risks and benefits, and re-evaluating the decision at each follow-up visit.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.
Implementation Considerations (See Figure 3)
  • - Factors favoring treatment: Older age (>40 years), male sex, low normal platelet counts (<180 k/mm3).
  • - Advanced fibrosis (F3/F4) requires treatment. Non-invasive thresholds include FIB-4 >1.45 and VCTE ≥8 kPa.
  • - Monitoring if untreated: ALT and HBV DNA every 3-6 months for the first year, then every 6 months.

Evidence & Rationale
  • - Individuals in this phase are at risk of HCC (pooled annual incidence rate of 0.32%).
  • - A meta-analysis suggested antiviral treatment was associated with a lower incidence of HCC (aIRR 0.36).
  • - The evidence is of low certainty, and must be balanced against the burden of long-term therapy.



PICO Question 5: Discontinuation of NA Therapy
  • - Question: Should HBsAg-positive individuals without cirrhosis on NA therapy for ≥3 years (HBeAg-negative, undetectable HBV DNA) remain on or discontinue therapy?
Recommendation 5
  • - AASLD suggests not withdrawing nucleos(t)ide analogue therapy until HBsAg loss is achieved.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.
Implementation Considerations (See Table 3)
  • - For persons with a strong desire to stop, engage in shared decision-making.
Strict Criteria to Consider Stopping
  • - MUST meet ALL:
  • - No history of cirrhosis, decompensation, or HCC.
  • - HBeAg-negative/anti-HBe positive for ≥1 year (if initially positive).
  • - HBV DNA undetectable for ≥2 years.
  • - Quantitative HBsAg level <100 IU/mL.
  • - No co-infection with HIV or HDV.
  • - Willing to adhere to frequent monitoring.

Post-Withdrawal Monitoring
  • - Monitor ALT and HBV DNA every 1-3 months for the first 6 months.
  • - Then every 3 months for the next 6-12 months.

Criteria for IMMEDIATE Re-treatment
  • - Meet ANY ONE of the following:
  • - HBV DNA level ≥10,000 IU/mL.
  • - ALT ≥5 x ULN.
  • - Total bilirubin >2.5 mg/dL.
  • - Any symptom of hepatic decompensation.


Evidence & Rationale
  • - Consensus that persons with cirrhosis should never stop therapy.
  • - RCTs show modest rates of HBsAg loss after stopping (~10.6% at 2 years) but substantial risks.
  • - Risks include ALT flares (26.9% at 2 years) and need for re-treatment (42% at 5 years).
  • - The best predictor for successful discontinuation is a quantitative HBsAg level <100 IU/mL at the end of treatment.



PICO Question 6: HCC Surveillance in Special Populations
  • - Question: Should individuals with CHB who cleared HBsAg or have co-infections (HCV, HDV, HIV) receive HCC surveillance?
Recommendations & Evidence
  • - Surveillance method: Ultrasound and serum AFP every 6 months.
After HBsAg Loss (Rec. 6)
  • - Suggest continued surveillance for those with:
  • - Cirrhosis
  • - Family history of HCC
  • - Men who lost HBsAg after age 40
  • - Women who lost HBsAg after age 50
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.

HBV-HDV Co-infection (Rec. 7)
  • - Suggest HCC surveillance for all adults, independent of cirrhosis status.
  • - Decision for children should be individualized.
  • - Rationale: HCC incidence is significantly higher (18.65 vs 4.84 per 1,000 person-years) compared to HBV mono-infection, even without cirrhosis.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.

HBV-HIV Co-infection (Rec. 8)
  • - Suggest HCC surveillance for:
  • - Men ≥18 years of age
  • - Women ≥40 years of age
  • - Rationale: Risk is higher in men and increases with age.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.

HBV-HCV Co-infection (Rec. 9)
  • - Recommend treatment for HCV.
  • - SuggestHCC surveillance as per criteria for HBV mono-infection.
  • - Rationale: After HCV cure with DAA therapy, HCC risk is expected to be similar to that of HBV mono-infection.
  • - Strength: Conditional; Certainty: Very Low.


Implementation Considerations
  • - For triple/quadruple infections (HBV + HIV, HDV, HCV), surveillance should follow the recommendation for the coinfection with the highest associated risk (e.g., HBV-HDV).
  • - For prior HCV infection (anti-HCV positive, HCV RNA negative), surveillance is the same as for HBV alone.
  • - For prior HDV infection (anti-HDV positive, HDV RNA negative), surveillance is the same as for HBV alone.


Limitations & Research Gaps
  • - Most recommendations have low or very low certainty of evidence, highlighting the need for higher quality data.
Nomenclature Harmonization Needed
  • - Terminology for phases of CHB infection.
  • - Defining upper limits of normal (ULN) for ALT in adults and children.
  • - Consensus on 'indeterminate' vs 'grey zone' terminology.


Topics Covered in 2018 AASLD Guidance (Unchanged)
  • - This guideline updates specific topics; the 2018 guidance remains applicable for many areas.
Key Unchanged Topics
  • - Treatment of children.
  • - Treatment of immune active CHB (HBeAg-positive & negative) with ALT >2x ULN.
  • - Management of suboptimal response (low-level viremia, breakthrough) (See Figure 4).
  • - Treatment of persons with cirrhosis (See Figure 5).
  • - Management in special populations (transplant recipients, acute HBV).


Unaddressed & Controversial Topics
  • - Several topics were not addressed in this focused update.
Future Topics for Guidance
  • - Role of newer biomarkers: HBsAg quantitation, HBV RNA, HBcrAg.
  • - Treatment of inactive carriers with high HBsAg levels (>1000 IU/mL).
  • - Role of peginterferon add-on or switch therapy to achieve functional cure.

Comparison with WHO Guideline
  • - AASLD: Continues to use HBeAg status and multiple HBV DNA thresholds.
  • - WHO: Excludes HBeAg status and uses a single HBV DNA threshold (>2000 IU/mL).
  • - This difference reflects easier access to comprehensive lab testing in the U.S.


Plans for Updating
  • - Uncovered topics will be addressed in future guideline documents.
  • - Updated guidelines are anticipated as new therapies focused on functional cure become FDA-approved.
