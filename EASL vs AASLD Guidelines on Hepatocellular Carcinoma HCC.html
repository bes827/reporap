EASL vs AASLD Guidelines on Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
  • - A comparative mind map of the 2024 EASL and 2023 AASLD clinical practice guidelines on HCC management.
  • - EASL Source: Journal of Hepatology, Feb 2025, vol. 82, 315–374
  • - AASLD Source: Hepatology, 2023, vol. 78, 1922–1965
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the recommended threshold for future liver remnant (FLR) in patients with cirrhosis undergoing resection according to AASLD?
  • - What is the 5-year HCC recurrence risk after surgical resection?
  • - In the HIMALAYA trial, what was the 36-month survival rate for the Durvalumab + Tremelimumab (STRIDE) regimen?
  • - According to EASL, what is the key imaging feature difference between HCC and intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma on CEUS?
  • - What is the RETREAT score used for?

Guideline Comparison: Scope & Key Differences
  • - Consensus Areas: Both guidelines endorse BCLC staging, LI-RADS for diagnosis, surveillance for at-risk populations (cirrhosis, high-risk HBV), and combination immunotherapy as first-line systemic therapy.
  • - Key Divergences & Scope Differences:
  • - Adjuvant Therapy: AASLD strongly recommends adjuvant Atezolizumab + Bevacizumab for high-risk patients based on initial IMbrave050 results. EASL is more cautious, noting the RFS benefit was not sustained in later follow-up and does not recommend it.
  • - Surveillance Recall: AASLD provides a specific, detailed algorithm for managing surveillance findings (e.g., US-1, US-2, US-3 lesions).
  • - Imaging Nuances: EASL offers a more in-depth discussion on the comparative evidence for different imaging modalities (e.g., ECA-MRI vs. HBA-MRI) for diagnosis and staging.
  • - Transplant Criteria: AASLD focuses on US-specific criteria (UNOS, MELD, MMaT-3), while EASL's discussion is more generalized.
  • - Advance Care Planning: AASLD includes a dedicated section and guidance statement on Advance Care Planning (ACP).
  • - Locoregional Therapy Evidence: EASL provides a more granular review of RCTs comparing different embolization techniques (cTACE vs DEB-TACE vs TAE) and TACE vs SIRT/EBRT.

Prevention & Surveillance
  • - Primary prevention focuses on managing underlying liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis) to reduce HCC incidence.
  • - Surveillance aims to detect HCC at an early, treatable stage to reduce mortality.
Primary Prevention
  • - Focuses on treating underlying causes of liver disease and modifying lifestyle risk factors.
Antiviral Therapy
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines strongly recommend antiviral therapy for HBV and HCV to reduce HCC risk.
Hepatitis B (HBV)
  • - EASL: NUCs (nucleoside/nucleotide analogues) significantly decrease HCC risk (RR 0.48).
  • - Recommended NUCs (entecavir, tenofovir) are superior to non-first-line NUCs.
  • - Tenofovir may reduce late recurrence risk compared to entecavir (adjusted HR 0.58).

Hepatitis C (HCV)
  • - EASL: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) reduce the risk of cirrhosis and its complications, including HCC.
  • - Impact on HCC recurrence after treatment is inconclusive.
  • - Benefit of reduced decompensation and improved OS likely outweighs potential recurrence risk.


Lifestyle & Chemoprevention
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines recommend lifestyle modifications like alcohol/tobacco cessation and weight management.
Lifestyle Modifications
  • - EASL: Recommends alcohol cessation, tobacco cessation, and weight loss in obesity to reduce liver-related adverse outcomes.
  • - AASLD: Smoking increases HCC risk by 20%-86%, which normalizes after 30 years of cessation.

Coffee Consumption
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines suggest coffee may be recommended due to observational data showing reduced HCC risk.
  • - EASL: Notes a dose-response protective effect, but the effect is much less strong than alcohol/smoking cessation.

Medications (Chemoprevention)
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines state there is insufficient evidence to recommend statins, aspirin, or metformin solely for HCC chemoprevention.
  • - AASLD: Notes these agents can be used if other indications are present.



Surveillance Strategy
  • - Consensus: Surveillance is recommended for patients with cirrhosis and other high-risk groups to improve survival through early detection.
Target Population
  • - Consensus: Patients with cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A/B) and certain non-cirrhotic HBV patients are the primary targets.
AASLD View
  • - Surveillance recommended for HCC incidence ≥1.0% per year in cirrhosis, lower than the traditional 1.5%.
  • - For non-cirrhotic HBV, recommends surveillance for specific groups (e.g., men from endemic country >40y, family history, PAGE-B score ≥10).
  • - Not recommended for Child-Pugh C patients not on transplant list or those with life expectancy 1-2 years.

EASL View
  • - Recommends surveillance for all patients with cirrhosis who are candidates for curative-intent treatment.
  • - Acknowledges emerging risk stratification models (e.g., PAGE-B) to personalize surveillance intensity.

Populations with Insufficient Evidence
  • - Consensus: Routine surveillance is not currently recommended for patients with advanced fibrosis (F3) without cirrhosis (e.g., post-SVR HCV, NAFLD) due to low HCC incidence (~0.5 per 100 person-years).


Surveillance Test & Interval
  • - Consensus: The recommended interval is every 6 months.
Recommended Test
  • - AASLD: Recommends combination of ultrasound (US) + AFP.
  • - US alone has sensitivity of 53% for early HCC.
  • - US + AFP increases sensitivity to 63%.
  • - EASL: Recommends ultrasound as the primary test.
  • - Combined use with AFP is a 'reasonable option' that increases sensitivity but decreases specificity.

Alternative Modalities
  • - Consensus: Routine use of CT or MRI for surveillance is not recommended due to cost, radiation (CT), and capacity issues.
  • - AASLD: Suggests contrast-enhanced MRI may be considered in patients where US is suboptimal (e.g., obesity, parenchymal heterogeneity).
  • - EASL: Notes abbreviated MRI is a promising area of research but awaits results from ongoing trials.

Emerging Biomarkers
  • - Consensus: Other biomarkers (DCP, AFP-L3, GALAD score) are promising but not yet recommended for routine surveillance.
  • - EASL: GALAD score for early HCC showed sensitivity of 73% and specificity of 87% in a meta-analysis, but studies had high selection bias.


Recall & Management of Findings (AASLD)
  • - AASLD provides a specific algorithm for managing surveillance results.
Negative/Benign Screen
  • - US LI-RADS 1 (no lesion) + normal AFP ---> Continue routine 6-month surveillance.

Sub-centimeter Lesion
  • - US LI-RADS 2 (<1 cm lesion) ---> Repeat US + AFP in 3-6 months.
  • - If stable on ≥2 follow-up exams, return to routine 6-month surveillance.

Suspicious Findings
  • - US LI-RADS 3 (≥1 cm lesion) OR AFP ≥20 ng/ml ---> Proceed to diagnostic multiphasic CT or MRI.

Inadequate Visualization
  • - US LI-RADS Visualization B (moderate limits) or C (severe limits) ---> Consider alternative imaging like MRI.




Diagnosis & Staging
  • - Accurate diagnosis and staging are critical for determining prognosis and guiding therapy.
  • - Non-invasive diagnosis is possible in high-risk patients using specific imaging criteria.
Non-Invasive Diagnosis
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines endorse the use of LI-RADS to standardize imaging acquisition, interpretation, and reporting for non-invasive HCC diagnosis in at-risk patients (cirrhosis, chronic HBV).
Diagnostic Criteria (LI-RADS)
  • - Diagnosis is based on major features: APHE (arterial phase hyperenhancement) and washout (portal venous/delayed phase).
  • - Other features include enhancing capsule and threshold growth.
LI-RADS Categories
  • - LR-5 (Definitely HCC): Probability of HCC is ~95-99%. No biopsy needed for treatment decisions.
  • - LR-4 (Probably HCC): Probability of HCC is ~75%. Requires biopsy or close imaging follow-up.
  • - LR-3 (Intermediate Probability): Probability of HCC is ~30%. Requires imaging follow-up in 3-6 months.
  • - LR-M (Malignant, not specific for HCC): High probability of malignancy (93-100%), but only 29-44% are HCC. Biopsy is mandatory.
  • - LR-TIV (Tumor in Vein): Highly specific for HCC.


Choice of Imaging Modality
  • - Consensus: Multiphase CT or dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI are the recommended first-line modalities.
CT vs. MRI
  • - AASLD: MRI has slightly higher sensitivity than CT (82% vs 66%) with similar specificity. Both are equally recommended.
  • - EASL: A prospective trial showed similar sensitivity for MRI (71%) and CT (70%) for nodules 1-3 cm.

MRI Contrast Agents (ECA vs. HBA)
  • - EASL: Recommends extracellular contrast agents (ECA) over hepatobiliary agents (HBA, e.g., gadoxetic acid) for diagnosis.
  • - Head-to-head studies show ECA-MRI has higher sensitivity (78% vs 66%) and accuracy (82% vs 73%) for LR-5 diagnosis.
  • - HBA-MRI is associated with transient respiratory motion artifacts and may identify APHE in fewer cases.

Contrast-Enhanced Ultrasound (CEUS)
  • - Consensus: CEUS is not a first-line diagnostic tool due to lower sensitivity and inability to stage the whole liver.
  • - EASL: Can be used if CT/MRI are inconclusive. Specific criteria for HCC are APHE followed by late (>60s) and mild washout, which helps differentiate from cholangiocarcinoma (often has early, marked washout).



Pathological Diagnosis (Biopsy)
  • - Consensus: Biopsy is required for diagnosis in patients without cirrhosis or chronic HBV, or for indeterminate nodules (e.g., LR-4, LR-M) in at-risk patients.
Biopsy Utility & Technique
  • - AASLD: Biopsy sensitivity is 70-93%, but can be as low as ~60% for tumors <2 cm.
  • - EASL: Recommends percutaneous access under US or CT guidance. Suggests sampling non-tumoral liver to aid diagnosis.

Histological & Molecular Analysis
  • - EASL: Recommends reporting prognostic features like differentiation grade and WHO subtype (e.g., macrotrabecular/massive, progenitor).
  • - A panel of 3 immunostains (glypican-3, glutamine synthetase, HSP70) has 100% specificity for HCC.
  • - Routine molecular analysis is not yet recommended as it does not currently inform therapeutic decisions.


Staging
  • - Consensus: The BCLC (Barcelona Clinic Liver Cancer) classification is the recommended staging system as it incorporates tumor burden, liver function, and performance status.
Staging Workup
  • - Consensus: Initial staging requires contrast-enhanced chest, abdominal, and pelvic scans (CT is often used).
  • - Bone scan/brain MRI are not needed unless symptoms are present.

Role of HBA-MRI in Staging
  • - EASL: Suggests gadoxetic acid-enhanced MRI (HBA-MRI) for patients who are candidates for curative treatments (resection, ablation, transplant).
  • - HBA-MRI has higher sensitivity for detecting small nodules, which may improve local tumor staging and alter treatment plans in up to 13.3% of cases.

Role of PET/CT
  • - Consensus: 18F-FDG-PET/CT is not recommended for routine staging due to low sensitivity (<40% of HCCs show uptake).

Role of AFP
  • - Consensus: AFP should be measured at diagnosis for prognostic information.
  • - An AFP level > 400 ng/ml is consistently associated with worse prognosis across all treatment modalities.


Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Approach
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines strongly emphasize that all patients with HCC should be managed by an MDT.
  • - AASLD: MDT review can alter imaging interpretation in 18.4% of cases and change management plans in 41.7% of patients.
  • - EASL: MDT should include hepatobiliary/transplant surgeon, medical oncologist/hepatologist, radiation oncologist, interventional/diagnostic radiologist, pathologist, and a dedicated nurse.


Treatment
  • - Treatment decisions are guided by BCLC stage, liver function, and patient factors, requiring an MDT approach.
Surgical Therapies: Resection & Transplantation
  • - Surgical interventions are performed with curative intent and include Liver Resection (LR) and Liver Transplantation (LT).
Liver Resection (LR)
  • - Consensus: LR is the treatment of choice for localized HCC in non-cirrhotic patients and in well-selected patients with compensated (Child-Pugh A) cirrhosis.
Patient Selection for LR in Cirrhosis
  • - Consensus: Selection requires a multi-parametric assessment to keep perioperative mortality <3%.
  • - Key factors: liver function (Child-Pugh, ALBI), portal hypertension, and future liver remnant (FLR) volume.
  • - AASLD: Recommends FLR > 40% in patients with cirrhosis.

Contraindications to LR
  • - EASL: Clinically significant portal hypertension (HVPG >10 mmHg) and Child-Pugh B are absolute contraindications to major resections (>2 segments).
  • - However, they are not absolute contraindications for limited resections, especially with minimally invasive surgery (MILS).

Minimally Invasive Surgery (MILS)
  • - Consensus: Laparoscopic or robotic approaches should be favored over open surgery when feasible.
  • - MILS is associated with reduced blood loss, morbidity, and length of hospital stay.

LR vs. Ablation for Small HCC (<2-3 cm)
  • - Consensus: For solitary HCC <2-3 cm, LR and thermal ablation offer comparable survival.
  • - EASL: LR is associated with higher morbidity than ablation. Ablation is preferred if a major hepatectomy would be required.


Liver Transplantation (LT)
  • - Consensus: LT is the optimal treatment for patients with early-stage HCC and decompensated cirrhosis or significant portal hypertension, as it cures both the cancer and the underlying liver disease.
Selection Criteria
  • - Milan Criteria (1 lesion ≤5 cm, or up to 3 lesions each ≤3 cm) is the standard, yielding 5-year survival rates approaching 80%.
  • - AASLD: Discusses US-specific allocation (MMaT-3 MELD exception) and downstaging criteria (UNOS-DS).

Downstaging
  • - Consensus: Patients with HCC beyond Milan criteria can be considered for LT after successful downstaging with locoregional therapies.
  • - EASL: AFP > 1,000 ng/ml is an absolute contraindication to LT. Patients must be downstaged to AFP < 1,000 ng/ml for at least 3 months before listing.
  • - AASLD: For UNOS exception, patients with baseline AFP ≥1000 ng/ml must have it fall below 500 ng/ml.

Bridging Therapy
  • - Consensus: Locoregional therapy is recommended for patients on the waitlist for >6 months to reduce dropout risk.

Living Donor LT (LDLT)
  • - Consensus: LDLT is a viable alternative to deceased donor LT, especially where wait times are long. It reduces waitlist dropout risk.

Post-LT Recurrence
  • - AASLD: Recurrence occurs in 10-15% of patients post-LT. The RETREAT score can stratify 5-year recurrence risk from <3% to 75% based on explant pathology.


Adjuvant & Neoadjuvant Therapy
  • - Aims to reduce the high risk of recurrence after resection/ablation (50-70% at 5 years).
Neoadjuvant Therapy
  • - Consensus: Neoadjuvant therapy is not recommended outside of clinical trials.

Adjuvant Therapy
  • - AASLD: Recommends adjuvant atezolizumab + bevacizumab for high-risk patients after resection or ablation, based on the IMbrave050 trial which met its primary RFS endpoint (HR 0.72).
  • - EASL: Does not recommend this combination, noting that in a later follow-up of IMbrave050, the RFS benefit was not sustained (updated HR 0.90) and OS data was immature.



Locoregional Therapies
  • - Includes percutaneous ablation, intra-arterial embolotherapies, and external beam radiation therapy (EBRT).
  • - Primarily used for BCLC Stage A (unresectable) and B patients.
Ablative Therapies
  • - Consensus: Thermal ablation (radiofrequency or microwave) is the treatment of choice for unresectable early-stage HCC (≤3 cm).
Radiation Segmentectomy (SIRT) & EBRT as Ablative Alternatives
  • - Consensus: For tumors >3 cm or in locations difficult for thermal ablation (e.g., near large vessels, subdiaphragmatic), radiation segmentectomy or EBRT can be considered.
  • - EASL: Radiation segmentectomy with a dose > 190 Gy achieved complete pathological necrosis in 67% of cases.


Transarterial Embolotherapies (BCLC Stage B)
  • - Consensus: TACE is the standard primary option for BCLC Stage B.
TACE Techniques
  • - EASL: RCTs have not shown superiority of DEB-TACE over cTACE. Bland TAE is also a reasonable alternative to cTACE/DEB-TACE.
  • - AASLD: Conventional TACE and DEB-TACE have similar responses and safety profiles.

TACE Refractoriness
  • - Consensus: Patients not responding after 1-2 TACE sessions should be considered for other therapies (e.g., systemic therapy).
  • - Repeated TACE can lead to liver function deterioration (Child-Pugh A to B in 9-14% of cases), precluding future systemic therapy.

TACE Unsuitable Patients
  • - Consensus: Patients with extensive, diffuse, or infiltrative tumors (e.g., beyond Up-to-7 criteria) have poor outcomes with TACE and may be better suited for systemic therapy.


SIRT & EBRT for Intermediate/Advanced HCC
  • - Consensus: For patients with advanced stageHCC due to portal vein invasion, SIRT or EBRT are not recommended as alternatives to modern systemic immunotherapy combinations.
SIRT vs. Systemic Therapy
  • - EASL: Two major RCTs (SARAH, SIRveNIb) failed to show a survival benefit of SIRT over sorafenib.
  • - However, the DOSISPHERE-01 trial showed that personalized dosimetry (targeting >205 Gy to the lesion) significantly improved ORR (71% vs 36%) and OS (26.6 vs 10.7 months) compared to standard dosimetry.

EBRT vs. Systemic Therapy
  • - EASL: The NRG/RTOG1112 trial showed SBRT followed by sorafenib improved OS vs. sorafenib alone (median 15.8 vs 12.3 months), though the result was borderline significant (p=0.0554).


Combination of Locoregional + Systemic Therapy
  • - Consensus: Combining TACE with TKIs (e.g., sorafenib) has not shown a survival benefit and is not recommended.
TACE + Immunotherapy Combinations
  • - EASL: Two recent Phase III trials showed a PFS benefit for adding immunotherapy combinations to TACE.
  • - EMERALD-1: TACE + Durvalumab + Bevacizumab ---> Median PFS 15.0 months vs. 8.2 months for TACE alone (HR 0.77).
  • - LEAP-012: TACE + Pembrolizumab + Lenvatinib ---> Median PFS 14.6 months vs. 10.0 months for TACE alone (HR 0.66).
  • - OS data for both trials are immature. A recommendation awaits final OS results.



Systemic Therapies
  • - Reserved for advanced-stage HCC (BCLC C), TACE-unsuitable/refractory BCLC B, or progression after locoregional therapy.
  • - Consensus: Limited to patients with preserved liver function (Child-Pugh A) and good performance status (ECOG 0-1).
First-Line Therapy
  • - Consensus: Immunotherapy-based combinations are the preferred first-line standard of care.
Preferred Regimens
  • - Atezolizumab + Bevacizumab (IMbrave150): Superior OS vs. sorafenib (median 19.2 vs 13.4 months; HR 0.66).
  • - AASLD: Requires EGD before starting to manage variceal bleeding risk.
  • - Durvalumab + Tremelimumab (STRIDE regimen; HIMALAYA): Superior OS vs. sorafenib (median 16.4 vs 13.8 months; HR 0.78).
  • - Notably improved long-term survival, with a 36-month survival rate of 30.7% vs. 20.2% for sorafenib.

Alternative First-Line Options
  • - Consensus: For patients with contraindications to immunotherapy combinations (e.g., severe autoimmune disease, post-transplant), TKIs like sorafenib or lenvatinib are recommended.

Influence of Etiology
  • - EASL: The choice of systemic therapy should not be influenced by the underlying liver disease etiology (HBV, HCV, non-viral), as subgroup analyses from trials have been inconsistent.


Second-Line Therapy & Beyond
  • - Consensus: There is no high-quality RCT data to guide second-line choices after progression on modern first-line immunotherapy combinations.
AASLD Sequencing Guidance
  • - After Atezo+Bev: Preferred agents are first-line TKIs (sorafenib or lenvatinib).
  • - After Durva+Treme: Preferred agents are first-line TKIs (sorafenib or lenvatinib).
  • - After first-line TKI: Preferred agents are cabozantinib, regorafenib, or ramucirumab (if AFP ≥400 ng/ml).

EASL View
  • - TKIs can be considered in selected patients after progression on ICI-based combinations.


Special Populations
  • - Management in these groups requires careful consideration of risks and benefits.
Child-Pugh B Cirrhosis
  • - Consensus: Systemic therapy is not routinely recommended due to poor outcomes and lack of trial data.
  • - May be considered in carefully selected patients (e.g., Child-Pugh B7) after multidisciplinary discussion.
  • - Median OS in this population is poor, typically 3.6 to 7.6 months.

Post-Transplant Recurrence
  • - Consensus: ICIs are NOT recommended due to a high risk of acute graft rejection (~29%) and associated mortality.
  • - Systemic therapy with TKIs (sorafenib or lenvatinib) can be considered.


Treatment Management
  • - Key questions regarding duration and continuation of therapy.
Treatment Discontinuation
  • - EASL: The consequences of stopping ICIs after a sustained response (e.g., after 2 years) are unknown. The decision can be made in discussion with the patient.

Treatment Beyond Progression
  • - EASL: In the absence of other options, a patient deriving clinical benefit may continue systemic therapy beyond radiological progression, as was allowed in pivotal trials.




Response Assessment & Clinical Trial Endpoints
  • - Standardized criteria are essential for evaluating treatment efficacy in both clinical practice and research.
Radiological Response Criteria
  • - Consensus: The choice of criteria depends on the treatment modality.
Systemic Therapy
  • - Consensus: RECIST v1.1 should be the primary method for assessing response in clinical trials.
  • - mRECIST can be used as a secondary assessment.

Locoregional Therapy
  • - AASLD: mRECIST is the standard tool for assessing response after locoregional therapies as it evaluates viable, enhancing tumor.


Clinical Trial Endpoints
  • - Consensus: Overall Survival (OS) should remain the primary endpoint for phase III RCTs in advanced HCC.
Surrogacy of PFS
  • - EASL: Progression-Free Survival (PFS) is not a robust surrogate for OS.
  • - The relationship is inconsistent across trials (e.g., HIMALAYA met OS but not PFS endpoint; COSMIC-312 met PFS but not OS).



Advance Care Planning (ACP) (AASLD)
  • - AASLD: ACP should be offered to all patients receiving palliative-intent therapy or best supportive care.
  • - Facilitates shared decision-making, incorporates patient values, and can avoid unnecessary or futile treatments.
