Integrating Progressive Cervical Stabilization with Vestibular Rehabilitation for Vestibular Migraine: A Randomized Controlled Trial
  • Source: Research Grant Proposal (R21 Application)
  • Topic: Vestibular Migraine Rehabilitation - Combining Vestibular Therapy with Cervical Stabilization
  • Author(s): Research Team (Principal Investigator and collaborators)
1. Background & Problem
1.1. Migraine as a Public Health Issue
  • Migraine = one of the leading causes of neurological disability worldwide
  • US annual lost workplace productivity: >$13 billion due to absenteeism and impaired performance
  • Prevalence in developed countries: 8–14%
  • Women bear disproportionate burden → greater pain severity, higher disability, more frequent attacks
  • Annual healthcare costs: >$1 billion, with ~80% from female patients
1.2. Vestibular Migraine (VM)
  • Common migraine subtype combining headache with recurrent vertigo, dizziness, and balance impairment
  • Symptoms limit mobility, independence, and social participation
  • Pathophysiology involves abnormal integration of vestibular, visual, and somatosensory inputs
  • Many patients continue experiencing persistent dizziness despite pharmacologic therapy
1.3. Neck Pain & Cervical Dysfunction in Migraine
  • Up to 90% of migraine sufferers report neck pain
  • Neck pain linked to: ↑ headache severity, ↑ disability, ↑ psychological distress
  • Objective impairments in migraine populations:
  • ↓ cervical muscle endurance
  • impaired neuromuscular control
  • altered proprioceptive function
  • Mechanism: trigeminocervical complex → cervical and trigeminal afferents interact → central sensitization
  • Disrupted cervical proprioception interferes with postural control and gaze stability
2. Study Aims & Hypotheses
2.1. Primary Aim
  • Determine whether adding progressive cervical stabilization training (CST) to standard vestibular rehabilitation (VR) results in greater improvement in dizziness at 8 weeks compared with VR alone
2.2. Secondary Aim
  • Examine whether combined intervention (VR + CST) produces greater improvements in balance at 8 weeks
2.3. Exploratory Aim
  • Evaluate whether combined intervention leads to greater reductions in:
  • Headache frequency
  • Headache intensity
  • Migraine-related disability at 8 weeks
2.4. Hypotheses
  • H1: VR + CST will demonstrate greater improvement in Dizziness Handicap Inventory (DHI) at 8 weeks vs VR alone
  • H2: Combined intervention will show greater improvement in Mini-BESTest (balance) at 8 weeks vs VR alone
  • H3: Combined intervention will exhibit greater reductions in monthly headache frequency, VAS intensity, and MIDAS at 8 weeks vs VR alone
3. Study Design Overview
3.1. Trial Type
  • Prospective, randomized controlled exploratory trial
  • Randomization: 1:1 to VR alone or VR + CST
  • Stratification by migraine subtype: chronic vs episodic
  • Masking: outcome assessors and data analysts masked to allocation
3.2. Intervention Duration
  • 8 weeks of supervised treatment
  • Sessions: twice weekly in-person + daily home program
  • Follow-up: 3 months post-randomization
3.3. Participant Groups
VR Alone (Control Group)
  • Standardized vestibular rehabilitation program
  • Includes: gaze stabilization exercises, habituation exercises, progressive balance training
  • Individualized and progressed based on symptom tolerance
VR + CST (Intervention Group)
  • Same vestibular rehabilitation plus progressive cervical stabilization training
  • Cervical components:
  • Deep neck flexor activation
  • Cervical extensor endurance training
  • Cervical motor control retraining with progressive loading
4. Participant Criteria
4.1. Inclusion Criteria
  • Adults ≥18 years old
  • Diagnosis of vestibular migraine per ICHD-3 criteria
  • At least 3 headache days per month
  • Medically cleared to participate in supervised rehabilitation
4.2. Exclusion Criteria
  • Other primary vestibular disorders
  • Cervical radiculopathy or myelopathy
  • Recent cervical spine surgery
  • Uncontrolled neurological or cardiovascular conditions
  • Concurrent rehabilitation targeting cervical or vestibular dysfunction
4.3. Recruitment
  • Sources: neurology clinics, vestibular specialty clinics, community referrals
  • Diagnosis verification by neurologist using ICHD-3 criteria when possible
  • Target: demographically diverse sample
5. Outcome Measures
5.1. Primary Outcome
  • Dizziness Handicap Inventory (DHI) → change at 8 weeks
  • MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference): 18 points
  • Assessment timepoints: baseline, 8 weeks, 3 months
5.2. Secondary Outcomes
  • Balance: Mini-BESTest
  • Headache frequency & intensity: daily headache diaries + visual analog scales (VAS)
  • Migraine disability: MIDAS (Migraine Disability Assessment)
5.3. Exploratory Subgroups
  • Baseline migraine subtype (chronic vs episodic)
  • Presence of neck pain
  • Sex
6. Sample Size & Power
6.1. Calculations
  • MCID for DHI: 18 points
  • Expected effect size: Cohen's d ≈ 0.54–0.75 (based on pilot data and prior trials)
  • Parameters: α = 0.05, power = 80%
  • Required sample per group: ~29 participants
6.2. Target Enrollment
  • Total: 64 participants (32 per group)
  • Accounts for potential attrition
  • Recruitment rate: ~5–6 participants/month over 12 months
6.3. Retention Strategies
  • Financial compensation: $50/week + $50 completion bonus (max $450)
  • Free supervised rehabilitation (no insurance/copayment barriers)
7. Pilot Study Findings
7.1. Pilot Design
  • 20 consecutive patients with vestibular migraine
  • Alternately assigned to VR alone (n=10) or VR + CST (n=10)
  • Treatment period: 12 weeks
7.2. Results Summary
Outcome VR Alone (n=10) Mean ± SD VR + CST (n=10) Mean ± SD Cohen's d p-value 95% CI
DHI −18.3 ± 15.7 −27.1 ± 17.9 0.54 0.21 −22.4 to 4.8
Mini-BESTest +2.6 ± 2.5 +4.3 ± 3.1 0.61 0.18 −3.9 to 0.7
MIDAS −7.1 ± 13.6 −12.4 ± 14.2 0.39 0.36 −16.7 to 6.1
7.3. Key Findings
  • Both groups showed clinically meaningful improvements in dizziness-related disability
  • Combined intervention group exceeded MCID for DHI
  • Between-group difference favored VR + CST but not statistically significant (p=0.21) → likely due to small sample size
  • Moderate effect sizes observed
  • Adherence >85% for supervised and home exercise programs
  • No adverse events occurred
8. Statistical Analysis Plan
8.1. Analysis Framework
  • Intention-to-treat analysis
  • Mixed-effects repeated measures models for primary and secondary outcomes
  • Allows inclusion of participants with incomplete follow-up → reduces bias from missing data
8.2. Feasibility Benchmarks
  • ≥85% completion of 8-week primary endpoint
  • ≥80% completion of 3-month follow-up
  • ≥75% attendance at supervised sessions
  • ≥70% adherence to home exercise
  • No serious intervention-related adverse events
  • <10% discontinuation due to symptom exacerbation
8.3. Effect Size Reporting
  • Cohen's d and 95% confidence intervals calculated
  • Informs future power calculations for fully powered trial
8.4. Sensitivity Analyses
  • Per-protocol analyses conducted if attrition exceeds expectations
8.5. Exploratory Analyses
  • Subgroup differences based on:
  • Migraine subtype (chronic vs episodic)
  • Neck pain status
  • Sex
9. Significance & Rationale
9.1. Clinical Need
  • Current treatments often leave patients with persistent dizziness and balance dysfunction
  • Critical need for effective non-pharmacologic interventions targeting functional disability
9.2. Mechanism-Based Approach
  • Cervical dysfunction is linked to migraine pathophysiology via trigeminocervical convergence
  • These musculoskeletal impairments are modifiable through targeted rehabilitation
  • Standard vestibular protocols rarely include cervical stabilization despite neurophysiological links
9.3. Supporting Evidence
  • Cervical strengthening trials show: ↓ headache intensity, ↓ migraine-related disability, ↑ physical function
  • Resistance exercise in VM reduces dizziness handicap and vertigo severity
  • Exercise at moderate doses ↓ migraine frequency and intensity
9.4. NCCIH Alignment
  • Supports non-pharmacologic, integrative approaches for chronic pain/neurological dysfunction
  • Prioritizes accessible, low-risk interventions that improve function
  • Reduces long-term medication reliance
10. Innovation
10.1. Integrated Rehabilitation Model
  • First randomized trial to test whether progressive cervical stabilization enhances vestibular rehabilitation in VM
  • Targets neurophysiological convergence between cervical and vestibular systems
10.2. Patient-Centered Outcomes
  • Prioritizes dizziness-related disability and balance performance over just headache metrics
  • Expands treatment focus from pain reduction to restoration of sensorimotor function and participation
10.3. Scalable Intervention
  • Uses low-cost, widely available rehabilitation techniques
  • No specialized equipment required
  • Can be implemented in standard physical therapy settings
11. Potential Limitations & Mitigation
11.1. Limitation: Participant Masking
  • Not feasible due to rehabilitation intervention nature
  • Mitigation: Both groups receive active therapy with equal therapist contact; outcome assessors and analysts remain masked
11.2. Limitation: Symptom Heterogeneity
  • Mitigation: Stratified randomization + exploratory subgroup analyses
11.3. Limitation: Exacerbation Risk
  • Mitigation: Graded exercise progression + therapist monitoring
11.4. Limitation: Short Follow-Up
  • Limited to 3 months
  • Mitigation: Enables assessment of short-term durability; informs future longer-term trials
