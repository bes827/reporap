Bilirubin Metabolism: Physiology & Importance
  • Bilirubin processing reflects liver function, specifically its ability to clear metabolic waste.
  • Understanding bilirubin metabolism is essential for recognizing jaundice and liver pathophysiology.
Bilirubin Sources
  • 70% of bilirubin → catabolism of hemoglobin from senescent red blood cells.
  • 10% → ineffective erythropoiesis.
  • 20% → breakdown of heme-containing proteins in hepatocytes.
Unconjugated Bilirubin (UCB)
  • Hydrophobic; must bind albumin in plasma for transport to liver.
  • Albumin binding is crucial for hepatic uptake.
Albumin Role
  • Low albumin levels → impair bilirubin clearance.
  • ↓ binding capacity & affinity for bilirubin → ↓ hepatic uptake.
Conjugated Bilirubin (CB)
  • Hydrophilic; enables biliary excretion w/o transport.
  • Excreted as waste products: stercobilin and urobilin.
Hyperbilirubinemia: Impaired Processing
  • Hyperbilirubinemia occurs w/ bilirubin accumulation.
  • Normal bilirubin level: <1 mg/dL.
  • Clinical features appreciated when bilirubin levels >3 mg/dL.
Clinical Features
  • Jaundice: yellow discoloration of skin, sclera (scleral icterus), mucous membranes.
  • Dark urine: due to ↑ urinary excretion of conjugated bilirubin.
  • Pale (acholic) stools: due to ↓ bilirubin reaching intestines.
Types of Hyperbilirubinemia
  • Differentiating between unconjugated and conjugated hyperbilirubinemia is key for diagnosis.
  • Alterations in bilirubin metabolism lead to various conditions.
Unconjugated Hyperbilirubinemia (UCB)
  • Occurs w/ impaired hepatic bilirubin uptake AND/OR ↑ heme turnover/hemolysis.
  • Key enzyme for glucuronidation is UGT1a1.
Key Enzyme: UGT1a1
  • Uridine diphosphate-glucuronosyltransferase 1a1 (UGT1a1) catalyzes glucuronidation.
  • Glucuronidation: converts unconjugated bilirubin to conjugated bilirubin.
  • Gene mutations → reduced enzyme activity → UCB.
Genetic Conditions
  • Conditions caused by UGT1a1 gene mutations.
Gilbert Syndrome
  • Benign hereditary condition; mild ↓ UGT1a1 enzyme activity (~30% gene activity).
  • Prevalence: ≈3-7% of individuals.
  • Patients generally asymptomatic outside of jaundice.
  • Jaundice commonly occurs w/ stressors: dehydration, fasting, illness, menstruation.
  • Serum bilirubin level: modest, usually <3 mg/dL.
  • Diagnosis: inferred from mild ↑ UCB, clinical picture, rule out hemolysis/liver disease.
  • Genetic testing rarely pursued; condition is benign, self-resolves w/ stressor resolution.
Crigler-Najjar Syndrome
  • Inherited autosomal recessive condition.
  • Results from moderate (Type 2) or complete (Type 1) absence of UGT1a1 enzyme.
  • UCB present @ birth; persistent elevation in serum bilirubin.
Type 1
  • Complete absence of UGT1a1 enzyme.
  • Serum bilirubin range: 20-45 mg/dL.
Type 2
  • Moderate reduction of UGT1a1 enzyme.
  • Serum bilirubin range: 5-25 mg/dL.
Hemolysis
  • ↑ red blood cell turnover → rate of heme metabolism & UCB production exceeds conversion rate.
  • Serum bilirubin levels rarely exceed 4-5 mg/dL in hemolysis disorders.
  • Patients tend to have normal liver function.
Evaluation
  • Blood tests to evaluate for hemolysis: LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count, peripheral blood smear.
Causes
  • Blood transfusion reactions (intra- & extra-vascular hemolysis).
  • Trauma w/ severe injury: induces intravascular hemolysis, ↑ lysis of native & transfused RBCs.
Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia (CB)
  • Occurs w/ intrahepatic AND/OR extrahepatic cholestasis.
  • Intrahepatic cholestasis: conditions impairing hepatic excretion of CB into bile canaliculi.
  • Extrahepatic cholestasis: obstruction of bile ducts outside liver, preventing CB from reaching intestines.
Cholestasis Impact
  • Primary impact: bile acid transport.
  • Also results in CB due to impaired hepatobiliary transport & transport protein function.
  • Metabolism of bilirubin is mostly independent from bile acid metabolism.
Genetic Conditions
  • Rare, benign autosomal inherited disorders.
Dubin-Johnson Syndrome
  • Impaired hepatic excretion of bilirubin due to gene mutations.
  • Results in impaired function of multidrug resistance-associated protein 2 (MRP2) transporter.
  • Hallmark feature: liver appears black due to impaired metabolite excretion.
  • Patients generally asymptomatic outside of mild jaundice; normal LFTs, no hemolysis.
Rotor Syndrome
  • Impaired hepatic uptake of bilirubin due to gene mutations.
  • Results in impaired function of organic anion-transporting polypeptides (OATPs).
  • Some CB enters hepatocytes from bloodstream via OATPs re-uptake.
  • Patients generally asymptomatic outside of mild jaundice; normal LFTs, no hemolysis.
Bilirubin as Liver Dysfunction Indicator
  • Elevated bilirubin can be an early warning signal of hepatic dysfunction.
  • Important clinical clue to a patient’s synthetic dysfunction, informing decision-making in advanced liver disease.
Scoring Systems & Models
  • Several systems leverage bilirubin's role in liver's waste metabolism.
  • Examples found on mdcalc.com.
  • Bilirubin is a key component, alongside abnormal coagulation factors, for assessing synthetic dysfunction.
Take-Home Points
  • Bilirubin metabolism: series of steps following hemoglobin breakdown → excretion of conjugated bilirubin as stercobilin & urobilin.
  • Impaired bilirubin metabolism: occurs w/ intrahepatic & extrahepatic cholestasis → unconjugated AND/OR conjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
  • Clinical features of hyperbilirubinemia: scleral icterus, jaundice, pale stools, dark urine.
  • Elevated bilirubin levels: reflection of liver disease & synthetic dysfunction; key component of commonly used liver disease scoring systems.
