AGA Clinical Practice Update on GI Manifestations and Autonomic or Immune Dysfunction in Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome: Expert Review
  • Purpose: Describe key principles for evaluating + managing patients w/ disorders of gut-brain interaction (DGBI) + hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS) / hypermobility spectrum disorders (HSDs) w/ coexisting postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) +/or mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS).
  • Methods: Expert review commissioned + approved by American Gastroenterological Association (AGA) Institute Clinical Practice Updates Committee.
  • Guidance based on published literature + expert opinion; no systematic reviews performed.
  • No formal ratings for evidence quality/strength.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS) patients have the hypermobile subtype (hEDS)?
  • 2. What is the heart rate increase threshold for diagnosing POTS in adults during an upright posture test?
  • 3. What is the required increase in serum tryptase level from baseline to suggest mast cell activation?
  • 4. What is the Beighton score primarily used for in gastroenterology practice regarding hEDS/HSDs?
  • 5. In a cross-sectional survey of hEDS/HSD individuals, what percentage met diagnostic criteria for disorders of gut-brain interaction (DGBI)?
Introduction to hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS
  • hEDS: Inherited connective tissue disorder → musculoskeletal symptoms, joint hypermobility, tissue fragility.
  • Most common EDS subtype: 80%-90% of EDS patients.
  • Part of larger HSDs group: common pain + joint hypermobility, but not meeting hEDS criteria.
GI Symptoms in hEDS/HSDs
  • GI symptoms frequently reported in hEDS/HSDs.
  • Associated w/ ↓ quality of life + ↑ healthcare utilization.
  • 98% of hEDS/HSD patients in one survey met DGBI criteria.
Overlapping Syndromes
  • Growing literature links hEDS/HSDs w/ POTS + MCAS.
  • Data limited to observational studies, often lacking rigorous diagnostic criteria.
  • Clinicians face challenges in recognizing + treating GI symptoms in these comorbid conditions.
Pathophysiology & Associations
  • Theoretical mechanistic link exists between hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS.
  • Experimental evidence for biological mechanisms is limited + evolving.
  • Clinicians should be aware of observed associations + overlapping GI manifestations (BPA 1).
hEDS/HSDs Mechanisms
  • Skin biopsies in hEDS/HSDs + classical EDS → altered collagen fibril structure.
  • Collagen changes → fibroblast dysfunction w/ altered adhesion + cytoskeletal response.
  • Connective tissue in hEDS is softer + less stiff than controls.
MCAS Mechanisms
  • Mast cells derived from multipotential hematopoietic stem cells.
  • Differentiate into progenitors → migrate into connective tissue + mucosa.
  • Proliferate into mast cells containing granules (histamine, heparin, cytokines, proinflammatory molecules).
Mast Cell Degranulation
  • Early/excessive degranulation → mast cell activation disorders.
  • Causes multisystemic symptoms: skin, soft tissue, GI tract, respiratory tract, cardiovascular system.
  • MCAS: subtype of mast cell activation disorder.
MCAS Definition
  • Characterized by mast cell activation ← triggers (food, heat, emotion, mechanical stimuli).
  • Due to abnormal sensitivity of mast cells w/o mast cell proliferation.
  • Symptoms typically involve ≥ 2 body systems (e.g., GI tract, skin, cardiac, nervous system).
POTS Mechanisms
  • Form of dysautonomia → impaired autonomic responses.
  • Leads to clinical symptoms of orthostatic intolerance.
  • Autonomic nervous system innervates every organ → controls HR, BP, body temperature.
Dysfunction Sites
  • Dysfunction @ various levels: peripheral nerves, autonomic ganglia, spinal cord, brain.
  • Results in cardiac, respiratory, alimentary, urinary, reproductive, ocular, sudomotor defects.
  • Different POTS phenotypes: hypovolemic, neuropathic, primary hyperadrenergic.
POTS in hEDS/HSDs
  • Patients w/ hEDS/HSDs commonly experience autonomic dysregulation + orthostatic intolerance.
  • Mechanistic basis not fully understood; may be confounded by medications (e.g., stimulants).
  • Theorized roles: vascular laxity +/or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Newer evidence implicates possible autoimmune etiologies.
Observed Associations & Prevalence
  • hEDS/HSDs patients may have ↑ incidence of MCAS + POTS vs. general population.
  • Study of 139 MCAS patients w/ refractory GI symptoms: 23.7% had EDS, 25.2% had POTS, 15.1% had both.
  • Small study: 1/3 of POTS patients reported IBS diagnosis.
  • Survey of 616 hEDS/HSDs patients: 37.5% reported POTS diagnosis.
GI Symptoms w/ Comorbid POTS
  • Study investigating hEDS/HSDs: >60% had ≥ 1 GI symptom.
  • Those w/ concomitant POTS more likely to have fibromyalgia, IBS, GERD, or dysmotility.
  • Caveat: hEDS/HSDs diagnosis often not based on 2017 criteria in these studies.
  • MCAS + POTS not always categorically established; further investigation needed.
Mechanisms of DGBI Symptoms
  • Many common DGBI mechanisms likely contribute to GI symptoms in hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS.
  • Mechanisms: altered motility, visceral hypersensitivity, immune activation, altered secretion, disordered central processing.
  • Some evidence suggests unique predisposition to altered sensorimotor functions + central processing.
Autoimmune Dysfunction
  • Possible role for autoimmune dysfunction in common pathogenesis of these conditions.
  • Pasricha et al. demonstrated this recently.
hEDS/HSDs & GI Physiology
  • Changes in collagen structure/extracellular matrix molecules → changes in intestinal motility, tone, sensation, permeability.
  • Retrospective studies: high prevalence of abnormal GI transit + structural/functional pelvic floor abnormalities in EDS.
  • Inconsistent reports: some studies show no differences in gastric emptying/accommodation or anorectal physiology.
  • Prospective studies on GI physiology in hEDS/HSDs are lacking.
POTS & GI Physiology
  • Dysautonomia (incl. POTS) pathophysiology may involve GI motor dysfunction + visceral sensitization.
  • One study (163 patients): 2/3 of POTS patients w/ GI symptoms had abnormal gastric emptying.
  • Retrospective study: ↑ prevalence of delayed small bowel transit + hypocontractility patterns in POTS (wireless motility capsule).
  • Others described impaired gastric myoelectrical activity + central sensitization in POTS.
MCAS & GI Physiology
  • Low-grade inflammation, barrier dysfunction, visceral hypersensitivity may be relevant in MCAS patients w/ DGBI symptoms.
  • Aguilera-Lizarraga et al. demonstrated mast cell activation → local IgE antibodies against dietary antigens → visceral sensitization.
  • Mast cells may modulate nociception in common DGBIs (e.g., IBS, functional dyspepsia).
Clinical Features & Risk Factors
  • When to suspect hEDS/HSDs, POTS, +/or MCAS in DGBI patients.
hEDS/HSDs Diagnostic Criteria (2017)
  • International consensus guideline defines hEDS/HSDs by 3 major criteria (BPA 3).
Criterion 1: Generalized Hypermobility
  • Assessed using the Beighton score (screening tool).
  • Gastroenterologists should consider incorporating Beighton score into practice.
Criterion 2: Connective Tissue Manifestations
  • Presence of ≥ 2 of the following:
  • - Other connective tissue disorder manifestations (e.g., soft/velvety skin, striae, pelvic organ/rectal prolapse in children/men/nulliparous women).
  • - Family history.
  • - Musculoskeletal complications (e.g., joint instability, widespread pain).
Criterion 3: Exclusion of Other Conditions
  • Absence of unusual skin fragility (prompts exclusion of other EDS forms).
  • Exclusion of other heritable/acquired connective tissue disorders.
  • Exclusion of other joint disorders involving hypotonia +/or connective tissue laxity.
POTS Diagnostic Criteria & Symptoms
  • Defined in adults as ↑ HR ≥ 30 beats/min within 10 min of upright posture (- orthostatic hypotension).
  • In adolescents (12-19 yrs): ↑ HR ≥ 40 beats/min.
  • Symptoms of orthostatic intolerance must be present for ≥ 6 months.
  • Symptoms should not be explained by dehydration, medications, diet, primary anxiety, eating disorders, or other medical conditions.
Common POTS Symptoms
  • Palpitations, tremulousness, lightheadedness, fatigue, blurred vision, generalized weakness.
  • Cognitive + bladder dysfunction, GI symptoms are not uncommon.
  • GI symptoms (nausea, abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea, bloating, severe constipation) common.
  • Nausea, pain, early satiety are most predictive of abnormal GI motility.
MCAS Classification & Symptoms
  • Part of a debated global classification: primary, secondary, idiopathic types (Table 1).
  • Generation of vasoactive + inflammatory mediators (histamine, cytokines, chemokines) → allergic reactions + various symptoms.
  • GI symptoms of MCAS often overlap w/ DGBIs (e.g., nausea, vomiting, heartburn, dysphagia, abdominal pain, atypical chest pain, bowel dysfunction).
MCAS Classification (Table 1 Summary)
  • Primary Clonal MCAS: Most mast cells display CD25 + KIT D816V8a mutation detected.
  • - W/ confirmed cutaneous mastocytosis (CM) or systemic mastocytosis (SM).
  • - W/ only 2 minor SM criteria (SM/CM not fulfilled).
  • Secondary MCAS: IgE mediated, or related to other hypersensitivity/immunologic disorder.
  • - Absence of neoplastic mast cells or KIT D816V8 mutation.
  • Idiopathic MCAS: Criteria for MCAS met, but absence of clonal mast cells or IgE-mediated allergy.
Consensus Guideline for MCAS (Table 1 Summary)
  • A) Typical clinical signs of severe, recurrent (episodic) systemic MC activation (≥ 2 organ systems, often anaphylaxis).
  • B) Involvement of MCs documented by biochemical studies: ↑ serum tryptase level from baseline to +20% + 2 ng/mL (preferred marker).
  • C) Response of symptoms to therapy w/ MC-stabilizing agents, drugs against MC mediator production, or drugs blocking mediator release/effects.
Other MCAS Symptoms
  • Fatigue, tinnitus, conjunctivitis, headaches (incl. migraines), brain fog, palpitations, flushing, pruritus, urticaria, myalgias, lymphadenopathy.
Prior Infection as Risk Factor for Dysautonomia
  • Up to 40% of POTS patients self-report viral upper respiratory or GI infection as precipitating event.
  • Association between acute + long COVID-19 + POTS described.
  • Immunologic cause for POTS supported by ↑ prevalence of autoimmune disease in POTS patients + family members.
  • ↑ frequency of nonspecific autoantibodies (e.g., cardiovascular G protein–coupled membrane receptors, ganglionic acetylcholine receptors, antinuclear antibody, Sjogren’s antibodies) + antibodies to norepinephrine, acetylcholine, angiotensin II.
COVID-19 & GI Dysmotility
  • Evidence: COVID-19 virus exhibits neuronal tropism + may induce GI dysmotility via COVID-induced angiotensin converting enzyme 2 depletion.
  • Differences in gut microbiome between controls, POTS, + post-COVID syndrome patients.
  • Microbiota composition associations w/ IBS symptoms.
  • History of infectious insult may prompt consideration for POTS/MCAS, as gastroparesis, functional dyspepsia, IBS also → prior infection.
Diagnostic Evaluation
  • Evaluation of GI symptoms/DGBI in hEDS/HSDs patients w/ POTS +/or MCAS should largely follow general population recommendations (BPA 8).
  • Strategy: positive symptom-based approach, limited noninvasive testing.
  • Careful attention to individual factors (diet, lifestyle, psychological distress) + drugs (opioids) underlying chronic GI symptoms.
Screening for Joint Hypermobility
  • Gastroenterologists should inquire about joint hypermobility.
  • Strongly consider incorporating Beighton score as a screening tool (BPA 3).
  • If screen positive: consider applying 2017 diagnostic criteria for hEDS or refer to specialist.
Celiac Disease Testing
  • Risk of celiac disease ↑ in hEDS/HSDs +/or POTS vs. general population.
  • Serological testing for celiac disease + endoscopic biopsies may be considered earlier in hEDS/HSDs patients w/ various GI symptoms (not just diarrhea) (BPA 9).
Endoscopy Safety
  • Rates of endoscopy-related complications (e.g., perforation) do not appear ↑ among adults w/ hEDS/HSDs (except vascular EDS).
  • Age- + indication-appropriate endoscopic evaluation should be performed.
Opioid Use
  • For chronic pain, opioid medications should be avoided.
  • If already prescribed: manage through careful multidisciplinary approach.
  • Facilitate opioid cessation where possible.
Food-Related Symptoms & Diet Testing
  • Food-related symptoms (incl. exacerbation of dysautonomia + orthostatic intolerance) described in EDS + HSDs.
  • Theorized symptoms → physiologic responses (e.g., splanchnic vasodilation).
  • Insufficient research on prevalence of specific food-mediated mechanisms (e.g., disaccharidase deficiencies) in hEDS/HSDs.
  • Routine testing for carbohydrate maldigestion/malabsorption not supported (BPA 9).
Mast Cell-Mediated Pain
  • Aguilera-Lizarraga et al. described mast cell-dependent mechanisms of abdominal pain → dietary antigens.
  • Conceivable mast cell dysfunction may underlie GI symptoms (incl. visceral hypersensitivity) in hEDS/HSDs.
  • Validated clinical tests for mast cell–mediated abdominal pain are lacking.
  • Limited evidence for MCAS-specific tests (serum tryptase, mast cell staining on biopsies) for isolated GI symptoms w/o generalized mast cell disorder.
GI Motility Testing
  • DGBI may exist along continuum w/ other altered GI sensorimotor functions, relevant in hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS.
  • Retrospective studies: GI dysmotility relatively common in hEDS/HSDs.
  • Enrichment of EDS gene variants described in idiopathic gastroparesis.
  • Reports inconsistent; others failed to show clear ↑ risk of abnormal GI sensorimotor function in hEDS/HSDs.
  • Currently, GI motility testing guided by symptoms + responses to first-line treatments.
  • No clear evidence for alternative approach to motility testing in hEDS/HSDs.
Earlier Motility Testing Considerations
  • Reasonable to consider earlier testing of gastric motor functions (gastric emptying/accommodation) in patients w/ coexisting POTS (BPA 11).
  • Underlying autonomic dysfunction may predispose to gastroparesis or GI motility perturbations.
  • Diagnostic testing for functional defecation disorders (anorectal manometry, balloon expulsion test, defecography) should be considered in hEDS/HSDs w/ lower GI symptoms (e.g., incomplete evacuation) (BPA 10).
  • High prevalence of pelvic floor dysfunction, esp. rectal hyposensitivity, in this population.
POTS Evaluation
  • Evaluation for POTS may be performed w/ appropriate clinical history (BPA 2).
  • When POTS suspected: assess for exaggerated orthostatic tachycardia using postural vital signs (BPA 4).
  • Consider referral for autonomic function testing (tilt table, sudomotor testing) + expanded lab testing w/ autoantibodies if POTS confirmed.
  • Postural vital signs: symptomatic ↑ HR ≥ 30 beats/min w/ 10 min standing during active stand or head-up tilt table test (- orthostasis).
POTS Testing Indications
  • Considered in hEDS/HSDs patients w/ refractory GI symptoms + orthostatic intolerance.
  • After exclusion of medication side effects + attempted lifestyle/behavioral modifications (e.g., adequate hydration, physical exercise).
  • Not required for all hEDS/HSDs patients reporting GI symptoms alone.
MCAS Evaluation
  • Testing for mast cell disorders (incl. MCAS) should be considered in hEDS/HSDs + DGBI patients (BPA 5).
  • Patients must present w/ episodic symptoms suggesting generalized mast cell disorder.
  • Symptoms: visceral/somatic pain, pruritus, flushing, sweating, urticaria, angioedema, wheezing, tachycardia, abdominal cramping, vomiting, nausea, diarrhea, urogynecological + neurological complaints.
  • Involving ≥ 2 physiological systems (e.g., cutaneous, GI, cardiac, respiratory, neuropsychiatric).
MCAS Testing Limitations
  • Current data do not support routine use of these tests for GI symptoms in all hEDS/HSDs patients.
  • No clinical/laboratory evidence of primary/secondary mast cell disorder.
  • If MCAS suspected: diagnostic testing w/ serum tryptase levels collected @ baseline + 1-4 hours following symptom flares (BPA 6).
  • ↑ of 20% above baseline + 2 ng/mL necessary to demonstrate mast cell activation.
Referral for Confirmed MCAS
  • If MCAS diagnosis supported by clinical +/or laboratory features: refer to allergy specialist or mast cell disease research center (BPA 7).
  • Additional testing may be performed: urinary N-methylhistamine, leukotriene E4, 11b-prostaglandin F2.
Mast Cell Staining on Biopsies
  • Some proposed counting mast cells per high power field from intestinal biopsies.
  • Threshold distinguishing normal from abnormal mast cell counts is debated.
  • CD-117 immunohistochemical staining thought to be more sensitive marker in duodenum (second portion) or ileum.
  • Consensus for these guidelines, even among allergists, is controversial.
Management Strategies
  • Few clinical trials guide management of GI symptoms in hEDS/HSDs, POTS, or MCAS.
  • Management largely follows general DGBI treatment principles.
  • Addition of specific treatments for orthostatic symptoms in POTS + mast cell activation symptoms.
  • Management should be supportive + symptom-focused.
  • Follow principles of integrated/multidisciplinary care, focusing on multisystemic nature of conditions (BPA 12).
General Principles
  • Avoidance/cessation of opioids in pain-predominant features.
  • Psychological support w/ brain-gut behavioral therapies.
  • ↑ rates of anxiety + vulnerabilities to psychological distress in hypermobility, possibly mediated by autonomic dysfunction.
GI Symptom Management (Table 2 Summary)
  • Focus on treating most prominent GI symptoms + abnormal GI function test results.
  • Similar to DGBI + GI motility disorder management.
Nausea/Vomiting
  • Antiemetics: ondansetron, promethazine, prochlorperazine, aprepitant, off-label carbidopa.
  • Prokinetics: metoclopramide, domperidone, erythromycin, prucalopride, pyridostigmine (off-label prucalopride).
  • Complementary medicine: aromatherapies, ginger tea, STW5.
  • Caution: monitor for QT prolongation w/ multiple therapeutic agents.
Abdominal Pain
  • Acid suppression: PPIs, H2RAs.
  • Antispasmodics: hyoscyamine, dicyclomine, peppermint oil.
  • Neuromodulators: TCAs, SSRIs, SNRIs, neuroleptics, anticonvulsants.
  • Psychological therapies: CBT, hypnotherapy, relaxation therapies.
  • Caution: opiates should not be used specifically to treat abdominal pain.
Constipation
  • Fiber supplements: psyllium, methyl cellulose.
  • Osmotic laxatives: PEG 3350, lactulose, milk of magnesia.
  • Stimulant laxatives: bisacodyl, senna.
  • Chloride channel activator: lubiprostone.
  • Guanylate cyclase-C agonists: linaclotide, plecanatide.
  • 5-HT4 agonist: prucalopride.
  • Sodium hydrogen exchanger 3 inhibitor: tenapanor.
Diarrhea
  • Dietary modification: low FODMAP, gluten-free, soluble fiber.
  • Microbiome modification: rifaximin, Bifidobacterium infantis 35624.
  • Antidiarrheals: loperamide, diphenoxylate.
  • Bile acid sequestrants: cholestyramine, colestipol, colesevelam.
  • Mixed opioid receptor agonist/antagonist: eluxadoline (contraindicated w/ ≥ 3 alcoholic beverages/day, post-cholecystectomy, moderate-to-severe hepatic insufficiency).
  • 5-HT3 receptor antagonist: alosetron (female patients only).
  • Cholinesterase inhibitors: pyridostigmine.
Pelvic Floor Symptoms
  • Common in hEDS/HSDs.
  • Treat according to general principles for anorectal disorders.
POTS Treatment
  • Treatment of orthostatic symptoms (may include GI symptoms) (BPA 13).
  • Lifestyle: ↑ fluid (2 L/d) + salt intake (10 g/d), exercise training, compression garments.
  • Pharmacological treatment for severe cases: fludrocortisone, midodrine, beta-blockers.
  • Integrated care w/ multiple specialties (e.g., cardiology, neurology) for non-responders to conservative measures.
POTS Pharmacological Treatments (Table 2 Summary)
  • Blood volume expanders: fludrocortisone, desmopressin, erythropoietin.
  • Heart rate lowering agents: propranolol, ivabradine.
  • Central nervous system sympatholytics: clonidine, methyldopa.
  • Other: midodrine, pyridostigmine, droxidopa, modafinil.
MCAS Treatment
  • When MCAS suspected (allergic reactions + characteristic symptoms) (BPA 14).
  • First-line: nonsedating H1 histamine + H2 histamine receptor antagonists.
  • Second-line: oral cromolyn mast cell stabilizers +/or leukotriene receptor antagonists.
  • Avoid triggers: certain foods, alcohol, strong smells, temperature changes, mechanical stimuli (e.g., friction), emotional distress (e.g., pollen, mold), specific medications (e.g., opioids, NSAIDs, iodinated contrast).
  • Response rates to antimediator treatment estimated @ 75% for diarrhea, 82% for abdominal pain (unreliable estimates, expert opinion-based).
  • Role of immunotherapy remains under active study.
MCAS Pharmacological Treatments (Table 2 Summary)
  • H2 receptor antagonist: famotidine, nizatidine, ranitidine.
  • Second-generation H1 antagonist: cetirizine, levocetirizine, fexofenadine, loratadine.
  • Mast cell stabilizer: cromolyn sodium, ketotifen (not FDA approved, can be compounded).
  • Leukotriene receptor antagonist: montelukast.
  • Corticosteroids or immunoglobulins for autoimmunity (refer to allergy/immunology or mast cell specialists).
Nutritional Support & Diet
  • Special attention to nutrition + hydration; dietitian involvement frequently needed.
  • Screen for weight loss + disordered eating.
  • Special diets for symptom improvement frequently used, but efficacy studies lacking (BPA 15).
Specific Diets
  • Gastroparesis/small particle diet: advised if upper GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting) predominate.
  • Modified food composition, consistency, volume.
  • Lower GI symptoms: dietary advice for IBS + other bowel disorders (e.g., low fermentable carbohydrates, wheat-/gluten-free, low-histamine diets).
  • Efficacy in hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS requires further study.
  • Dietary interventions w/ appropriate nutritional counseling/guidance to avoid restrictive eating pitfalls.
Severe Symptoms & Malnutrition
  • For severe symptoms, weight loss, or dehydration despite optimal medical management:
  • Parenteral fluid +/or enteral/parenteral nutrition sometimes necessary as stabilizing measure.
  • Administer through multidisciplinary model.
MCAS Trigger Avoidance
  • Reduce exposure to triggers: physical sensitivities (temperature, UV radiation), antigenic sensitivities (pollen, mold), food/alcoholintolerances.
  • Low-histamine, gluten-free, dairy-free diets recommended based on clinical experience (formal studies absent).
Management w/o POTS/MCAS
  • Management of chronic GI symptoms in hEDS/HSDs patients w/o POTS or MCAS symptoms should align w/ existing DGBI + GI motility disorder approaches in general population (BPA 16).
  • Includes integrated multidisciplinary care involving multiple specialties (e.g., cardiology, rheumatology, dietician, psychology).
Summary & Future Directions
  • Accumulating evidence suggests DGBI/GI motility disorders, hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS may be linked.
  • Mechanisms explaining associations not fully understood.
  • Gastroenterology providers should be aware of hEDS/HSDs, POTS, MCAS features.
  • Recognize full complexity of patients w/ multisystemic symptoms.
  • Offer informed counseling + guide patients away from unreliable sources/fragmented care.
  • Foster therapeutic relationships + evidence-based care.
Key Considerations for Providers
  • Early diagnostic testing of gastric motor or anorectal functions may be considered.
  • Treatment of POTS symptoms.
  • Use of histamine receptor antagonists.
  • Referral to allergy specialist or mast cell research center may be considered in some cases.
  • Overall approach should follow general recommendations for DGBI + GI motility disorder management.
Future Research Needs
  • Identify biological mechanisms connecting these disorders.
  • Develop new diagnostic tools.
  • Identify novel therapeutics (e.g., immunotherapy).
  • Optimize care + improve long-term outcomes.
