Hemorrhoids
  • - Normal variations of the inferior hemorrhoidal plexus (anal cushions) contributing to the anal barrier.
  • - The third most common outpatient gastrointestinal diagnosis in the United States.
Classification & Pathophysiology
  • - Internal Hemorrhoids: Originate above the dentate line, covered with rectal columnar epithelium.
  • - External Hemorrhoids: Originate below the dentate line, covered by anoderm and skin.
Locations
  • - Internal hemorrhoids primarily occur in the right anterior, right posterior, and left lateral locations.

Pathophysiology
  • - Deterioration of anchoring connective tissue due to age, straining, or pregnancy causes hemorrhoids to bulge and descend.
  • - Association with diarrhea has been noted in some studies, challenging the traditional link to only constipation.


Clinical Features & Grading
  • - Symptoms vary by grade and presence of complications.
Grading of Internal Hemorrhoids (See Figure 1)
  • - Stage I: Cause bleeding but do not prolapse.
  • - Stage II: Prolapse beyond the anal margin but reduce spontaneously.
  • - Stage III: Prolapse requires manual reduction.
  • - Stage IV: Prolapse is irreducible.

Symptoms
  • - Bleeding: Typically bright red, occurring after defecation.
  • - Prolapse Symptoms: Sensation of a lump, discomfort, difficulty wiping, mucous discharge, fecal seepage, pruritus ani.
  • - Significant Pain: Suggests strangulation or a thrombosed external component. Uncomplicated internal hemorrhoids are not painful.

External Hemorrhoids
  • - May thrombose, presenting as an acutely painful lump at the anal verge.
  • - Resolution may leave skin tags.


Diagnosis
  • - Visual inspection and digital rectal examination are key first steps.
  • - Anoscopy is used to diagnose internal hemorrhoids.
Excluding Malignancy
  • - Hemorrhoids should not be presumed to be the sole cause of bleeding.
  • - Perform flexible sigmoidoscopy or colonoscopy to exclude cancer, especially with alarm symptoms (blood mixed with stool, change in bowel habits, weight loss).

Differential Diagnosis for Acute Anal Pain
  • - Main differentials: anal fissure, perianal abscess, and thrombosed external hemorrhoids.
  • - Severe unexplained pain warrants urgent examination under anesthesia.


Elective Treatment
  • - Treatment is based on the grade of hemorrhoids and symptom severity (See Figure 1).
Conservative Management
  • - Appropriate for all grades, especially grades I-II.
  • - High-fiber diet: Improves symptoms and reduces bleeding.
  • - Other measures (sitz baths, proper hygiene, topical preparations) have scarce supporting evidence.

Phlebotonics
  • - Plant-derived agents (e.g., diosmin, troxerutin) available as dietary supplements in the US.
  • - Cochrane Review showed improvement in: bleeding (OR, 0.12), pruritus (OR, 0.23), and discharge/leakage (OR, 0.12).
  • - No significant improvement in pain.

Office-Based Procedures
  • - Next step for grades I-II, and some grade III hemorrhoids.
Rubber Band Ligation (RBL)
  • - Recommended for grades I, II, and some III hemorrhoids.
  • - Bands are placed at least 1â€“2 cm above the dentate line to avoid somatic pain.
  • - Rare complications (~1%): severe bleeding (typically 3-14 days post-procedure), perianal sepsis, vasovagal reactions.
Contraindications & Precautions
  • - Contraindicated in patients on warfarin and those with portal hypertension.
  • - ASCRS recommends considering pausing antiplatelet/anticoagulation therapy after weighing risks.
  • - Relatively contraindicated in immunocompromised patients due to sepsis risk.


Other Procedures
  • - Sclerotherapy: Common outside the US for grades I-II. Good short-term relief but high recurrence rate. Sclerosants are used off-label in the US.
  • - Infrared Coagulation: No significant difference in effectiveness compared to RBL, but RBL causes more post-op pain.
  • - Bipolar Electrocoagulation: RBL is more effective and requires fewer sessions.


Surgical Treatment
  • - Reserved for higher-grade hemorrhoids or failure of less invasive treatments.
Excisional Hemorrhoidectomy
  • - Standard of care for grade IIIâ€“IV internal hemorrhoids and mixed hemorrhoids.
  • - Offers excellent long-term outcomes with a much lower retreatment rate than RBL (RR, 0.20).
  • - Associated with significant postoperative pain and higher complication rates.
Post-op Pain Management
  • - Best managed with a multimodal regimen including topical anesthetics and diltiazem.
  • - Oral metronidazole is not superior to placebo for post-hemorrhoidectomy pain.


Stapled Hemorrhoidopexy
  • - Less postoperative pain and faster recovery than excisional hemorrhoidectomy.
  • - Associated with higher long-term recurrence rates (32% vs 14% for excisional).
  • - Serious complications reported: rectovaginal fistula, rectal perforation, sepsis, and death.

Newer Procedures
  • - Aim to avoid drawbacks of excisional surgery.
Transanal Hemorrhoidal De-arterialization (THD)
  • - Doppler-guided ligation of terminal branches of the superior rectal artery.
  • - Pooled recurrence rate was 17.5% across 28 studies.
  • - 1-year recurrence was lower than RBL (30% vs 49%) in one large RCT.

Radiofrequency Ablation (Rafaelo)
  • - FDA-approved procedure.
  • - Meta-analysis reported a 4.8% recurrence rate and a 17.6% complication rate for grades II-III hemorrhoids.




Emergency Treatment
  • - For acute, painful presentations.
Thrombosed External Hemorrhoid
  • - Most resolve with nonoperative management (sitz baths, stool softeners, topicals).
  • - Surgical excision (not just incision) should be considered if patient presents within 48â€“72 hours of onset for immediate pain relief.

Strangulated Hemorrhoids
  • - Caused by irreducible prolapse with vascular compromise.
  • - Requires inpatient-based management due to risk of gangrene and life-threatening infection.
  • - Treatment ranges from expectant management to emergency excisional surgery.



Anal Fissure
  • - A linear or oval break in the squamous epithelium of the distal anal canal.
Characteristics & Pathophysiology
  • - Location: Most common in the posterior midline (85%â€“90%) or anterior midline (10%â€“15%).
  • - Symptoms: Sharp, severe pain during and after defecation, with scant bright-red rectal bleeding.
Acute vs. Chronic Fissure
  • - Acute: Lasts less than 6 weeks; appears as a simple longitudinal tear.
  • - Chronic: Has additional features like raised edges, exposed internal sphincter fibers, external skin tags (sentinel piles), or internal hypertrophied anal papillae.

Pathophysiology
  • - Considered a nonhealing 'ischemic ulcer' due to increased sphincter tone and decreased anodermal blood flow.
  • - Posterior location is a watershed area for blood supply with less muscular support.


Diagnosis
  • - Diagnosis is made by gentle inspection of the anus.
  • - Digital rectal exam and anoscopy are often excruciatingly painful and should be avoided initially.
Atypical Fissures
  • - Off-midline fissures are atypical and require further investigation.
  • - Must be biopsied to rule out secondary causes like Crohnâ€™s disease, HIV/AIDS, leukemia, or anal carcinoma.
  • - An examination under anesthesia is necessary if a fissure is not visible or is off-midline.


Treatment
  • - Treatment approach differs for acute and chronic fissures.
Acute Fissure Treatment
  • - Most heal quickly with conservative measures.
  • - Management: Optimize bowel habits, warm sitz baths, topical anesthetic creams (e.g., lidocaine).
  • - Acute fissures should not be treated surgically.

Chronic Fissure Treatment (See Figure 2)
  • - Usually requires intervention for healing and to prevent recurrence.
First-Line: Topical Therapy
  • - Mechanism: Reduce internal anal sphincter (IAS) tone to improve blood flow.
  • - Agents: Topical calcium channel blockers (CCBs) or nitrates (nitroglycerin).
  • - ASCRS Recommendation: Topical calcium channel blockers are first-line therapy.
Efficacy & Side Effects
  • - Nitroglycerin healing rates are ~60% (vs 40% placebo).
  • - CCBs are at least as effective as nitroglycerin but with a much lower risk of headache (7% vs 56%).


Second-Line: Botulinum Toxin (BT)
  • - Considered if 8 weeks of topical therapy fails.
  • - Dose: Typically 50â€“100 IU injected into the IAS.
  • - Efficacy: Data is mixed and subsequent RCTs are less conclusive than early trials.
  • - Recurrence: High, with rates of 50% at 1 year and 80% at 10 years reported.

Third-Line: Lateral Internal Sphincterotomy (LIS)
  • - The gold standard operation for chronic anal fissures when topical agents fail.
  • - Healing Rate: Approximately 90%, one of the most effective operations in surgery.
  • - Outcome: Low recurrence and long-term continence is generally preserved.




Dyssynergic Defecation (DD)
  • - A condition characterized by uncoordinated muscle movements during attempted defecation.
Epidemiology & Pathogenesis
  • - Incidence is higher in women (31.8 per 100,000) than men (6.6 per 100,000).
  • - A substantial proportion of adults with laxative-refractory constipation have DD.
Pathophysiology
  • - Core Problem: Inadequate rectal propulsive force and/or impaired anal relaxation during defecation.
  • - Most patients have both inadequate propulsion and pelvic floor spasticity.
  • - Risk factors include childhood trauma, abuse, anxiety, depression, opioids, Parkinson's disease, and diabetes.


Clinical Features & Investigations
  • - Symptoms like incomplete emptying, excess straining, and digitation are more predictive than stool frequency.
  • - Anorectal physiology tests are required for diagnosis as symptoms are not specific.
Anorectal Manometry (ARM)
  • - Measures anorectal pressures at rest, squeeze, and evacuation.
  • - Key Finding in DD: A reduced (less positive or more negative) rectoanal gradient during evacuation.
  • - A reduced gradient is 36% sensitive and 85% specific for predicting a prolonged BET.

Rectal Balloon Expulsion Test (BET)
  • - Measures time to expel a balloon filled with 50 mL of warm water.
  • - A prolonged BET is a highly sensitive and specific index for diagnosing DD.
  • - A BET of â‰¤60 seconds is considered normal for most catheters.

Defecography (Fluoroscopic or MR)
  • - Performed for inconclusive ARM/BET results or suspected structural abnormalities.
  • - Can reveal rectoceles, intussusception, or enteroceles.
  • - Clinical Pearl: Small rectoceles (<4 cm) and intrarectal intussusception are common even in asymptomatic people.


Management
  • - Requires a multidisciplinary approach focusing on improving symptoms and quality of life.
Anorectal Biofeedback Therapy (BFT)
  • - Cornerstone of managing DD.
  • - Effective in approximately 70%â€“80% of patients in randomized trials.
  • - Superior to laxatives, sham therapy, and standard therapy.
Mechanism & Practice
  • - Involves coaching patients with visual feedback from electromyography or manometry (See Figure 3).
  • - Teaches patients to protrude the abdominal wall (diaphragmatic descent) while monitoring rectal pressure, aiming for >45 mm Hg.
  • - Includes abdominal breathing exercises and rectal sensory retraining for altered sensation.

Availability
  • - Demand for BFT far exceeds its availability in the US.
  • - Often provided by pelvic floor physical therapists, but only 50% incorporate biofeedback techniques.

Predictors of Better Outcomes
  • - Hard stools
  • - Anal digitation
  • - Strong patient engagement
  • - Prolonged BET


Adjunct & Refractory Management
  • - Nonpharmacologic strategies are safe and likely beneficial.
Lifestyle & Behavioral Strategies
  • - Encourage adequate intake of fluids, calories, and fiber.
  • - Regular exercise.
  • - Heed the call to defecate promptly.
  • - Attempt defecation 30 minutes after breakfast to utilize the gastrocolonic response.

Management of BFT-Refractory DD
  • - Options are limited.
  • - Sacral neuromodulation (SNM) and pelvic floor injection of BT are not effective.
  • - Surgery may be considered for large, non-emptying rectoceles or enteroceles, but correcting structure does not always correct function.




Fecal Incontinence (FI)
  • - Defined as the unintentional loss of solid or liquid stool.
  • - Affects approximately 1 in 12 adults.
Epidemiology & Pathophysiology
  • - More prevalent in people aged >60 years (9.3%) and in women (9.1%) than men (7.4%).
Pathophysiology
  • - Continence is multifactorial, depending on motility, stool consistency, rectal function, and the anal barrier.
  • - Common dysfunctions: anal sphincter weakness, stiff/small rectum, altered rectal sensation.
  • - Leading risk factors in women: aging and obstetric trauma.


Clinical Features & Investigations
  • - Clinicians should assess severity, duration, risk factors, and impact on quality of life.
  • - A stool diary is more accurate than patient recall.
Types of FI
  • - Urge FI: Preserved (often increased) awareness before leakage; linked to rectal urgency.
  • - Passive FI: Occurs without awareness.
  • - Many patients have both types.

Investigations (See Figure 5)
  • - A stepwise approach is recommended, starting with conservative therapy.
  • - Evaluate for diarrhea triggers (celiac disease, microscopic colitis, bile acid malabsorption).
  • - ARM/BET: Can identify anal weakness, DD, and rectal sensory disturbances to tailor BFT.
  • - Endoanal Ultrasound/MR Imaging: Can detect sphincter defects, scars, or atrophy.


Management
  • - Begins with conservative measures, escalating to BFT and then surgical options if needed.
Diet, Lifestyle & Pharmacotherapy
  • - Address modifiable risk factors: low fiber, bowel dysfunction, smoking, physical inactivity, increased BMI.
Dietary Measures
  • - Psyllium 15 g/d reduces loose or liquid FI compared with placebo.
  • - Limiting caffeine, alcohol, fatty foods, fructose, and lactose may be helpful.

Pharmacotherapy
  • - Loperamide: Increases stool consistency and anal pressure, slows transit.
  • - Bile Acid Sequestrants: Effective for diarrhea due to bile acid malabsorption.
  • - Intrarectal Botulinum Toxin (200 U): Reduced combined FI and urgency episodes vs placebo in urge-predominant FI.


Behavioral & Barrier Treatments
  • - Focus on improving sphincter function and providing physical barriers.
Biofeedback Therapy (BFT)
  • - Aims to improve strength and endurance of anal sphincter contraction.
  • - More effective than pelvic floor exercises alone in patients unresponsive to initial therapy.
  • - In a large study, two-thirds of patients reported a â‰¥50% reduction in FI frequency at 6 weeks.
  • - Home-based BFT was not inferior to office-based therapy.

Barrier Devices
  • - Transanal irrigation may benefit patients with neurogenic bowel dysfunction.
  • - Newer anal and vaginal insert devices seem more effective than older plugs.
  • - An FDA-approved vaginal device led to a â‰¥50% reduction in FI in 70% of users at 12 months.


Surgical Options
  • - Considered for refractory FI.
Sacral Neuromodulation (SNM)
  • - Preferred surgical option, especially with reduced anal contractility or overactive bladder.
  • - Long-term success (â‰¥50% reduction in FI episodes) is approximately 50% at 5â€“10 years.
  • - Requires lifelong device management; surgical revision required in up to one-third of patients.

Anal Sphincteroplasty
  • - Typically reserved for younger women (<45 years) with obstetric defects.
  • - Long-term 'good or excellent' continence rates are low (mean 38%).
  • - Risks include wound infection/breakdown (~20%) and iatrogenic fistula (~5%).

Other Options
  • - Anal Dextranomer Injections: Improve continence vs placebo, but not vs BFT.
  • - Colostomy: Remains an option for severe, refractory FI.




Conclusions & Future Directions
  • - Benign anorectal diseases are common and associated with bowel symptoms and/or anorectal sensorimotor dysfunctions.
  • - A stepwise approach is recommended, starting with simple treatments and escalating as needed.
  • - Surgery may be required for hemorrhoids, and less commonly for fissures and FI.
Key Topics for Future Research
  • - Clarify the natural history of these diseases.
  • - Define the role of DD in hemorrhoids and anal fissures.
  • - Devise more effective and accessible therapies (e.g., for fissures and DD).
  • - Evaluate long-term efficacy of stepwise treatments.
  • - Develop individualized therapies guided by predictive biomarkers.
