IBD Overview & Monitoring Challenges
  • - Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), including Crohnâ€™s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), is a chronic GI inflammatory disorder.
  • - Leads to significant morbidity: bowel obstructions, fistulas, and cancer.
  • - Primary treatment goal is to reverse endoscopically visible inflammation to achieve long-term clinical remission.
Limitations of Current Monitoring
  • - Clinical remission does not always correlate with endoscopic healing.
  • - Frequent endoscopies are burdensome for patients.
  • - Biomarkers like fecal calprotectin (FC) and C-reactive protein can be challenging to access.

Emergence of Intestinal Ultrasound (IUS)
  • - IUS is a valuable tool for objectively assessing and monitoring IBD activity.
  • - It offers a noninvasive, radiation-free alternative to endoscopy and other imaging.
  • - Provides real-time, high-resolution examination of the bowel wall, mesentery, and adjacent structures.
  • - Facilitates point-of-care decisions and timely treatment adjustments.


Intestinal Ultrasound (IUS) Technique
  • - IUS is a transabdominal examination of the colon and small intestine.
  • - It is performed without bowel preparation, fasting, or intravenous/oral contrast.
Equipment
  • - High-resolution ultrasound system.
  • - Low-frequency convex transducer for initial global assessment.
  • - High-frequency linear transducer for detailed disease activity evaluation.
Special Considerations
  • - In patients with a small body habitus and in children, a linear transducer alone is often adequate.


Key Measures of Disease Activity
  • - Primary measures: Bowel Wall Thickness (BWT) and Bowel Wall Hyperemia (using color Doppler signal).
  • - Indicators of more severe disease include inflammatory mesenteric fat, loss of bowel wall stratification, and surrounding lymphadenopathy.

Identifiable Complications
  • - Strictures
  • - Fistulae
  • - Sinus tracts
  • - Phlegmons
  • - Abscesses


IUS vs. Alternative Modalities
  • - IUS, performed by well-trained practitioners, is comparable in accuracy to MRE and CTE for defining disease activity of the ileum (See Table 1).
vs. MRE & CTE
  • - MRE and CTE are the cross-sectional imaging modalities of choice for small intestinal CD.
  • - IUS has practical advantages: no contrast, no radiation (vs. CTE), and can be performed in-clinic for real-time decision making.
Inter-observer Reliability
  • - IUS demonstrates moderate to excellent inter-observer reliability.
  • - A sub-study showed higher agreement for serial IUS compared to serial MRE.
  • - IUS agreement: 82% (kappa 0.61-0.64).
  • - MRE agreement: 69% (kappa 0.36-0.56).

Complication Detection
  • - A meta-analysis shows that IUS, MRE, and CTE have comparable accuracy for detecting IBD-related complications like fistula, stricture, and abscesses.


vs. Endoscopy (Ileocolonoscopy & VCE)
  • - Endoscopy Advantages: Direct mucosal visualization, ability to perform biopsies or dilations.
  • - Endoscopy Disadvantages: More time-intensive, expensive, often requires sedation, and examination is limited to the terminal ileum and colon.


Clinical Applications of IUS
  • - IUS has multiple roles in clinical care, from screening to monitoring treatment response.
Screening & Diagnosis
  • - Can be used as a screening tool, similar to FC, to rule out IBD.
  • - Positive findings (increased BWT or hyperemia) can help expedite ileocolonoscopy for diagnosis.

Monitoring Disease Activity

Crohn's Disease (CD)
  • - Treatment response on IUS can be identified within 3 months of starting biologic therapy.
  • - Treatment failure prediction: Patients with a higher baseline BWT are 42% less likely to achieve transmural healing at 1 year.

Special Populations
  • - IUS is particularly valuable in patient groups where other modalities are challenging.
Pregnant Patients
  • - Effective for recognizing flares and monitoring treatment response.
  • - Avoids radiation from CTE and sedation for ileocolonoscopy.
  • - Shows a sensitivity of 84% and specificity of 98% correlating with clinical activity and FC.
  • - The colon and terminal ileum can be visualized in up to 92% of cases in the third trimester.

Patients with Serious Comorbidities
  • - Plays a key role for patients with conditions like renal failure who are high-risk for sedation or cannot receive IV contrast.

Obese Patients
  • - While image quality may be limited, a recent study showed no significant difference in IUS findings by weight.
  • - Obesity does not preclude the use of IUS.



Patient Experience & System-Level Benefits
  • - IUS offers a more patient-centered approach and can address systemic issues in healthcare.
Patient-Centered Care
  • - Qualitative research shows patients prefer noninvasive monitoring options like IUS.
  • - Patients receiving IUS-driven care reported more confidence in making informed decisions about their disease management.

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion
  • - Point-of-care IUS can improve access for patients in rural and underserved areas.
  • - Helps overcome barriers such as scheduling, finding dependent care, or travel for CTE, MRE, or ileocolonoscopy.

Environmental Sustainability
  • - IUS requires fewer plastic and paper supplies compared to colonoscopy or MRE.
  • - Use of non-reusable plastic is limited to gel bottles, reducing healthcare-related waste.


IUS Training & Implementation
  • - Integrating IUS into clinical practice requires careful consideration of equipment, workflow, and training.
Equipment & Image Storage
  • - System choice depends on clinical need, budget, and portability (full-scale vs. portable/hand-held).
  • - Image storage options include local hard drives, institutional servers, or cloud-based solutions (must be HIPAA compliant).

Clinical Workflow Models
  • - Consecutive appointments: IUS performed just before a clinic visit.
  • - Integrated visit: IUS performed by the clinician during the visit.
  • - Standalone IUS clinic: Patients are referred by other healthcare professionals.

Billing
  • - Specific Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes for IUS are not yet available.
  • - Other codes, such as 76705 (limited abdominal ultrasound), can be used.

Training & Certification
  • - There is no official United States-based certification for gastroenterologists yet.
  • - The International Bowel Ultrasound Group (IBUS) offers a comprehensive training and certification program.
IBUS Certification Process
  • - Module 1: Intensive introductory course with fundamental knowledge and hands-on experience.
  • - Module 2: Hands-on training with at least 40 patients at a certified IBUS training center.
  • - Module 3: Advanced workshop and a final exam.



Limitations of IUS
  • - Despite its versatility, IUS has several limitations.
Technical Limitations
  • - Precise measurement of disease length is difficult in extensive small bowel CD; MRE is better for this.
  • - Overlying bowel gas may obscure proximal (duodenal) disease.
  • - Rectal disease may be difficult to visualize without a distended bladder.

Clinical Limitations
  • - IUS is not an advised modality for dysplasia surveillance.
  • - Endoscopy or flexible sigmoidoscopy remains the most reliable method for assessing mucosal disease in the duodenum/rectum and for dysplasia screening.


Conclusions & Future Directions
  • - IUS has the potential to transform the diagnosis and monitoring of IBD.
  • - It objectively identifies treatment response, supporting a treat-to-target approach.
  • - The value of IUS is comparable to established modalities like MRE and CTE.
Future Goals
  • - Current efforts focus on training gastroenterologists and Advanced Practice Providers to increase adoption.
  • - A key future goal is to incorporate transmural healing as an endpoint for remission in IBD clinical trials.
