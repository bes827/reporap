The Anatomy of a Malpractice Lawsuit
  • Source: Aesthetic Surgery Journal Open Forum, 2023
  • Topic: Medical Malpractice Lawsuits in Healthcare/Plastic Surgery
  • Author(s): Attaluri et al.
1. Introduction and Background
  • Medical malpractice expenditures in the US → $56 billion annually
  • Surgeons face higher rates of malpractice claims vs nonsurgical providers
  • Most surgeons will encounter medical malpractice at some point in their careers
  • Unexpected outcome + lawsuit → ↓ personal and professional satisfaction for physicians
2. Criteria of Medical Malpractice: Patient-Physician Relationship
2.1 Definition and Establishment
  • Requires mutual consent (except emergency care)
  • Can be entered explicitly: outpatient consultation, inpatient consulting service request
  • Can be entered implicitly: informal discussions via phone or social settings
  • Both explicit and implicit relationships can hold physician accountable --> adverse events, even w/o direct patient care
2.2 Case Examples
  • Adams v. Via Christi Regional Medical Center: Physician liable for wrongful death → offered medical advice during phone call, found guilty --> established patient-physician relationship
  • Estate of Kundert v. Illinois Valley Community Hospital: Mother called for fever advice → provider gave informal opinion, later child died of bacterial meningitis --> case dismissed: informal advice ≠ acceptance of patient relationship
2.3 When Physicians Can Decline Relationship
  • Care falls outside scope of practice
  • Care not indicated
  • Resources not readily available (if not discriminatory)
  • Patient is abusive or threatening
3. Criteria of Medical Malpractice: Standard of Care
3.1 Definition
  • Healthcare professional provides same care as peers in similar situations
  • Definition varies from state to state
3.2 Landmark Cases Establishing Standard of Care
  • T.J. Hooper (1932): Not medical case → established framework: if practice/precaution is reasonable but not universally adopted, it can still be standard of care
  • Helling v. Carey: Patient sued for glaucoma-induced blindness → Supreme Court ruled: even if condition rare in patients <40 years, tonometer is inexpensive and harmless --> should have been used
3.3 Establishing Breach in Standard of Care
  • Res ipsa loquitor ("the thing speaks for itself"): obvious error (e.g., retained sponge) → obviates need for legal proceedings
  • Expert witness: needed when error not obvious --> translates complex medical concepts into layperson vernacular
  • American Society of Plastic Surgeons Code of Ethics → members must testify as expert witnesses when appropriate
  • US system: plaintiff and defendant teams hire/pay their own expert witnesses --> inherent biases possible
4. Criteria of Medical Malpractice: Proximate Cause
4.1 Definition and Components
  • Determination that event is related to injury
  • Plaintiff must establish: (1) existence of duty by physician, (2) breach of that duty, (3) resulting injury to patient, (4) causal connection between breach and injury
4.2 Two Principles for Establishing Causation
  • Cause-in-fact (sine qua non): tested with "but for" test --> event B would not have happened without event A
  • Example: "Without a rhinoplasty, patient would not have saddle nose deformity"
  • Foreseeability: act/omission must be such that physician using ordinary care would have foreseen that event or similar event might reasonably result
  • Example: Malignant hyperthermia → physician may not anticipate if no previous surgery/family history
4.3 Legal Process
  • Plaintiffs attempt to establish each component of proximate cause during litigation
  • Goal: make case for compensation commensurate with injuries
5. Structure of the Court System
5.1 Hierarchy and Jurisdiction
  • Trial courts: where civil disputes are tried first
  • Appeals courts: review trial court decisions
  • Ultimate judicial power lies within the state's supreme court
  • Structure relatively constant across states
5.2 Medical Malpractice Court Proceedings
  • Lawsuits generally filed within state trial court system
  • Unless settlement before trial → each defendant has right to jury trial in US
  • Jury = group of impartial peers from general public → assess plaintiff's and defendant's arguments, decide based on legal precedent
5.3 Out-of-Court Settlements
  • Majority of medical malpractice suits settled outside of court
  • Most common reason: resource-intensive nature of trial is detrimental to clinical productivity
  • If cost of pursuing trial > cost of settling → physician may be advised to settle
5.4 Legal Representation Options
  • Insurance carrier retains counsel when lawsuit is filed
  • Carrier will often honor physician's request for specific attorney
  • If physician chooses personal lawyer → physician incurs costs of those legal fees
5.5 Conflicts of Interest Scenarios

Insurance carrier might not be willing to seek counsel for physician due to conflict of interest in:

  • Claims for punitive damages: insurance carriers prohibited by law from paying punitive damages
  • Bad Faith Doctrine: physician wants to settle but carrier refuses
  • Uncovered claims: physician sued as both attending physician on record AND medical director of facility
  • Professional liability insurance covers claims to represent physician as healthcare provider → not as facility director
6. Overview of the Trial Process
6.1 Discovery Period
  • After suit is filed → laborious period of discovery, depositions, interrogations
  • Goal: sharing information between plaintiff's and defendant's teams --> achieving out-of-court agreements
6.2 Documents Requested
  • Entire medical record
  • Billing records
  • Other records related to patient encounter
6.3 Interrogatories and Depositions
  • After document review → interrogatory (line of questioning) developed to gather information about other party
  • Followed by deposition: party questioned under oath
  • Judicial system hopes to gather reliable information that can still promote out-of-court settlement
  • Any information obtained during legal proceedings can later be used in court
6.4 Discoverable vs Protected Information
  • Discoverable: personal communications, information in medical chart
  • Not discoverable:
  • Information protected by peer review in an institution
  • Information protected by attorney-client privilege
6.5 Protected Peer Review Process
  • Includes conclusions reached by hospital-wide committee after thorough review of cases with established morbidity or mortality
6.6 Trial Proceedings
  • If case proceeds to trial → plaintiff's legal team must convince jury with burden of proof that physician was not providing adequate care
  • Defense serves to negate proof presented by plaintiff
6.7 Burden of Proof
  • Medical malpractice lawsuits require only "preponderance of evidence"
  • Translates to: finding greater than 50% probability that professional negligence occurred → find physician guilty
6.8 Appeals and Reporting
  • Losing party can file for appeal → variety of reasons including outcome or settlement
  • If court mandates monetary transaction → must be reported to National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB)
  • Pretrial exchange of monetary awards must also be reported to NPDB
7. Significance of the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB)
7.1 Establishment and Purpose
  • Established by Congress in 1986
  • Goals:
  • Improve quality of healthcare
  • Encourage state licensing boards, hospitals, other health care entities, professional societies to identify and discipline those who engage in unprofessional behavior
  • Restrict ability of incompetent physicians, dentists, other healthcare practitioners to move from state to state without disclosure of previous medical malpractice payment and adverse action history
7.2 Reporting and Querying Entities
  • Medical malpractice payers
  • Hospitals
  • State licensing boards
  • Professional societies with formal peer review
  • US Drug Enforcement Agency
7.3 Mandatory Reporting Criteria

Payments must be reported when all 4 of following criteria met:

1. Payments made by an organization, not an individual

2. Made on behalf of an individual, not a group

3. In settlement of written complaint related to lack of provision of care

4. Request of money

All 4 criteria must be met → irrespective of amount of money or merit of claim

7.4 Significance for Physicians
  • Hospitals can query NPDB as physicians apply for new jobs or expand existing privileges
7.5 Imperfections and Controversies
  • Argument 1: payments made on behalf of physicians do not translate to malpractice
  • Legal process expensive → incentivizes insurance companies to settle with payment rather than engage in costly malpractice trial
  • Argument 2: "Corporate shield" concept → controversial
  • Occurs when payments made on behalf of institution rather than individual --> effectively bypass entities with mandatory reporting responsibilities
  • Can prove unfair to practitioners in private practice (common in plastic surgery)
8. Understanding Tort Reform
8.1 Definition and Purpose
  • One of most instrumental strategies implemented to limit number of medical malpractice lawsuits
  • Described as comprehensive list of legal strategies and provisions implemented by state legislatures
  • Goals: limit medical malpractice costs, ensure patients injured by medical negligence can be fairly compensated
8.2 Historical Impact
  • Since 1970s: helped reduce number of baseless medical malpractice lawsuits and limit excessive awards from jury
8.3 Key Provisions
  • Caps on noneconomic damage
  • Limitations on proportion of contingency fees collected by attorneys
  • Limitation on collateral source rule
9. Caps on Noneconomic Damages
9.1 Definition
  • Limit amount of monetary damages plaintiff may collect for pain and suffering associated with injury
  • Attempts to capture intangible losses suffered by victim → separate from financial losses
9.2 California MICRA Example
  • Cap set at $250,000US → first established by state of California under Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (MICRA)
  • Created after noticing exodus of physicians from state due to increasing insurance premiums resulting from high malpractice awards
  • Left noticeable gap in healthcare throughout state → forced legislature to create more hospitable environment to practice medicine
9.3 Recent Changes: AB-35 (2023)
  • California recently passed law (AB-35) → significant changes to preexisting cap
  • Cases not involving death:
  • $350,000US in 2023
  • Gradual increase over next 10 years → $750,000US by 2033
  • Cases involving patient's death:
  • $500,000US in 2023
  • Incremental increases over next 10 years → $1,000,000US by 2033
10. Contingency Fee Caps
10.1 Definition and Mechanism
  • Form of payment to lawyer for legal services
  • Lawyers only receive compensation when client wins or settles the case
  • Differs from fixed payment
10.2 Sliding Scale Regulation
  • Some states further regulate payment of lawyers via sliding scale
  • Allocates higher contingency fees for first $50,000US of recovery
  • Reduction of contingency fee for amounts exceeding $50,000US
10.3 Benefits and Criticisms

Benefits (patient advocacy standpoint):

  • Allows access to quality legal representation
  • Lawyers motivated to work diligently for plaintiff
  • Prompted lawyers to be judicious about case selection → may limit number of malpractice lawsuits
  • Incentivizes lawyers to prefer defending plaintiffs when factual
10.3 Opponents' Perspective
  • Opponents believe contingency fees deter most able defense attorneys from taking on cases in which they believe compensation is not worth time and resources
11. Limiting Collateral Source Rule
11.1 Traditional Definition
  • Law that prevents reduction of damages awarded to plaintiff
  • Common law states: plaintiff entitled to be fully compensated by negligent party → even if losses already paid by separate party (i.e., plaintiff's insurance company)
11.2 Purpose of Limitation
  • Decreases payouts by not allowing plaintiff to profit from losses already appropriately compensated by separate party
  • Prevents "double-dipping" from plaintiff's insurance company
11.3 Current Status
  • Most states have modified or abrogated the rule to be more compliant with economic realities of today's healthcare system
11.4 Punitive Damages
  • Traditionally used in cases where defendant committed intentional or negligent crimes against plaintiff
  • Purpose: punish defendant, deter future crimes of similar nature
  • Has also been eliminated in many jurisdictions
12. Common Mistakes in Plastic Surgery
12.1 Unhappiness with Result
  • Most common adverse event in lawsuits against plastic surgeons
  • While unhappiness may stem from subjective and objective measures of poor performance → indicates failure to meet patient's expectations
12.2 Traditional Informed Consent Limitations
  • Process by which surgeons explain risks, benefits, alternatives, expected preoperative and postoperative care
  • Does not necessarily address concept of "happiness"
12.3 Effective Physician-Patient Relationship
  • Essential in decision making for informed consent process
  • Optimized relationship leads to better therapeutic strategy
  • Engaging patient, understanding emotional motivation behind surgery → greater satisfaction in informed consent process
12.4 Informed Consent Requirements
  • Who should obtain consent: surgeon providing treatment OR associated clinician with sufficient knowledge of surgery, potential complications, alternative treatment options
  • Patient must be: competent, adequately informed, not coerced
12.5 AMA Minimum Required Elements
  • Diagnosis
  • Nature and purpose of recommended intervention
  • Burdens, risks, expected benefits of all options including forgoing treatment
  • Conversation must be meticulously documented in totality → serves as verification of informed consent process
12.6 Communication Requirements
  • Must be tailored to patient's need material to decision-making process
  • Places onus on physician to determine what is necessary based on medical judgment
12.7 Shared Decision Making (SDM) in Plastic Surgery
  • Aesthetic surgeons long recognized importance in developing treatment plan meeting patient's specific goals
12.8 Three Phases of SDM (Elwyn et al.)

1. "Choice talk": patient made aware options available for treatment

2. "Option talk": specific options for treatment of condition reviewed

3. "Decision talk": patient preferences regarding treatment options conveyed → reach mutual decision

12.9 Perceived Barriers to SDM
  • Perceived to take too long during consultations
  • Many believe they are already incorporating SDM in their practice
12.10 ASAPS Member Survey Results
  • 63% of respondents believed consent documents did not help teach patients essential information needed from informed consent process
12.11 Gap in Expectations
  • Highlights difference between what plastic surgeons understand to be needed (includes SDM) vs current model focused on disclosure of information without demonstration of understanding
  • Inevitably, gaps in expectations and goals between patient and surgeon exist → eroding foundational relationship --> opening door for future litigation
13. Strategies to Avoid a Lawsuit
13.1 Statistical Context
  • Most plastic surgeons will be named in lawsuit at least once during career
  • Yet compared to all physicians → plastic surgeons have some of lowest indemnity payments
  • Study by Boyd et al: plastic surgeons comprised only 3.31% of reported claims and 3.16% of paid claims between 2006-2016
  • 58% of payments were less than $100,000US
13.2 Most Prevalent Claims Against Plastic Surgeons

1. Failure to perform procedure properly: 60%

2. Failure to supervise

3. Failure to recognize a complication

4. Error in diagnosis

5. Treatment delay

13.3 Strategies to Optimize Patient Safety and Reduce Litigation Risk
13.3.1 Certification Requirements
  • Maintain board certification by American Board of Plastic Surgeons
  • Ensure maintenance of mandatory certifications as determined by local, state, federal laws for surgeons, nurses, other clinic staff (ACLS, BLS)
13.3.2 Facility and Procedure Standards
  • Ensure surgical procedures performed in accredited facilities when appropriate based on:
  • Procedure type
  • Patient's medical comorbidities
  • Availability of emergency services or equipment
13.3.3 Documentation and Consent
  • Provide clear written information on surgical procedures → remains up to date with current practices
  • Ensure informed consent obtained with focus on shared decision making
  • Precise documentation of preoperative discussions
13.3.4 Scope of Practice
  • Do not offer surgical procedures:
  • Outside scope of practice
  • Not clinically indicated
  • In cases where risks outweigh benefits
13.3.5 Patient Selection
  • Do not offer surgical procedures if:
  • Surgeon or staff do not like the patient
  • Surgeon cannot understand patient's goals
  • Patient has unrealistic expectations
13.3.6 Safety Protocols
  • Abide by Universal Protocol as outlined by The Joint Commission
14. Conclusions
14.1 Importance of Understanding Malpractice Landscape
  • Of paramount importance for plastic surgeons to have thorough understanding of medical malpractice landscape
14.2 Legal Outcomes
  • Though most legal outcomes favor surgeon → necessary to be cognizant of potential for litigation in specific patient care situations
14.3 Recommendations
  • By implementing recommendations from governing bodies → hope to reduce medical malpractice lawsuits and poor outcomes for surgeon and patient
14.4 Future Responsibility
  • In arena where change is only constant → field's responsibility to educate surgical trainees and providers to ensure:
  • Optimal patient safety
  • Prevention of medical malpractice lawsuits
