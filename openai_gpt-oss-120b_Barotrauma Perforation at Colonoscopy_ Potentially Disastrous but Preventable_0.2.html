Barotrauma Perforation at Colonoscopy: Potentially Disastrous but Preventable
  • Source: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy, Vol 103 No 2 (2026)
  • Topic: Colonoscopic Barotrauma
1. Epidemiology & Legal Impact
  • Barotrauma = least common cause of colonoscopic perforation in series¹˒².
  • Expert witness experience: leading cause of malpractice claims for perforation.
  • First 4 months 2025: reviewed 6 negligence cases; 5/6 caused by barotrauma.
2. Typical Clinical Scenario
  • Patient: tight, angulated, narrowed sigmoid colon → often severe diverticular disease.
  • Endoscopist: uses gas insufflation, struggles to pass sigmoid; water filling not used.
  • Bowel prep: frequently suboptimal; poor preparation worsens visualization of narrowing.
3. Consequences of Barotrauma Perforation
  • Rupture perforations in rectosigmoid → large, recognized during withdrawal; repaired endoscopically or sent to surgery with limited contamination.
  • Barotrauma → extensive immediate peritoneal contamination, large intraperitoneal gas volumes → risk of abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Rapid sepsis development; endoscopic repair usually infeasible due to multiple mucosal breaks and poor prep obscuring perforation site.
  • Diagnosis: astute endoscopist notes diseased sigmoid + palpates/taps tympanitic abdomen → immediate needle decompression, antibiotics, emergent surgical consult required.
4. Prevention Strategies Overview
  • Acute barotrauma occurs during colonoscopy; delayed perforation rare, linked to retained gas causing proximal ischemia.
  • Carbon dioxide insufflation prevents delayed perforation (rapid absorption) but does not prevent acute intra‑procedure barotrauma.
  • Key risk factor: diseased sigmoid colon (severe diverticular disease). Most patients with severe disease do NOT develop barotrauma, yet all barotrauma cases have a diseased sigmoid → high specificity.
5. Pathophysiology of Risk
  • Closed‑loop formation when gas cannot escape retrograde via anus and ileocecal valve is competent → pressure builds between sigmoid and valve → barotrauma.
  • Additional risk: brittle mucosa (e.g., collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis) → water filling recommended in such patients.
6. Immediate Preventive Actions During Insertion
  • Recognize diseased sigmoid entry → stop gas insufflation and switch to water‑filling of lumen.
  • Switch triggers: need for thinner scope to pass sigmoid or struggle with angulation/narrowing.
  • Water does not cause barotrauma; exact disease severity threshold unclear, but prudent to act on any notable angulation/narrowing.
7. Acute Management of Suspected Barotrauma
Recognition and Immediate Steps
  • Palpate tympanitic abdomen → suspect intraperitoneal gas accumulation.
  • Perform needle decompression of peritoneal cavity immediately.
  • Initiate broad‑spectrum antibiotics w/ anaerobic coverage.
  • Call surgical team for emergent consultation.
Endoscopic Intervention Limitations
  • Multiple mucosal lacerations → perforation site often indeterminate.
  • Poor bowel prep obscures visualization → endoscopic repair rarely feasible.
Surgical Considerations
  • Prompt laparotomy or laparoscopy preferred when perforation identified.
  • Assess for abdominal compartment syndrome; consider decompressive laparotomy if needed.
  • Resect necrotic colon segments if ischemia evident.
8. Detailed Prevention Protocol (Table 1 Summary)
Patient‑Related Risk Assessment
  • Identify severe sigmoid diverticular disease on prior imaging or endoscopic history.
  • Evaluate for competent ileocecal valve (no prior ileocolonic surgery).
  • Screen for brittle mucosa: collagenous colitis, microscopic colitis.
Procedural Decision Tree

1. Enter diseased sigmoid → stop CO₂/air insufflation.

2. Switch to water‑filling of lumen.

3. If still unable to advance → consider thinner colonoscope or pediatric scope.

4. Abort procedure if closed loop suspected and cannot be safely traversed.

Insufflation Modality Guidance
  • CO₂: use for routine cases; does not prevent acute barotrauma.
  • Air: avoid in high‑risk sigmoids; higher risk of retained gas.
  • Water: primary modality when risk factors present; eliminates pressure buildup.
9. Training, Education, and Systemic Measures
Endoscopist Competency Development
  • Incorporate barotrauma recognition drills into fellowship curricula.
  • Simulate closed‑loop scenarios using ex‑vivo models with pressure sensors.
  • Require documentation of water‑filling switch when encountering angulated sigmoid.
Institutional Policies
  • Mandate pre‑procedure checklist item: “Assess sigmoid disease severity.”
  • Implement automatic alerts in endoscopy reporting software for documented severe diverticulosis.
  • Track barotrauma incidents quarterly; perform root‑cause analysis on each case.
Dissemination to Trainees and Partners
  • Conduct monthly morbidity & mortality conferences highlighting barotrauma cases.
  • Publish institutional protocol in internal newsletters and online learning portals.
  • Encourage mentorship: senior endoscopists model water‑filling technique live.
10. Conclusions and Recommendations
  • Barotrauma perforations are rare but carry high morbidity, mortality, and legal risk.
  • Prevention = immediate recognition of diseased sigmoid → stop gas insufflation → switch to water filling.
  • Educate all endoscopy personnel; embed protocol into standard operating procedures.
  • Ongoing audit and feedback essential to sustain low barotrauma rates.

14. Table 1 – Keys to Understanding and Preventing Colonoscopic Barotrauma
  • Risk Concentration: Occurs almost exclusively in patients with narrowed, angulated sigmoids (typically severe diverticular disease).
  • Specificity Note: Most severely diseased sigmoids do not develop barotrauma, but all barotrauma cases have a diseased sigmoid → high specificity.
  • Insufflation Modality: Carbon dioxide insufflation does not prevent acute intra‑procedure barotrauma.
  • Timing Insight: When perforation occurs, injury has usually already happened by the time scope reaches proximal colon; thus prevention is paramount.
  • Treatment Challenge: Perforation site often hard to identify endoscopically; patients typically need immediate needle decompression and surgical consultation.
  • Preventive Action: Stop gas insufflation and switch to water‑filling only in every severely diseased sigmoid colon encountered.
