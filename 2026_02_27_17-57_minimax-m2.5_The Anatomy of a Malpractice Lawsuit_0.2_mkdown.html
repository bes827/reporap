The Anatomy of a Malpractice Lawsuit
  • Source: Aesthetic Surgery Journal Open Forum, 2023
  • Topic: Medical Malpractice Litigation in the United States
  • Author(s): Attaluri PK, Wirth PJ, Moura SP, Shaffrey EC, Rao VK
1. Introduction and Medical Malpractice Landscape
  • US medical malpractice expenditures → $56 billion annually in healthcare spending
  • Malpractice lawsuits costly at individual level: direct costs (surgeon/hospital) + indirect costs (lost clinical time)
  • Surgeons face higher rates of malpractice claims vs nonsurgical providers
  • Unexpected outcome + lawsuit → distress → ↓ personal and professional satisfaction
  • Most surgeons will encounter malpractice at some point in career
2. Criteria of Medical Malpractice: Patient-Physician Relationship
2.1 Definition and Establishment
  • Requires mutual consent (except emergency care)
  • Relationship can be explicit or implicit
  • Explicit examples: outpatient consultation, inpatient consulting service request
  • Implicit examples: informal discussions via telephone or social settings
  • Both explicit and implicit relationships can hold physician accountable for adverse events → even without direct patient care
2.2 Landmark Cases: Relationship Boundaries
  • Adams v. Via Christi Regional Medical Center: Physician liable for wrongful death after providing phone advice to patient's mother about abdominal pain in early pregnancy → established patient-physician relationship despite no direct examination
  • Estate of Kundert v. Illinois Valley Community Hospital: Mother called for fever advice → provider gave Tylenol/tepid bath guidance, stated hospital lacked infant equipment → court ruled no relationship established due to refusal to initiate care
2.3 When Physicians Can Decline Relationship
  • Care falls outside scope of practice
  • Care not indicated
  • Resources not readily available (if not discriminatory)
  • Patient is abusive or threatening
3. Criteria: Standard of Care
3.1 Definition and Legal Framework
  • Healthcare professional provides same care as peers in similar situations
  • Definition varies by state
3.2 Landmark Cases Establishing Standard of Care
  • T.J. Hooper (1932): Not medical case → established framework: practice not universally adopted but reasonable can still = standard of care
  • Helling v. Carey: Patient sued for glaucoma-induced blindness → Supreme Court ruled tonometer should have been used (inexpensive/harmless) despite rarity in patients <40 → led many states to legally define standard of care
3.3 Establishing Breach in Standard of Care
  • Res ipsa loquitor ("the thing speaks for itself"): Obvious error (e.g., retained surgical sponge) → no legal proceedings needed
  • Expert witness: Required when error not obvious → translates complex medical concepts to layperson
  • American Society of Plastic Surgeons Code of Ethics: members must testify as expert witnesses when appropriate
  • US system bias: plaintiff and defendant teams hire/pay their own expert witnesses
4. Criteria: Proximate Cause
4.1 Four Elements Required

1. Existence of duty by physician

2. Breach of that duty

3. Resulting injury to patient

4. Causal connection between breach and injury

4.2 Two Principles for Establishing Causation
  • Cause-in-fact (sine qua non): Tested with "but for" test → event B would not happen without event A
  • Example: "Without a rhinoplasty, patient would not have saddle nose deformity"
  • Foreseeability: Act/omission must be such that physician using ordinary care could have foreseen similar event might result
  • Example: Malignant hyperthermia → physician may not anticipate if no previous surgery/family history
5. Structure of the Court System
5.1 Hierarchy Overview
  • Trial courts: Civil disputes tried here → medical malpractice lawsuits filed here
  • Appeals courts: Review trial decisions
  • State Supreme Court: Ultimate judicial power
5.2 Trial Rights and Settlements
  • Each defendant has right to jury trial in US → impartial peers assess arguments, decide based on legal precedent
  • Majority of cases settle outside court
  • Reasons for out-of-court settlement: resource-intensive nature detrimental to clinical productivity
5.3 Legal Representation Options
  • Insurance carrier typically retains counsel when lawsuit filed → honors physician's request for specific attorney if requested
  • Physician can pursue personal lawyer → incurs legal fees
  • Insurance carrier may refuse counsel for physician (conflict of interest):
  • Claims for punitive damages (insurers prohibited by law from paying)
  • Bad Faith Doctrine: physician wants to settle but carrier refuses
  • Uncovered claims: physician sued as attending physician AND medical director → professional liability only covers healthcare provider role, not facility director
6. Overview of the Trial Process
6.1 Discovery Phase
  • Laborious period of discovery, depositions, interrogations
  • Goal: achieve out-of-court agreements between parties
  • Documents requested: entire medical record, billing records, patient encounter records
  • Interrogatories: line of questioning developed after document review
  • Deposition: party questioned under oath → hoping to gather reliable information promoting settlement
  • Information obtained can be used in court
6.2 Discoverable vs Protected Information
  • Discoverable: personal communications, medical chart information
  • Protected: peer review conclusions (hospital committee morbidity/mortality reviews), attorney-client privilege
6.3 Trial Proceedings
  • Plaintiff must convince jury with burden of proof: "preponderance of evidence" → >50% probability that professional negligence occurred
  • Defense negates plaintiff's proof
  • Losing party can file appeal (outcome or settlement)
  • Monetary transactions must be reported to National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB) → including pretrial exchange of monetary awards
7. Significance of the National Practitioner Data Bank
7.1 Purpose and Establishment
  • NPDB established by Congress in 1986
  • Goals:
  • Improve healthcare quality → encourage licensing boards, hospitals, professional societies to identify/discipline those engaging in unprofessional behavior
  • Restrict incompetent physicians/dentists/healthcare practitioners from moving state-to-state without disclosure of malpractice payment and adverse action history
  • Qualified entities can report/query: medical malpractice payers, hospitals, state licensing boards, professional societies with formal peer review, US Drug Enforcement Agency
7.2 Reporting Requirements
  • Medical malpractice payments mandated to report when all 4 criteria met:

1. Payments made by organization, not individual

2. On behalf of individual, not group

3. In settlement of written complaint related to lack of provision of care

4. Request of money

  • All 4 criteria must be met → irrespective of amount or merit of claim
7.3 Imperfections and Controversies
  • Argument 1: Payments made on behalf of physicians do not translate to malpractice → legal process expensive → incentivizes insurance companies to settle with payment rather than engage in costly trial
  • Argument 2: "Corporate shield" concept controversial → payments made on behalf of institution rather than individual to bypass mandatory reporting entities → unfair to private practice practitioners (common in plastic surgery)
8. Understanding Tort Reform
8.1 Overview and History
  • Tort reform: comprehensive list of legal strategies/provisions implemented by state legislatures to limit malpractice costs + ensure fair compensation for patients injured by medical negligence
  • Since 1970s: helped reduce baseless malpractice lawsuits and limit excessive jury awards
8.2 Caps on Noneconomic Damages
  • Limits amount of monetary damages plaintiff may collect for pain and suffering associated with injury
  • Attempts to capture intangible losses separate from financial losses
  • California: first state to create cap ($250,000) under Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (MICRA) → after noticing physician exodus due to increasing insurance premiums from high malpractice awards → created more hospitable practice environment
8.3 Recent Legislative Changes: AB-35
  • California passed law (AB-35) with significant changes to noneconomic damage caps:
  • Cases not involving death:
  • $350,000 in 2023 → gradual increase over 10 years
  • Reaches $750,000 by 2033
  • Cases involving death:
  • $500,000 in 2023 → gradual increase over 10 years
  • Reaches $1,000,000 by 2033
8.4 Contingency Fee Caps
  • Contingency fee: form of payment to lawyer → lawyers only receive compensation when client wins or settles case
  • Some states regulate with sliding scale:
  • Higher contingency fees for first $50,000 of recovery
  • Reduction of fee for amounts exceeding $50,000
  • Benefits: allows access to quality legal representation → lawyers motivated to work diligently for plaintiff
  • Prompted lawyers to be judicious about case selection → may limit number of malpractice lawsuits
  • Lawyers prefer defending plaintiffs when factual error believed → decreases burden on judicial system
  • Opponents: contingency fees deter most able defense attorneys from taking cases where compensation not worth time/resources
8.5 Limiting Collateral Source Rule
  • Collateral source rule: law preventing reduction of damages awarded to plaintiff
  • Common law: plaintiff entitled to full compensation by negligent party → even if losses already paid by separate party (plaintiff's insurance company)
  • Limitation decreases payouts → prevents "double-dipping" (plaintiff profiting from losses already compensated by insurance)
  • Most states have modified or abrogated the rule to be more compliant with economic realities
  • Additionally: punitive damages eliminated in cases where defendant committed intentional or negligent crimes → invoked to punish defendant and deter future similar crimes
9. Common Mistakes in Plastic Surgery
9.1 Unhappiness with Results
  • Most common adverse event in plastic surgery lawsuits
  • Unhappiness may stem from subjective and objective measures of poor performance
  • Indicates failure to meet patient expectations
9.2 Informed Consent Process Limitations
  • Traditional informed consent: surgeons explain risks, benefits, alternatives, expected pre/postoperative care
  • Does not necessarily address concept of "happiness"
  • Effective physician-patient relationship essential in decision making
  • Optimized relationship → better therapeutic strategy
  • Engaging patient, understanding emotional motivation behind surgery → greater satisfaction in informed consent process
9.3 Informed Consent Requirements
  • Consent should be obtained by surgeon providing treatment OR associated clinician with sufficient knowledge of surgery, complications, alternatives
  • Patient must be: competent, adequately informed, not coerced
  • AMA minimum required informed consent:
  • Diagnosis
  • Nature and purpose of recommended intervention
  • Burdens, risks, expected benefits of all options including forgoing treatment
  • Conversation must be meticulously documented in totality → serves as verification
  • Communication tailored to patient's need material to decision-making → physician determines what's necessary based on medical judgment
9.4 Shared Decision Making (SDM) in Plastic Surgery
  • Aesthetic surgeons recognize importance of SDM in developing treatment plan meeting patient's specific goals
  • Elwyn et al. model: 3 phases of "talk"
  • "Choice talk": patient made aware options available for treatment
  • "Option talk": specific treatment options for condition reviewed
  • "Decision talk": patient preferences regarding treatment options conveyed → reach mutual decision
9.5 Barriers to Effective SDM
  • Perceived to take too long during consultations
  • Many believe already incorporating SDM in practice
  • ASAPS member survey: 63% believed consent documents did not teach patients essential information needed from informed consent process
  • Highlights gap: what plastic surgeons understand needed (includes SDM) vs current model (focused on disclosure without demonstration of understanding)
  • Gaps in expectations/goals between patient and surgeon → erode foundational relationship → open door for future litigation
10. Strategies to Avoid a Lawsuit
10.1 Malpractice
10.1 Malpractice Statistics for Plastic Surgeons
  • Most plastic surgeons will be named in a lawsuit at least once during career
  • When compared to all physicians, plastic surgeons have some of the lowest indemnity payments
  • Boyd et al study (2006-2016):
  • Plastic surgeons comprised only 3.31% of reported claims
  • 3.16% of paid claims
  • 58% of payments were less than $100,000US
10.2 Most Common Claims Against Plastic Surgeons
  • Failure to perform a procedure properly: 60% (most prevalent)
  • Failure to supervise
  • Failure to recognize a complication
  • Error in diagnosis
  • Treatment delay
10.3 Strategies to Optimize Patient Safety and Reduce Litigation Risk

1. Maintain board certification by the American Board of Plastic Surgeons

2. Ensure maintenance of mandatory certifications as determined by local, state, and federal laws for surgeons, nurses, and other clinic staff (ACLS, BLS)

3. Ensure surgical procedures are performed in accredited facilities when appropriate based on procedure type, patient's medical comorbidities, and availability of emergency services or equipment

4. Provide clear written information on surgical procedures that remains up to date with current practices

5. Ensure informed consent is obtained with focus on shared decision making and precise documentation of preoperative discussions

6. Do not offer surgical procedures outside scope of practice, not clinically indicated, or where risks outweigh benefits

7. Do not offer surgical procedures if surgeon or staff do not like the patient, surgeon cannot understand patient's goals, or patient has unrealistic expectations

8. Abide by Universal Protocol as outlined by the Joint Commission

11. Conclusions
  • Paramount importance for plastic surgeons to have thorough understanding of medical malpractice landscape
  • Most legal outcomes favor surgeon → but necessary to be cognizant of potential for litigation in specific patient care situations
  • By implementing recommendations from governing bodies → hope to reduce medical malpractice lawsuits and poor outcomes for surgeon and patient
  • In arena where change is only constant → field's responsibility to educate surgical trainees and providers to ensure optimal patient safety and prevention of medical malpractice lawsuits
