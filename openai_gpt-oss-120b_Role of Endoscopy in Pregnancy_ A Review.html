Role of Endoscopy in Pregnancy: A Review
  • Source: Razjouyan & Maranki, Gastrointestinal Endoscopy 2025
  • Topic: Endoscopic diagnosis and therapy during gestation
1. Indications for Endoscopy in Pregnant Patients
  • Persistent/refractory GI bleeding → urgent upper endoscopy
  • Severe nausea‑vomiting/abdominal pain w/ ulcer suspicion → consider EGD
  • Dysphagia or odynophagia → evaluate for infection/erosive esophagitis
  • Suspected colonic mass or refractory diarrhea → colonoscopy indicated
1.1 Upper Endoscopy Specifics
  • Mallory‑Weiss tear = most common non‑variceal bleed source
  • Portal hypertension ⇒ risk of variceal rupture, need urgent EGD
1.2 Lower Endoscopy Specifics
  • High suspicion of neoplasm or unexplained diarrhea → colonoscopy
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy preferred for IBD assessment (no full prep)
1.3 ERCP / Biliary Indications
  • Symptomatic choledocholithiasis, biliary pancreatitis, cholangitis → ERCP with stent if needed
  • Bile‑duct or pancreatic‑duct injury ⇒ therapeutic ERCP
2. Peri‑Procedural Considerations & Maternal–Fetal Safety
  • Mandatory obstetric consult before any endoscopic intervention
  • Fetal heart rate monitoring: Doppler pre/post sedation; electronic monitoring after 24 wks gestation
  • Contraindications: eclampsia, ruptured membranes, imminent delivery, placental abruption
2.1 Timing & Positioning
  • Second trimester optimal: organogenesis complete, uterus smaller → less vascular compression
  • Left lateral tilt or left pelvic tilt mandatory after 24 wks to avoid IVC/aortic compression
  • Upright sitting reduces vascular pressure; supine avoided in late gestation
2.2 Sedation & Medication Safety (Table 2 summary)
  • Category B: Meperidine, Propofol (requires anesthesia), Glucagon, Topical lidocaine – generally safe
  • Category C: Fentanyl (low dose ok), Morphine, Naloxone, Simethicone – use cautiously
  • Category D: Benzodiazepines – avoid first trimester; midazolam only if essential
2.3 Bowel Preparation & Enemas
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy → tap‑water enema preferred
  • Polyethylene glycol electrolyte lavage = Category C, low risk
  • Sodium phosphate solutions = Category C, may cause fluid/electrolyte shifts – use with caution
3. Safety Data for Specific Endoscopic Modalities
  • Upper endoscopy: 60 pregnant pts (first trimester) → esophagitis 43 %, gastritis 17 %, diaphragmatic hernia 17 %; diagnostic yield for bleed 82 %; no fetal malformations reported.
3.1 Diagnostic Yield & Outcomes
  • 83 pregnant vs 48 controls: EGD diagnostic yield 95 % for acute bleed, 50‑82 % for other indications; 62 % esophagitis prevalence; 95 % healthy deliveries, 4 adverse outcomes unrelated to procedure.
3.2 Flexible Sigmoidoscopy in IBD
  • 48 pts (suspected/known IBD) → 78 % changed management; no obstetric adverse events reported.
3.3 Colonoscopy Experience
  • 20 pts (mostly 2nd trimester): cecum reached 55 %; mild maternal hypotension 2 cases; no fetal distress; unfavorable fetal outcomes comparable to controls.
3.4 Lower GI Endoscopy Across Trimesters
  • 100 lower GI procedures: 1st tri 1 AE, 2nd tri 3 AEs, 3rd tri 2 AEs → overall safety across gestation.
  • Sigmoid volvulus (3rd trimester) – successful endoscopic decompression reported.
4. Advanced Endoscopic Procedures: ERCP in Pregnancy
  • Most common advanced procedure due to increased choledocholithiasis risk.
  • Strong indication required; documented in record; multidisciplinary team essential.
4.1 Timing & Positioning for ERCP
  • Preferred second trimester (organogenesis complete, less uterine compression).
  • Left lateral tilt or pelvic tilt preferred; supine only acceptable in first trimester.
4.2 Radiation Considerations
  • Fetal threshold 100‑200 mGy (organogenesis weeks 2‑8); deterministic effects <50 mGy not observed.
  • Typical ERCP exposure .01‑5.77 mGy → well below 10 mGy monitoring limit.
4.2.1 Exposure Data Examples
  • Cohort of 15 pts: mean fluoroscopy time 14 s; fetal dose 0.4 mGy (range 0.001‑1.80).
4.3 Techniques to Minimize Radiation (Table 3 summary)
  • Increase distance from x‑ray tube, bring image receptor close → ↓ dose
  • Use collimation, brief‑pulse low‑dose mode, avoid digital acquisition
  • Wire‑guided cannulation, bile aspirate confirmation, cholangioscopy assistance, real‑time transabdominal US guidance
4.4 ERCP Outcomes & Complications
  • Technical success comparable to non‑pregnant cohorts.
  • Post‑ERCP pancreatitis ↑ up to 16 % (historical) → recent high‑volume centers report 9.6‑12 %.
  • Maternal mortality similar to national estimates; preterm labor lower in pregnant cohort (1.87 % vs 11.5 %).

5. Medications Used During Endoscopy in Pregnancy
5.1 Sedative and Analgesic Agents
  • Meperidine – FDA Category B; not teratogenic, preferred narcotic for moderate sedation.
  • Fentanyl – Category C; short‑acting, no teratogenicity reported, embryocidal in rats only at high doses; safe in low clinical doses.
  • Propofol – Category B; narrow therapeutic index, requires anesthesia support; first‑trimester safety not well studied.
5.2 Benzodiazepines and Adjuncts
  • Midazolam – Category D; preferred benzodiazepine if unavoidable, avoid in first trimester.
  • Diazepam – Category D; contraindicated in pregnancy.
  • Flumazenil – Category C; limited safety data, used only for reversal of benzodiazepine effects.
5.3 Opioid Antagonists & Others
  • Naloxone – Category B; rescue medication for respiratory depression or hypotension.
  • Morphine – Category C; crosses fetal blood‑brain barrier, use with caution.
5.4 Ancillary Medications
  • Simethicone – Category C; likely safe, no evaluated teratogenicity.
  • Glucagon – Category B; used for therapeutic purposes (e.g., to relax sphincter).
  • Topical lidocaine – Category B; may reduce gag reflex; advise patient to gargle and spit.
6. Radiation Exposure: Risks, Monitoring, and Long‑Term Effects
6.1 Fetal Dose Thresholds & Guidelines
  • Organogenesis (weeks 2‑8): fetal sensitivity highest; deterministic effects reported >100–200 mGy.
  • Deterministic threshold <50 mGy not associated with observable effects.
  • International Commission on Radiological Protection: monitor if expected dose >10 mGy.
6.2 Reported ERCP Radiation Doses
  • Typical ERCP exposure range 0.01–5.77 mGy (well below 10 mGy).
  • Study of 15 pregnant patients: mean fluoroscopy time 14 s, estimated fetal dose 0.4 mGy (range 0.001‑1.80 mGy).
6.3 Stochastic Effects & Long‑Term Follow‑Up
  • Childhood leukemia risk noted with fetal exposures as low as 10 mGy; causality remains uncertain.
  • Limited case series: no definitive long‑term adverse events reported after diagnostic pelvic radiation ≤10 mGy.
  • Need for systematic, longitudinal registries to assess developmental outcomes.
6.4 Strategies to Minimize Radiation (Technical & Procedural)
6.4.1 Fluoroscopy‑Based Techniques
  • Position patient far from x‑ray tube, image receptor close → dose reduction.
  • Use collimation to limit beam size; employ brief‑pulse, low‑dose settings.
  • Avoid digital acquisition modes that increase exposure.
6.4.2 Procedure‑Based Techniques
  • Wire‑guided cannulation with bile aspirate as confirmation → may eliminate fluoroscopy.
  • Cholangioscopy‑assisted cannulation for direct visualization of ductal anatomy.
  • Real‑time transabdominal ultrasound guidance (Li et al.) to verify CBD access and stone clearance without radiation.
6.4.3 Positioning Adjustments
  • Place grounding pad on right thorax, away from amniotic sac, to prevent current conduction through fetus during sphincterotomy.
7. Future Directions & Research Priorities
7.1 Data Aggregation & Evidence Synthesis
  • Multi‑center registries needed to define best practices for maternal comorbidities (e.g., obesity) influencing endoscopic safety.
7.2 Radiation Technology Advancements
  • Development of ultra‑low‑dose fluoroscopy and dose‑modulation algorithms; require prospective validation in pregnant cohorts.
7.3 Non‑Invasive & Sedation‑Free Approaches
  • Transnasal endoscopes with larger channels → potential for diagnostic/therapeutic work without sedation.
  • Combination of ERCP + transabdominal US or cholangioscopy to reduce fluoroscopy time.
7.4 Longitudinal Outcomes Research
  • Systematic follow‑up of children exposed in utero to endoscopic procedures; focus on neurodevelopment, growth, and oncologic risk.
8. Conclusions
  • Endoscopy is a critical tool for managing GI pathology during pregnancy when benefits outweigh risks.
  • Multidisciplinary coordination (gastroenterology, obstetrics, anesthesia) essential for optimal maternal‑fetal outcomes.
  • Key safety pillars: clear indication, second‑trimester timing, left lateral positioning, appropriate sedation, and radiation minimization.
  • ERCP remains safe but carries a modestly increased risk of post‑procedure pancreatitis; meticulous technique and dose reduction are paramount.
9. Practical Workflow Checklist for Endoscopy in Pregnancy
9.1 Pre‑Procedure Planning
  • Obstetric consultation obtained; document fetal gestational age and any maternal complications.
  • Verify indication clarity; if uncertain, perform non‑invasive imaging (RUQ US, MRCP) first.
  • Review medication list for FDA pregnancy categories; select Category B/C agents when possible.
  • Obtain informed consent: discuss aspiration risk, fetal hypotension/hypoxia, teratogenic potential, radiation exposure, and post‑procedure pancreatitis (ERCP).
9.2 Intra‑Procedure Management
9.2.1 Positioning & Monitoring
  • Place patient in left lateral tilt or left pelvic tilt; use pillow/wedge under right hip.
  • For gestations >24 wks: attach electronic fetal heart rate monitor (if available).
  • Continuous maternal blood pressure, oxygen saturation, and airway protection monitoring.
9.2.2 Sedation Protocol
Drug Category Typical Dose (Pregnant) Notes
Meperidine B 25‑50 mg IV Preferred narcotic; avoid high doses.
Fentanyl C ≤1 µg/kg IV Short‑acting; safe low dose.
Propofol B 0.5‑1 mg/kg bolus + infusion Requires anesthesia support; monitor depth.
Midazolam D (use if needed) ≤2 mg IV Avoid first trimester; use minimal effective dose.
  • Avoid benzodiazepines in first trimester unless absolutely necessary.
  • Maintain SpO₂ > 95 %, avoid maternal hypoxia.
9.2.3 Radiation Safety (ERCP)
  • Set fluoroscopy to low‑dose, pulsed mode; limit exposure time <30 s when possible.
  • Use collimation to confine beam to target area only.
  • Position image receptor close, x‑ray tube far from abdomen.
  • Employ wire‑guided cannulation or US‑guided confirmation to reduce fluoroscopy need.
9.3 Post‑Procedure Care
  • Re‑assess fetal heart rate (Doppler) immediately after sedation wears off.
  • Monitor maternal vitals for at least 30 min; watch for hypotension, bleeding, or pancreatitis signs.
  • Provide post‑procedure counseling: activity restrictions, signs of preterm labor, and when to seek care.

11. Key Take‑Home Points
  • Indication clarity supersedes procedural hesitancy; only proceed when maternal/fetal risk of non‑intervention exceeds procedural risks.
  • Second trimester is the safest window for most endoscopic interventions due to completed organogenesis and reduced uterine compression.
  • Left lateral positioning throughout the procedure mitigates aortic/IVC compression, preserving placental perfusion.
  • Sedation should favor Category B agents; limit benzodiazepines, especially in first trimester.
  • Radiation exposure during ERCP is typically far below harmful thresholds; employ dose‑reduction strategies rigorously.
  • Post‑procedure monitoring must include both maternal vitals and fetal heart rate assessment to detect early complications.
