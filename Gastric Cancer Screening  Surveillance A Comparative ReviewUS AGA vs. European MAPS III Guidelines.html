Gastric Cancer Screening & Surveillance: A Comparative Review
US (AGA) vs. European (MAPS III) Guidelines
  • - Source 1 (US): AGA Clinical Practice Update on Screening and Surveillance in Individuals at Increased Risk for Gastric Cancer in the United States: Expert Review (Gastroenterology 2025).
  • - Source 2 (Europe): Management of epithelial precancerous conditions and early neoplasia of the stomach (MAPS III): European Society of Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ESGE), European Helicobacter and Microbiota Study Group (EHMSG) and European Society of Pathology (ESP) Guideline update 2025 (Endoscopy 2025).
Knowledge Test
  • - What is the recommended surveillance interval for high-risk gastric precancerous conditions (e.g., OLGIM III/IV) according to the MAPS III guideline?
  • - The AGA guideline notes that in some US populations, GC incidence approaches that of colorectal cancer. Which group is this?
  • - According to MAPS III, what is the lymph node metastasis (LNM) risk threshold for a resection to be considered 'curative/low-risk'?
  • - The AGA guideline states that H. pylori eradication reduces GC incidence by what percentage in non-US RCTs?
  • - For a differentiated gastric lesion with ulceration, what is the maximum size for which ESD is recommended as standard treatment according to MAPS III?

Rationale & Epidemiology
  • - Gastric Cancer (GC) is a leading cause of preventable cancer death worldwide.
  • - Early detection is critical, as 5-year survival is poor when diagnosed at a late stage (32% in the US) but excellent for early-stage disease (95-99%).
  • - Both guidelines emphasize the slow, stepwise progression (Correa cascade) from inflammation ---> atrophy ---> intestinal metaplasia ---> dysplasia ---> carcinoma, which provides a window for intervention.
US Perspective (AGA)
  • - Estimated 26,500 new cases and 11,130 deaths in the US in 2023.
  • - GC in the US is a disease of disparity, disproportionately affecting racial/ethnic minorities, immigrants, and underserved populations.
  • - Incidence in Korean Americans (25.9 per 100,000) approaches combined colon and rectal cancer incidence in non-Hispanic White Americans (29.8 per 100,000).

European Perspective (MAPS III)
  • - In 2017, over 1 million incident cases and nearly 865,000 deaths occurred worldwide.
  • - Focuses on varying risk across different European regions, necessitating a geographically-stratified screening approach.


Screening Recommendations: Who & When
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines advocate for a risk-stratified approach rather than universal screening for the general population.
MAPS III (European) Approach: Population-Level Risk
  • - Recommends screening based on regional age-standardized rate (ASR) of GC.
Screening Criteria by ASR
  • - High-risk regions (ASR > 20 per 100,000): Endoscopic screening every 2 to 3 years.
  • - Intermediate-risk regions (ASR 10–20 per 100,000): Endoscopic screening every 5 years, if cost-effectiveness is proven.
  • - Low-risk regions (ASR < 10 per 100,000): No population-based screening is recommended.


AGA (US) Approach: Individual-Level Risk
  • - Focuses on personalized risk assessment due to the highly heterogeneous US population.
  • - Does not use ASR cutoffs for population-level recommendations.
Identifiable High-Risk Groups (Best Practice Advice 1)
  • - First-generation immigrants from high-incidence regions (GC incidence ≥ 10–12 per 100,000), including Eastern Europe, Andean Latin America, and East Asia.
  • - Individuals with a family history of GC in a first-degree relative.
  • - Non-White racial and ethnic groups (e.g., Asian, Hispanic, Black Americans) as surrogates for underlying risks.
  • - Individuals with certain hereditary cancer syndromes (e.g., Lynch, FAP, Peutz-Jeghers).

Recommended Screening Age
  • - Suggests initiating screening at age 45, to potentially align with average-risk colorectal cancer screening.
  • - For those with a family history of GC: begin screening 10 years earlier than the youngest age of diagnosis in the family.


Consensus on Family History
  • - Both guidelines identify a first-degree relative with GC as a significant risk factor warranting screening.
MAPS III Specifics
  • - Suggests non-invasive H. pylori screening and eradication for first-degree relatives between ages 20 and 30.
  • - Recommends endoscopic screening at age 45 or 10 years before the index case's diagnosis.



Primary Screening Modality: High-Quality Endoscopy
  • - Consensus: High-quality upper endoscopy (EGD) is the best and only currently acceptable test for GC screening and surveillance.
Essential Components of a High-Quality Exam
  • - Consensus: Both guidelines emphasize the following for optimal detection:
Technology & Visualization
  • - Use of high-definition white-light (HD-WLI) endoscopy systems.
  • - Routine use of virtual chromoendoscopy (VCE) / image-enhanced endoscopy (IEE) like NBI or LCI.
  • - Thorough gastric mucosal cleansing and adequate insufflation.

Procedure & Documentation
  • - Adequate visual inspection time.
  • - Systematic photodocumentation.
  • - Use of a systematic biopsy protocol for mucosal staging when indicated.


Biopsy Protocol for Staging Precancerous Conditions
  • - Consensus: A systematic biopsy protocol (e.g., Updated Sydney System) is crucial for histologic staging of atrophy and intestinal metaplasia.
Core Protocol
  • - Obtain biopsies from at least two topographic sites: antrum/incisura and corpus.
  • - Place samples in separately labeled jars.
  • - Biopsy any additional suspicious areas separately.

Minor Differences in Biopsy Number
  • - AGA: Recommends a minimum of 5 total biopsies (antrum/incisura and corpus).
  • - MAPS III: Recommends 2 biopsies from antrum/incisura and 2 from the corpus (total of 4). An additional incisura biopsy is optional.



Role of H. pylori Eradication
  • - Strong Consensus: H. pylori eradication is essential for both primary and secondary prevention of GC.
Evidence for Eradication
  • - AGA: Cites meta-analysis of non-US RCTs showing H. pylori eradication significantly reduces GC incidence by 46% (NNT=72) and mortality by 39% (NNT=135).
  • - MAPS III: Recommends eradication for all patients with precancerous conditions and after endoscopic or surgical therapy for GC.

Screening for H. pylori
  • - AGA: Recommends opportunistic screening in high-risk individuals and considering 'familial-based testing' (screening adult household members).
  • - MAPS III: Suggests non-invasive screening for first-degree relatives of GC patients between ages 20-30.

Important Caveat
  • - Consensus: Eradication is an adjunct, not a replacement for endoscopic surveillance in patients who have already developed advanced precancerous changes (atrophy/GIM), as the risk of progression remains.


Management of Endoscopic & Histologic Findings
  • - Management is determined by the findings on the index screening/surveillance endoscopy.
Normal Mucosa (No Atrophy, GIM, or Neoplasia)
  • - AGA: The decision to continue screening is based on individual risk factors and patient preference. Consider ongoing screening every 3-5 years for those with strong risk factors (e.g., family history).
  • - MAPS III: Follows population-based risk intervals (e.g., every 5 years in intermediate-risk regions).

Atrophic Gastritis (AG) & Gastric Intestinal Metaplasia (GIM)
  • - Consensus: Risk stratification is key. Patients with advanced changes require surveillance.
High-Risk Features Warranting Surveillance
  • - AGA: Severe atrophic gastritis, multifocal or incomplete-type GIM, extensive GIM, and family history of GC.
  • - MAPS III: Extensive endoscopic changes (Kimura C3+ or EGGIM 5+) or advanced histologic stages (OLGA/OLGIM III/IV).

Surveillance Interval for High-Risk AG/GIM
  • - Strong Consensus: High-quality endoscopy every 3 years.
  • - AGA adds that shorter intervals may be advisable for those with multiple risk factors.

Low-Risk AG/GIM: No Surveillance
  • - MAPS III: Explicitly recommends no surveillance for patients with mild to moderate atrophy or GIM restricted to the antrum, without other risk factors.
  • - AGA: Implies a similar approach by focusing surveillance on high-risk individuals.


Dysplasia
  • - Consensus: All dysplasia diagnoses should be confirmed by an experienced GI pathologist due to interobserver variability.
Non-Visible Dysplasia (on biopsies)
  • - Consensus: Requires a repeat, high-quality EGD by an experienced endoscopist after H. pylori eradication.
  • - Follow-up Interval: Repeat EGD in 6 months for High-Grade Dysplasia (HGD) and 6-12 months for Low-Grade Dysplasia (LGD) or Indefinite for Dysplasia (IND).

Visible Dysplasia (Endoscopic Lesion)
  • - Consensus: Should undergo endoscopic resection (diagnostic and therapeutic) with the goal of en bloc, R0 resection.
  • - AGA: Notes that nearly 25% of lesions with LGD on biopsy are up-staged after resection (17% to HGD, 7% to carcinoma).


Early Gastric Cancer (EGC) & High-Grade Dysplasia (HGD)
  • - Consensus: Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD) is the treatment of choice for most lesions.
MAPS III: Detailed ESD Indications
  • - Provides highly specific criteria for selecting lesions for ESD.
Standard Indications for ESD
  • - Differentiated lesions (dysplasia or intramucosal carcinoma):
  • - Any size if not ulcerated.
  • - ≤30 mm if ulcerated (UL-I).

Expanded/Conditional Indications for ESD
  • - Differentiated lesions with minimal submucosal invasion (SM1, ≤500μm) if size ≤30 mm.
  • - Undifferentiated lesions (intramucosal) if size ≤20 mm and not ulcerated.


MAPS III: Post-Resection Risk Stratification
  • - Defines management based on the pathology of the resected specimen.
Curative / Very Low-Risk Resection
  • - Criteria: En bloc R0, pT1a, differentiated, no lymphovascular invasion (LVI), size ≤30mm if ulcerated.
  • - LNM Risk: < 0.5%–1%.
  • - Action: No further treatment needed.

Curative / Low-Risk Resection
  • - Criteria: En bloc R0, no LVI, AND either:
  • - pT1b (SM1 ≤500μm), differentiated, size ≤30mm.
  • - pT1a, undifferentiated, size ≤20mm, no ulceration.
  • - LNM Risk: < 3%.
  • - Action: Complete staging; further treatment generally not needed after multidisciplinary discussion.

High-Risk (Non-Curative) Resection
  • - Criteria: Any of the following:
  • - Positive vertical margin (VM+).
  • - Lymphovascular invasion (LVI+).
  • - Deep submucosal invasion (> 500μm).
  • - Undifferentiated with ulceration or size > 20mm.
  • - Differentiated pT1b with size > 30mm.
  • - Action: Complete staging and strong consideration for additional treatment (surgery).



Post-Resection Surveillance
  • - Consensus: All patients with resected gastric neoplasia require ongoing endoscopic surveillance indefinitely.
  • - AGA: Notes that specific intervals require more data for the US population but refers to other specialized guidelines.
  • - MAPS III: Implies surveillance is needed but does not specify intervals in the main recommendations.


Management of Type I Gastric Carcinoids (gNETs)
  • - AGA Specific Guidance: This topic is covered in detail by the AGA CPU but not the MAPS III guideline.
Pathophysiology & Risk
  • - Type I gNETs account for 70-80% of all gNETs.
  • - Arise in the setting of corpus atrophy (from H. pylori or autoimmune gastritis) leading to hypergastrinemia.
  • - Typically indolent with low metastatic potential, especially when small.

Management by Size
  • - < 1 cm: May consider resection.
  • - 1–2 cm: Should be endoscopically resected.
  • - > 2 cm: High risk of metastasis. Requires cross-sectional imaging and referral for surgical resection.

Surveillance
  • - Patients with Type I gNETs require surveillance, but intervals are not well defined.


De-escalation of Screening & Surveillance
  • - Strong Consensus: Screening and surveillance should only be performed on individuals who are fit for subsequent endoscopic or surgical treatment.
Criteria for Stopping
  • - AGA: Stop when a person is no longer fit for treatment. Decision should balance life expectancy, risks, and patient preferences.
  • - MAPS III: Suggests discontinuing or not starting in asymptomatic individuals over age 80 and considering comorbidities.


Implementation & Health Equity
  • - AGA Emphasis: The US guideline places a strong emphasis on achieving health equity, addressing modifiable risk factors (poverty, diet, smoking), and overcoming barriers to care that disproportionately affect high-risk groups.
  • - MAPS III Emphasis: Focuses on cost-effectiveness, feasibility, and minimizing environmental impact ('green endoscopy').
Future Directions & Research Gaps
  • - Consensus: Need for better, non-invasive biomarkers for risk stratification to reduce the burden of endoscopy.
  • - AGA: Highlights that GC is among the lowest-funded cancers by the NIH/NCI in the US.
  • - MAPS III / AGA: Both see promise in Artificial Intelligence (AI) for improving detection, but agree data is too preliminary for routine recommendation.
