Why Gastroenterologists Should Be More Interested in Death
  • Source: Gastroenterology 2024;167:197–199
  • Topic: Gastroenterologists and end-of-life care engagement
  • Author(s): O'Mahony, S. (Centre for the Humanities and Health, King's College London)
Author's Journey Into Thanatology
1.1 Accidental Entry Into Death and Dying Research
  • Gastroenterologist by training, not palliative care specialist
  • PEG (percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy) feeding --> gateway issue sparking broader engagement with death and dying
  • Wrote several articles on PEG difficulties, patient selection, and decision-making strategies
1.2 Historical Context of Gastroenterology Practice
  • Late 1980s and early 1990s: gastroenterologists expected to function as technicians
  • Carried out instructions from neurologists and geriatricians
  • Minimal involvement in discussions on PEG appropriateness
1.3 Return to Cork, Ireland (2001)
  • Relentless demand for PEG tube insertion
  • Even in patients with advanced dementia
  • Led to long, difficult conversations with families
  • Families often had unrealistic expectations of:
  • Prognosis of underlying condition
  • Benefits of PEG feeding
Gastroenterology Practice in Ireland
2.1 Scope of Practice
  • Must be jack of all trades
  • Includes:
  • Endoscopy (EGD, colonoscopy, ERCP)
  • General gastroenterology clinics
  • Inpatient acute gastroenterology (including liver disease)
  • Acute unselected general medical take
2.2 Subspecialization Limitations
  • Only hepatology and inflammatory bowel disease clinics available in author's hospital
  • Acute inpatient work --> roughly equal parts gastroenterology and general medicine
2.3 Patient Populations
  • Decompensated chronic liver disease patients (predominantly alcohol-related)
  • Frail elderly patients with multiple comorbidities
  • Author described self as "endoscoping gerontologist"
2.4 High Mortality in Acute Settings
  • 10% of patients died after admission (audited over 3-month winter period)
  • "Difficult conversations" --> almost daily occurrence
  • Conversations covered:
  • Resuscitation decisions
  • ICU admission appropriateness
  • Recognition that dying had begun
  • Palliative services involvement
The Annus Horribilis: 2012
3.1 Case 1: Woman in Her 40s
  • Decompensated alcoholic liver disease
  • 6 months in hospital before death
  • Multiple complications:
  • Recurrent spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
  • Hepatorenal syndrome
  • Encephalopathy
  • Several ICU admissions
3.2 Case 1: Transplant Evaluation
  • Irish National Liver Transplant Unit --> only one in Ireland
  • Patient never a transplant candidate due to:
  • Persistent sepsis
  • Encephalopathy
  • Renal impairment
  • Failure to achieve out-of-hospital abstinence from alcohol
  • Family pressured for transplant opinion despite ineligibility
  • Family refused to accept transplant decision
3.3 Case 2: Man in His 80s
  • Advanced dementia
  • Recurrent aspiration pneumonia
  • PEG tube inserted inappropriately at another hospital
  • Multiple chest infection episodes
3.4 Case 2: ICU Admission
  • Ill-advised weekend admission to intensive care
  • Died after many months in hospital
3.5 Institutional Failures
  • Both families refused to accept prognosis or ceiling of care
  • Both families were hostile in interactions with doctors and nurses
  • Hospital management --> unable or unwilling to intervene
  • No institutional process existed to manage such situations
3.6 Aftermath
  • Author --> exhausted, spiritually and professionally
  • Coffee debrief with intensivist colleague who also managed both patients
  • Led to conference presentation --> eventually led to book
Writing "The Way We Die Now"
4.1 Conference Origin
  • Intensive care conference (British–Irish initiative)
  • Held in Dingle, County Kerry, Ireland
  • Author was last speaker of final session
  • Most British delegates had already left
  • Audience --> no more than 12 people
  • Several were hungover from previous night's dinner
4.2 Writing Process
  • Worked evenings over several months
  • Writing came quickly and easily
  • Had something urgently wanted to say
4.3 Book's Core Arguments
  • Medicalization (hospitalization) of death --> not going well
  • Modern secular societies had lost rituals and practices that guided previous generations through:
  • Suffering
  • Death
  • Grief
4.4 Schism in Modern Medicine
  • Palliative care on one side
  • Oncology on the other
  • Essential conversations happening too late or not at all
4.5 Critique of Modern Medicine
  • Overfocused on process and intervention
  • At expense of relief of suffering
  • Medically assisted suicide --> distraction from more pressing issues
4.6 Publication and Reception
  • Published March 2016 by Head of Zeus (London)
  • Title: The Way We Die Now
  • Sold moderately well
  • Won British Medical Association award in 2017
4.7 Author's Self-Assessment of Book
  • Perhaps too polemical for complex subject
  • Section on medically assisted suicide --> lacks nuance
  • Mocked earnestness of palliative care too glibly
  • Too much quotation from other sources
  • Despite imperfections --> book needed to be written
Lancet Commission on "The Value of Death"
5.1 Commission Formation (2018)
  • Invited by Richard Smith (former BMJ editor, Commission Chairman)
  • 25+ people from many countries and backgrounds
  • Included:
  • Economics
  • Philosophy
  • Social science
  • Religious life
5.2 Medical Representation
  • About half were doctors
  • Palliative care particularly well represented
5.3 Work Process
  • Some meetings in person, mostly virtual (COVID-19 pandemic)
  • Occasional conflict but mostly achieved consensus
  • Author worked on Commission for 4 years
5.4 Core Proposition: Rebalancing Death
  • High-income countries: healthcare --> main context for death encounters
  • Families and communities marginalized
  • Low-income countries: many die without access to:
  • Opioids
  • Formal palliative care
5.5 Commission's Recommendation
  • Dying should be demedicalized, not unmedicalized
  • Migration of power from:
  • Institutions and professions
  • To families and communities
5.6 Report Publication (March 2022)
  • Published in Lancet
  • Author --> proud Commission member
5.7 Influence Assessment
  • Questioned influence a year-and-a-half after publication
  • May have convinced the already convinced
  • Doubted large-scale conversion among oncologists and biogerontologists
  • No appetite for sweeping societal changes evident
5.8 Critique of Reports as Change Agents
  • Well-intentioned reports --> not enough
  • COVID-19 pandemic reinforced healthcare as custodian of death
  • Biomolecular project and pharmaceutical industry emerged stronger
5.9 Harari's Prediction (Early Pandemic)
  • Question posed: "Will coronavirus change our attitudes to death?"
  • Conclusion: "Quite the opposite"
  • Predicted:
  • No significant increase in philosophy department budgets
  • Massive increase in medical school and healthcare system budgets
Five Principles of a Realistic Utopia
6.1 Principle 1: Social Determinants
  • Social determinants of death, dying, and grieving --> tackled
6.2 Principle 2: Understanding Dying
  • Dying understood as:
  • Relational process (not just physiological event)
  • Spiritual process
6.3 Principle 3: Networks of Care
  • Networks of care lead support for people:
  • Dying
  • Caring
  • Grieving
6.4 Principle 4: Conversations About Death
  • Conversations and stories about everyday death, dying, grieving --> become common
6.5 Principle 5: Value of Death
  • Death recognized as having value

Chronic Liver Disease: Missed Opportunities for Essential Conversations
9.1 Problem Description
  • Opportunities for difficult conversations often spurned
  • Discussions then inevitably take place at time of crisis
9.2 Timing Issues
  • When disease has decompensated
  • Patient may not be able to engage (e.g., due to encephalopathy)
9.3 Setting Issues
  • Complex discussions --> worst possible time and place
  • Should have been outpatient conversations over several meetings
  • With active family participation
9.4 Emergency Department Reality
  • Essential dialogue conducted in emergency department instead
  • Major decisions forced on:
  • Frightened patients
  • Bewildered relatives
  • Doctors they don't know
The Essential Conversation Framework
10.1 Reframing Language
  • Drop the word "difficult"
  • Reframe as "Essential Conversation"
  • Should be:
  • Right of every patient (assuming they want it, most do)
  • Duty of every doctor
10.2 Trigger Question: The "Surprise Question"
  • Ask: Would you be surprised if this patient died in the next 6–12 months?
  • If answer --> "no"
  • Then Essential Conversation should begin
10.3 Nature of the Conversation
  • Not a single event, but a process
  • Should explore and clarify:
  • Patient's understanding
  • Patient's expectations
  • Family's understanding
  • Family's expectations
10.4 Content of the Conversation
  • Facilitate preparation of agreed plan for inevitable decompensation
  • Include clarity on:
  • Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR)
  • Escalation to intensive care
10.5 Reality Check
  • Too often plans made:
  • Out-of-hours
  • "On the hoof" (without proper preparation)
Key Philosophical Shift
11.1 Three Decades to Learn
  • Took author 30 years to realize core principle
11.2 Death Is Not Medical Failure
  • Care of dying patients --> core part of the job
  • Not something to be delegated to other doctors
11.3 Dying Is Messy and Unpredictable
  • Like living --> often messy
  • Stubbornly refuses to submit to:
  • Protocols
  • Tick-boxes
11.4 Death Is Ultimately Unknowable
  • Cannot be fully predicted or controlled
The Most Important Attribute: Interest
12.1 Trotter's Wisdom (1872–1939)
  • Wilfred Trotter, great English surgeon
  • Quote: "To the deep unreason with which all patients approach the medicine man, his interest is more potent than knowledge and skill, the latest development in science, or the utmost virtuosity in art"
12.2 Application to Dying Patients
  • When caring for dying patients
  • Most important attribute doctors can cultivate --> interest
  • Outweighs:
  • Knowledge
  • Skill
  • Latest scientific developments
