Adenomatous Polyposis Coli Gene Polyposis-related Syndromes
  • Source: American Journal of Gastroenterology (AJG), 2026
  • Topic: APC Gene-Related Polyposis Syndromes (FAP, AFAP, GAPPS)
  • Author(s): Maratt JK, Frie A, Weiss JM
1. Introduction: APC Gene and Hereditary Cancer Syndromes
  • APC (adenomatous polyposis coli) gene --> located on chromosome 5q21-22, functions as tumor suppressor
  • P/LP (pathogenic/likely pathogenic) germline variants --> loss of tumor suppressor function via B-catenin regulation impairment in Wnt signaling
  • Wnt signaling disruption --> leads to impaired cell migration, proliferation, and adhesion
  • APC variants associated with GI polyposis syndromes: FAP (familial adenomatous polyposis), AFAP (attenuated FAP), GAPPS (gastric adenocarcinoma and proximal polyposis of the stomach)
  • These syndromes increase risk for intestinal cancers (gastric, small bowel, colorectal) and extraintestinal cancers
  • Genotype-phenotype associations --> essential for determining optimal screening, surveillance, and management strategies
2. Diagnosis of APC Gene-Related Polyposis Syndromes
  • Diagnosis begins with clinical suspicion or molecular testing
  • Up to 30% of APC variants arise de novo (during embryogenesis), so family history may be unknown
  • Genetic testing recommendations:
  • First-degree relatives (FDR) with known APC P/LP variant --> single site testing for same variant
  • Unknown APC variant presence --> multigene panel testing to optimize yield of actionable gene variants
  • Multigene panel testing yield increases with greater polyp burden (from large cross-sectional study of 8,676 individuals):
  • 10-19 adenomas: 5% (95% CI 3-5) had APC variant
  • 20-99 adenomas: 10% (95% CI 9-11) had APC variant
  • 100-999 adenomas: 56% (95% CI 54-59) had APC variant
  • >1000 adenomas: 80% (95% CI 71-87) had APC variant
  • Positive molecular testing (heterozygous APC P/LP variant) --> diagnostic of APC-related polyposis syndrome, further classification based on clinical features
3. Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP) and Attenuated FAP (AFAP): Epidemiology
  • Equal presentation in men and women, autosomal dominant inheritance pattern
  • Manifestations become apparent in adolescent and young adult years
  • Germline APC P/LP variants --> responsible for both classic FAP and AFAP
  • Phenotypic variability due to: differences in germline variants, mosaicism, modifier genes, environmental factors
  • No significant phenotypic differences found among ethnic groups in U.S.
  • Scandinavian countries birth prevalence data (1990s):
  • Birth prevalence: 1:10,000 to 1:31,260
  • Incidence rate: 0.62 to 2.38 per million
4. FAP/AFAP: Diagnostic Criteria and Testing Thresholds
  • Genetic testing recommended for individuals with:
  • Personal history of colorectal polyposis (≥10 cumulative/lifetime adenomas)
  • CRC diagnosis at age <50 years
  • FDR with either of these clinical manifestations
  • Odds of having APC PGV (pathogenic germline variant) by adenoma count (vs. <10):
  • 10-19 adenomas: OR 2.4 (95% CI 1.6-3.6)
  • 20-99 adenomas: OR 6.0 (95% CI 4.3-8.2)
  • 100-999 adenomas: OR 40.1 (95% CI 29.2-55.1)
  • >1000 adenomas: OR 124.0 (95% CI 69.7-220.7)
  • Odds of APC PGV by age at adenoma diagnosis (vs. >50 years):
  • Age 40-49: OR 2.7 (95% CI 2.2-3.4)
  • Age 30-39: OR 6.1 (95% CI 4.8-7.8)
  • Age <30: OR 15.4 (95% CI 12.2-19.5)
  • Additional clinical features prompting testing:
  • Desmoids
  • Multifocal or bilateral CHRPE (congenital hypertrophy of retinal pigment epithelium)
  • Hepatoblastoma
  • Papillary thyroid cancer (cribriform-morular variant)
  • Serrated polyposis syndrome (SPS) with synchronous adenomas: up to 1 in 5 may have germline variant in polyposis gene
5. FAP/AFAP: Clinical Manifestations
  • Classic FAP --> ≥100 cumulative colorectal adenomas; AFAP --> <100 adenomas
  • Upper GI tract findings similar between classic and attenuated forms: gastric polyps, duodenal polyps, papillary thyroid cancer
  • Extraintestinal manifestations more common in classic FAP unless AFAP due to alterations in 3' region of APC gene
  • Desmoid tumors: third most common cause of mortality in FAP after CRC and duodenal cancer
  • Histologically benign tumors originating from fibroblasts
  • Potential for locally invasive behavior
  • Risk factors: APC P/LP variants in 3' region, family history of desmoid disease, abdominal surgery
  • Median time to development after surgery: 28.8-36 months
  • Other extraintestinal manifestations often precede colorectal polyposis by ~10 years
5.1 Gastric Polyps in FAP/AFAP
  • Most common type: fundic gland polyps (FGP) with prevalence 20-88%
  • Estimated 25% of individuals with FAP have FGP with low-grade dysplasia
  • Adenomas also present but less common than fundic gland polyps
5.2 Duodenal Adenomas
  • Characterized by number, size, and histology based on Spigelman criteria (guides management)
  • Estimated 50% of duodenal adenomas arise at or surrounding the ampulla
  • Risk factors for progression (increase in size >10mm or advanced histologic features):
  • Male gender
  • Abnormal appearance of ampulla at time of adenoma detection
  • Cholecystectomy
  • History of extracolonic malignancy
5.3 Extraintestinal Malignancies
  • Well-established: upper and lower GI tract cancers, papillary thyroid cancers, medulloblastomas, hepatoblastomas
  • Pancreatic cancer risk: recently studied using Danish Polyposis Register data (508 individuals with FAP matched to 1,890 controls)
  • Significantly higher risk of pancreatic cancer in FAP vs. controls: HR 6.45 (95% CI 2.02-20.64)
  • None of FAP patients with pancreatic cancer had FDR with pancreatic cancer
  • AFAP vs. controls: no increased risk (HR 3.19; 95% CI 0.61-16.42)
  • Classic FAP vs. controls: HR 7.66 (95% CI 1.67-35.26)
  • Most pancreatic cancers were adenocarcinomas
  • Further multicenter, prospective studies needed to clarify risk and inform screening recommendations
6. Management: Upper GI Tract Polyposis
  • Overall goal: early cancer detection and prevention via downstaging of duodenal/papillary disease
6.1 Duodenal Disease Management (Spigelman Staging)
  • Spigelman staging system guides management due to association with duodenal cancer risk
  • High inter- and intra-observer agreement
  • Classification based on: number, size, histology, and dysplasia of duodenal polyps
  • Recent systematic review and meta-analysis provided pooled estimates of duodenal cancer risk at each Spigelman stage
  • Limitations: not all components uniformly predict duodenal cancer risk, lack of papilla-specific variables
  • If endoscopic downstaging achieved --> continue monitoring based on highest previous Spigelman stage
  • Surgical management recommended for:
  • Endoscopically unmanageable Spigelman Stage III with high-grade dysplasia
  • Spigelman Stage IV
  • Duodenal or ampullary cancer
6.2 Gastric Polyposis Management
  • Less well-studied than duodenal/papillary management
  • Most common feature: proximal polyposis with fundic gland polyps with or without low-grade dysplasia
  • Rising incidence of gastric adenocarcinoma in 1.3-5% of individuals with FAP/AFAP in Western countries
  • Precursor lesions: gastric adenomas and fundic gland polyps with high-grade dysplasia (may be difficult to detect due to extent of polyposis)
  • Endoscopic features associated with increased risk of proximal gastric cancer:
  • Carpeting of gastric polyps
  • Solitary polyps >20mm
  • Well-circumscribed large white mucosal patch overlying polyposis
  • Polypoid mounds ≥2cm in width and depth
  • Indications for gastrectomy:
  • Intramucosal or invasive cancer
  • Polypoid mounds with high-grade dysplasia
  • Endoscopically unresectable lesions with high-grade dysplasia
  • Multi-focal high-grade dysplasia
7. Management: Lower GI Tract Polyposis
  • Management determined by features of colorectal polyps: number, size, histology
7.1 Colonoscopy Surveillance
  • Lifetime CRC risk without intervention: nearly 100% for classic FAP, 70% for attenuated phenotype
  • Colonoscopy starting age: 10-15 years, continued every 1-2 years based on polyp burden (classic FAP vs. AFAP)
  • Important to note: estimated number of polyps (remaining and removed), rectal polyp burden at each colonoscopy
  • These factors inform timing and type of surgery over time
7.2 Risk-Reducing Surgery
  • Cornerstone of management for FAP/AFAP
  • Genotype informs decision-making but should not be used in isolation (phenotypic variability among family members)
  • APC P/LP variants involving codon 1309: higher polyp burden, early cancer development
  • Variants involving 5' region of codon 160 and 3' region of codon 1450: later onset, fewer polyps
  • Indications for colectomy or proctocolectomy:
  • Polyps no longer endoscopically manageable (dense polyposis or advanced features: size >10mm, villous features, high-grade dysplasia)
  • Symptoms related to polyp burden (i.e., rectal bleeding)
  • Presence of CRC
7.3 Surgical Options
  • Total colectomy with ileorectal anastomosis (IRA)
  • Total colectomy with ileal pouch anal anastomosis (IPAA)
  • Proctocolectomy with end ileostomy
  • Decision based on: genotype, distribution of polyp burden, presence of CRC, overall health status/comorbidities, patient preferences
  • Early referral to experienced colorectal surgeon recommended for informed decision-making
7.4 Post-Operative Surveillance
  • Anal transition zone/rectal cuff (IRA or IPAA): annual surveillance
  • Stoma/ileum (proctocolectomy with end ileostomy): every 3 years with visual inspection, or sooner based on polyp burden and features
8. Management: Extraintestinal Manifestations
8.1 Thyroid Cancer Screening
  • Mortality benefit unknown
  • NCCN recommendations: baseline thyroid ultrasound in late teenage years; if normal, consider repeating every 2-5 years with shorter intervals based on family history
  • Based on few single-center prospective screening studies
8.2 Desmoid Tumor Management
  • No current recommendations for standard screening in FAP/AFAP
  • Majority develop after colectomy; higher risk after IPAA than colectomy with IRA
  • Management requires multidisciplinary input: oncology, radiology, gastroenterology, surgery
8.3 Hepatoblastoma Screening
  • Rare, typically occurs before age 5 years
  • High-quality evidence for screening lacking
  • Some guidelines recommend considering abdominal ultrasound with serum alpha-fetoprotein every 3-6 months until age 5 years
9. Chemoprevention in FAP/AFAP
  • Most data exist for chemoprevention with NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs)
9.1 Randomized Clinical Trials: Sulindac
  • Sulindac 100mg three times daily vs. placebo for 4 months (FAP with IRA and rectal polyps, N=10):
  • With sulindac: 6 patients had complete regression, 3 had almost complete regression
  • With placebo: increase (5 patients), no change (2), relative decrease (2)
  • Sulindac 150mg twice daily for 9 months vs. placebo (FAP with intact colon or IRA, N=22):
  • Statistically significant decrease in mean number and diameter of polyps
  • At 9 months: number decreased 44% from baseline (p=0.014), size decreased to 35% of baseline (p<0.001)
  • No patient had complete resolution
  • Sulindac 200mg twice daily vs. placebo (FAP post-colectomy with advanced duodenal polyposis, Spigelman stage 3 or 4, N=24):
  • Trend towards duodenal polyp regression (p=0.12)
  • Significant rectal polyp regression (p=0.01)
  • Sulindac 75 or 150mg twice daily vs. placebo for 48 months (individuals ≤25 years with genotypic FAP but no polyps, N=41):
  • Adenomas developed in 43% sulindac group vs. 55% placebo (p=0.54)
  • No significant differences in mean number (p=0.69) or size (p=0.17)
  • Did not slow development of adenomas
9.2 Randomized Clinical Trials: Aspirin
  • Aspirin (600mg/day) plus resistant starch (30g/day) factorial design (FAP ages 10-21, N=206):
  • Neither intervention significantly reduced polyp count in rectum and sigmoid colon
  • Aspirin RR=0.77 (95% CI 0.54-1.10); resistant starch RR=1.05 (95% CI 0.73-1.49)
  • Aspirin 100mg daily vs. placebo for 6-10 months (FAP with intact rectum, N=34):
  • Trend towards greater reduction in polyp diameter in aspirin group (response ratio 2.33, 95% CI 0.72-7.55)
  • Number of polyps significantly decreased in aspirin group vs. placebo (2.18 ± 1.69 vs. 2.53 ± 1.38, p<0.05)
  • Aspirin 100mg daily plus mesalazine 2g/day factorial design for 8 months (patients with >100 polyps and intact colon, N=104):
  • Significant reduction in polyp recurrence with any aspirin (OR 0.37; 95% CI 0.16-0.86)
  • No significant reduction with any mesalazine (OR 0.87; 95% CI 0.38-2.00)
9.3 Randomized Clinical Trials: Celecoxib
  • Celecoxib 100mg or 400mg twice daily vs. placebo for 6 months (FAP ages 18-65, N=77):
  • Celecoxib 400mg twice daily: 28% reduction in mean number of polyps vs. 4.5% placebo (p=0.003)
  • Celecoxib 400mg: 30.7% reduction in polyp burden vs. 4.9% placebo (p=0.001)
  • No significant results in 100mg twice daily group
  • Celecoxib 16mg/kg/day vs. placebo for up to 5 years (FAP ages 10-17, N=106):
  • Disease progression in 12.7% intervention group vs. 25.5% placebo
  • Median time to progression: 2.1 years intervention vs. 1.1 years placebo
  • No patients developed CRC
9.4 Combination Therapies and Other Agents
  • Eflornithine 750mg daily, sulindac 150mg daily, or both for 48 months (FAP ≥18 years, N=171):
  • Disease progression HR 0.71 (95% CI 0.39-1.32) for combination vs. sulindac (p=0.29)
  • HR 0.66 (95% CI 0.36-1.24) for combination vs. eflornithine
  • Eflornithine plus sulindac post-hoc analysis (N=158):
  • When censoring endoscopic excision of adenomas ≥10mm: major surgery needed in 0/54 combination, 7/53 (13.2%) sulindac, 8/51 (15.7%) eflornithine
  • Risk reductions approaching 100% between combination and sulindac (p=0.005) or eflornithine (p=0.003)
  • Celecoxib 400mg twice daily plus DFMO 0.5g/m²/day vs. celecoxib plus placebo for 6 months (FAP ages 18-65, N=112):
  • Mean percent change in adenoma count: -13.0% combination vs. -1.0% celecoxib plus placebo (p=0.69)
  • Sulindac 150mg twice daily plus erlotinib 75mg daily vs. placebo for 6 months (FAP with colorectal adenomas, N=92):
  • Median change in proximal duodenal polyp number: -2.8 combination vs. +4.3 placebo (p<0.001)
  • Median change in polyp burden: -8.5mm vs. +8.0mm (p<0.001)
  • Sulindac plus erlotinib post-hoc analysis: colorectal polyp count significantly different with net percentage change of 69.4% in intact colorectum vs. placebo (p=0.009)
  • EPA-FFA 500mg twice daily vs. placebo for 6 months (FAP with IRA and ≥3 polyps, N=55):
  • Significant reduction in polyp number (mean 22.4% reduction, p=0.012)
  • Significant reduction in polyp size (mean 29.8% decrease in sum of diameters, p=0.027)
  • Curcumin 1500mg twice daily vs. placebo for 12 months (FAP ages 18-85, N=44):
  • No significant difference in mean number of polyps
9.5 Clinical Recommendations for Chemoprevention
  • Most convincing data: sulindac 150mg twice daily can result in colorectal polyp regression, may allow delays in surgery
  • NCCN guidelines: "not known if decrease in colorectal polyp burden decreases cancer risk"
  • No FDA-approved medications for duodenal polyposis chemoprevention
  • "Data insufficient regarding definitive endpoints such as prevention of duodenal/ampullary cancer or need for surgical management"
  • Chemoprevention should NOT replace surgical management
  • Can be considered on case-by-case basis to use in conjunction with endoscopic surveillance
10. Family Planning in FAP/AFAP
  • Following colectomy, fertility (ability to get pregnant) and fecundity (time to achieving pregnancy) can be significantly affected
10.1 Impact on Fertility and Fecundity
  • Three nationwide studies: fertility rate after IPAA or IRA of 8% vs. Japan's national average of 18.3%
  • Infertility rate between 10-17% vs. Netherlands' estimated infertility rate of 10%
  • Meta-analyses: baseline infertility rates after IPAA for FAP or ulcerative colitis were 13-20% prior to surgery, rose to 48-63% after surgery
  • 2003 multi-nation Scandinavian survey (230 women with FAP): fecundability ratios dropped from 1.05 before surgery to 0.46 after IPAA
  • Likely causes: anatomic changes following pelvic surgery leading to uterine retroflexion, post-surgical pelvic adhesions with hydrosalpinx, fimbrial damage, or tubal
