Rethinking Post-Colonoscopy Colorectal Cancer Risk
  • Study by van Roermund NS, Coupé VMH, Spaander MCW, et al. Published in Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology (2025).
  • Examines the impact of endoscopist performance (ADR & PSPDR) vs. high-risk polyps on post-colonoscopy colorectal cancer (PCCRC) risk.
  • Key finding: Endoscopist performance is a stronger predictor of PCCRC risk in the initial years post-colonoscopy than the presence of high-risk polyps.
  • Fun Fact: Colonoscopies can detect polyps smaller than a grain of rice!
Background and Aims
  • Current post-polypectomy guidelines base surveillance intervals solely on resected polyp features.
  • ADR and PSPDR have an inverse relationship with PCCRCs, but are not considered in surveillance intervals.
  • This study aims to compare the impact of endoscopist's ADR/PSPDR with polyp characteristics on PCCRC development within 3 years.
Methods
  • Analyzed colonoscopy data (2014-2020) from the Dutch CRC screening program (FIT-based).
  • Individuals categorized: high-risk polyps vs. no/low-risk polyps.
  • Association between 3-year PCCRC risk and high-risk polyps, ADR, or PSPDR studied using Cox proportional hazard regression.
Study Population
  • Included individuals aged 55-75 years participating in the Dutch FIT-based CRC screening program (2014-2020).
  • FIT positivity threshold: ≥15ug/g (first 6 months), increased to ≥47ug/g after.
  • Endoscopists accredited, must achieve ADR ≥40% and polyp retrieval/removal rate ≥90%.
Study Endpoints and Outcome Definitions
  • Primary endpoint: Relative risk of PCCRC within 3 years after complete colonoscopy (no CRC found).
  • Compared PCCRC risk based on endoscopist's ADR/PSPDR vs. high-risk polyps.
  • Secondary endpoint: 3-year incidence rate of PCCRC in subgroups stratified by polyp risk and endoscopist performance (high/medium/low ADR/PSPDR).
Definitions
  • ADR: Proportion of colonoscopies with ≥1 adenoma detected.
  • PSPDR: Proportion of colonoscopies with ≥1 serrated polyp proximal to the descending colon detected.
  • PCCRC: CRC diagnosed ≥6 months after complete colonoscopy (no CRC found), within 36 months of baseline colonoscopy.
Risk Classification
  • High-risk polyps: Adenomas ≥10 mm, high-grade dysplasia, or ≥5 low-risk adenomas; serrated polyps ≥10 mm, dysplasia, or traditional serrated adenomas.
  • No/low-risk polyps: All other polyp findings or no polyps detected.
  • ESGE guideline recommends 3-year follow-up for high-risk, no surveillance for no/low-risk.
Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria
  • High-quality colonoscopies w/ adequate bowel prep and complete procedures included.
  • Exclusion: Follow-up <6 months, further endoscopic treatment (EMR/ESD), CT-colonography referral, early polypectomy scar evaluation, cancer diagnosis at baseline or within 6 months.
Data Collection
  • Colonoscopy data from Dutch CRC screening database.
  • CRC data from Dutch Cancer Registry.
  • Histopathology data from PALGA (Dutch nationwide histopathology database).
  • Data linked using Dutch citizen service numbers.
Statistical Analyses
  • Shared frailty Cox regression (endoscopist as random effect) to evaluate PCCRC risk within 3 years.
  • HRs with 95% CIs presented.
  • Independent variables: ADR/PSPDR (continuous), presence of high-risk polyps.
  • Models adjusted for age and sex.
Results
  • 277,555 colonoscopies included for ADR/PSPDR calculation (441 endoscopists).
  • Median PSPDR: 11.9% (IQR 8.3-15.8). Median ADR: 66.3% (IQR 61.4-69.9).
  • 202 PCCRCs diagnosed within 36 months among 239,217 individuals (2.62 cases per 10,000 PYFU).
Endoscopist Categories
  • Low ADR: <63%. Medium ADR: 63-68.7%. High ADR: ≥68.7%.
  • Low PSPDR: <9.5%. Medium PSPDR: 9.5-14.5%. High PSPDR: ≥14.5%.
PCCRC Incidence
  • 164,928 individuals: no/low-risk polyps. 74,289 individuals: high-risk polyps.
  • PCCRC incidence: 2.54 cases per 10,000 PYFU (high-risk) vs. 2.66 (no/low-risk).
  • Median time to cancer diagnosis: 24 months in both groups.
Association with ADR/PSPDR
  • Univariate analysis: ADR & PSPDR inversely associated with PCCRC risk within 3 years.
  • Multivariate analysis: 1% ↑ in ADR → 6% ↓ in PCCRC risk (HR 0.94; CI 0.92-0.96). 1% ↑ in PSPDR → 8% ↓ in PCCRC risk (HR 0.92; CI 0.89-0.95).
Stratified Analysis
  • Individuals w/ no/low-risk polyps examined by high ADR endoscopists: lowest PCCRC incidence (1.16 cases per 10,000 PYFU).
  • Individuals w/ high-risk polyps examined by low ADR endoscopists: highest PCCRC incidence (4.18 cases per 10,000 PYFU).
  • No/low-risk polyps + low ADR endoscopist: HR of PCCRC 2.11 (1.21-3.65) compared to high-risk polyps + high ADR endoscopist.
Sensitivity Analysis
  • Excluding 2014 data (different FIT threshold): Results consistent.
  • ≥20mm threshold for high-risk polyps: ↑ PCCRC 3-year incidence compared to low-risk/no polyps.
Discussion
  • Endoscopist's ADR/PSPDR significantly impacts PCCRC risk within 3 years; high-risk polyps do not.
  • Individuals w/ no/low-risk polyps examined by low ADR/PSPDR endoscopists have ≥2x higher PCCRC risk vs. high-risk polyps examined by high ADR/PSPDR endoscopists.
  • Current guidelines would advise surveillance for the latter, but not the former.
Comparison to Previous Studies
  • Prior studies (long-term data) showed higher cancer risk in individuals examined by low ADR endoscopists and those with high-risk polyps.
  • This study's focus on the first 3 years suggests endoscopist quality is more relevant short-term, while polyp features may play a greater role long-term.
Implications for Surveillance
  • Incorporate endoscopist's ADR/PSPDR into prediction models alongside polyp features.
  • Establish quality assurance programs with accreditation, monitoring ADR, PSPDR, CIR, BBPS, and withdrawal time.
  • Raise ADR benchmark (currently 40% in the Netherlands) based on evidence.
Alternative Strategies
  • Distal attachment devices, longer withdrawal times, second observer can improve ADRs.
  • These are more practical in the short-term for settings where implementing a quality assurance program is challenging.
Limitations
  • Potential confounders (endoscopist demographics, participant risk factors) not analyzed.
  • High FIT cut-off, high baseline ADR/PSPDR limit generalizability.
  • PCCRC definition restricted to 36 months.
Conclusion
  • Individual's PCCRC risk in initial years primarily influenced by endoscopist's ADR/PSPDR, not high-risk polyps.
  • Reduce PCCRCs by monitoring and auditing endoscopist quality indicators, besides risk-stratified surveillance intervals.

What You Need to Know
  • Current guidelines don't consider ADR/PSPDR in post-polypectomy surveillance.
  • High-risk polyps at baseline not associated with PCCRC risk within 3 years; ADR/PSPDR strongly associated.
  • Emphasizes monitoring endoscopist quality indicators for PCCRC prevention, not solely risk-stratifying based on polyp features.
