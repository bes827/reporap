  • - Source: Study Proposal, University of Kentucky Pharmacist Care Clinic.
  • - Focus: Comparing outcomes for patients receiving care by pharmacists vs. traditional providers for UTIs, GAS, and COVID-19 under Board Authorized Protocols (BAPs).

Introduction & Background
  • - This study evaluates the effectiveness of expanding pharmacists' scope of practice via Board Authorized Protocols (BAPs) to address primary care shortages.
Problem: Healthcare Professional Shortage
  • - 78 million people in the U.S. live in a primary care Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA).
  • - A shortage of over 13,000 healthcare professionals is projected as of May 2025.
  • - Kentucky alone has over 1,000 designated HPSAs.

Proposed Solution: Pharmacist Scope Expansion
  • - Utilize pharmacists as accessible healthcare providers, especially in rural areas.
  • - Kentucky's approach involves Board Authorized Protocols (BAPs), allowing pharmacists to perform Point of Care Testing (POCT) and dispense specific medications without a direct contract with another provider.
Opposition
  • - The American Medical Association (AMA) has strongly opposed expanding pharmacy practice.
  • - Specifically opposed a bill allowing pharmacists to independently treat COVID-19, influenza, RSV, and streptococcal pharyngitis.


Existing Evidence & Gaps
  • - Data on pharmacist-led acute care outcomes is limited.
Veterans Health Administration (VA)
  • - Pharmacist prescribing is common, but data focuses primarily on chronic conditions, not acute needs.

International Studies
  • - UTIs: A Canadian study showed similar clinical cure rates; a UK study found no difference in time to symptom resolution.
  • - Group A Strep (GAS): Studies mainly reviewed cost-effectiveness rather than clinical effectiveness.

COVID-19 (Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir)
  • - Federal authority was granted to pharmacists, but practical implementation issues and restrictions limited widespread use and data collection.



Study Design & Methodology
  • - A retrospective single cohort study design will be used.
Hypothesis
  • - Patients receiving care under pharmacist-driven BAPs will have similar outcomes compared to those receiving care from traditional providers.

Study Population
  • - Any patient receiving testing and treatment for UTIs, GAS, and COVID-19 by a pharmacist at UK HealthCare.
  • - Study Period: October 1, 2022 to September 30, 2025.
  • - A single patient may be included multiple times for separate encounters.

Data Source
  • - Data will be extracted from the Electronic Health Record (EHR).
  • - Some data points will require manual chart review.

Peer Critique & Methodological Review
  • - This section summarizes critical feedback on the study's design, highlighting key weaknesses and suggestions for improvement.
Overall Assessment
  • - The research question is clinically relevant and timely.
  • - The study has high external validity for similar practice settings.
  • - However, its impact is limited by significant methodological weaknesses that need to be addressed.

Major Weaknesses & Suggested Fixes
  • - Key issues identified in the peer review that affect the validity and interpretation of the results.
1. Inappropriate Comparator Group
  • - Weakness: The plan compares outcomes to "literature values" (historical controls) instead of a local, concurrent control group. This introduces significant history bias and confounding.
  • - Fix: Extract a contemporaneous control group of patients treated by traditional providers (MD/NP) from the same health system and timeframe. Use propensity score matching or multivariable adjustment to balance covariates.

2. Flawed Outcome Definition
  • - Weakness: Defining "clinical cure" as "no repeat visit to UK HealthCare within 7 days" lacks Construct Validity. It risks misclassification, as patients could seek care elsewhere and be incorrectly counted as cured.
  • - Fix: Extend the follow-up window to 14-30 days. Re-label the outcome more accurately as "Rate of re-presentation to the health system" instead of "Clinical Cure."

3. Incorrect Statistical Analysis
  • - Weakness: The proposal to use a Studentâ€™s t-test for comparing proportions (e.g., cure rates) is statistically incorrect.
  • - Fix: Use appropriate tests for categorical data, such as Chi-square or Fisherâ€™s exact tests. For adjusted analyses, use logistic regression. For time-to-event data (COVID-19 hospitalization), use survival analysis (e.g., Cox proportional hazards models).

4. Selection Bias (COVID-19 Arm)
  • - Weakness: Including only patients who accepted and received nirmatrelvir/ritonavir introduces selection bias.
  • - Fix: Ensure the comparator group is similarly defined (i.e., treated patients vs. treated patients). Adjust for potential confounders like vaccination status, comorbidities, and symptom onset timing.

5. Vague Hypothesis Framing
  • - Weakness: The hypothesis of "similar outcomes" implies a non-inferiority trial, but the study lacks a formal non-inferiority design (e.g., pre-specified margin, power analysis).
  • - Fix: Either formally design it as a non-inferiority study or rephrase the hypothesis to avoid this language and present it as a comparative effectiveness study.

6. Limited Generalizability (UTI Arm)
  • - Weakness: The strict exclusion criteria for the UTI protocol (e.g., excluding patients >65, diabetics) reduces the external validity to populations who commonly experience UTIs.
  • - Fix: Acknowledge this limitation. Document the proportion of real-world UTI patients who would be excluded by these criteria.
