Introduction: Two Species
  • - A central theme is the difference between two conceptual beings in economic and psychological models.
Econs (Economic Theory)
  • - The agent of economic theory is described as: Rational, Selfish, and having stable tastes.
  • - Econs are assumed to be as consistent and logical as possible.

Humans (Psychology)
  • - Humans are neither fully rational nor completely selfish.
  • - Their tastes are not stable.
  • - They possess a System 1, which limits their worldview based on currently available information (WYSIATI).

The Study of Decision Making
  • - The core question is: What rules govern people's choices between risky options (gambles) and sure things?
Simple Gambles as a Model
  • - Simple gambles (e.g., '40% chance to win $300') are the 'fruit fly' for decision-making research.
  • - They model the fact that the consequences of real-life choices are never certain.



üìú Expected Utility Theory
  • - The foundational theory of the rational-agent model in social sciences.
  • - It is a logic of choice, not a psychological model.
Core Axioms
  • - Based on elementary rules of rationality.
  • - Example: If you prefer an apple to a banana, you should also prefer a 10% chance to win an apple over a 10% chance to win a banana.

Dual Role in Economics
  • - Prescriptive: How decisions should be made.
  • - Descriptive: How Econs make choices.


Bernoulli's Theory & Its Errors
  • - An early attempt to explain decision-making that dominated for centuries but contained a fundamental flaw.
Bernoulli's Insight: Utility of Wealth
  • - People's choices are based not on dollar values, but on the psychological values of outcomes (utilities).
  • - He proposed that utility is a logarithmic function of wealth.
Diminishing Marginal Utility
  • - The psychological response to a change of wealth is inversely proportional to the initial amount of wealth.
  • - Example: Adding $1M to a wealth of $1M gives 20 utility points, but adding $1M to a wealth of $9M gives only 4 points (See Table 3).
  • - This explains risk aversion: the preference for a sure thing over a favorable gamble of equal or higher expected value.

Application: Insurance
  • - Explains why poor people buy insurance and richer people sell it.
  • - A loss of $1M is a much larger utility loss for a poorer person than for a richer person.


‚ùå The Fundamental Error: Ignoring the Reference Point
  • - Bernoulli's theory assumes utility depends only on the current state of wealth, not on one's history or starting point.
  • - This leads to incorrect predictions because happiness is determined by the recent change in wealth.
Example: Jack and Jill
  • - Scenario: Both have 5 million today. Yesterday, Jack had 1 million and Jill had 9 million.
  • - Bernoulli's Theory: They should be equally happy (same wealth).
  • - Reality: Jack is elated, and Jill is despondent. Their happiness depends on their reference points (1 million for Jack, 9 million for Jill).

Example: Anthony and Betty
  • - Scenario: Anthony has 1M, Betty has 4M. Both are offered a choice: a) 50/50 chance to have 1M or 4M, or b) guaranteed 2M.
  • - Bernoulli's Theory: They face the same final states of wealth, so they should choose the same.
  • - Reality: Their choices differ based on their reference points.
  • - Anthony (starts at 1M): Sees the sure thing as a gain ('double my wealth'). He is likely risk-averse.
  • - Betty (starts at 4M): Sees the sure thing as a loss ('lose half my wealth'). She is likely risk-seeking.


Theory-Induced Blindness
  • - A cognitive bias where accepting a theory makes it extraordinarily difficult to notice its flaws.
  • - Explains why Bernoulli's obvious error survived for so long.


üí° Prospect Theory
  • - Developed by Kahneman and Tversky as a more psychologically realistic model of decision-making under risk.
  • - It is a purely descriptive model, explaining systematic violations of rationality.
Core Idea: Gains and Losses
  • - The crucial shift from Bernoulli's theory.
  • - Outcomes are defined as gains and losses relative to a reference point, not as states of wealth.

Key Demonstration
  • - Two problems are presented that are identical in terms of final wealth but framed differently.
Problem 3 (Gain Frame)
  • - Given $1,000. Choose: 50% chance to win $1,000 OR get $500 for sure.
  • - Most people are risk-averse and choose the sure $500.

Problem 4 (Loss Frame)
  • - Given $2,000. Choose: 50% chance to lose $1,000 OR lose $500 for sure.
  • - Most people are risk-seeking and choose the gamble.

Conclusion
  • - This is a decisive counterexample to utility theory.
  • - It shows the all-important role of the reference point. The 'sure thing' is a gain in Problem 3 but a loss in Problem 4.


Three Cognitive Features (System 1)
  • - These are the heart of prospect theory, describing automatic evaluation processes.
1. Neutral Reference Point
  • - Evaluation is relative to a reference point (status quo, expectation, etc.).
  • - Outcomes above the reference point are gains; below are losses.

2. Diminishing Sensitivity
  • - The subjective difference between $900 and $1,000 is much smaller than between $100 and $200.
  • - This applies to both gains and losses.

3. Loss Aversion
  • - Losses loom larger than gains.
  • - The response to losses is stronger than the response to corresponding gains.
  • - Has an evolutionary basis: threats are more urgent than opportunities.


The Value Function (Figure 10)
  • - A graphical representation of prospect theory's principles.
Key Characteristics
  • - S-shaped curve: Represents diminishing sensitivity for both gains and losses.
  • - Asymmetrical: The slope is steeper for losses than for gains, showing loss aversion.



Loss Aversion in Detail
  • - The principle that 'losses loom larger than gains' is a powerful driver of human choice.
Mixed Gambles
  • - Gambles with a risk of loss and an opportunity for gain.
  • - Example: 50% chance to win $150, 50% chance to lose $100.
  • - Most people reject this favorable gamble because the fear of losing $100 is more intense than the hope of gaining $150.

Loss Aversion Ratio
  • - Measures the extent of aversion to losses.
  • - Question: What is the smallest gain needed to balance an equal chance to lose $100?
  • - For many, the answer is about $200.
  • - The ratio is typically in the range of 1.5 to 2.5.

No Contradiction
  • - It's important to distinguish between two scenarios:
Mixed Gambles ---> Risk Aversion
  • - Loss aversion dominates. The possible loss looms twice as large as the possible gain.

Bad Choices ---> Risk Seeking
  • - Diminishing sensitivity dominates. When choosing between a sure loss and a probable larger loss, the pain of the sure loss drives people to gamble.


Rabin's Theorem
  • - A mathematical proof showing that explaining small-stakes loss aversion with the utility of wealth is absurd.
  • - It implies that anyone rejecting a 50/50 bet to lose $100/win $200 would also reject a 50/50 bet to lose $200/win $20,000, which is foolish.


üôà Blind Spots of Prospect Theory
  • - Despite its advantages, prospect theory is not a complete model of decision-making and has its own flaws.
Inability to Handle Disappointment
  • - Prospect theory assumes a zero value for the reference point (e.g., winning nothing).
  • - However, winning nothing is very different when the alternative is a 90% chance to win $1 million versus a 1% chance to win $12.
  • - In the high-probability case, winning nothing is intensely disappointing, a large loss relative to expectations, which the theory cannot capture.

Inability to Handle Regret
  • - The theory assumes options are evaluated independently.
  • - In reality, the experience of an outcome depends on options you could have adopted but did not.
  • - Example: Choosing a gamble over a sure $150,000 and losing is much more painful (regret) than if the sure thing was only $50.

Why It's Still Accepted
  • - Simpler models that incorporate regret and disappointment make few striking new predictions.
  • - Prospect theory's concepts (reference point, loss aversion) were worth the complexity because they explained phenomena that utility theory could not.


üéÅ The Endowment Effect
  • - A manifestation of loss aversion where owning a good increases its perceived value.
Flaw in Indifference Maps
  • - Standard economic indifference curves (Figure 11) show combinations of goods with equal utility.
  • - The Flaw: They omit the individual's current position (the reference point).
  • - This implicitly and incorrectly assumes the past is irrelevant and preferences are stable.
Status Quo Bias
  • - Loss aversion induces a bias that favors the status quo.
  • - Making concessions hurts, so disadvantages of a change loom larger than advantages.
  • - Example: Unemployed workers' minimal acceptable wage for a new job averages 90% of their previous wage.


Classic Example: Professor's Wine
  • - Professor R, a wine lover, would not sell a bottle from his collection for less than $100.
  • - However, he would never pay more than $35 for the same bottle at an auction.
  • - This gap is explained by loss aversion: giving up the bottle (a loss) is more painful than getting it (a gain) is pleasurable.

Experimental Evidence: The Mugs Study
  • - A famous experiment highlighting the contrast between goods held for use vs. for exchange.
Setup
  • - Three groups: Sellers (given a mug), Buyers (had to buy a mug), and Choosers (could take a mug or money).
  • - The mug was a good held 'for use'.

Results
  • - Sellers' Price: $7.12
  • - Choosers' Price: $3.12
  • - Buyers' Price: $2.87
  • - The selling price was more than double the buying/choosing price, a ratio of about 2:1, consistent with loss aversion.

Conclusion
  • - The high price from Sellers reflects the reluctance (pain) of giving up an object they already own.


Goods for Use vs. Goods for Exchange
  • - The endowment effect is strong for goods held 'for use' (e.g., wine, concert tickets, mugs).
  • - It is weak or absent for goods held 'for exchange' (e.g., money, shoes a merchant sells).


Thinking Like a Trader: Exceptions to the Rule
  • - The endowment effect is not universal. Certain groups and situations eliminate it.
Experienced Traders
  • - Experimental economist John List studied baseball card traders.
Mug/Chocolate Bar Study
  • - Replicated an earlier experiment where participants were given a gift (mug or chocolate) and then offered to trade.
  • - Inexperienced Traders: Only 18% were willing to trade (strong endowment effect).
  • - Experienced Traders: 48% traded, showing no trace of an endowment effect.

Reasoning
  • - Experienced traders learn to ask the 'Econ' question: 'How much do I want this compared to other things?'
  • - This removes the emotional asymmetry of gains vs. losses.


The Poor
  • - The poor are another group not expected to show the endowment effect.
Living 'In the Losses'
  • - In prospect theory terms, being poor is living below one's reference point.
  • - All choices are between losses (spending on one good means losing another good).
  • - For the poor, costs are not just costs; they are experienced as losses.
