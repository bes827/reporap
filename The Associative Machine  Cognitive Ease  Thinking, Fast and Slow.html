The Associative Machine & Cognitive Ease | Thinking, Fast and Slow
  • - An exploration of the automatic, unconscious workings of System 1, focusing on associative activation, priming, and the concept of cognitive ease.
  • - From the book 'Thinking, Fast and Slow' by Daniel Kahneman.

  • - What is the 'Lady Macbeth effect'?
  • - What percentage of Princeton students made at least one mistake on the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) in normal font?

The Associative Machine
  • - System 1 operates through a process of associative activation.
  • - Ideas are conceptualized as nodes in a vast network called associative memory.
  • - Activation spreads like ripples, triggering a cascade of related ideas, most of which are unconscious.
Example: 'Bananas Vomit'
  • - Seeing these words triggers a complex, automatic, and uncontrollable response.
  • - Physical reactions: Disgust expression, increased heart rate, sweat gland activation.
  • - Cognitive reactions: Assumption of a causal link (bananas ---> vomit), temporary aversion to bananas.
Associative Coherence
  • - The mind creates a self-reinforcing pattern of cognitive, emotional, and physical responses.
  • - Word ---> Memories ---> Emotions ---> Facial Expressions ---> Physical Reactions.
  • - Each element is connected, supporting and strengthening the others to make as much sense of the situation as possible.


Principles of Association (Hume)
  • - David Hume's original three principles from 1748:
  • - 1. Resemblance
  • - 2. Contiguity in time and place
  • - 3. Causality

Modern View of Associative Memory
  • - A great deal happens at once, not in a linear sequence of conscious ideas.
  • - An activated idea triggers many other ideas simultaneously.
  • - Most of this associative thinking is silent and unconscious, hidden from our conscious selves (System 2).

Embodied Cognition
  • - Cognition is embodied; you think with your body, not just your brain.
  • - The body's reaction (e.g., physical recoil from the word 'vomit') is part of the interpretation of an event.


The Marvels of Priming
  • - Priming Effect: Exposure to one stimulus (the prime) influences a response to a subsequent stimulus without conscious guidance or intention.
Conceptual Priming
  • - Exposure to a word causes measurable changes in the ease of evoking related words.
  • - Example: Seeing 'EAT' primes you to complete SO_P as SOUP.
  • - Example: Seeing 'WASH' primes you to complete SO_P as SOAP.

Ideomotor Effect
  • - A form of priming where an idea unconsciously influences an action.
Experiment: The 'Florida Effect' (John Bargh)
  • - Students assembled sentences using words associated with the elderly (e.g., Florida, forgetful, bald).
  • - Afterward, they were timed walking down a corridor.
  • - Result: Students primed with elderly words walked significantly more slowly than the control group.
  • - This involves two stages: Words prime thoughts of old age ---> Thoughts prime behavior of walking slowly.


Reciprocal Priming
  • - The link between ideas and actions works in both directions, creating a coherent, self-reinforcing loop.
Acting Old ---> Thinking Old
  • - Students asked to walk slowly (at one-third their normal pace) for 5 minutes.
  • - Result: They were much quicker to recognize words related to old age afterward.

Smiling & Amusement
  • - Being amused ---> Smiling.
  • - Smiling ---> Feeling amused.
  • - Experiment: Students holding a pencil in their teeth (forcing a smile) found cartoons funnier than those holding it with their lips (forcing a frown).

Head Nodding & Acceptance
  • - Participants listened to radio editorials while moving their heads.
  • - Those instructed to nod up and down (a 'yes' gesture) tended to accept the message.
  • - Those instructed to shake side to side (a 'no' gesture) tended to reject it.


Primes That Guide Us
  • - Priming effects can reach into every corner of our lives, influencing judgments and choices in ways we are not aware of.
Voting Behavior
  • - Finding: Support for increasing school funding was significantly greater when the polling station was located in a school.
  • - Exposing people to images of classrooms also increased support for a school initiative.

Money Priming
  • - Reminders of money (even subtle, unconscious ones) prime individualism and selfishness.
Effects of Money Priming
  • - Increased self-reliance: Persevered almost twice as long on a difficult problem before asking for help.
  • - More selfish: Less willing to help a confused student; picked up fewer dropped pencils.
  • - Greater preference for being alone: Set up chairs for a conversation much farther apart (118 cm vs. 80 cm).


The 'Lady Macbeth Effect'
  • - Feeling that one's soul is stained triggers a desire to cleanse one's body.
  • - Thinking about a shameful action makes people more likely to complete W__H as WASH and S__P as SOAP.
  • - The cleansing is specific to the body part involved: Lying on the phone ---> preference for mouthwash. Lying in email ---> preference for soap.

Symbolic Reminders of Being Watched
  • - Experiment: Honesty box in an office kitchen.
  • - For 10 weeks, a poster was displayed above the price list, alternating between images of eyes and flowers.
  • - Result: People contributed almost three times as much money in 'eye weeks' than in 'flower weeks'. (See Figure 4)
  • - A purely symbolic reminder of being watched improved behavior.



Cognitive Ease
  • - A measure, maintained by System 1, of how well things are going.
  • - Ranges from 'Easy' to 'Strained'.
  • - Easy: No threats, no major news, no need to mobilize effort.
  • - Strained: A problem exists, requires increased mobilization of System 2.
Causes & Consequences (Figure 5)
  • - A single dial of cognitive ease is connected to a large network of diverse inputs and outputs.
Causes of Cognitive Ease
  • - Repeated experience
  • - Clear display (font, contrast)
  • - Primed idea
  • - Good mood

Consequences of Cognitive Ease
  • - Feels familiar
  • - Feels true
  • - Feels good
  • - Feels effortless

Causes of Cognitive Strain
  • - Novel experience
  • - Poor display (bad font, low contrast)
  • - Complex language
  • - Bad mood

Consequences of Cognitive Strain
  • - Feels unfamiliar
  • - Feels false
  • - Feels bad
  • - Feels effortful
  • - Leads to mobilization of System 2 ---> more vigilant, analytical, less error-prone.


Illusions of Remembering
  • - The experience of familiarity has a powerful quality of 'pastness' that is an illusion created by cognitive ease.
Experiment: 'Becoming Famous Overnight' (Larry Jacoby)
  • - People see a list of made-up names (e.g., David Stenbill).
  • - A few days later, they see a list with minor celebrities and the previous made-up names.
  • - Result: They are likely to identify the made-up name as a celebrity.
  • - Mechanism: The previously seen name is processed with greater cognitive ease, which System 2 misinterprets as the familiarity of fame.


Illusions of Truth
  • - Familiarity is not easily distinguished from truth.
  • - If a judgment is based on an impression of cognitive ease, predictable illusions occur.
Repetition
  • - A reliable way to make people believe falsehoods is frequent repetition.
  • - Even repeating a part of a statement (e.g., 'the body temperature of a chicken') makes the whole statement feel more true.

How to Write a Persuasive Message
  • - The general principle is to do anything you can to reduce cognitive strain.
Maximize Legibility
  • - Use high-quality paper.
  • - Maximize contrast between text and background.
  • - Use bright colors like blue or red over middling shades like green or yellow.

Use Simple Language
  • - Pretentious language is taken as a sign of poor intelligence and low credibility.

Make it Memorable
  • - Ideas put in verse or rhyme are more likely to be taken as truth.
  • - 'Woes unite foes' is judged more insightful than 'Woes unite enemies'.

Use Easily Pronounced Sources
  • - People give more weight to reports from a source with an easy name (e.g., Artan) than a complex one (e.g., Taahhut).



Strain and Effort
  • - Cognitive strain, whatever its source, tends to mobilize System 2, shifting from a casual, intuitive mode to a more engaged, analytic mode.
Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT)
  • - A test with questions that evoke an immediate, intuitive, but incorrect answer.
CRT Questions
  • - Bat-and-ball: A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost? (Intuitive: 10 cents. Correct: 5 cents)
  • - Widgets: If it takes 5 machines 5 minutes to make 5 widgets, how long would it take 100 machines to make 100 widgets? (Intuitive: 100 mins. Correct: 5 minutes)
  • - Lily pads: A patch of lily pads doubles in size every day. If it takes 48 days to cover the entire lake, how long to cover half the lake? (Intuitive: 24 days. Correct: 47 days)

Bad Font Experiment
  • - Half of students saw the CRT in normal font, half in a washed-out, barely legible font.
  • - Result: Performance was better with the bad font.
  • - Normal font: 90% made at least one mistake.
  • - Bad font (cognitive strain): Only 35% made at least one mistake.



The Pleasure of Cognitive Ease
  • - Cognitive ease is associated with good feelings.
Mere Exposure Effect (Robert Zajonc)
  • - The link between the repetition of an arbitrary stimulus and the mild affection people eventually have for it.
  • - Turkish words shown repeatedly in a student newspaper were rated as meaning something more 'good' the more frequently they appeared.
  • - Effect is stronger for stimuli that the individual never consciously sees.
Biological Basis
  • - An organism should react cautiously to a novel stimulus.
  • - If repeated exposure is followed by nothing bad, the stimulus becomes a safety signal, and safety is good.
  • - Forms a primitive basis for social attachments and cohesion.


Ease, Mood, and Intuition
  • - Good mood, intuition, creativity, gullibility, and reliance on System 1 form a cluster.
  • - Sadness, vigilance, suspicion, an analytic approach, and reliance on System 2 also go together.
Remote Association Test (RAT)
  • - A test of creativity where one must find a word associated with three other words (e.g., cottage, Swiss, cake ---> cheese).
  • - People can feel that a triad has a solution (a sense of cognitive ease) before they know the answer.
  • - Effect of Mood: Putting participants in a good mood more than doubled accuracy on the intuitive task. Unhappy subjects were no better than random.




Norms, Surprises, and Causes
  • - The main function of System 1 is to maintain and update a model of your personal world, representing what is normal in it.
Assessing Normality
  • - The model is built by associations linking events that co-occur with some regularity.
  • - A capacity for surprise is the most sensitive indication of how we understand our world and what we expect from it.
Types of Expectations
  • - Active Expectations: Consciously waiting for a particular event to happen. Surprise occurs if it *doesn't* happen.
  • - Passive Expectations: Events that are normal in a situation but not actively awaited. You are not surprised when they happen.

Rapid Norm Adaptation
  • - A single incident can make a recurrence less surprising.
  • - Meeting an acquaintance Jon by coincidence on vacation makes a second coincidental meeting in London feel less surprising.
  • - Seeing a car on fire on a specific stretch of road makes a second fire there the next week feel less surprising, creating an active expectation for 'the place where cars catch fire'.

The Moses Illusion
  • - Question: 'How many animals of each kind did Moses take into the ark?'
  • - Most people do not detect the error (it was Noah, not Moses).
  • - Mechanism: 'Moses' is not abnormal in a biblical context. The associative coherence between 'Moses' and 'ark' is high enough that System 1 accepts it without flagging an error for System 2.


Seeing Causes and Intentions
  • - Finding causal connections is an automatic operation of System 1.
  • - System 1 is adept at finding a coherent causal story that links available fragments of knowledge.
Automatic Causal Search
  • - Example: 'Fred’s parents arrived late. The caterers were expected soon. Fred was angry.'
  • - We instantly know Fred is angry because his parents are late, not because of the caterers.
  • - This search for causes can lead to errors, as any large event (e.g., Saddam Hussein's capture) will be used to 'explain' any market movement, even contradictory ones.

Inferred Causality
  • - Example: 'After spending a day exploring beautiful sights in the crowded streets of New York, Jane discovered that her wallet was missing.'
  • - In a recall test, the word pickpocket is more strongly associated with the story than 'sights', even though it wasn't in the sentence.

Perception of Causality
  • - We are born ready to have impressions of causality; they are products of System 1, not reasoning.
Physical Causality (Michotte)
  • - We see causality directly, like we see color.
  • - An animation of one square hitting another, which then moves, creates a powerful 'illusion of causality' of being 'launched'.
  • - Six-month-old infants show surprise when this cause-effect scenario is altered.

Intentional Causality (Heider & Simmel)
  • - An animation of simple shapes (triangles, circle) moving around is irresistibly seen as a story of agents with intentions and emotions (e.g., a bully, a victim).
  • - Infants under one year old can identify bullies and victims.


Causality and Religion (Paul Bloom)
  • - The inborn readiness to separate physical and intentional causality may explain the near universality of religious beliefs.
  • - It makes it possible to envision soulless bodies and bodiless souls.
  • - Allows for belief in: 1) An immaterial divinity as the cause of the physical world, and 2) Immortal souls controlling our bodies.
