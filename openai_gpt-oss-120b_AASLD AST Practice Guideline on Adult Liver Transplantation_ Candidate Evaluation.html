AASLD AST Practice Guideline on Adult Liver Transplantation: Candidate Evaluation
  • Source: American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases (AASLD) / American Society of Transplantation (AST) – 2025
  • Topic: Adult liver transplant candidate evaluation
1. Overview & Scope
  • Purpose: Template for systematic, equitable adult LT evaluation.
  • Principles: Justice, equity, benefit; organs treated as national scarce resource.
  • Historical context: Third AASLD guideline (first 2005); updates reflect literature through Aug 2023 + Jan 2025 refresh.
  • Target diagnoses: All etiologies except liver tumors (addressed separately).
2. Evidence Development Process
  • Writing group: Multidisciplinary transplant experts + librarian; 24‑month consensus.
  • Literature search: Ovid MEDLINE & Embase (Jan 1 2016 – Aug 31 2023); gray literature via Cochrane & societies.
  • Screening flow: 1,066 studies → 344 full‑text review → 183 data extraction → 261 informing recommendations.
  • Evidence grading: Oxford Center for Evidence‑Based Medicine levels; strong vs weak based on robustness, risk–benefit, patient preferences.
  • Consensus voting: Alchemer platform; each statement voted by all writing members.
3. Referral Criteria – Chronic Liver Disease & Acute‑on‑Chronic Liver Failure (ACLF)
3.1 Decompensating Events Prompt Referral (Strong, Level 4)
  • Events: Ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, variceal hemorrhage (portal hypertension complications).
  • MELD threshold: No absolute cutoff; survival benefit observed > 12 on waitlist; MELD > 15 often used when no decompensation.
  • Policy note: MELD alone must not block referral; “under‑represented by MELD” patients may qualify based on clinical symptoms.
3.2 Acute‑on‑Chronic Liver Failure (ACLF) (Strong, Level 4)
  • Definition: Acute decompensation in chronic disease with systemic inflammation, multi‑organ failure.
  • Common precipitants: Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, variceal bleed, alcohol‑associated hepatitis.
  • Scoring: ACLF scores stratify mortality risk via liver, kidney, brain, coagulation, circulation, respiration failures.
3.3 MELD‑Na & Adjustments (Supporting Data)
  • MELD‑Na utility: Survival on waitlist with scores as low as 12; incorporates hyponatremia risk.
  • Exception cases: HCC, liver‑lung syndromes not captured by MELD; require separate consideration.
3.4 Live‑Donor Liver Transplant (LDLT) Benefit at Low MELD (Supporting Data)
  • Evidence: Survival benefit reported for recipients with MELD ≈ 11; LDLT can be offered even at low scores if prognosis favorable.
4. Referral Criteria – Acute Liver Failure (ALF)
4.1 Universal Urgent Referral (Strong, Level 2)
  • Definition: < 26 weeks liver disease, no chronic liver history, with encephalopathy + coagulopathy.
  • Referral timing: Within first few hours of presentation; before full ALF diagnostic criteria met to avoid transfer delays.
4.2 Transfer Logistics (Strong, Level 5)
  • Center readiness: Must respond promptly to transfer requests; systems for rapid referral should be in place.
  • Barriers: Insurance, substance use disorder, systemic infection may impede listing but must not preclude referral.
5. Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) Evaluation
5.1 Indications & Staging (Strong, Levels 1‑2)
  • Eligibility: HCC without extrahepatic metastases; staging via multiphasic contrast CT or MRI + chest CT at transplant center.
  • Milan criteria: Standard for listing & downstaging goals; beyond Milan allowed only with favorable tumor biology.
5.2 Alpha‑Fetoprotein (AFP) Thresholds (Strong, Level 2)
  • Cutoffs: AFP < 1,000 ng/mL; if ever > 1,000 ng/mL, must be reduced < 500 ng/mL before transplant.
5.3 Surveillance & Downstaging (Supporting Data)
  • Imaging frequency: Abdominal CT/MRI every 3 months on waitlist; chest CT every 6‑12 months.
  • MELD exception: Six‑month observation required for HCC MELD exception; early exception possible after complete response per NLRB review.
5.4 Outcomes & Recurrence (Supporting Data)
  • Survival: 5‑year post‑LT survival > 70%; recurrence ≈ 10%.
  • Downstaging success: > 80% of patients meeting UNOS downstaging criteria achieve successful downstage; outcomes comparable to native Milan candidates.

1. Cholangiocarcinoma (CCA) Evaluation
1.1 Indications for Transplant Consideration
  • Perihilar CCA (pCCA): Unresectable lesions < 3 cm radial diameter → transplant evaluation (Strong, Level 3).
  • Intrahepatic CCA (iCCA) & Mixed HCC‑CCA: Biopsy‑proven unresectable tumors ≤ 3 cm, no extrahepatic disease → consider transplant (Weak, Level 4).
1.2 Required Pre‑Transplant Protocols
  • Neoadjuvant therapy: Mandatory radiation + chemotherapy before listing (Strong, Level 3).
  • Staging: Operative staging performed pre‑transplant to exclude lymphatic/peritoneal spread; ~20% positive at surgery.
1.3 Exclusion Criteria & Contraindications
  • Uncontrolled infection, prior radiation/chemotherapy, prior biliary resection, intrahepatic metastases, extrahepatic disease → absolute contraindication.
1.4 Evidence Summary
  • Small observational series show excellent oncologic outcomes with strict selection; no robust prospective data.
  • MELD exception (MMAT‑3) granted after ≥ 6 months radiographic stability post‑locoregional/systemic therapy.
2. Comprehensive Transplant Evaluation Framework
2.1 Multidisciplinary Structure (Strong, Level 4)
  • Core team: Hepatology, transplant surgery, anesthesia, cardiology, pulmonology, infectious disease, nutrition, psychiatry/social work, dentistry.
  • Process flow: Individual assessments → integrated into unified treatment plan → consensus decision at multidisciplinary conference.
2.2 Evaluation Objectives (Three‑Question Model)

1. Prognosis without LT – aligns with “sickest first” allocation policy.

2. Physical, mental, psychosocial suitability – avoids futile transplants.

3. Patient wishes & autonomy – informed consent and shared decision‑making.

2.3 Standard Assessment Domains (Figure 2 reference)
  • Cardiac: ECG, transthoracic echo, CAD risk stratification.
  • Pulmonary: Chest imaging, HPS/IPVD screening, POPH evaluation.
  • Infectious disease: Comprehensive serologies, colonization screens, vaccination status.
  • Oncologic: Age‑appropriate cancer surveillance; HCC/CCA staging as above.
  • Nutritional & Metabolic: Dietitian assessment, sarcopenia screening (RFH‑NPT, SGA).
  • Bone health: Vitamin D, DEXA when feasible; calcium/vitamin D supplementation.
  • Physical function/frailty: Liver Frailty Index (LFI), Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS), 6‑min walk test.
  • Dental: Oral exam if stable; urgent dental issues addressed before transplant unless MELD prohibitive.
3. Organ‑Specific Pre‑Transplant Testing
3.1 Cardiac Evaluation
3.1.1 Baseline Screening (Strong, Level 2)
  • Electrocardiography + comprehensive transthoracic echocardiography for structural/functional defects.
3.1.2 CAD Risk‑Based Advanced Testing (Strong, Level 2)
  • Trigger factors: Metabolic dysfunction‑associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), CKD, LVH, premature CAD family history, tobacco use, coronary calcium > 0.
  • Algorithm options: Stress echo → coronary CT angiography → invasive catheterization, selected per center resources.
3.1.3 Management of Asymptomatic CAD (Strong, Level 4)
  • Multidisciplinary review (hepatology, transplant anesthesia, interventional cardiology, cardiac surgery).
  • Decision on revascularization timing: pre‑ vs peri‑transplant based on risk/benefit.
3.1.4 Rationale & Evidence Highlights
  • Peri‑operative stresses (hypovolemia, vasoplegia, graft reperfusion) increase mortality with CAD or systolic dysfunction.
  • CT‑based coronary assessment gaining favor due to limited stress test accuracy in cirrhosis (blunted chronotropy).
  • CAD‑LT risk scores (e.g., CAD‑LT) aid targeted testing; meta‑analysis of 15,880 LT recipients supports combined risk scoring + CT imaging.
3.2 Pulmonary Evaluation
3.2.1 Baseline Imaging (Strong, Level 4)
  • Chest X‑ray or CT for all candidates – screens for parenchymal disease, nodules, infection.
3.2.2 Routine PFTs (Strong, Level 2)
  • Not indicated without concomitant lung disease or risk factors (tobacco, occupational exposure).
3.2.3 Hepatopulmonary Syndrome (HPS) Screening (Weak, Level 1)
  • Agitated saline contrast echocardiography – most sensitive for intrapulmonary vascular dilatations (IPVD).
  • If unavailable, use clinical suspicion (dyspnea, platypnea‑orthodeoxia) → proceed to bubble echo.
3.2.4 HPS Diagnostic Criteria & MELD Exception (Supporting Data)
  • Microbubbles in left heart ≥ 3 cardiac cycles after right‑heart injection + A‑a gradient > 15 mmHg (< 20 mmHg if age > 64).
  • MELD exception: PaO₂ < 60 mmHg with portal hypertension, no other pulmonary disease → exception points.
3.2.5 Portopulmonary Hypertension (POPH) Work‑up (Strong, Level 1)
  • Echo threshold: RV systolic pressure > 45 mmHg or structural RV abnormalities → right heart catheterization.
  • Contraindication criteria: Mean PAP ≥ 45 mmHg + PVR > 3 Wood units despite optimized therapy.
3.2.6 POPH Management & MELD Exception (Weak/Strong)
  • Controlled POPH on targeted therapy → transplant considered; sustained response required for listing.

3.3.2 Vaccination Recommendations (Strong, Level 3)
  • COVID‑19: Full primary series + booster ≥ 2 months later.
  • Hepatitis A & B: HAV vaccine (2 doses, 6–12 mo apart) OR Twinrix; HBV vaccine (Heplisav‑B 2‑dose or Engerix‑B/Twinrix 3‑dose).
  • Influenza: Annual inactivated dose.
  • Pneumococcal: PPSV23 (19–64 yr) or sequential PCV13→PPSV23 (> 65 yr).
  • Tdap: One dose then Td/Tdap q10 yr.
  • MMR & Varicella: Live vaccines only if > 4 weeks before transplant; contraindicated post‑transplant.
  • HPV (≤ 26 yr): 2–3 dose series.
  • Meningococcal ACWY/B, Hib: For specific risk groups (asplenia, immunocompromise).
3.3.3 Rationale & Clinical Impact
  • Infections remain leading cause of post‑LT morbidity/mortality; pre‑transplant prophylaxis reduces early sepsis and graft loss.
  • Live vaccines ineffective under immunosuppression → must be administered pre‑listing when feasible.

3.5 Nutritional Assessment & Intervention
3.5.1 Mandatory Dietitian Consultation (Strong, Level 2)
  • Screen for malnutrition using RFH‑NPT or Subjective Global Assessment; assess sarcopenia via imaging/handgrip.
3.5.2 Indications for Pre‑Transplant Nutrition Therapy (Weak, Level 4)
  • Moderate/high risk of malnutrition or documented malnutrition → initiate tailored nutrition plan (caloric/protein targets, micronutrient repletion).
3.5.3 Epidemiology & Outcomes
  • Malnutrition prevalence 45–95% in cirrhosis; associated with ↑ pre‑LT morbidity, ↓ post‑LT survival.
  • Underweight (BMI < 18.5) or morbid obesity (BMI > 40) linked to higher 90‑day mortality but not long‑term survival differences.
3.6 Hepatic Osteodystrophy Management
3.6.1 Baseline Testing (Strong, Level 2)
  • Vitamin D level; DXA bone mineral density if medically stable.
3.6.2 Therapeutic Recommendations (Strong, Levels 2‑3)
  • Calcium supplementation + vitamin D repletion for low BMD.
  • Bisphosphonates considered individually for high fracture risk patients.
3.6.3 Clinical Burden
  • Osteopenia/osteoporosis in up to 55% of cirrhotics; fracture odds ≈ 2× higher vs non‑cirrhotic.
  • Post‑LT bone loss peaks 3–6 mo, then rises after first year; ~20% experience fractures within 5 yr.
3.7 Physical Function & Frailty Assessment
3.7.1 Recommended Tools (Strong, Level 2)
  • Liver Frailty Index (LFI) – cirrhosis‑specific, 90‑sec bedside test.
  • Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS) – rapid visual scale.
3.7.2 Alternative/Complementary Measures
  • Fried Frailty Phenotype, Short Physical Performance Battery, 6‑min walk test, CPET (requires equipment).
3.7.3 Prognostic Evidence
  • LFI, KPS, and CPET correlate with waitlist mortality & post‑LT outcomes; frailty ≈ 2‑fold ↑ risk of death, graft failure, ICU LOS.
  • Frailty improvements observed after LT but only ~40% achieve “robust” status at 1 yr → need adjunctive rehabilitation.
3.8 Dental Evaluation (Strong, Level 3)
  • Conduct oral health exam if patient medically stable; treat active infection before transplant when feasible.
  • Non‑urgent dental procedures should not delay life‑saving LT; defer only in high MELD/bleeding risk cases.
4. Psychosocial & Substance Use Assessment
4.1 Core Psychosocial Domains (Strong, Levels 2‑3)
  • Adherence potential: Evaluate understanding of medical directives, prior medication compliance.
  • Support system: Identify caregiver(s), assess ability to assist peri‑ and post‑operative period.
  • Mental health stability: Screen for depression, anxiety, psychosis; ensure treatment plan in place.
4.2 Alcohol‑Associated Liver Disease (ALD) Specifics
  • Early referral: Encourage evaluation before decompensation to allow addiction treatment planning.
  • Abstinence requirement: No fixed sobriety duration mandated; MELD > 15 patients may be listed despite short abstinence if medical urgency outweighs risk.
  • Monitoring: Ongoing alcohol use surveillance (interviews, toxicology) for listed candidates.
4.3 Medication‑Assisted Therapy (MAT) for Opioid Use Disorder (Strong, Level 3)
  • Patients on methadone or buprenorphine should not be excluded; transplant listing does not require dose reduction.
4.4 Tobacco & Marijuana Policies (Strong/Weak)
  • Tobacco cessation: Expected before listing; serial nicotine/aginasine testing may be required in some programs.
  • Marijuana: Inhaled use discouraged due to infection risk; oral/edible medicinal forms may be accepted by certain centers.
4.5 Assessment Tools & Documentation (Supporting Data)
  • SIPAT, PHQ‑9, AUDIT‑C, Tobacco Use Questionnaire – aid structured data capture but not eligibility determinants.
  • Toxicology testing recommended for all candidates at initial evaluation to verify self‑reported abstinence.
4.6 Psychosocial Contraindications (Strong, Level 3)
  • Active, untreated severe mental illness or uncontrolled substance use → relative contraindication; treatable conditions should be stabilized before listing.
5. Contraindications & Relative Exclusions
5.1 General Principles (Strong/Weak)
  • High MELD (> 40) and ACLF are not absolute contraindications; assess trajectory, comorbidities, futility risk.
  • Physical frailty/function → not sole exclusion; use standardized metric to objectify assessment.

5.3 Evidence Highlights
  • ACLF: Survival > 80% post‑LT across grades; high lactate, severe respiratory failure, vasopressor dependence signal higher peri‑operative risk but not absolute exclusion.
  • Frailty: Strong predictor of waitlist mortality (HR ≈ 2); however, frail patients still derive net survival benefit when selected appropriately.

6. Living Donor Liver Transplantation (LDLT)
6.1 Indications & Patient Education
  • Universal education: All candidates must be informed of deceased‑donor and living‑donor options (Strong, Level 2).
  • Age considerations: Recipient age alone should not preclude LDLT; physiologic age and comorbidities guide decision (Strong, Level 2).
6.2 Technical Feasibility Criteria
  • Graft‑to‑Recipient Weight Ratio (GRWR): Target ≥ 0.8% (≥ 0.008) for adequate graft volume (Strong, Level 1).
  • High MELD recipients: LDLT acceptable across MELD spectrum; caution advised when MELD > 30 due to increased peri‑operative risk (Strong, Level 2).
6.3 Outcomes & Evidence Summary
Study Design Population Key Findings
Anouti et al 2024 Retrospective cohort (registry) 3,558 LDLT recipients No significant difference in 1‑ and 5‑yr graft survival between high‑MELD LDLT vs DBDLT; lower graft survival noted at MELD ≥ 30.
Rosenthal et al 2024 Retrospective cohort (registry) 4,495 LDLT recipients Superior patient survival for MELD < 20 and 20–24; equivalent survival 25–29; worse graft survival ≥ 30, especially in NASH era.
Roll et al 2022 (ERAS4OLT) Systematic review (35 studies) Mixed LDLT MELD > 25 not absolute contraindication; optimal donor/graft characteristics (GRWR > 0.8, donor age < 50 yr, no steatosis) crucial for MELD ≥ 35.
Jayant et al 2023 (CHALICE) Systematic review & meta‑analysis (10 studies) 2,180 LDLT recipients Comparable mortality at 1‑, 3‑, 5‑yr between low and high MELD groups; higher pulmonary infection, abdominal fluid collection, ICU stay in high‑MELD cohort.
Yun et al 2023 Retrospective single‑center 223 DDLT & 126 LDLT No graft survival difference for MELD ≥ 35; risk factors for failure: age, ACLF, retransplant, pre‑op ICU stay, intra‑op RBC transfusion.
Matoba et al 2023 Retrospective single‑center 102 LDLT recipients Similar post‑LDLT survival across low (≤ 20), moderate (21–30), high (≥ 31) MELD groups.
6.4 Special Considerations
  • Obesity: BMI > 35 not absolute barrier; assess sarcopenic obesity, muscle mass, and nutritional status.
  • Donor safety: Comprehensive donor evaluation, informed consent, peri‑operative pathways to minimize morbidity (e.g., biliary complications, donor liver failure).
7. Non‑Hepatic Malignancies & Transplant Eligibility
7.1 Neuroendocrine Tumors (NET)
  • Eligibility: Unresectable NET liver metastases meeting strict selection → transplant evaluation (Strong, Level 1).
  • Survival data: 5‑yr survival ranges 60–97% in well‑selected cohorts; recurrence 31–57%.
  • Selection criteria: Low‑grade histology, ≤ 50% hepatic tumor burden, primary tumor drained by portal system, stable disease ≥ 6 mo.
7.2 Hereditary Hepatic Epithelioid Hemangioendothelioma (HEHE)
  • Eligibility: Biopsy‑proven unresectable HEHE without extrahepatic disease → transplant evaluation (Strong, Level 4).
  • Outcomes: 1‑yr survival 90–100%; 5‑yr survival ≈ 70%.
7.3 Colorectal Liver Metastases (CRLM)
  • Eligibility: Unresectable oligometastatic CRLM meeting stringent criteria → transplant with MELD exception (Strong, Level 2).
  • Key trial data:
  • SECA‑II: 1‑yr OS 100%, 3‑yr 83%, 5‑yr 83% (n=15).
  • TransMet RCT: 5‑yr OS 56.6% (LT+chemo) vs 12.6% (chemo alone); HR 0.37, p = 0.0003.
  • MELD exception points: MMAT‑20 or 15 (whichever higher) after ≥ 6 mo observation; early exception possible for complete response per NLRB.
7.4 Other Non‑Hepatic Malignancies
  • General principle: No routine transplant for extrahepatic disease; case‑by‑case evaluation when tumor biology suggests potential benefit.
8. Multi‑Organ Transplantation (MOT)
8.1 Indications & Organ Combinations
Combination Typical Indications
Heart + Liver Congenital heart disease with Fontan‑associated liver disease; alcohol‑related cardiomyopathy; amyloidosis.
Kidney + Liver Chronic kidney disease not expected to recover post‑LT; acute renal failure persisting > 4 wk; metabolic disorders (e.g., cystinosis).
Lung + Liver Cystic fibrosis, α1‑antitrypsin deficiency with end‑stage lung & liver disease.
Multi‑visceral Extensive portal vein thrombosis (Yerdel IV) precluding isolated LT; complex abdominal malignancies requiring en bloc resection.
8.2 Evaluation Process
  • Consensus decision: Separate organ‑specific multidisciplinary teams must jointly assess candidacy (Strong, Level 5).
  • Center expertise: Programs with established MOT protocols and experienced surgeons preferred for high‑complexity cases (Weak, Level 5).
8.3 Outcomes & Evidence Highlights
  • Heart‑Liver: Post‑LT survival at 1 yr ≈ 93% vs 74% for heart‑only; 5‑yr survival 86% vs 52%.
  • Lung‑Liver: Mixed data – some studies show comparable survival to isolated lung transplant, others demonstrate lower long‑term survival.
  • Kidney‑Liver (SLKT): Policy implementation reduced SLKT rates, lowered waitlist mortality for kidney‑after‑liver recipients; 1‑yr patient/graft survival similar to kidney‑only transplants.
8.4 Allocation & Ethical Considerations
  • Utility vs equity: Balancing benefit to a single dual‑organ recipient against two separate recipients; OPTN policies evolving to incorporate medical eligibility criteria rather than geography alone.
9. Conclusions, Gaps & Future Directions
9.1 Core Take‑aways
  • LT remains life‑saving for acute and chronic liver failure across diverse etiologies.
  • Structured, multidisciplinary evaluation ensures equitable, transparent candidate selection.
  • MELD score is essential but must be interpreted alongside clinical decompensation, frailty, and disease‑specific exceptions.
