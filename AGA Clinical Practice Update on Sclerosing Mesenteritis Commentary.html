AGA Clinical Practice Update on Sclerosing Mesenteritis: Commentary

Definition & Nomenclature
  • - Sclerosing Mesenteritis (SM) is an uncommon disorder of the bowel mesentery.
  • - Characterized by a combination of inflammation, fat necrosis, and fibrosis.
  • - The etiopathophysiology is still poorly understood.
Synonyms
  • - Various terms likely represent a continuum of the same disease with differing ratios of the core pathological features.
  • - The unifying term "sclerosing mesenteritis" is proposed.
  • - Other terms include: Mesenteric panniculitis, retractile mesenteritis, “misty mesentery,” mesenteric lipodystrophy, liposclerotic mesenteritis.


Epidemiology
  • - Frequency on retrospective CT studies: 0.6% to 1.1%.
  • - Mean age at diagnosis: 55 years.
  • - More likely to be male and of White race.

Clinical Features
  • - The diagnosis is increasingly common due to the widespread use of CT imaging for abdominal pain.
Signs & Symptoms
  • - Up to 60% of patients with SM findings on CT are asymptomatic.
  • - Most common symptom: Abdominal pain (up to 75%).
  • - Other symptoms: Bloating (10%–25%), diarrhea or constipation (10%–25%), nausea/vomiting (5%–15%), weight loss (20%).
  • - A palpable abdominal mass is often not found.

Associated Conditions
  • - Patients do not have a higher prevalence of autoimmunity.
  • - May have increased rates of: Metabolic syndrome, obesity, coronary artery disease, and urolithiasis.


Diagnosis
  • - A presumptive diagnosis can often be made based on imaging without a biopsy, especially if there are no alarm features.
Imaging (CT)
  • - CT is the primary imaging modality for diagnosing SM.
Coulier's CT Diagnostic Criteria
  • - The presence of 3 or more of these features is suggestive of SM.
  • - 1. A hazy mesenteric fat mass displacing the intestine.
  • - 2. Higher attenuation in the mass than adjacent mesentery (no enhancement with IV contrast).
  • - 3. Presence of lymph nodes < 1 cm.
  • - 4. A hypoattenuated halo sign around central vessels.
  • - 5. A thin surrounding fibrotic pseudocapsule.

Non-Diagnostic Findings
  • - A hazy mesentery without nodes, halo sign, or pseudocapsule is not diagnostic for SM.
  • - If asymptomatic, these findings typically do not require follow-up.


Laboratory Findings
  • - Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) may be elevated, but not in all cases.
  • - The role of these biomarkers in management or follow-up is uncertain.

Biopsy
  • - Definitive diagnosis requires biopsy, but it is often unnecessary if imaging is classic and there are no alarm features (fever, night sweats, unintentional weight loss).
  • - The risk of malignancy is very low in such cases.
When to Biopsy
  • - Consider biopsy for features concerning for malignancy.
  • - Also consider for calcifications and retraction of surrounding bowel, unless an association with IgG4 disease is established.


IgG4-Related Disease
  • - Rarely, SM can be a manifestation of IgG4-related disease, often with an atypical presentation.
  • - On CT, it can mimic malignancy with indistinct margins, invasion, and multiple enlarged lymph nodes.
  • - Only half of these patients have elevated serum IgG4 levels.
  • - A surgical biopsy with IgG4 staining may be required if IgG4 levels are normal but imaging is concerning.


Severe Disease & Differential Diagnosis
Features Suggesting a More Severe Clinical Course
  • - Involvement: Omental, retroperitoneal, pelvic fat, or colonic mesentery.
  • - Complications: Bowel obstruction/ischemia, venous thrombosis, sepsis, protein losing enteropathy, or renal failure.
  • - Other: Marked CRP/ESR elevations, coexisting autoimmune disease, arterial narrowing, ascites.

Differential Diagnosis
  • - The differential includes other mesenteric conditions that are usually distinctly different on imaging.
Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma
  • - Can present with mesenteric infiltration or a mass lesion, difficult to distinguish from SM on imaging alone.

Peritoneal Carcinomatosis
  • - Usually associated with ascites and multiple enhancing mesenteric, peritoneal, and/or omental nodules.

Carcinoid Tumor
  • - Often associated with focal bowel and liver lesions.

Mesenteric Fibromatosis (Desmoid Tumors)
  • - Commonly seen in familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP).
  • - Appear non-inflamed, mass-like, and can encase vessels.
  • - Preferred diagnostic test: Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).

Encapsulating Peritoneal Sclerosis
  • - Most common in peritoneal dialysis patients.
  • - Classic finding: 'cauliflower' or 'cocoon' appearance of encased bowel loops.
  • - Pockets of ascites occur in 90%, and peritoneal calcifications in 70%.

Retroperitoneal Fibrosis
  • - Soft tissue mass from L4-L5 to iliac arteries, encasing the ureters.
  • - Symptoms: Lower back or flank pain, distinct from the focal abdominal pain of SM.

Pseudomyxoma Peritonei
  • - Progressive intraperitoneal accumulation of mucinous ascites from a neoplasm (e.g., appendiceal).
  • - Classically results in a 'scalloped' appearance of coated organs.



Relationship with Cancer
  • - Current evidence does not support SM being a paraneoplastic syndrome.
  • - However, there is an overlap with certain cancers, including lymphoma, gynecologic malignancies, and carcinoid tumors.
Red Flags for Malignancy
  • - Mesenteric mass or intra-abdominal lymph node >10 mm in size.
  • - Lymphadenopathy in another intra-abdominal location.
  • - Spiculated, necrotic, calcified, or invasive appearance.
  • - Absence of the central halo sign.
Role of PET/CT
  • - Helpful for characterization and identifying biopsy targets in suspicious cases.
  • - Lymph nodes with SUVmax ≥ 3.0 on 18FDG PET/CT are concerning.


Risk of Subsequent Malignancy
  • - In a prospective study, the rate of subsequent malignancy was 1%; all were B-cell lymphomas.
  • - These cases had either a soft tissue nodule >10 mm or abdominopelvic lymphadenopathy at baseline.
  • - Another study reported that 80% of lymphomas were detectable within the first year of follow-up.

Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor-Associated SM
  • - SM has been reported following cancer therapy with immune checkpoint inhibitors.
  • - It is often asymptomatic and generally responsive to steroids.


Management
  • - Management is based on presentation; treatment is generally reserved for symptomatic patients.
  • - Fever should prompt an evaluation for infection before being attributed to SM.
Asymptomatic Patients
  • - Usually not treated.
  • - In several studies, only 1%–6% of patients who presented with imaging findings of SM required treatment.
Follow-Up Imaging
  • - For asymptomatic cases without features concerning for malignancy, a suggested approach is annual CT scans for 2 years to ensure stability.
  • - Thereafter, imaging is performed only for concerning symptoms.
  • - Most patients achieve stable disease without radiologic progression after 2 years.


Symptomatic Patients
  • - Therapy is aimed at symptom relief.
  • - There is limited evidence that treatment can improve imaging or prevent complications.
General Measures
  • - Treat concomitant GI disorders (e.g., constipation, GERD, IBS).
  • - Tricyclic antidepressants can be used for mild pain associated with SM.

First-Line Medical Therapy
Corticosteroids (Prednisone)
  • - Monotherapy (30–40 mg/day) may improve short-term symptoms.
  • - Typical course: Start at 40 mg/day, continue for 3–4 months, then taper by 5 mg/week.
  • - Benefit is typically lost after drug discontinuation; long-term use is not advised.

Tamoxifen
  • - The most commonly used therapy.
  • - Dose: 10 mg twice daily, alone or with prednisone.
  • - Maximal response occurs around 4–6 months, with a 50%–60% response rate.
  • - When combined, prednisone is usually tapered after 3 months, and tamoxifen is continued for maintenance.
Side Effects & Contraindications
  • - Side Effects: Hot flashes, sweating, alopecia, fatigue, thrombosis, elevated liver enzymes.
  • - Contraindications: History of VTE, PE, or significant cardiovascular disease.
  • - Active tobacco use is a relative contraindication.



Other Medical Therapies
Colchicine
  • - Used with prednisone, with a response rate of around 50%.
  • - Dose: 0.6 mg twice a day with prednisone 40 mg daily (prednisone tapered after 3 months).
  • - Contraindicated in renal insufficiency.

Azathioprine
  • - Dose: 2–2.5 mg/kg/day in normal TPMT metabolizers.
  • - Has a slow onset of action, so co-administration with prednisone is used early in therapy.

Second-Line Therapies
  • - Reserved for severe, refractory disease due to potential for significant side effects.
  • - Agents with limited data include: Rituximab, thalidomide, cyclophosphamide, methotrexate, infliximab, and ustekinumab.



Management of Severe Disease & Complications
  • - Surgery does not cure SM.
  • - In one study, only 10% of patients who underwent surgery required no additional treatment.
Bowel Obstruction
  • - Managed with nonoperative management when feasible.
  • - If unsuccessful, surgical bypass may be required.
  • - Complete resection is usually impossible due to vascular compromise.

Chronic Ischemia
  • - Caused by mesenteric vascular obstruction.
  • - Revascularization (endovascular or surgical) is rarely feasible.

Radiation Therapy
  • - Has had equivocal success.

Post-Surgery Management
  • - For persistent or recurrent symptoms, medical management is suggested, similar to patients with non-obstructive symptoms.



Conclusions & Future Directions
  • - SM is an idiopathic autoimmune disease of the mesenteric fat.
  • - Most cases are asymptomatic, do not require treatment, and can be followed.
  • - Biopsy is not required if CT criteria are met and there are no features of malignancy.
  • - There is no surgical cure.
  • - The most severe cases should be considered for referral to tertiary centers.
Future Research
  • - Future investigation should focus on the nature of the inflammatory response (e.g., causative cytokines).
  • - The goal is to develop targeted therapy with fewer side effects and a more rapid clinical response.
