AI in Healthcare 2025–2026: Regulation, Reimbursement, and Liability in the US and EU
  • Source: Health Law & Policy
  • Topic: AI Regulation, Reimbursement, and Liability in US and EU (2025–2026)
  • Author(s): Various legal and policy analysts
1. Executive Overview
Key Takeaways
  • US regulation: Refines device-centric and health-IT transparency frameworks (FDA AI guidance, ONC HTI-1/HTI-4, OCR Section 1557, Joint Commission guidance)
  • EU regulation: Implements horizontal AI Act layered on MDR/IVDR + modernized PLD treating healthcare AI as high-risk and strictly liable
  • US reimbursement: More concrete but narrow coding/payment mechanisms (NTAP for AI SaaS, TCET coverage, Category I/III CPT codes)
  • EU reimbursement: Relies more on project-based and institutional budgets, limited explicit AI reimbursement
  • US liability: Malpractice-centric with product liability grafted case-by-case, emphasis on documentation and contracting
  • EU liability: Heavier reliance on statutory strict product liability for AI/software with strengthened claimant-friendly presumptions
  • Both systems struggle with: Continuously learning models, opaque algorithms, real-world performance drift, bias, cross-vendor supply chains
2. US Regulatory Approval and Oversight
2.1 FDA Device and Software Regulation
Current Status (Late 2025–2026)
  • January 2025: FDA issued comprehensive draft guidance "Artificial Intelligence‑Enabled Device Software Functions: Lifecycle Management and Marketing Submission Recommendations"
  • Covers total product lifecycle (TPLC)
  • Emphasizes: model description, data provenance, performance evidence, bias analysis/mitigation, human-AI workflow, post-market monitoring
  • Includes Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) for post-market model updates
  • PCCP Guidance (August 2025): Clarifies what changes can occur under approved PCCP vs. new submissions
  • Applies to 510(k), De Novo, and PMA pathways
  • Generative AI Engagement (November 2025): FDA Digital Health Advisory Committee met to discuss:
  • Generative AI-enabled digital mental health devices
  • Risk management, human oversight, clinical trial design
  • Long-term safety for adaptive systems
  • Real-World Performance RFI (September 2025): FDA requested input on methods to measure AI-enabled device performance in real-world use
  • FDA Internal AI Use (2025): Guidance addresses use of AI in supporting regulatory decision-making
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Draft guidance uncertainty: Core January 2025 AI DSF guidance remains in draft form → uncertainty about investment in alignment
  • Adaptive systems gap: Guidance accommodates PCCPs and continuous learning but may not fully capture highly adaptive/self-updating systems or foundation-model-based devices
  • Generative AI concerns: Evolving behavior, crisis management (e.g., suicidal ideation in mental health chatbots), control arms/endpoints under-specified
  • Traditional pathway strain: FDA regulates most AI tools through traditional device classifications (510(k), De Novo, PMA) → "substantial equivalence" strain when predicate is evolving algorithm
  • Cross-jurisdictional complexity: Manufacturers must navigate parallel requirements from MDR/IVDR and EU AI Act for global products
Proposed/Emerging Changes
  • Comment period feedback expected to inform final guidance integrating PCCPs, real-world performance plans, and generative AI considerations
  • Movement toward standardized post-market surveillance frameworks
  • If finalized → more predictable premarket/post-market expectations, reduced reliance on serial new submissions
2.2 Data, Bias, and Safety Governance (HHS/OCR and ONC)
Current Status
  • OCR Section 1557 Final Rule
  • Effective July 5, 2024 with full enforcement through 2025
  • Covers "patient care decision support tools" including AI algorithms for:
  • Screening, diagnosis, treatment, triage, resource allocation
  • Requires covered entities to:
  • Take reasonable steps to identify and mitigate discriminatory impacts of AI tools
  • Mandate periodic evaluation of input variables and outputs
  • Makes entities—not vendors—responsible for discriminatory use of AI in covered health programs
  • Encourages alignment with NIST AI Risk Management Framework
  • Coordinates with ONC algorithm-transparency requirements
  • ONC HTI‑1 Final Rule
  • Replaces Clinical Decision Support (CDS) certification criterion with "Decision Support Interventions" (DSI)
  • Effective for base EHR from January 1, 2025
  • Distinguishes between:
  • Evidence-based DSIs
  • Predictive DSIs → subject to extensive "source attribute" transparency + mandatory intervention risk management (IRM)
  • Developers must disclose:
  • Training data characteristics
  • Validation methods
  • Performance metrics
  • Bias-mitigation practices
  • Must publicly summarize IRM practices
  • ONC HTI‑4 Rule (2025)
  • Adds standardized workflow triggers for DSIs
  • Enables EHRs to call external decision-support applications via APIs at defined clinical workflow points
  • Creates "plug-and-play" ecosystem for AI-powered tools
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Compliance burden: Substantial for smaller providers/developers navigating overlapping expectations from civil-rights, health-IT, and device regulators
  • Responsibility assignment: OCR rule assigns discrimination responsibility to covered entities even when bias originates in third-party algorithms → forces providers to develop internal AI validation/monitoring capabilities
  • Sophistication gap: HTI-1's extensive disclosures and IRM requirements demand sophisticated model documentation → challenging for smaller health-IT vendors and provider-developed tools
  • Methodology gap: Frameworks focus on transparency/process but don't provide standardized fairness/performance measurement methodology across diverse patient populations
Proposed/Emerging Changes
  • ONC and OCR expected to release additional guidance, templates, examples for DSI source-attribute disclosures, risk-management summaries, nondiscrimination assessments
  • Converging federal view of trustworthy healthcare AI: documented risk analysis, human oversight, continuous monitoring
  • HTI-4 workflow triggers should make AI integration easier → lower barriers to safer/more effective AI products
2.3 State and Accreditation Requirements
Current Status
  • Joint Commission + CHAI Guidance (September 2025)
  • First national accreditation-linked framework for organizational health AI governance
  • Defines "health AI tools" broadly: clinical, administrative, operational solutions using predictive or generative methods
  • Sets seven core elements for responsible deployment:

1. AI governance structures

2. Fit-for-purpose validation on local populations

3. Lifecycle monitoring

4. Risk and bias assessment

5. Workforce training

6. Transparent communication with patients

  • Plans subsequent playbooks and voluntary AI certification program
  • State Healthcare AI Statutes (2025)
  • Illinois: Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act
  • Prohibits unlicensed/unqualified AI systems from independently providing therapy
  • Bans AI from making independent therapeutic decisions or direct therapeutic communication without licensed professional oversight
  • Authorizes penalties for violations
  • Texas: Law allowing AI for diagnostic purposes with conditions:
  • Practitioner review of AI recommendations
  • Patient disclosure required
  • Significant civil penalties for misuse involving protected health information (PHI)
  • California: Rules preventing health plans from denying, delaying, or modifying healthcare services based solely on AI algorithms
  • Reinforces that AI cannot be exclusive basis for adverse utilization decisions
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Joint Commission guidance: Nonbinding and high-level → variation in implementation substantial; link to accreditation requirements still evolving
  • State patchwork: Significant differences in AI treatment across states:
  • Prohibitions on autonomous AI therapy (Illinois)
  • Permissive diagnostic use with safeguards (Texas)
  • Limitations on AI-only coverage decisions (California)
  • Liability gaps: Few states have comprehensive frameworks for documentation, evidence standards, disclosure obligations tailored to AI-related medical injuries
Proposed/Emerging Changes
  • Joint Commission and CHAI plan detailed playbooks + voluntary certification scheme → quasi-regulatory benchmark
  • State legislators/attorneys general increasingly issuing advisories and targeted statutes focusing on:
  • Prohibitions on AI-only determinations
  • Requirements for human review
  • Transparency to patients
  • Enforcement mechanisms through state agencies
3. US Reimbursement and Payment
3.1 Medicare Inpatient Payment and NTAP/TCET
Current Status
  • Inpatient Prospective Payment System (IPPS): Primary reimbursement vehicle for AI-enabled technologies
  • New Technology Add-on Payments (NTAPs)
  • Temporary additional reimbursement (up to 3 years)
  • Qualifying criteria: inadequate payment under existing MS-DRGs + substantial clinical improvement
  • Viz.ai stroke-detection tool: First AI application to secure NTAP status → ~$1,000 per use on top of DRG payment
  • Subsequent AI devices: cardiac ultrasound guidance, digital therapeutics
  • By 2025: 4 SaaS tools held NTAP status (up from 1 in 2023)
  • Transitional Coverage for Emerging Technologies (TCET)
  • Finalized 2024
  • Expedited route to national Medicare coverage for FDA breakthrough devices
  • Includes AI-enabled devices fitting within existing benefit categories
  • Goal: finalize coverage decisions within ~6 months of FDA authorization
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Temporary nature: NTAPs are temporary + narrow → many AI tools unfunded or cross-subsidized within DRGs
  • Uncertainty: Hospitals uncertain about when/if AI tools transition from NTAP to permanent DRG adjustments
  • Historical data: Actual NTAP spending often below projections → questions about program sufficiency for sustainable AI adoption
  • TCET limitations: Limited to small number of breakthrough devices per year; substantial evidence development challenging for context-dependent AI
  • SaaS economics: TCET and NTAP don't address per-user/per-month licensing models
Proposed/Emerging Changes
  • Health Tech Investment Act (bipartisan, discussed 2026): Would assign FDA-approved algorithm-based healthcare services to New Technology APCs (outpatient) for minimum 5 years → more stable pathway than current NTAPs
  • MedPAC work: Refinements to how NTAP/DRG adjustments treat SaaS and AI tools:
  • Better cost data collection
  • Consideration of ongoing software update/maintenance costs
  • Integration with FDA lifecycle guidance + real-world performance monitoring → coverage with evidence development models
3.2 Outpatient and Physician Fee Schedule
Current Status
  • CPT/HCPCS Codes: Primary billing mechanism under Medicare Physician Fee Schedule (PFS) and commercial fee schedules
  • As of January 2026:
  • 26 CPT codes specific to clinical AI solutions
  • Only 3 services assigned Category I status (stable national payment rates):
  • CT-based fractional flow reserve
  • Cardiovascular risk-prediction service using CT data
  • AI-based diabetic retinopathy detection tool
  • Most other AI-related codes remain Category III (temporary tracking codes)
  • Reimbursed under carrier pricing by Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs)
  • Substantial geographic and payer variation
  • CPT 2026 Code Set
  • New codes for remote monitoring and AI diagnostics
  • Expands reimbursement opportunities for AI-assisted services
  • Emphasizes:
  • AI diagnostic tools moving from provisional to permanent recognition
  • Shorter remote monitoring periods
  • Increased flexibility for digital behavioral health services
  • CY 2026 PFS Final Rule
  • CMS acknowledges practice-expense (PE) methodology based on 2007–2008 data poorly captures SaaS/AI costs
  • Since CY 2022: begun reimbursing certain SaaS and AI applications
  • Received updated AMA survey data
  • Declined to revise PE inputs for 2026 due to data-quality concerns
  • Solicited comments on:
  • How to value SaaS tools
  • Account for subscription/licensing costs
  • Incorporate physician work in interpreting AI outputs
  • Many SaaS expenses treated as indirect costs → exploring new payment frameworks
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Category III instability: Heavy reliance creates unstable, geographically uneven reimbursement → discourages investment
  • SaaS misalignment: Subscription/enterprise licenses misaligned with PFS structures built around per-service, per-time, per-facility costs
  • CMS methodology: 2007–2008 PE data doesn't reflect modern AI costs
  • Code status: CPT 2026 expands AI codes but CMS may not assign active payment status/RVUs to all → some may be "not valid for Medicare purposes"
  • Commercial payer follow-on: Commercial payers often follow Medicare's lead → restrictive coverage policies
Proposed/Emerging Changes
  • CMS explicit solicitation on SaaS/AI PE inputs + AMA survey data capturing AI expenses → future PFS rulemaking may move toward explicit recognition
  • Continued migration from Category III to Category I CPT status → more stable payment baselines for evidence-backed AI tools
  • Potential payment models:
  • Dedicated APCs for algorithm-based services
  • Bundling AI costs into enhanced care-management and advanced primary-care management G-codes
3.3 Commercial Payer Developments
Current Status
  • Coverage variability: Commercial payers reimbursing certain AI-enabled services (mirroring Medicare CPT/HCPCS codes for radiology, cardiology, ophthalmology) but coverage highly variable → often negotiated in contracts
  • AI in claims analytics: Insurers/government contractors increasingly using AI for real-time claims analytics/audits → flag anomalies for post-payment review and overpayment recovery
  • State-level measures: California's prohibition on plan decisions based solely on AI algorithms requires human oversight + multi-factor utilization review
  • Contracting evolution: Payers/providers increasingly specifying documentation expectations for AI-assisted services → requiring proof of FDA clearance or validated clinical benefit
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Uncertainty: Lack of stable, transparent coverage criteria across commercial payers → discourages implementation investment
  • Asymmetrical AI use: Payers use AI to tighten oversight/recoup payments; providers see fewer AI-driven gains → reinforces perceptions that AI increases administrative burden
  • Opaque audit algorithms: Providers may face financial/administrative pressure even when AI doesn't improve clinical reimbursement
Proposed/Emerging Changes
  • Payment innovation models: Specialty-care episodes and advanced primary-care management assume integrated, technology-supported care (including AI) funded through per-member payments + shared-savings → internalizes AI costs
  • Standardization potential: As evidence from TCET, NTAP, Category I AI codes accumulates → commercial payers may standardize coverage for high-value AI tools
4. Liability and Accountability for AI in Healthcare
4.1 US Liability: Clinicians, Institutions, and Vendors
Current Status (Late 2025–2026)
  • General framework: AI-related patient harm addressed under existing medical malpractice + product-liability frameworks (not AI-specific federal statutes)
  • Malpractice elements: Duty, breach of standard of care, causation, damages
  • Standard of care = what reasonably prudent, similarly situated clinician would do
  • AI complications: Each element complicated when outcomes depend on human judgments + algorithmic outputs (opaque/proprietary)
  • Physician responsibility: Must exercise independent clinical judgment when using AI tools
  • Blind reliance on AI recommendations without critical appraisal = breach of standard of care
  • Reasonableness evaluated based on:
  • Whether AI tool was FDA-cleared
  • Used within intended purpose
  • Known limitations/biases
  • Documentation of decision process (including reasons for following/overriding AI outputs)
  • Local standards of practice
  • Institutional liability: Hospitals/health systems face potential liability for:
  • Negligent selection, validation, deployment, monitoring of AI tools
  • Inadequate governance structures
  • Insufficient staff training
  • Failure to respond to known performance issues/bias
  • Vendor liability: Product-liability claims for AI tools classified as devices:
  • Design defects, manufacturing defects, inadequate warnings, misrepresentation
  • Learned-intermediary rule can shift responsibility to clinicians
  • Informed consent: Emerging focus:
  • Liability can arise from consent malpractice even when treatment meets technical standard of care
  • Failure to disclose material information about AI's role, limitations, data use could support consent-based claims
  • Particularly where AI is experimental or materially alters risk profiles
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal
  • Fault allocation unclear: Multiple actors (clinician, institution, vendor) may be implicated → no AI-specific apportionment rules
  • Evolving standard of care:
  • Failing to use widely available, high-performing AI tools might be alleged as negligence
  • Early adopters risk criticism for using immature technology or relying too heavily on poorly understood tools
  • Insurance coverage lags: Malpractice carriers/product-liability insurers calibrating policies for AI risks:
  • Some introducing AI-specific exclusions
  • Requiring provider training/governance as coverage conditions → potential coverage gaps
  • Evidentiary challenges: Proprietary/opaque algorithms + complex data pipelines make it difficult to prove defectiveness or causation
Proposed/Emerging Changes and Practical Improvements
  • State AI-specific health statutes:
  • Illinois (2025): Effectively prohibits autonomous AI therapy → requires licensed professionals maintain control over therapeutic decisions
  • Texas (2025): Authorizes AI use in diagnosis conditioned on practitioner review + patient disclosure → establishes civil penalties for PHI misuse
  • California (2025): Prohibits AI-only coverage decisions by health plans → reinforces algorithmic outputs must be considered alongside human judgment
  • Contractual and governance best practices:
  • Contractual risk allocation: Robust indemnification for algorithmic errors, bias, data breaches, regulatory noncompliance; clearer warranties on data sources/training/validation/support; explicit division of responsibilities
  • Auditability and documentation: Detailed audit logs, performance benchmarks validated across multiple sites, vendor obligations to maintain/share logs/risk-management documentation
  • Governance and incident response: AI governance committees, formal validation/approval processes, bias monitoring, incident reporting, board-level oversight
4.2 EU Liability: AI Act, MDR, and Product Liability Directive
Current Status (Late 2025–2026)
  • Regulatory framework intersection: AI-powered medical devices at intersection of:
  • Medical Device Regulation (MDR)
  • AI Act
  • Revised Product Liability Directive (PLD) (Directive (EU) 2024/2853)
  • AI Act classification
  • Under Article 6(1) and Annex I: AI systems used as safety components of products subject to MDR/IVDR = high-risk AI systems
  • Most standalone AI medical software expected to fall into MDR Class IIa → high-risk AI category
  • High-risk provisions become fully applicable:
  • Core obligations: August 2026
  • Classification rules for certain high-risk systems (Article 6(1)): August 2027
  • High-risk AI obligations
  • Risk management, data governance, documentation, human oversight, post-market monitoring
  • Must implement lifecycle risk-management systems (integrated with ISO 14971, IEC 62304)
  • Maintain high-quality training data
  • Provide explainability/transparency (within IP constraints)
  • Ensure meaningful human oversight
  • Monitor real-world performance and algorithm drift
  • MDCG 2025‑6 Guidance: Clarifies when medical device = high-risk AI system
  • Devices in MDR risk class IIa or higher generally automatically high-risk under AI Act
  • Necessitates dual compliance with MDR + AI-Act requirements
  • Revised Product Liability Directive (PLD)
  • Extends strict liability beyond tangible products to include:
  • Software, AI systems, digital design files, connected services essential to product safety
  • Explicitly encompasses digital health products and AI-based decision-support tools
  • Key changes:
  • Removes prior caps on liability for personal injury + certain data harms
  • Permits extension of limitation period up to 25 years for latent injuries
  • Broadens circle of liable economic operators (manufacturers, importers, component/software suppliers, updaters, authorized representatives)
  • Introduces claimant-friendly disclosure obligations and presumptions of defectiveness/causation in complex, black-box AI cases
  • National malpractice: Continues to govern clinicians/hospitals
  • Strict PLD liability attaches to product provider
  • Fault-based claims remain available against healthcare professionals
Why Current Status Is Suboptimal and Key Concerns
  • Compliance matrix: Overlapping MDR, AI Act, PLD creates dense compliance requirements
  • Must satisfy: MDR safety/performance + AI-Act high-risk obligations + PLD defectiveness expectations
  • Non-conformity under any regime potentially supports finding of defect
  • Notified-body capacity: Practical constraints:
  • Many notified bodies must become accredited under both MDR/IVDR and AI Act
  • Bottlenecks possible for evaluating complex AI systems (frequent updates, external data, general-purpose AI components)
  • Litigation exposure: Revised PLD's broadened disclosure obligations + presumptions:
  • Courts may presume defectiveness/causation if defendants fail to provide necessary documentation
  • Significant exposure for AI providers who can't maintain comprehensive logs, risk assessments, update records
  • Liability allocation uncertainty: Uncertainty about how liability allocated among:
  • Device manufacturers
  • Embedded AI-software providers
  • Integrators
  • Healthcare institutions (harm from interactions among multiple components or post-market modifications)
Proposed Refinements and Expected Improvements
  • Practical guidance needs: Emphasis on evidence/documentation expectations for AI-related harm:
  • Standardized logging
  • Version control
  • Change-management practices satisfying both AI-Act and PLD requirements
  • Expected improvements:
  • Easier for patients to obtain redress where AI systems fail safety expectations
  • Stronger incentives for manufacturers to design robust risk-management processes, ensure transparency, maintain control over software updates/third-party components
  • AILD withdrawal: Previously proposed AI Liability Directive withdrawn → modestly reduces regulatory density, but AI Act + PLD still tight liability framework
4.3 Comparative Insights: US vs EU Liability Approaches
Aspect United States European Union
Primary liability basis Fault-based medical malpractice + common-law/product-liability doctrines Combination of strict product liability (revised PLD) + national fault-based professional liability
Who is typically liable Clinicians (failure to exercise independent judgment/obtain adequate consent); hospitals/health systems (negligent selection/governance/training); device/software manufacturers (design/warning/manufacturing defects) Manufacturers/economic operators (strictly liable for defective AI products/updates); hospitals/clinicians (fault-based when failing to oversee or appropriately use AI tools)
AI vendor/developer role Product-liability exposure for AI devices/software; contracting increasingly allocates risk via indemnities/warranties/insurance/documentation; vendors often rely on learned-intermediary doctrine Clearly within scope of PLD as producers of products (including standalone software/AI systems); subject to strict liability, extended limitation periods, disclosure duties, presumptions of defectiveness/causation if safety requirements (AI Act obligations) breached
Key 2025–2026 changes State statutes limiting/conditioning AI use (Illinois, Texas); prohibiting AI-only coverage decisions (California); Joint Commission-CHAI AI governance guidance; growing legal/contractual guidance on AI service agreements, indemnification, governance AI Act classification of most healthcare AI as high-risk with extensive governance; revised PLD explicitly extending strict liability to software/AI, removing compensation caps, expanding liable operators, strengthening evidentiary presumptions; MDCG 2025-6 guidance clarifying high-risk classification and AI Act-MDR interplay
5. EU as Regulatory and Reimbursement Comparator
5.1 Regulatory Status of the AI Act in Healthcare
  • EU AI Act (entered into force August 2024): Risk-based framework with categories:
  • Prohibited
  • High-risk
  • Limited-risk
  • Minimal-risk
  • Healthcare AI classification: Generally treated as high-risk
  • Includes AI embedded in MDR/IVDR-regulated medical devices
  • Many clinical decision-support tools
  • High-risk obligations: Data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, post-market monitoring
  • Timeline:
  • Prohibited AI + AI literacy provisions: early 2025
  • General-purpose AI model obligations: August 2025
  • Most high-risk AI obligations: August 2026
  • Classification rules for certain high-risk systems (including MDR/IVDR devices under Article 6(1)): August 2027
  • Guidance: MDCG 2025-6 and sector-specific advisories explain:
  • How medical-device manufacturers integrate AI-Act requirements into MDR conformity assessments
  • Notified bodies need AI expertise
  • Technical documentation expansion: model architecture, training/validation approaches, performance metrics, AI-specific risk assessments
5.2 EU Reimbursement and Adoption Levers
  • No single payer: Reimbursement largely determined at member-state level + within regional/institutional budgets
  • 2025 European Commission report on AI deployment in healthcare:
  • Most member states lack dedicated reimbursement pathways for AI tools
  • Hospitals (particularly smaller ones) struggle justifying investments without stable funding streams
  • Organizational/financial barriers limit AI deployment primarily to larger academic centers
  • Some member states have structured assessment/certification pathways:
  • Germany, France, Belgium → precursors to reimbursement models
  • Finland, Netherlands, Spain developing national frameworks for responsible AI innovation
  • EU-level initiatives:
  • European Health Data Space
  • AI and digital-health strategies
  • Invest in: data infrastructure, AI testing facilities, supercomputing access
  • These are enabling measures → not direct reimbursement mechanisms
5.3 Key Contrasts with US
Aspect EU US
Regulatory scope/centralization Horizontal, binding AI framework explicitly covering healthcare; harmonized across member states; layered on MDR/IVDR Sectoral regulators (FDA, ONC, OCR) using guidance/rulemaking within existing statutory frameworks; significant state-level variation
Liability structure Revised PLD creates explicit strict liability for software/AI products with robust presumptions/disclosure duties Remains grounded in malpractice + traditional product-liability doctrines shaped through case law/contracts
Reimbursement orientation More fragmented; reimbursement relies on project funding, institutional budgets, early national frameworks Medicare/commercial payers explicitly recognize AI via CPT/HCPCS codes, NTAPs, TCET pathways (even if incomplete)
Data/infrastructure levers Strong emphasis on shared data infrastructure, testing facilities, innovation funding (AI Factories, TEF-Health, European Health Data Space) Focus more on coding, payment, entity-level governance, civil-rights compliance

6.2 Summary Assessment

US developments: Progress driven by sectoral regulators, professional bodies, state legislatures

  • More mature regulatory frameworks but fragmented liability

EU developments: Driven by horizontal AI regulation + strict product-liability reform layered on medical-device law

  • Reimbursement still emerging primarily at national level

Overall trajectory: Both systems moving toward more mature, though still evolving, frameworks for:

  • Regulating AI in healthcare
  • Paying for AI-enabled services
  • Assigning responsibility for AI-related harm
