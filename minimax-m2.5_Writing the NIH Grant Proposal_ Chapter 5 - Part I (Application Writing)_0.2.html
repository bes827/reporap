1. Understanding Innovation in NIH Proposals
1.1 NIH Definition of Innovation
  • Innovation challenges current research/clinical practice paradigms using novel theoretical concepts, approaches, methodologies, instrumentation, or interventions
  • Can be novel to one field OR novel in a broad sense
  • May involve refinement, improvement, or new application of existing concepts
1.2 Four Categories of Innovation
  • New Idea: Underlying concept already tested in several studies (less innovative)
  • New Population: Studying understudied populations—sex (women), race/ethnicity (minorities at greater risk for illness)
  • Novel Technology/Statistics: New devices or statistical methods showing promise over previous approaches
  • Untested Methodological Improvements: Larger sample size, longer follow-up duration
1.3 Key Insight: Simplification vs. Complexity
  • Science proposed is inevitably a simplification of complex reality
  • Must acknowledge complexities without getting sidetracked
  • Avoid over-complication that prevents completion
  • Writing principle: Be ruthless with prose—cut anything that doesn't advance the story
2. The Approach Section: Overview
2.1 Purpose and Structure
  • Describes methodology to address Specific Aims
  • Typically 8–9 pages in length
  • Two main components:
  • Preliminary Studies (new application) OR Progress Report (renewal/revision)
  • Research Methods and Design
2.2 Preliminary Studies Section
  • Demonstrates capabilities to carry out proposed research
  • Showcases pilot data supporting hypotheses
  • For new applications: presents Preliminary Studies (not Progress Report)
2.3 What to Include in Preliminary Studies
  • Team Skills: Research abstracts demonstrating competence in relevant areas (e.g., heart rate variability, baroreflex sensitivity expertise)
  • Pilot Data: Direct data bearing on research question—more important for R01 than K awards or R21
  • Experience: Any team member's data supporting capabilities becomes part of PI's experience
2.4 Pilot Data Importance by Grant Mechanism
  • R01: Crucial—provides evidence for: (1) research experience, (2) project feasibility, (3) hypothesis support
  • R21: Less required (exploratory), but still recommended—may be reviewed with R01 applications
  • Recommendation: Always obtain feasibility data, even on few subjects

  • Sample: 5 hypertensive patients (3 male, 2 female)
  • Design: Open-label trial, no control condition

4. Research Methods and Design: Core Elements
4.1 Essential Components
  • Overall strategy, methodology, analyses to accomplish Specific Aims
  • Data collection, analysis, and interpretation methods
  • Resource-sharing plans as appropriate
  • Potential problems, alternative strategies, benchmarks for success
  • Early-stage development: feasibility strategy and high-risk management
  • Safety precautions (hazardous materials, select agents)
4.2 Detailed Requirements Checklist
  • Study design with strengths/advantages and alternatives
  • Population characteristics, inclusion/exclusion criteria
  • Informed consent procedures
  • Recruitment/attrition plan with flowchart and backup plan
  • Sample size and power calculations for each primary hypothesis
  • Manipulation, intervention, independent variables, control conditions
  • Outcome descriptions
  • Procedures with quality control measures
  • Randomization method (stratification, matching considerations)
  • Study timeline
  • Measures used in study
  • Data management and missing values handling
  • Statistical analysis approach
  • Dissemination of results
  • Potential limitations and solutions
4.3 Writing Guidance
  • Must include exhaustive methodological detail despite 12-page limit
  • Cut unnecessary phrases, sentences, paragraphs—revise repeatedly
  • Maintain natural flow and readability even in methodological detail
5. Study Design Example: Guided Breathing RCT
5.1 Overview and Design Structure
  • RCT with four conditions:
  • Standard duration treatment (N = 80)
  • Extended duration treatment (N = 80)
  • Placebo control (N = 80)
  • Usual care control (N = 80)
  • Design: Mixed design—within-subjects (change in ABP) × between-subjects (conditions)
5.2 Condition Details
  • Standard Duration: Device used during first 8 weeks only, then discontinued
  • Extended Duration: Device used throughout entire 12-month period
  • Placebo: Identical device appearance, but plays random musical tones (not guided to 6–10 breaths/min)—creates 15-minute relaxation period; use ends at 8 weeks
  • Usual Care: No device given
5.3 Intervention: Device-Guided Breathing
  • Device: Respirco (Taos, NM) guides breathing to 6 breaths/minute
  • Manufacturer reprogrammed devices for placebo condition (see letter in Chapter 7)
  • Placebo device allows evaluation of: (a) relaxation effect on BP, (b) guided breathing effect over and above relaxation
5.4 Primary and Secondary Outcomes
  • Primary outcome: Difference between conditions in ABP change from baseline to 8 weeks
  • Secondary outcome: Difference between conditions in ABP change from baseline to 12 months
  • Additional outcomes: Ambulatory respiration rate, HRV, baroreflex sensitivity, self-report measures (activity level, anxiety)

1. Design Considerations and Methodological Justifications
1.1 Why Not Use a Crossover Design
  • Considered but rejected for this study
  • Crossover design useful when: no possibility of carryover effects
  • Problem: Study specifically interested in testing carryover effects (extended duration intervention)
  • Crossover design would not allow testing of primary and secondary hypotheses
1.2 Usual Care Control Condition Complexity
  • All RCTs face challenge designing control conditions
  • Simply participating in trial often affects patient behavior
  • Decision: Use home BP monitoring in UC condition as compromise
  • Must have comparable measures across conditions to understand processes
  • Does not wish to impinge on that effect
  • Note: "UC condition is not really usual care"—subjects aware they are in trial; contact and measurements reinforce awareness
  • Trial is efficacy (not effectiveness) trial—current configuration best compromise

3. Sample Size, Power Analysis, and Statistical Approach
3.1 Power Analysis: 8-Week Outcomes
  • Design: 4 arms, 80 subjects completing per arm
  • Assumed within-group SD: 13 mmHg
  • Standardized effect size (f): 0.26 (medium effect)
  • Power for overall ANOVA: 0.98 at α = 0.05
3.2 Specific Comparisons at 8 Weeks
Comparison Expected SBP Change Cohen's d Power
Intervention + placebo vs. UC 8 mmHg (intervention) vs. 2 mmHg (UC) 0.32 0.84
Intervention vs. placebo 10 mmHg vs. 5 mmHg 0.36 0.89
  • First comparison tests device effect (guided breathing or placebo) relative to UC
  • Second comparison establishes intervention effect relative to placebo device
3.3 Power Analysis: 12-Month Outcomes
  • Placebo and UC groups expected to have 2 mmHg SBP change at 12 months
  • Placebo group combined with UC (N = 160)
  • Extended intervention: maintain 10 mmHg decrease at 12 months
  • Standard intervention: partially maintain effect—6.6 mmHg decrease after 12 months
  • Overall ANOVA effect size: f = 0.19, power: 0.86
3.4 Specific Comparisons at 12 Months
Comparison Expected SBP Change Cohen's d Power
Intervention (Standard + Extended) vs. UC 8 mmHg vs. 2 mmHg 0.18 0.77
Extended vs. Standard Intervention 10 mmHg vs. 3.3 mmHg 0.41 0.83
3.5 Statistical Analysis Approach
  • Primary analyses: intention-to-treat with baseline observations carried forward for non-completers
  • Method: One-way ANOVA on change scores (baseline to 8 weeks for primary aim; baseline to 12 months for secondary aim)
  • Power and sample size based on this analytic strategy
3.6 Preliminary Analyses and Data Preparation
  • Screen all variables for inconsistent/abnormal values
  • Assess continuous measures for skewness and outliers
  • Use transformations to reduce heteroscedasticity and extreme value effects
  • Use exploratory graphical methods to assess transformation effects on linearity
3.7 Missing Data Handling
  • Assess missing data rates and patterns by treatment group
  • Primary analyses: intention-to-treat with carry-forward
  • Secondary measures (including questionnaires):
  • Maximum likelihood estimation for scattered missing responses
  • Assessment of correlates of "missingness"
  • Complete case analysis
  • Tabulate reasons for withdrawal/loss to follow-up by experimental group

4.6 Randomization Procedures
  • Follow CONSORT guidelines for clinical trial reporting
  • Unit of randomization: Individual
  • Allocation schedule generation: Dr. McBurney (project statistician) generates random numbers, places assignments in sealed envelopes
  • Allocation concealment: Randomly sized blocks prevent project staff from anticipating group assignment by process of elimination
  • Separation of generator and executor: Sequence held by Dr. McBurney or data manager in Queens, NY (off-site); project staff contact by phone when ready to randomize
  • Access limited: nobody connected with trial conduct has access to randomization records
  • Dr. McBurney's office maintains record of assignments and verifies correct group analysis

5. Intervention Description, Control Conditions, and Potential Confounders
5.1 Device Details
  • Manufacturer: Acme, Inc., Paramus, NJ
  • Guides participants to reduce breathing rate to 6 breaths/minute
5.2 Control Conditions Description
  • Placebo device identical in appearance to intervention device
  • Exception: does not guide to 6–10 breaths/minute; plays random musical tones instead
  • Creates 15-minute relaxation period (control for relaxation effect on BP)
  • Placebo device use ends at 8 weeks
5.3 Potential Confounding: Medication Changes
  • Concern: In intervention group, improvement predicted; control condition participants may receive additional treatment from physicians
  • Minimization strategies:
  • At screening: inform participants their BP is higher than recommended
  • RA suggests they see a physician
  • If planning to see physician for treatment: not admitted immediately, only after physician visit
  • If new treatment prescribed (new medications or increased dosages): wait 2 months, then rescreen to allow stabilization
  • Depending on results: may be admitted to study

7. NIH Review Criteria
7.1 Significance
  • Addresses important public health problem: excess morbidity, mortality, and economic burden due to uncontrolled hypertension (HTN)
  • Scientific gains if aims achieved:
  • Better understanding of role of characteristic breathing patterns and respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) in BP regulation
  • Understanding non-pharmacological approaches to HTN treatment as supplement or alternative to pharmacological therapy
7.2 Approach
  • RCT design: powerful, provides important scientific information concerning causal relation between intervention and outcome
  • Provides feasibility and utility information about proposed intervention
  • Measures: Based on assessment of current literature—most useful, cutting-edge techniques:
  • Ambulatory assessment of BP and respiration
  • Statistical analyses: Use cutting-edge techniques
  • Alternative research strategies and rationales for methodological decisions described (e.g., design considerations like crossover rejection)
7.3 Innovation
  • Represents innovative treatment intervention
  • Departure from traditional HTN treatment consideration
  • Potentially important advancement in public health
7.4 Investigators: Team Expertise
  • Research team has done seminal work in:
  • BP measurement
  • Psychosocial causes of essential hypertension
  • Behavioral interventions for HTN treatment
  • Extensive experience in conduct of behavioral clinical trials
7.5 Institutional Resources and Support
  • NYMC: Strong institutional support for research over past several years
  • Resources available:
  • Research space
  • Computer and Internet resources
  • Clinical needs: ABP monitors, Holter monitors, ambulatory respiratory assessment monitors
  • Patient population: Diverse, largely economically disadvantaged—need non-pharmacological treatments

9. Key Methodological Principles and Hints
9.1 Writing Principles
  • Revise repeatedly: Every time you open the file, read with goal of cutting—no matter how carefully written
  • Don't always start at beginning—may end with finely crafted beginning but weak middle/ending
  • Focused editing forces careful word choice
  • Every word should be there for a reason (like poetry)
  • Hear sentence in head—are connections still there? Is meaning clear?
9.2 Getting Feedback
  • Read stuff out loud to others
  • They will hear something missed
  • May receive feedback that story is out of order, paragraph needs moving, or content unclear
9.3 Statistical Citation Guidelines
  • Simple statistics (t-tests, correlations, ANOVA, regressions): no citations needed
  • Complex procedures (MANOVA, simultaneous equation modeling, hierarchical modeling): provide references
  • Controversial procedures: provide references for validity
9.4 Avoiding Type I Error (Fishing Expeditions)
  • Reviewers jump on proposals appearing to capitalize on chance
  • Strongly justified hypothesis → Type I error less of an issue (but always an issue)
  • Multiple outcome measures: Make specific predictions for each
  • If only one/two show predicted effect: is that sufficient to say hypothesis supported?
  • No specific hypotheses about each outcome: use statistical controls
  • Greater problem with exploratory hypotheses vs. primary well-grounded hypotheses
9.5 Junior Investigator Considerations
  • NIH interested in encouraging junior investigators
  • NI (New Investigator) or ESI (Early Stage Investigator) status provides "handicap" advantage
  • Some grant mechanisms: attain lesser score (vs. senior investigators) to reach funding range
9.6 Anticipating Reviewer Concerns
  • Think like reviewer—identify where doubt might occur
  • Cleverly undermine possible effect by showing anticipation and allaying concern before it occurs
  • If weakness cannot be avoided (due to patient population characteristic, assay that doesn't exist): describe conundrum at exact point where reviewer will spot it
