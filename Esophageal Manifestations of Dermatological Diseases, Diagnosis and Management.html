Esophageal Manifestations of Dermatological Diseases, Diagnosis and Management
  • Article Type: Review Article
  • Journal: Curr Treat Options Gastroenterol
  • Publication Date: 2022 December; 20(4): 513–528.
  • DOI: 10.1007/s11938-022-00399-6
  • Purpose: Discuss diagnosis & treatment of diseases affecting both skin & esophagus.
  • Key Findings: Diagnosis often requires endoscopy + biopsy; some need serology, immunofluorescence, manometry, genetic testing. Many conditions successfully treated w/ systemic steroids + immunosuppressants. Strictures common → endoscopic dilation. Many diseases are pre-malignant → vigilance + surveillance endoscopy.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What percentage of scleroderma patients experience esophageal involvement?
  • 2. Which dermatological condition affecting the esophagus is associated with a significantly increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma, with up to 92% of late-onset cases developing it by age 45?
  • 3. In pemphigoid diseases, what is the reported risk for esophageal perforation after dilation, and when is dilation generally recommended?
  • 4. What specific manometric finding is most common in scleroderma patients, observed in 56% of cases?
  • 5. What percentage of patients with severe esophageal lichen planus were treated with systemic steroids in one case series?
Categorization of Diseases
  • Diseases affecting skin & esophagus grouped by underlying etiology.
Autoimmune Conditions
  • Group of diseases where the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy body tissues.
Scleroderma
  • Autoimmune, systemic connective tissue disorder.
  • Categorized into systemic sclerosis & limited cutaneous scleroderma.
  • Esophageal involvement in ≈90% of patients.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Raynaud’s phenomenon; telangiectasias; sclerodactyly; calcinosis; digital scars; hyperpigmentation; contractures.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Dysmotility → pathologic GERD.
  • Consequences: peptic strictures, erosive esophagitis, Barrett’s esophagus, adenocarcinoma.
  • Endoscopic findings: telangiectasias.
Symptoms
  • Heartburn; dysphagia; chest pain; regurgitation; weight loss.
Diagnosis
  • Serology may be helpful.
  • HREM abnormalities common (75–80%).
Serology
  • Associated antibodies: anti-nuclear antibodies; anti-topoisomerase antibodies; anti-RNA polymerase III antibodies; anti-centromere antibodies.
High-Resolution Esophageal Manometry (HREM)
  • Common findings: Absent contractility (56% most common); Ineffective Esophageal Motility (IEM) (10%).
  • Specific findings: hypomotility of smooth muscle esophagus (distal ⅔); hypotonic lower esophageal sphincter (LES).
  • Classic scleroderma esophagus (absent contractility w/ ↑ esophagogastric pressures) in 33% of patients; normal manometry in 26%.
Management
  • Individualized based on symptom severity.
  • Focus on underlying esophageal dysmotility & reflux predisposition.
Medical Therapy
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs): mainstay of treatment; high-dose preferred due to suboptimal response to conventional doses.
  • Alginates: added for breakthrough symptoms w/ incomplete PPI response.
  • Prokinetics (erythromycin, metoclopramide, domperidone): controversial; limited substantive data.
  • Buspirone, bethanechol, pyridostigmine: trial for severe dysphagia w/o obvious stricture.
Endoscopic Therapy
  • Endoscopy: warranted for dysphagia to assess for stricture.
  • Endoscopic dilation: performed if stricture encountered; no data suggests one method > another.
Surgical Considerations
  • Antireflux surgeries: considered w/ caution → frequent underlying esophageal dysmotility + predisposition to post-fundoplication dysphagia.
Dermatomyositis
  • Autoimmune disorder; arises in genetically susceptible individuals after unknown environmental insult.
  • Cutaneous changes in 30–40% adults, 95% children.
  • Dysphagia in ≈15–50% of patients.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Pathognomonic: Gottron’s papules (lichenoid erythematous papules over knuckles); Gottron’s sign (erythematous patches over extensor surfaces).
  • Heliotrope rash; Shawl sign; Periungual telangiectasias.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Frequent abnormal esophageal motility.
  • Consequences: Barrett’s esophagus, adenocarcinoma.
Symptoms
  • Dysphagia; heartburn; regurgitation; odynophagia.
Diagnosis
  • Evaluation of skin, muscle strength, & lab results.
Serology
  • Elevated enzymes: CK, LDH, AST, aldolase.
  • Dermatomyositis specific autoantibodies: Mi2, TIF1, MDA5, NXP2, SAE.
  • Antisynthetase autoantibodies: Jo-1, PL-7, PL-12, EJ, OJ.
  • Other connective tissue disease antibodies: ANA, Ro/La, dsDNA, anti-SM, Scl-70.
Esophageal Diagnostics for Dysphagia
  • Endoscopy; barium studies (esophagram / modified barium swallow); HREM.
  • HREM findings: frequently normal; may display ineffective esophageal motility, absent contractility, or jackhammer esophagus.
Management
  • Depends on disease severity & organ systems involved.
Medical Therapy
  • PPIs: for reflux symptoms.
  • Systemic steroids; immunosuppressants.
Cancer Risk & Surveillance
  • ↑ risk of developing cancer; prevalence ≈20%.
  • Risk highest within 1 year after diagnosis, remains elevated 5 years after.
  • Patients should receive age-appropriate screening & symptom-targeted diagnostics.
Pemphigus & Pemphigoid Diseases
  • Autoimmune skin disorders characterized by autoantibodies.
  • Pemphigus: targets desmosomes → loss of cell-to-cell adhesion.
  • Pemphigoid: targets basement membrane.
Pemphigus Vulgaris
  • Most common form of pemphigus.
  • Patients often display painful oropharyngeal lesions preceding cutaneous manifestations.
  • Esophagus involved in 47–68% of patients.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Intraepithelial skin & mucous membrane vesiculobullous lesions.
  • Nikolsky’s sign positive: mechanical contact → exfoliation of outermost layer.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Esophagus may be involved w/o affecting skin.
  • Proximal esophagus more likely affected.
  • Esophageal lining may easily detach during endoscopy (Nikolsky’s sign positive).
  • Endoscopic findings: strictures, erosions, ulcers, blisters, vesiculobullous lesions, furrows throughout esophagus.
Symptoms
  • Odynophagia; dysphagia; weight loss.
Diagnosis
  • Histologic features similar to dermatological features.
Pathology
  • Acantholytic cells in basal layer w/ “tombstone” appearance; intraepithelial bullae.
Immunofluorescence
  • Autoantibody deposition of IgG, IgA, & C3 complement in a net-like pattern.
Management
  • Systemic steroids & immunotherapy are mainstays.
  • Patients requiring ≥3 treatment modalities more likely to receive EGD & present w/ dysphagia.
  • Good response to treatment: complete symptom resolution & pathology on EGD reported.
Medical Therapy
  • Systemic corticosteroids: 100% resolution on endoscopy in one study of oral pemphigus w/ esophageal involvement.
  • Aggressive treatment (rituximab, IVIG): for non-responders to corticosteroids → better disease control in refractory cases.
Endoscopic Therapy for Strictures
  • Endoscopic dilation: w/ balloons or bougies.
  • Stricturoplasty; stenting.
  • Intralesional steroid injection + topical steroids: successful for recurrent strictures failing multiple balloon dilations (case report).
Pemphigoid Disorders (Mucous Membrane Pemphigoid - MMP)
  • Caused by autoantibodies targeting basement membrane.
  • Characterized by sub-epidermal bullae; Nikolsky’s sign negative.
  • MMP more frequently displays oral & ocular lesions > skin manifestations.
  • Esophageal involvement in 4–11% of patients w/ MMP.
  • Diagnosis of esophageal involvement may be delayed (17–156 months reported).
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Sub-epidermal bullae; Nikolsky’s sign negative.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Esophagus may be involved w/o affecting skin.
  • Proximal esophagus more likely affected.
  • Esophageal lining may easily detach during endoscopy (Nikolsky’s sign positive).
  • Endoscopic findings: esophageal webs, stenosis, erosions, exudates w/ fibrin, subepithelial hematomas.
Symptoms
  • Odynophagia; dysphagia; weight loss.
Diagnosis
  • Clinical findings, dermal pathology, immunofluorescence.
  • Esophageal biopsy infrequently displays same immunofluorescent patterns/markers.
Histology
  • Skin: sub-epithelial blister formation.
Immunofluorescence
  • Linear deposits of IgG at basement membrane (anti-BP-180, antiBP-230).
Management
  • Distinguish active vs. cicatricial (scarred) lesions; active lesions require medical therapy.
  • Endoscopy only in symptomatic patients & preferably by experienced providers → fragile mucosa.
  • ↑ risk for esophageal perforation after dilation.
Medical Therapy
  • Systemic steroids; immunosuppressants (rituximab, mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide).
Endoscopic Therapy for Strictures
  • Esophageal dilation: may be required.
  • Balloon dilation: successful in a case series of 12 MMP patients; recommended *only after* disease adequately controlled w/ medical therapy.
  • Less likely successful in severe dysphagia w/ uncontrolled disease.
Infectious Conditions
  • Diseases caused by pathogenic microorganisms affecting both skin & esophagus.
HSV/CMV/HIV Esophagitis
  • Viral esophagitis typically presents w/ odynophagia & dysphagia in immunosuppressed individuals.
  • HSV can occur in immunocompetent individuals.
Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Esophagitis
  • Typically presents in distal esophagus.
  • Endoscopic findings: multiple small, painful, discrete ulcers; punched-out ulcers w/ yellow-rim & vesicles.
  • Histology: intranuclear eosinophilic inclusions (Cowdry A bodies).
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Cold sores; genital herpes; herpetic whitlow; herpetic eczema.
Symptoms
  • Odynophagia; dysphagia.
Diagnosis
  • Double-contrast esophagography can assist; upper endoscopy w/ biopsy necessary for tissue diagnosis.
Treatment
  • Acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir.
  • Duration: 7–10 days for immunocompetent; 14–21 days for immunocompromised.
  • Prophylactic antiviral therapy: may be indicated in immunocompromised individuals.
Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Esophagitis
  • Classically characterized by fewer, larger ulcers.
  • Endoscopic findings: more common in distal esophagus; deep, longitudinal, & linear ulcers.
  • Histology: large eosinophilic or basophilic intranuclear inclusions (H&E staining).
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Petechiae; morbilliform rashes; cutaneous vasculitis.
Symptoms
  • Odynophagia; dysphagia.
Diagnosis
  • Double-contrast esophagography can assist; upper endoscopy w/ biopsy necessary for tissue diagnosis.
Treatment
  • Ganciclovir or valganciclovir.
  • IV ganciclovir daily for 3–6 weeks.
Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) Esophagitis
  • Classically characterized by fewer, larger ulcers.
  • Endoscopic findings: deep ulcers in middle of esophagus.
  • Histology: eosinophilic infiltrate; inflammatory infiltrate.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Kaposi sarcoma; anal papillomas; candida; eosinophilic folliculitis.
Symptoms
  • Odynophagia; dysphagia.
Diagnosis
  • Double-contrast esophagography can assist; upper endoscopy w/ biopsy necessary for tissue diagnosis.
Treatment
  • Steroids.
Inflammatory Conditions
  • Diseases characterized by inflammation affecting both skin & esophagus.
Lichen Planus
  • Inflammatory mucocutaneous disease of unknown etiology.
  • May be associated w/ infections, medications, or autoimmune factors.
  • Esophageal lichen planus disproportionately affects women.
  • Esophageal involvement seen in 26% to 50% of patients.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Pruritic, violaceous papules & plaques (most commonly over extensor surfaces).
  • Oral lichen planus: white lace-like pattern (Wickham striae).
  • Skin, nails, hair, genital, & oral mucosa may be involved.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Many patients w/ esophageal involvement are asymptomatic.
  • Esophageal strictures are a frequent finding.
  • Mucosal detachment during endoscopy/dilation may be a specific sign.
Symptoms
  • Dysphagia; odynophagia; heartburn; regurgitation.
Endoscopic Findings
  • Concentric rings, mucosal edema, white plaques (similar to eosinophilic esophagitis).
  • Other findings: loss of vascularity, friability, erythema; ringed esophagus; peeling mucosa; papular lesions; submucosal plaques; white papules.
Diagnosis
  • Consider characteristic skin & mucosal findings, endoscopy, histology, & immunofluorescence.
Histology
  • Lichenoid inflammation; detachment of epithelium; necrotic keratinocytes (Civatte bodies).
Immunofluorescence
  • Fibrin deposition along basement membrane.
Management
  • Relapse rates after treatment may be as high as 85%.
Medical Therapy
  • Severe disease: systemic steroids followed by maintenance w/ swallowed steroids (44% of severe cases in one series).
  • Milder disease: often no systemic/topical steroids needed; swallowed steroids if necessary.
  • Immunomodulators: azathioprine, tacrolimus, cyclosporine, rituximab (some patients).
Endoscopic Therapy
  • Endoscopic dilation: for dysphagia; can lead to rapid improvement.
  • Caution: Koebner phenomenon (new lesions after trauma) described after dilation.
Malignancy Risk & Surveillance
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) reported in long-standing esophageal lichen planus.
  • 6% of patients developed esophageal SCC in one study.
  • Periodic surveillance endoscopy w/ mucosal biopsies should be considered.
Crohn’s Disease
  • Idiopathic inflammatory bowel disease w/ genetic & environmental influences.
  • Incidence: ≈3.1 to 14.6 per 100,000 individuals/year.
  • Esophageal Crohn’s disease is rare; higher prevalence in children & potentially racial/ethnic minority populations.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Pyoderma gangrenosum; erythema nodosum; fistulas; Sweet syndrome; oral aphthous ulcers.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Most frequently involves distal esophagus.
  • Early stages: erythema, edema, aphthous ulcers, granularity.
  • Later stages: ulcers, strictures, fistulas.
Symptoms
  • Dysphagia (54%); odynophagia (33%); reflux (25%); chest pain (13%).
Diagnosis
  • Suspect in patient w/ Crohn’s & atypical esophageal findings.
  • May be difficult due to potential for multiple diagnoses (GERD, Eosinophilic Esophagitis).
  • Screening endoscopy: recommended in all children newly diagnosed w/ Crohn’s (vs. adults).
Histology
  • May reveal non-caseating granulomas (frequently absent).
  • Intraepithelial lymphocytes.
Management
  • Similar to Crohn’s w/o esophageal involvement.
  • Stepwise approach often used.
Medical Therapy
  • PPIs; H2 blockers; systemic steroids (often utilized for superior efficacy in upper GI tract); 5-aminosalicylates.
  • Swallowed topical steroids: effectively treat esophageal Crohn’s.
  • Immunosuppressants; biologics (e.g., infliximab).
  • Infliximab: effective for patients failing other modalities; complete clinical symptom resolution in 5/5 patients in one series; combination w/ dilation & immunomodulators effective for structuring disease.
  • Successful treatment of gastro-esophageal fistula w/ infliximab after failure of other therapies (1 patient).
Endoscopic Therapy for Strictures
  • Alternative between medical & surgical therapy.
  • Balloon dilation: first-line for non-complex strictures (<4–5 cm), single strictures, or strictures w/o pre-stenotic dilation.
  • Endoscopic electroincision (stricturotomy & strictureplasty): utilized if repeat balloon dilation unsuccessful.
Surgical Therapy
  • Rarely required: esophagectomy for fistulas w/ tracheobronchial tree, paraoesophageal abscesses, mediastinitis.
Genetic Conditions
  • Inherited disorders affecting both skin & esophagus.
Epidermolysis Bullosa
  • Rare genetic syndrome w/ blistering mucocutaneous lesions.
  • Defect in anchoring protein between epidermis & dermis.
  • Esophageal involvement normally associated w/ autosomal recessive inheritance & dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa subtype.
  • Seen in children.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Blistering lesions; erosions; scars; milia; sometimes missing nails.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Most notable for development of esophageal strictures.
  • Proximal esophageal strictures seen 2x as frequently as distal strictures.
  • Microstomia due to oropharyngeal scarring.
Symptoms
  • Dysphagia; odynophagia; malnutrition.
Diagnosis
  • Route light microscopy not recommended (difficult to interpret).
  • Immunofluorescence antigenic mapping & transmission electron microscopy: provide diagnostic info on subtype.
Management of Esophageal Stricture
  • Dietary modification; careful balloon dilation; placement of gastrostomy tube for nutritional purposes.
  • Dedicated anesthesia team: may assist during endoscopy for IV catheter placement & potential intubation.
Dietary Management
  • Encouragement of soft foods; avoidance of hot foods & hyperalimentation.
Dilation Challenges
  • Esophageal dilation may be attempted via retrograde approach from gastrostomy tube if microstomia present.
Cowden Syndrome
  • Autosomal dominant disease secondary to PTEN gene mutation.
  • Prevalence: ≈1 in 200,000 to 1 in 250,000.
  • Dominant features: multiorgan hamartomas & ↑ risk of multiple malignancies.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Near universal (90% affected individuals): trichilemmomas (hamartomas of hair follicles); mucocutaneous papillomas.
  • Lipomas; fibromas.
Gastrointestinal Manifestations
  • Polyposis throughout GI tract: hamartomatous polyps, hyperplastic changes, gangliocytomas, adenomas.
  • Esophageal lesions: glycogenic acanthosis (benign finding, associated w/ GERD, incidental in ≈3.5% endoscopies).
  • Glycogenic acanthosis observed in ↑80% of patients w/ Cowden syndrome.
Endoscopic Findings
  • Hamartomatous polyps; hyperplastic changes; gangliocytomas; adenomas.
  • Glycogenic acanthosis.
Diagnosis
  • Made through clinical findings w/ specific criteria.
  • PTEN genetic testing confirms diagnosis.
Pathognomonic Criteria
  • Trichilemmomas; acral keratosis; papillomatous papules; mucosal lesions.
Major Criteria
  • Breast, thyroid, endometrial carcinoma; macrocephaly; Lhermitt-Duclos disease.
Minor Criteria
  • Other thyroid lesions; Intelligence Quotient <75; GI hamartomas; fibrocystic disease of breasts
  • , lipomas, fibromas, genitourinary tumors.
Diagnostic Confirmation
  • Pathognomonic skin findings; OR two major criteria; OR one major + 3 minor criteria; OR four minor criteria.
Endoscopist Consideration
  • Consider Cowden syndrome if glycogenic acanthosis observed w/ colonic polyposis.
Management & Surveillance
  • Close follow-up required due to associated malignancies.
Cancer Screening
  • Colonoscopy: age 35 or 10 years before first-degree relative's colorectal cancer diagnosis.
  • Surveillance endoscopy: every 1–5 years depending on polyp number & characteristics.
Focal Dermal Hypoplasia (Goltz Syndrome)
  • Rare X-linked dominant genetic disorder; ≈300 cases reported worldwide.
  • 9:1 female to male ratio (often fatal in utero for males).
  • Caused by PORCN gene mutation → defective mesodermal & ectodermal development.
  • Patients can present w/ dysphagia secondary to esophageal papillomas.
  • Many patients have GERD; theorized to contribute to esophageal papilloma formation.
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Skin nodules; pigment changes; abnormal nails; dysmorphic facial features; telangiectasias.
  • Mucocutaneous papillomas over mouth, nose, larynx, anus, genitals.
Skeletal Manifestations
  • Syndactyly; ectrodactyly; malformations of truncal skeleton.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Papillomas.
Symptoms
  • Dysphagia; heartburn.
Diagnosis
  • Combination of characteristic clinical findings (limb malformations, skin findings) & molecular genetics.
Management & Surveillance
  • Patients should be monitored for GERD & dysphagia symptoms.
Treatment
  • Argon plasma coagulation: debulk papillomas & successfully relieve dysphagia (one case).
  • PPI: for GERD.
Malignancy Risk
  • Malignant conversion of esophageal papillomas to SCC described, but not frequently observed.
Tylosis
  • Rare genetic disorder; epidermolytic palmoplantar hyperkeratosis.
  • ↑ risk of developing SCC of the esophagus.
  • Associated w/ RHBDF2 gene; autosomal dominant inheritance.
  • Prevalence: <1 in 1,000,000.
  • Classified into early onset (<1 year) & late onset (age 5–15).
Dermatological Manifestations
  • Skin plaques of hands & feet; fissures on extremities; ↑ risk of tinea pedis.
Esophageal Manifestations & Symptoms
  • Early stages: may be asymptomatic.
  • Esophageal neoplasms most commonly discovered in lower ⅔ of esophagus.
Symptoms
  • Dysphagia; odynophagia; weight loss (symptoms of esophageal cancer).
Endoscopic Findings
  • White polypoid lesions; diffuse hyperkeratosis; longitudinal grooves; strictures.
  • No correlation between endoscopic findings & disease progression due to rarity.
Diagnosis
  • Combination of family history, skin findings, endoscopic features, & evidence of RHBDF2 gene mutation.
  • Esophageal histology: non-specific; keratohylaine granules, parakeratosis, inflammatory infiltrate.
Management & Surveillance
  • Late onset tylosis: very high risk of developing esophageal cancer (40–92%).
  • Patients develop esophageal cancer @ average age of 45 years.
Cancer Surveillance
  • Surveillance endoscopy: starting @ age 30, every 1–3 years.
  • Family members w/ tylosis: screened w/ biopsy of suspicious lesions + quadratic biopsies of upper, middle, & lower esophagus.
Risk Factor Modification
  • Counseling to avoid SCC risk factors: alcohol & smoking cessation.
Potential Therapies
  • Carotenoids: may delay progression or revert lesions to normal (evidence).
  • Dermatological manifestations: treated w/ footwear, emollients, retinoids, early treatment of fissures & infections.
Genetic Counseling
  • Family members should receive genetic counseling if tylosis diagnosed in an individual.
General Diagnostic & Management Principles
  • Connecting dermatological conditions to dysphagia of unknown etiology is important.
  • Diagnosis often relies on characteristic clinical features, endoscopy, & histology.
Diagnostic Modalities by Etiology
  • Autoimmune conditions: utilize serology.
  • Infectious conditions: diagnosed w/ endoscopy + biopsy; treated w/ antimicrobials.
  • Inflammatory conditions: often rely heavily on clinical features.
  • Genetic diseases: rely on clinical features; confirmed w/ genetic testing.
Common Treatment Strategies
  • Steroids; immunosuppressants; proton pump inhibitors (PPIs).
  • Esophageal strictures: common result → may require endoscopic treatment w/ dilation.
Malignancy Risk
  • Many diseases are pre-malignant → proper cancer surveillance & vigilance is important.
