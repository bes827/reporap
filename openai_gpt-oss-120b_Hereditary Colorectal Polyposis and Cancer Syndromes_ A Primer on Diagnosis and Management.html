Hereditary Colorectal Polyposis and Cancer Syndromes: A Primer on Diagnosis and Management
  • Source: The American Journal of Gastroenterology 2017;112:1509‑1525
  • Authors: N.J. Samadder, P. Kanth, R.W. Burt, J. Grimmett, M. Champine, N.J. Samadder
1. Epidemiology & Clinical Context
  • CRC = 3ᵗʰ most common cancer, 2ⁿᵈ leading cause of death in US men & women.
  • Hereditary CRC ≈ 5 % of all CRCs (mutations/defects in specific genes).
  • Familial CRC without known genetics ≈ 25‑30 % of patients.
  • High‑risk hereditary syndromes → 70‑100 % lifetime CRC risk; many also ↑ extra‑intestinal malignancies.
  • Patients with pathogenic mutations require intensified surveillance & comprehensive management vs. average‑risk population.
  • Genetic counseling is essential for risk communication and care coordination.
2. Lynch Syndrome (LS) – Overview
2.1 Prevalence & Cancer Risks
  • LS accounts for ~2‑4 % of all CRCs; most common hereditary colon cancer.
  • Lifetime CRC risk: 50‑80 %; endometrial cancer risk: 40‑60 %.
  • Overall prevalence ≈ 1 in 440 individuals.
  • Extra‑colonic cancers & approximate lifetime risks (95 % CI):
Organ LS Gene(s) Lifetime Risk % (95 % CI)
Colon/Rectum MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM 15‑52 (varies by gene)
Endometrium MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 21‑57
Stomach MLH1, MSH2 11‑19
Ovary MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 1‑38
Hepatobiliary All LS genes 2‑7
Upper urinary tract All LS genes 4‑5
Pancreas All LS genes 3‑4
Small bowel All LS genes 1‑4
CNS (glioblastoma) All LS genes 1‑3
  • Gene‑specific risk hierarchy: MLH1/MSH2 > MSH6 ≈ PMS2.
2.2 Genetics & Molecular Pathogenesis
  • Autosomal dominant disorder of DNA mismatch repair (MMR).
  • Causative genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM (deletion → silencing of MSH2).
  • MMR function: correct single‑nucleotide mismatches, insertion/deletion loops during replication.
  • Mutation distribution: MLH1/MSH2 ≈ 70 % of LS; MSH6 ≈ 14 %; PMS2 < 15 %.
2.3 Diagnostic Criteria & Testing Strategy
  • Amsterdam I: ≥3 first‑degree relatives with CRC, spanning ≥2 generations, ≥1 diagnosed <50 y, FAP excluded, pathology confirmed.
  • Amsterdam II: Same as I but includes LS‑related extra‑colonic cancers.
  • Revised Bethesda (tumor‑based): MSI testing or IHC indicated when any of the following:

a) CRC diagnosed <50 y;

b) Synchronous/metachronous LS‑associated tumors regardless of age;

c) CRC with MSI‑high histology diagnosed <60 y;

d) CRC + ≥1 first‑degree relative with LS cancer ≤50 y;

e) CRC + ≥2 first/second‑degree relatives with LS cancers any age.

  • Tumor testing: Immunohistochemistry (IHC) for MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2; MSI analysis. Absent protein → targeted germline testing of corresponding gene.
  • Germline testing algorithm:
  • If IHC abnormal → sequence the absent‑protein gene.
  • If MLH1 loss → reflex to BRAF V600E mutation or MLH1 promoter methylation to distinguish sporadic vs LS.
  • Predictive models (risk calculators): PREMM 1,2,6; MMRpro; MMRpredict – incorporate personal & family cancer history.
2.4 Surveillance Recommendations (NCCN)
Modality Initiation Age Interval
Colonoscopy 20‑25 y (or 10 y before earliest CRC in family) Every 1‑2 y
Upper endoscopy (gastric & duodenal) 30‑35 y Every 3‑5 y (consider earlier if family gastric cancer)
Endometrial sampling + transvaginal US (women) After childbearing completion Consider prophylactic hysterectomy ± BSO; otherwise annual or per protocol.
Other organ surveillance (e.g., pancreas, urinary tract) – individualized based on family history & gene‑specific risk.
  • Prophylactic hysterectomy + bilateral salpingo‑oophorectomy after childbearing for women with LS (especially MLH1/MSH2 carriers).
2.5 Chemoprevention Evidence
  • Aspirin: CAPP2 trial – ≥2 y daily aspirin (600 mg) → 63 % ↓ CRC incidence (IRR 0.37, 95 % CI 0.18‑0.78). Benefit persists despite obesity; dose–response unclear.
  • USPSTF “B” recommendation for low‑dose aspirin (81‑300 mg) in adults 50‑59 y with ≥10 % 10‑yr CVD risk – many LS patients qualify.
  • Bleeding risks: ↑ hemorrhagic stroke 32‑36 %; GI bleed 30‑70 % vs baseline; balance individualized.
2.6 Emerging & Adjunctive Strategies
  • Universal tumor screening for all CRC (MSI/IHC) – cost‑effective in identifying LS families.
  • Multi‑gene NGS panels (“panels”) increasingly used; interpretation complexity ↑ (VUS, incidental findings).
  • Ongoing trials: dose‑finding aspirin studies (CAAPP3), combination NSAID/EGFR inhibitor regimens for duodenal adenomas.

3. Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP) & Attenuated FAP (AFAP)
3.1 Overview & Cancer Risks
  • Classic FAP: ≥100 % lifetime CRC risk if untreated; polyps appear teens.
  • AFAP: 70 % CRC risk; fewer polyps (10‑100), later onset (>25 y).
  • Extra‑colonic malignancies: duodenal/periampullary cancer 4‑12 %, thyroid carcinoma ~2 %, desmoid tumors up to 20 %.
3.2 Genetics & Molecular Basis
  • Autosomal dominant APC gene mutation (tumor suppressor, WNT pathway).
  • EPCAM deletions not implicated in FAP.
  • Mutation hotspots: codons 1309‑1324 (classic), 5′/3′ regions (AFAP).
  • ~25‑30 % de novo APC mutations → family history may be absent.
3.3 Diagnostic Criteria & Testing
  • Clinical suspicion: hundreds–thousands of adenomatous polyps on colonoscopy.
  • Germline APC testing indicated when:
  • >10 cumulative adenomas in a single exam, or
  • ≥10 adenomas + personal CRC history, or
  • Lifetime >20 adenomas.
  • ~10‑30 % of clinically diagnosed FAP/AFAP lack identifiable APC mutation → consider deep intronic variants, large deletions, mosaicism, or other genes (POLE, POLD1, GREM1).
3.4 Surveillance & Management
3.4.1 Colonoscopic Surveillance
  • Initiate colonoscopy 10‑12 y (classic) or 20‑25 y (AFAP).
  • Interval: annual for classic FAP; every 1‑2 y for AFAP if polyp burden manageable.
3.4.2 Surgical Intervention
  • Indications: polyps >1 cm, advanced histology, or burden unmanageable endoscopically (often >20).
  • Options:
  • Total colectomy with ileorectal anastomosis (IRA) – preserves rectum; risk of rectal cancer → annual rectal surveillance.
  • Proctocolectomy with ileal‑pouch‑anal anastomosis (IPAA) – eliminates rectal reservoir; lower rectal cancer risk but higher pouch adenoma prevalence.
  • Comparative outcomes: IPAA shows improved long‑term survival vs. IRA; similar postoperative complication rates.
3.4.3 Upper GI & Duodenal Surveillance
  • Begin age 25‑30 y; upper endoscopy every 1‑3 y (Spigelman staging).
  • Spigelman points: number, size, histology, dysplasia → stage 0‑IV; surveillance interval ranges from every 4 y (stage 0) to every 6‑12 m (stage III/IV).
3.4.4 Extra‑Colonic Cancer Screening
  • Thyroid ultrasound annually starting late teens (risk ~2 %).
  • Desmoid tumor monitoring: clinical exam; MRI if symptomatic.
  • Hepatoblastoma screening in children ≤5 y: AFP + liver US every 3‑6 m (rare).
3.5 Chemoprevention in FAP
  • Sulindac (150 mg BID) → ↓ adenoma count ~56 % and size ~65 %; effect reversible after discontinuation.
  • Celecoxib (400 mg BID) shows adenoma regression at higher doses; FDA indication withdrawn for FAP.
  • Combination sulindac + erlotinib trial: 71 % ↓ duodenal polyp burden after 6 m; acne‑like rash common.
4. MUTYH‑Associated Polyposis (MAP)
4.1 Overview & Epidemiology
  • Autosomal recessive biallelic MUTYH mutations → ≈80 % lifetime CRC risk.
  • Typical phenotype: 15‑100 adenomas, often resembling AFAP; may include serrated polyps.
4.2 Genetics & Pathophysiology
  • MUTYH encodes base‑excision repair enzyme correcting oxidative DNA damage (8‑oxoguanine).
  • Most common pathogenic variants: Y179C and G396D.
  • Monoallelic carriers have minimal CRC risk unless strong family history.
4.3 Diagnostic Approach
  • Indications for MUTYH testing:
  • ≥10 adenomas without APC mutation, or
  • Multiple serrated polyps + adenomas, or
  • Family history suggestive of recessive inheritance.
  • Biallelic confirmation required for MAP diagnosis; heterozygous results reported separately.
4.4 Surveillance & Management
4.4.1 Colonoscopic Protocol
  • Initiate colonoscopy 25‑30 y; repeat every 2‑3 y if negative, 1‑2 y if polyps detected.
  • Monoallelic carriers: consider colonoscopy at 40 y, interval 5 y (adjust per family history).
4.4.2 Upper GI Surveillance
  • Baseline upper endoscopy 30‑35 y; repeat based on duodenal findings (similar Spigelman criteria as FAP).
4.4.3 Extra‑Colonic Cancer Risks
  • ↑ risk of pancreatic, ovarian, bladder, skin cancers (reported but no formal screening guidelines).
4.5 Chemoprevention & Therapeutics
  • Limited data; NSAIDs (sulindac) may reduce adenoma burden, but evidence weaker than in FAP.
5. Hamartomatous Polyposis Syndromes
5.1 Peutz‑Jeghers Syndrome (PJS)
5.1.1 Clinical Features & Cancer Risks
  • STK11/LKB1 germline mutation (≈50‑70 % of cases).
  • Characteristic mucocutaneous melanin macules (mouth, lips, perioral, genitalia).
  • GI hamartomatous polyps → intussusception risk.
  • Lifetime cancer risks: colorectal 39 %, pancreatic 11‑36 %, breast 54 %, gynecologic (ovarian 21 %, cervical) ~15‑20 %, gastric 29 %, lung 15 %.
5.1.2 Surveillance Recommendations
Modality Start Age Frequency
Colonoscopy & upper endoscopy Late teens Every 2‑3 y (if normal)
Small bowel imaging (CT/MR enterography, capsule) 8‑10 y Every 1‑3 y
Pancreatic MRI/EUS 25‑30 y Every 1‑2 y
Breast MRI & mammogram 25‑30 y Annually
Gynecologic exam + PAP 18‑20 y Annually; consider transvaginal US
Testicular US (males) 10 y Annually
5.1.3 Management of Complications
  • Endoscopic polypectomy for symptomatic polyps; surgical resection for recurrent intussusception.
5.2 Juvenile Polyposis Syndrome (JPS)
5.2.1 Genetics & Phenotype
  • SMAD4 or BMPR1A mutations (~50 % of cases).
  • ≥3–5 juvenile polyps in colon/rectum, often large, pedunculated; histology: inflamed lamina propria, mucin‑filled cystic glands.
5.2.2 Cancer Risks & Surveillance
  • CRC risk ≈ 39 %; gastric cancer risk modest but reported.
  • Colonoscopy start 15 y, repeat annually if polyps present, otherwise every 2‑3 y.
  • Upper endoscopy start 15 y, interval 1‑3 y based on findings.
5.2.3 Overlap with Hereditary Hemorrhagic Telangiectasia (HHT)
  • SMAD4 mutation carriers may exhibit HHT features (telangiectasias, AVMs).
5.3 PTEN Hamartoma Tumor Syndrome (PHTS) / Cowden Syndrome (CS)
5.3.1 Genetics & Clinical Spectrum
  • Germline PTEN loss‑of‑function; autosomal dominant.
  • Features: multiple hamartomatous polyps (colon, stomach, small bowel), macrocephaly, mucocutaneous lesions (trichilemmomas, papillomas).
5.3.2 Cancer Risks & Screening
Organ Lifetime Risk %
Breast (female) 54 %
Endometrium (epithelial) ↑ (data variable)
Thyroid (follicular) ~10‑15 %
Colon 9‑18 %
Renal cell carcinoma ≤5 %
Others (skin, CNS) Low‑moderate
  • Colonoscopy start 35 y (or 5‑10 y before earliest CRC in family).
  • Breast MRI/mammogram annually from 30‑35 y.
  • Thyroid US annually from diagnosis.
5.3.3 Diagnostic Criteria (PTEN)
  • Major: macrocephaly, Lhermitte‑Duclos disease, GI hamartomas, breast cancer, endometrial carcinoma, thyroid cancer, mucocutaneous lesions.
  • Minor: autism spectrum disorder, colon cancer, esophageal glycogenic acanthosis, lipomas, renal cell carcinoma, etc.
6. Serrated Polyposis Syndrome (SPS)
6.1 Definition & Diagnostic Criteria (WHO)
  • Criterion A: ≥5 serrated polyps proximal to sigmoid, ≥2 >10 mm.
  • Criterion B: Any number of serrated polyps throughout colon + first‑degree relative with SPS.
  • Criterion C: >20 serrated polyps of any size distributed throughout colon.
6.2 Epidemiology & Cancer Risk
  • Prevalence in screening cohorts: 0–0.5 % (initial colonoscopy) up to 0.8 % on repeat exams.
  • Lifetime CRC risk 16‑42 %; advanced adenoma prevalence 9‑34 %.
6.3 Surveillance & Management
  • Colonoscopy every 1‑3 y (some guidelines advocate annual).
  • Remove all serrated lesions ≥5 mm; consider chromoendoscopy for detection.
  • No specific extra‑colonic screening; follow general population recommendations unless family history dictates earlier initiation.
6.4 Genetic Landscape & Testing
  • No single causative gene identified; occasional overlap with MUTYH or MMR mutations.
  • MSI, CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP), and BRAF V600E frequently observed in serrated pathway lesions.

7. Genetic Counseling & Risk Assessment
7.1 Role of the Genetics Counselor
  • Provides comprehensive cancer‑family history collection (≥3 generations).
  • Interprets pedigree using Amsterdam, Bethesda, and NCCN criteria.
  • Discusses inheritance patterns, penetrance, variable expressivity, and psychosocial implications.
  • Facilitates informed consent for germline testing; explains variant of uncertain significance (VUS) handling.
7.2 Pre‑test Counseling Elements
  • Indications for testing (clinical criteria, predictive models).
  • Potential outcomes: pathogenic mutation, VUS, negative result.
  • Implications for insurance, employment, and family planning (e.g., prenatal or preimplantation genetic diagnosis).
7.3 Post‑test Follow‑up
  • Positive result → cascade testing of at‑risk relatives (first‑degree ≥50 % risk).
  • Negative result in high‑clinical‑suspicion cases → consider re‑testing with expanded panels or repeat surveillance per guidelines.
  • VUS → periodic re‑evaluation as databases update; may trigger functional studies.
8. Risk Prediction Tools & Clinical Decision Support
8.1 Predictive Models for LS
Model Input Variables Output
PREMM 1,2,6 Personal/family cancer history (CRC, endometrial, ovarian, gastric, etc.) Probability of pathogenic MMR/EPCAM mutation
MMRpro Detailed pedigree, tumor MSI/IHC results Mutation carrier probability
MMRpredict Age at diagnosis, tumor location, MSI status Likelihood of LS gene mutation
  • Thresholds (e.g., ≥5 % PREMM risk) trigger germline testing per NCCN.
8.2 Decision Algorithms for Surveillance Intervals
  • Spigelman scoring (duodenal adenomas): points → surveillance interval (0:4 y, I:2‑3 y, II:1‑3 y, III:6‑12 m, IV:3‑6 m + surgical eval).
  • Colorectal polyp burden in FAP/AFAP/MAP: >20 adenomas → consider colectomy; ≤20 → endoscopic control with 6‑12 m colonoscopy.
8.3 Integration into Electronic Health Records (EHR)
  • Automated alerts for age‑specific screening initiation based on genotype.
  • Clinical pathways embed chemoprevention eligibility (e.g., aspirin recommendation for LS carriers >50 y).
9. Multidisciplinary Management Framework
9.1 Core Team Members
  • Gastroenterologist (endoscopic surveillance)
  • Surgical oncologist / colorectal surgeon (colectomy, IPAA/IRA decisions)
  • Medical oncologist (chemoprevention, systemic therapy for extra‑colonic cancers)
  • Genetic counselor / medical geneticist (testing, family communication)
  • Gynecologic oncologist (endometrial/ovarian surveillance, prophylactic surgery)
  • Radiologist (MRI/EUS for pancreatic, small bowel imaging)
  • Dermatologist (desmoid, cutaneous manifestations)
9.2 Care Coordination Pathway

1. Initial referral → genetics clinic for pedigree & testing.

2. Result disclosure → genotype‑driven surveillance schedule entered into EHR.

3. Annual multidisciplinary tumor board reviews:

  • Surveillance findings (polyp burden, dysplasia).
  • Need for surgical intervention or chemoprevention adjustment.
  • Emerging extra‑colonic lesions (e.g., pancreatic cysts).

4. Patient education portal provides personalized reminders for colonoscopy, imaging, and medication adherence.

9.3 Psychosocial & Reproductive Counseling
  • Discuss fertility preservation, especially before prophylactic hysterectomy or colectomy in young women.
  • Offer psychological support for anxiety related to cancer risk and surveillance burden.

11. Emerging Research & Future Directions
11.1 Next‑Generation Sequencing Panels
  • Broad panels (≥30 genes) increase detection of atypical or overlapping syndromes but raise VUS burden.
  • Need for standardized reporting frameworks and functional assays to reclassify VUS.
11.2 High‑Definition Endoscopy & AI‑Assisted Polyp Detection
  • Chromoendoscopy, narrow‑band imaging (NBI), and high‑definition colonoscopes improve adenoma detection in LS; ongoing trials evaluating impact on interval cancer rates.
11.3 Immunoprevention & Vaccines
  • Investigational MMR peptide vaccines aim to boost immune surveillance in LS carriers; early phase studies show immunogenicity but clinical efficacy pending.
11.4 Personalized Chemoprevention
  • Pharmacogenomic profiling (e.g., CYP2C9, UGT1A6 variants) may predict aspirin metabolism and bleeding risk, guiding individualized dosing.
12. Consolidated Clinical Algorithm (High‑Level)

1. Identify high‑risk individual → apply Amsterdam/Bethesda criteria or predictive model threshold.

2. Perform tumor testing (MSI/IHC). If abnormal → proceed to germline sequencing of implicated gene(s).

3. If pathogenic mutation confirmed:

  • Register in hereditary cancer registry; initiate genotype‑specific surveillance schedule (colon, upper GI, gynecologic, etc.).
  • Offer genetic counseling for patient & relatives; cascade testing.
  • Discuss chemoprevention (aspirin for LS) and lifestyle modifications (smoking cessation, BMI control).

4. If no mutation detected but clinical suspicion remains:

  • Consider multi‑gene panel or repeat testing after 2–3 y.
  • Continue surveillance per highest‑risk phenotype (e.g., FAP‑like colonoscopy intervals).

5. Reassess annually in multidisciplinary tumor board; adjust surveillance, surgical timing, and chemoprevention based on evolving data and patient preferences.
