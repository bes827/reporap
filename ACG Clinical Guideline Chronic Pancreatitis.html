ACG Clinical Guideline: Chronic Pancreatitis
  • Journal: Am J Gastroenterol
  • Publication Date: February 5, 2020
  • DOI: https://doi.org/10.14309/ajg.0000000000000535
  • Target Audience: General Gastroenterologists
  • Purpose: Provide evidence-based, practical approach to CP diagnosis & management.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the primary imaging modality recommended as first-line for diagnosing Chronic Pancreatitis (CP)?
  • 2. According to the guideline, what percentage of pancreatic function loss typically results in clinically apparent symptoms of Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI)?
  • 3. Which intervention has shown superior long-term pain relief for obstructive CP when first-line endoscopic approaches have failed: continued endoscopic therapy or surgical intervention?
  • 4. What is the approximate lifetime risk of developing pancreatic adenocarcinoma in patients with well-characterized genetic polymorphisms predisposing them to CP?
  • 5. Is pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) recommended primarily for pain control or for improving complications of malnutrition in patients with CP and Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI)?
Chronic Pancreatitis (CP): Definition & Paradigm Shift
  • CP: Historically defined as irreversible inflammatory condition → varying exocrine/endocrine dysfunction.
  • New Paradigm: Focus on diagnosing underlying pathologic process early; holistic management → Δ natural course, minimize adverse effects.
Mechanistic Definition (2016)
  • Pathologic fibroinflammatory syndrome of pancreas.
  • Occurs in individuals w/ genetic, environmental, and/or other risk factors.
  • Develop persistent pathologic responses to parenchymal injury/stress.
End-Stage Disease Characteristics
  • Pancreatic atrophy; fibrosis; pain syndromes.
  • Duct distortion & strictures; calcifications.
  • Pancreatic exocrine dysfunction; pancreatic endocrine dysfunction.
  • Dysplasia.
Precision Medicine Approach
  • Complex gene + environmental interactions in individual patient.
  • Focus on early signs/symptoms of disease.
  • 'Bottom-up' approach for complex disorders w/ multiple etiologies, modifiers, complications, outcomes.
Traditional Clinicopathologic Definition (Pre-2016)
  • Chronic inflammation + irreversible fibrosis w/o infection.
  • Primary challenge: Obtaining pancreatic tissue (gold standard) due to high risk/benefit ratio of biopsies.
Marseille Consensus Conferences (1963-1989)
  • Defined CP based on clinical, functional, histologic evidence.
Cambridge Definition (1984)
  • Clinically useful alternative to biopsy; used ERCP scoring system as surrogate for tissue.
  • Defined as continuing inflammatory disease of pancreas → irreversible morphological change, typically causing pain and/or permanent loss of function.
  • Served as basis for imaging approaches & guidelines for 3 decades.
Limitations of Traditional Framework
  • Results in years of delay between symptom onset & diagnosis.
  • Usually fails to identify/address underlying etiology.
  • Cannot predict clinical course or direct preventative treatments.
  • Limited to symptomatic/supportive care & replacement of lost gland function.
  • Early CP (where targeted therapy is effective) cannot be diagnosed (requires irreversible morphologic change).
Fibrosis Correlation
  • Degree of fibrosis correlates poorly w/ pain, EPI, DM, progressive disease, or cancer risk.
  • These are primary concerns of clinical care.
Distinction of Pancreatic Conditions
  • Dysfunction: Dynamic term; variation in entity's action that deviates from normal negatively.
  • Disorder: Disruption of normal functions of specialized cells/systems → abnormal signs, symptoms, biomarkers, responses.
  • Disease: Abnormal condition defined by consensus criteria (abnormal signs/symptoms, biomarkers, typical pathologic features).
Example: CFTR Gene
  • Genetic mutation in CFTR gene locus → variations in RNA expression/splicing, or Δ amino acid sequence → defective processing, trafficking, or channel opening.
  • Dysfunction may be tolerated by adaptive mechanisms/limited cell stress.
  • Pancreatitis-related disorder: Protein dysfunction → pancreatic duct cell fails to respond normally to stimulation/stress → duct cell dysfunction.
  • When compensatory threshold exceeded → internal/external stress/injury signals → clinical signs/symptoms (e.g., acute pancreatitis episode).
  • Pancreatic diseases: Conditions w/ pathology (e.g., AP, RAP, CP, cystic fibrosis).
  • Precision medicine: Focus on determining dysfunction/disorder → targeted therapy to prevent progression to clinicopathologic-defined disease.
Clinical Presentation of CP
  • Most common symptom: Abdominal pain.
  • Other symptoms: Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI), diabetes mellitus (DM).
  • Development rates of EPI/DM are highly variable.
Causes & Risk Factors of CP
  • Most common causes: Toxins (alcohol, tobacco use), genetic polymorphisms, recurrent attacks of acute pancreatitis (RAP).
  • Note: Many patients have no history of acute pancreatitis.
Diagnosis of CP
  • Historically difficult due to debate over gold-standard test.
  • Often made using combination of modalities: exposure risk, underlying predisposition, cross-sectional imaging, direct/indirect pancreatic function tests.
  • Most useful diagnostic test: Careful history & physical examination → pretest probability & clinical suspicion integral.
Recommendation 1: First-Line Imaging
  • We recommend CT or MRI for the first-line diagnosis of CP.
  • Either test should be the first choice for CP diagnosis.
  • EUS should be used only if diagnosis is in question after cross-sectional imaging (due to invasiveness & lack of specificity).
  • Strength: Strong recommendation, low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • No RCTs comparing cross-sectional imaging w/ EUS for CP diagnosis.
  • Systematic review/meta-analysis (43 studies, 3460 patients): Sensitivity estimates for EUS (81%), MRI (78%), CT (75%) did not differ significantly.
  • Specificity estimates: EUS (90%), ERCP (94%), CT (91%), MRI (96%), US (98%) were comparable.
  • Limitation: Not all studies included histologic gold standard.
  • Rationale: Vast discrepancy in cost, availability, invasiveness, objectivity favors cross-sectional imaging as first-line.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Demonstrating typical morphologic changes is critical component of CP definition (imaging is surrogate for histology).
  • Cross-sectional imaging: Familiar, initial test for diagnosis.
  • EUS: Use if diagnosis still in doubt after cross-sectional imaging, or concern about 'minimal change disease' (CP w/o fibrosis evidence on cross-sectional imaging).
  • No specific EUS scoring system or number/types of criteria recommended.
  • CP remains a clinical diagnosis: Integrate clinical scenario, risk factors, exclusion of other diseases.
Recommendation 2: Secretin-Enhanced MRCP (s-MRCP)
  • We suggest performing s-MRCP when CP diagnosis following cross-sectional imaging or EUS is not confirmed and clinical suspicion remains high.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation, low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • s-MRCP: Better visualization of main- & side-branch ducts by stimulating bicarbonate release from pancreatic duct cells.
  • Allows quantification of duodenal filling → correlates w/ CP severity & pancreatic exocrine function.
  • No RCTs evaluating s-MRCP for CP diagnosis.
  • Systematic review (69 articles, 15 reviews): Dynamic thick-slab 2D MRCP most used sequence (86%). Main pancreatic duct diameter (75%) & visual grading of duodenal filling (67%) most evaluated features.
  • Smaller studies suggest diagnostic value in children w/ idiopathic CP & for minimal change disease.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Use if cross-sectional imaging & EUS are not diagnostic of CP.
  • Identifies subtle ductal abnormalities (dilated branches, ectatic duct) consistent w/ CP imaging criteria.
  • Structural imaging is a biomarker of pancreas morphology, most sensitive to changes from fibrosis w/ duct distortion & calcifications.
  • Diagnosis of CP should not be made solely on s-MRCP findings or other imaging modalities.
Key Concept 1: Pancreatic Function Testing (PFT)
  • PFT is important for diagnosing EPI; its role in establishing CP diagnosis is complementary.
Summary of Evidence
  • Controversy surrounds PFT use for CP diagnosis.
  • PFTs diagnose EPI; most CP patients lack clinically significant EPI.
  • Sensitivity of PFT for CP diagnosis is low.
  • Significant loss of function (>90%) required for clinical symptoms of steatorrhea, azotorrhea, vitamin deficiency.
  • EPI represents imbalance in 4 domains: nutritional intake, enzyme delivery, intestinal adaptation, nutritional needs.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on PFT for CP diagnosis.
  • Use as ancillary test for CP diagnosis.
Types of Pancreatic Function Tests (Table 4 Summary)
  • Hormonal (Direct) Tests:
  • CCK stimulation test (acinar cell function, trypsin/lipase): Direct, detects subtle EPI. Disadvantages: cumbersome, not widely available, specialized lab, patient discomfort (Dreiling tube), 2-3 hr test.
  • Secretin stimulation test (ductal cell function, bicarbonate): Direct, performed endoscopically, uses lab autoanalyzer, 60 min test. Disadvantages: not widely available, prone to error, risk/cost of endoscopy.
  • Non-Hormonal (Indirect) Tests:
  • Fecal elastase-1: Universally available, easily obtainable, non-invasive. Disadvantages: moderate sensitivity, limited specificity in diarrhea, limited use in mild disease.
  • 13C-mixed triglyceride test: Easily obtainable, high sensitivity (90%). Disadvantages: not universally available, long test duration (4-6 hr).
  • Serum trypsinogen/trypsin: Universally available, easily obtainable, non-invasive, quantifiable for tracking function. Disadvantages: does not measure digestive tract enzymes, elevated w/ pancreatic pain.
Fecal Elastase-1 Caveats
  • Accuracy depends on cutoff.
  • <200 mg/g stool: High false-positive rate.
  • <100 mg/g stool: Improves specificity, lowers sensitivity.
Serum Trypsin/Trypsinogen
  • Not typically performed due to reports of elevation in nonpancreatic pain syndromes & poor correlation w/ imaging findings.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • EPI alone does not establish CP diagnosis (e.g., CFTR variants can cause ↓ bicarbonate secretion w/o tissue loss/morphologic change).
  • PFTs should be adjunctive test for patients w/ unestablished CP diagnosis.
Recommendation 3: Histological Examination
  • We suggest histological examination as the gold standard to diagnose CP in high-risk patients when clinical & functional evidence of CP is strong, but imaging modalities are inconclusive.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation, very low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • Considered when CP diagnosis cannot be made by cross-sectional imaging or EUS.
  • EUS-guided fine-needle biopsy techniques make tissue acquisition safer/easier.
  • Sensitivity of histologic evaluation for CP (when tissue available) often no better than chance compared to morphologic evaluation.
  • Limitations: Sampling error, biopsy complications, patchy nature of inflammatory changes, subjective histologic interpretation.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses treating histology as diagnostic gold standard.
  • Value: Most important for ruling out CP.
  • Consider only in high-risk patients after clinical, functional, and imaging tests are inconclusive, w/ thoughtful informed consent.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Under current clinicopathologic approach, histology is gold standard to diagnose CP; often used to 'rule out' CP.
  • Both imaging & histology are biomarkers of underlying disorder, not necessarily true CP → sensitivity of histology for diagnosis is low.
  • As mechanistic model develops, histology likely ↓ importance in CP diagnosis.
Etiology of CP
  • Identifying the cause of CP is crucial for management and prognosis.
Key Concept 2: Comprehensive Risk Factor Review
  • In patients w/ clinical features of CP, comprehensive review of all risk factors should be performed.
  • Provides information on underlying mechanisms.
  • Identifies fixed & modifiable risk factors.
  • Identifies potential targets for therapies.
  • Provides clinically relevant prognostic information.
Summary of Evidence
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on order of testing for CP etiology.
  • TIGAR-O system: Categorizes etiology (Toxic-Metabolic, Idiopathic, Genetic, Autoimmune, Recurrent acute/severe pancreatitis, Obstructive); useful in international studies, recently revised.
  • M-ANNHEIM system: Similar multi-risk factor classification; adds info on disease activity/stage, evaluates impact/interaction of risk factors on CP course.
  • M-ANNHEIM provides diagnostic criteria for etiology, clinical/diagnostic stage, severity based on traditional clinicopathologic criteria.
TIGAR-O Version 2.0 Pancreatitis System Risk/Etiology Checklist (Table 5 Summary)
  • Toxic-metabolic: Alcohol-related (3-4 drinks/d, ≥5 drinks/d), Smoking (pack-years), Hypercalcemia (>12.0 mg/dL or 3 mmol/L), Hypertriglyceridemia (fasting >300 mg/dL; nonfasting >500 mg/dL), Hypertriglyceridemic acute pancreatitis (history of >500 mg/dL in first 72 hr), Medications, Toxins, CKD (stage 5-ESRD), Metabolic (e.g., DM w/ diagnosis date).
  • Idiopathic: Early onset (<35 yr), Late onset (>35 yr).
  • Genetic: Suspected/limited genotyping, Autosomal dominant (PRSS1 mutations), Autosomal recessive (CFTR, SPINK1), Complex genetics (modifier genes like PRSS1-PRSS1 locus, CLDN2 locus, hypertriglyceridemia genes).
  • AIP/steroid-responsive pancreatitis: AIP type 1 (IgG4-related), AIP type 2.
  • RAP and SAP: Acute pancreatitis (single episode w/ date), AP etiology (extrapancreatic: biliary, post-ERCP, traumatic, undetermined), RAP (number, frequency, dates).
  • Obstructive: Pancreas divisum, Ampullary stenosis, Main duct pancreatic stones, Widespread pancreatic calcifications, Main pancreatic duct strictures, Localized mass causing duct obstruction.
M-ANNHEIM Scoring System for CP Severity (Table 6 Summary)
  • Scores clinical features: Patient report of pain (0-4 points, from no pain w/o therapy to continuous pain), Pain control (0-4 points, from no medication to surgical intervention), Exocrine insufficiency (0-2 points, from absence to proven/marked steatorrhea), Endocrine insufficiency (0-4 points, absence of DM to presence of DM), Morphologic status on pancreatic imaging (Cambridge classification: 0-4 points, normal to marked), Severe organ complications (0-4 points, absence to irreversible complications).
  • Severity Index: Sum of points categorizes disease (M-ANNHEIM A: Minor 0-5, B: Increased 6-10, C: Advanced 11-15, D: Marked 16-20, E: Exacerbated >20).
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Initial approach: Thorough history & physical exam, biomarker tests.
  • History: Previous AP episodes (TIGAR-O/M-ANNHEIM), DM onset, maldigestion/malnutrition, weight loss, bone health, renal disease, CF-associated organ diseases.
  • Family history: ≥3rd-degree relatives for pancreatitis, CF, DM, pancreatic cancer.
  • TIGAR-O checklist: Guides recording alcohol use, smoking, medications, toxins, DM, diet, key biomarkers (serum calcium, triglycerides, fat-soluble vitamins, nutrition analysis).
  • Imaging: Identifies obstructive etiologies (malformations, stones, tumors).
  • Genetic testing: Indicated if etiology unclear, family history of pancreatic diseases, disease persists after intervention (e.g., RAP after biliary clearance), or young patient (<35 yrs).
  • Differential diagnosis: Autoimmune inflammation, islet cell-related fibrosis (long-standing DM), renal disease, immune-altering medications (cyclosporine), age-related atrophy/fibrosis, IPMN, acinar cell cystadenoma, desmoplastic response to neoplasm, inflammation upstream of duct-obstructing mass.
Recommendation 4: Genetic Testing
  • We recommend genetic testing in patients w/ clinical evidence of a pancreatitis-associated disorder or possible CP where etiology is unclear, especially in younger patients.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation, low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • Past 2 decades: Several genes associated w/ CP diagnosis.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on genetic testing for idiopathic CP.
  • Primary goal: Identify underlying pancreatitis-related disorders contributing to pathogenic process, assist decision-making, prevent irreversible CP.
  • Examples: CFTR variants (CFTR-related disorder or cystic fibrosis) presenting as RAP or CP → significant Δ in treatment strategy.
  • Genetic testing provides early info on etiology of pancreatitis-related disorders.
  • If etiology obscure: Determines dysfunctional genes, cell types, mechanisms.
  • This info drives confirmatory testing (e.g., sweat chloride for CFTR) & places patient in high/low disease prevalence population → affects predictive value of biomarkers.
  • May not lead to immediate treatment but ends exhaustive, invasive, expensive diagnostic testing.
  • Informs decisions about radical therapy (e.g., TPIAT).
  • Value to patient: Resolution of poorly defined symptoms, connection to support networks, prognosis/comorbidities, family planning.
  • Limited value in end-stage CP (irreversible destruction) except for familial disorders, patient insight beyond alcohol/smoking, or research.
Genetic Polymorphisms Implicated in CP (Table 7 Summary)
  • Acinar Cell Dysfunction:
  • PRSS1 (cationic trypsinogen gene): Complex low penetrance; misfolding, ER stress → Recurrent AP & CP.
  • CPA1 (carboxypeptidase A1 gene): Complex low penetrance; misfolding, ER stress → High relative risk of nonalcoholic CP.
  • CEL (carboxyl ester lipase): Autosomal dominant complex; misfolding, aggregations, ER stress → MODY-8 pancreatitis.
  • SPINK1 (serine protein inhibitor Kazal type 1): Autosomal recessive; loss of protein function due to blocked active site of trypsin; 2% of population; homozygotes w/ more severe disease.
  • CTRC (chymotrypsin C): Autosomal dominant; activated trypsin resistant to degradation → Associated w/ early-onset pancreatitis.
  • Ductal Cell Dysfunction:
  • CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator): Autosomal recessive; loss of protein function due to failure of ductal bicarbonate secretion; >2000 gene variants; widely divergent clinical presentation.
  • Disease-Modifying Genes:
  • CASR (calcium-sensing receptor gene): Autosomal recessive; loss of ability to regulate intracellular calcium; gain of function affects acinar cells, loss of function affects ductal cells.
  • CTRC (chymotrypsin C): Complex; low expression effect potentiated by smoking; pancreatitis when present w/ a trypsin-activating variant.
  • CLDN2 locus (Claudin-2 gene locus): Unknown; high risk w/ alcoholism; accelerates transition from RAP to CP.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Germline mutations testing: Not diagnostic of CP, but (i) identifies high prevalence population for pancreatitis-related disorders/CP → improves accuracy of less sensitive/specific biomarkers, and (ii) identifies dysfunctional mechanism underlying pathogenic processes.
  • Important for all ages: Therapies (e.g., CFTR-modulating drugs) can target mechanism, guiding drug/therapy selection.
  • Provides prognostic info for complex syndromes (e.g., TPIAT assessment) & answers for patients.
  • Referral to genetic counselor: Recommended, but experienced non-geneticist clinicians can order/evaluate results.
  • Minimum testing for idiopathic CP: PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR, CTRC gene mutation analysis.
  • Extended panels: >dozen susceptibility/modifier genes, hypertriglyceridemia genes, pharmacogenetics available.
Natural History & Clinical Symptoms of CP
  • Primary clinical outcomes: Debilitating abdominal pain, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (malnutrition, osteoporosis), pancreatic malignancy risk, endocrine insufficiency (DM).
  • Disease is irreversible at this stage; preventive/restorative therapy not possible.
Key Concept 3: Predicting Progression to CP
  • Identification of the disorder(s) underlying pancreatic inflammation is important in predicting progression to CP.
Summary of Evidence
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses specifically evaluating etiology of pancreatitis & natural history of pain.
  • CP evolves over years → opportunities to intervene.
  • ~60% of CP cases evolve from AP & RAP.
  • ~10% of AP & 30% of RAP progress to CP.
  • Etiology plays major role: Progression from AP to CP is 2x faster w/ alcoholic etiologies vs. genetic/idiopathic; 5x faster than biliary AP.
  • Genetic modifiers strongly affect RAP → CP progression risk.
  • Painful CP: Previously suggested to improve over time (especially alcohol-induced), but not reproduced; generally accepted pain does not 'resolve' or 'burn out'.
  • Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency: Occurs in minority of CP patients due to ↓ oral intake (pain) & malabsorption (limited enzyme production).
  • Prevalence of exocrine insufficiency: 40-75% in large natural history studies.
  • Risk highest in CP due to alcohol/tobacco use & fibrocystic (tropical) pancreatitis.
  • Pancreatic adenocarcinoma risk: Relatively higher than general population, but likely lower than previously reported.
  • Genetic polymorphisms: Lifetime risk of pancreatic adenocarcinoma 5-10% in well-characterized at-risk patients.
  • Data limited to case series & retrospective cohort studies.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Generally, specific CP etiology not proven to determine important clinical outcomes (rapid progression, EPI, severe pain, malignancy risk).
  • Exception: Autoimmune pancreatitis (pain not frequent complication) → etiology may determine outcomes/symptoms.
  • Identification of disorder causing pancreatic inflammation can be important in predicting CP progression.
Key Concept 4: Development of Diabetes Mellitus (DM)
  • Development of DM in CP is most likely related to duration of disease.
  • Other etiologic factors: BMI & smoking status may incur an ↑ risk.
Summary of Evidence
  • Risk of pancreatic endocrine failure (type 3c DM) not demonstrated to result from any specific CP etiology.
  • Duration of disease: Likely most important etiologic risk factor for endocrine failure.
  • Tobacco use: May also play a role in DM development.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on endocrine dysfunction risk in CP.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Obese patients: More likely to have type 2 DM.
  • Advanced CP patients: More likely to have low BMI & T3cDM from islet cell loss.
  • New-onset DM w/ weight loss: Potentially indicator of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma.
Recommendation 5: Alcohol Cessation
  • We recommend alcohol cessation in patients w/ CP.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation, very low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on alcohol cessation altering CP pain natural history.
  • Case series: Discontinuing alcohol improves CP pain, but doesn't necessarily alter progression to endocrine/exocrine dysfunction.
  • 1 RCT: Alcohol cessation counseling in acute alcoholic pancreatitis patients ↓ hospitalizations & pain attacks.
  • Alcohol cessation counseling recommended for CP patients, though extent of altering natural history is unknown.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Despite low-quality evidence, strict alcohol avoidance should be cornerstone of CP treatment.
Recommendation 6: Smoking Cessation
  • We recommend smoking cessation in patients w/ CP.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation, very low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • Smoking cessation challenging for CP patients.
  • Smoking tobacco: Widely believed CP risk factor, but only single-center studies available.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on smoking cessation benefiting CP natural history.
  • Case series: ↓ pancreatic calcification progression w/ smoking cessation at CP diagnosis.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Despite low-quality evidence, strict smoking avoidance should be cornerstone of CP treatment.
  • Recognize low long-term success rate of smoking cessation.
Key Concept 5: Pancreatic Malignancy Screening
  • There is a lack of evidence to suggest that performing screening examinations on patients w/ CP to detect pancreatic malignancy is beneficial.
Summary of Evidence
  • Very little quality evidence for screening all CP patients, even high-risk ones.
  • Overall prevalence of pancreatic malignancy ↑ in CP patients.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses support screening this population.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • No definitive benefit to screen CP patients for pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma.
  • Reasons: Invasive/costly testing, inherent difficulty due to CP structural changes, inability to alter natural history even w/ early detection.
Management of Pain in CP
  • Pain is a major symptom in CP, requiring careful management strategies.
Key Concept 6: Elective Interventions & Alcohol Use
  • Performing elective interventional procedures on patients actively using alcohol should be considered cautiously.
  • Patients requiring urgent/emergent procedures for CP complications should be considered separately.
Summary of Evidence
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses specifically addressing this issue.
  • Medical perspective: Aggressive endoscopic/surgical interventions may be ill-advised if ongoing alcohol abuse exacerbates CP pain.
  • Alcohol cessation could negate need for some interventions.
  • Social perspective: Patients continuing alcohol after education still warrant care; encourage cessation via counseling/programs.
  • Urgent interventions should be performed regardless of alcohol use.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Elective interventional procedures (e.g., celiac plexus interventions) w/ caution in active alcohol users.
  • Urgent interventions: Perform due to medical necessity.
  • Patients making good faith efforts to stop/reduce alcohol: Evaluate for interventional procedures case-by-case, recognizing cessation difficulties & need to treat symptoms.
Recommendation 7: Surgical vs. Endoscopic Drainage for Obstructive CP
  • We recommend surgical intervention over endoscopic therapy in patients w/ obstructive CP for long-term pain relief if first-line endoscopic approaches to pancreatic drainage have been exhausted or unsuccessful.
  • Strength: Strong recommendation, moderate quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • Duct obstruction: Pancreatic duct stones, strictures, or combination.
  • Endoscopic decompressive procedures: ERCP w/ sphincterotomy, stone clearance, stricture dilation, stenting; interventional EUS procedures (transluminal stent).
  • Surgical decompressive procedures: Puestow, Frey, Beger procedures (may include partial pancreatectomy).
  • No RCT/systematic review data reliably report if successful/unsuccessful endoscopic decompression predicts successful drainage surgery.
  • Cahen et al. (2007 RCT, 39 patients): Surgery group had lower pain scores (P < 0.001) & better physical health (P = 0.003).
  • 24-month follow-up: Complete/partial pain relief in 32% (endoscopic) vs. 75% (surgical) (P = 0.007).
  • Complication rates, length of stay, pancreatic function similar.
  • Endoscopic group required more interventions (median 8 vs. 3, P < 0.001).
  • Long-term follow-up (79 months): 68% of original endoscopic group underwent additional drainage vs. 5% surgical (P = 0.001).
  • 47% of endoscopic patients ultimately had surgery.
  • Mean difference in pain scores no longer significant (39 vs. 22; P = 0.12), but surgery superior for pain relief (80% vs. 38%; P = 0.042).
  • Overall QoL & pancreatic function not different.
  • Older study (Díte et al. 2003, 140 patients): Similar initial success rates, but complete pain absence lower in both groups; more common w/ surgery (37% vs. 14%). Partial relief equivalent (49% vs. 51%).
  • Surgery group gained more weight; new-onset DM equal in both.
  • Various studies compare different surgical drainage procedures; no clear superiority.
  • Note: Many studies are older; interventional endoscopic approaches more widely performed now. Newer comparative studies warranted.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Surgical approaches: Better long-term pain relief than endoscopic, but rarely first-line.
  • Many surgeons operate only after endoscopic approaches exhausted/unsuccessful.
  • Reasonable to perform endoscopic drainage (ERCP/EUS) as first-line for symptomatic, obstructed pancreatic duct.
  • Surgery reserved for treatment failures or those unwilling to undergo multiple endoscopic treatments.
  • Means of ductal decompression (e.g., lithotripsy) at endoscopist's discretion based on local expertise.
Recommendation 8: Antioxidant Therapy for Pain
  • We suggest considering antioxidant therapy for CP w/ pain, although benefit of pain reduction is likely limited.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation, moderate quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • Mechanism: Reduce oxidative stress, anti-inflammatory effect.
  • Could be alternative to narcotics.
  • RCT (2009): 5 antioxidant agents (selenium, ascorbic acid, b-carotene, a-tocopherol, methionine) vs. placebo.
  • Antioxidant group: Significantly fewer painful days/month, less analgesic use, more pain-free patients.
  • Double-blind RCT (70 patients, alcohol-induced CP): Antioxidant therapy (selenium yeast, D-a-tocopherol acetate, ascorbic acid, L-methionine, b-carotene) vs. placebo.
  • No statistically significant difference in pain scores at 6 months.
  • Opiate use, hospital admissions, outpatient visits similar.
  • Conclusion: Antioxidants did not improve pain/QoL in alcohol-induced CP.
  • Multimodality treatment (incl. antioxidants + interventional therapies): Resulted in long-term pain relief in non-RCT series.
  • Two meta-analyses (9 & 12 trials): Antioxidants appeared to reduce pain, but effect size was small.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Data limited, scientific plausibility questionable, not FDA-regulated.
  • Appear safe, may reduce pain.
  • Consider for clinical use, especially early in disease course.
  • Optimal type/dosage unclear; clinical trials generally include selenium, ascorbic acid, b-carotene, methionine.
Key Concept 7: Opiates for Pain
  • Opiates may be considered to treat painful CP only in patients in whom all other reasonable therapeutic options have been exhausted.
Summary of Evidence
  • Debated for decades.
  • Arguments for: Efficacy, low cost.
  • Arguments against: Addiction risk, abuse, tolerance; concerns about prescribing to patients w/ substance abuse history (alcoholism); side effects (constipation).
  • Few quality studies; no RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses.
  • Trial (18 CP patients): Transdermal fentanyl vs. sustained-release morphine (randomized cross-over).
  • Transdermal fentanyl not ideal: Dosage ↑ 50% above manufacturer rec, significantly more rescue morphine needed.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Avoid opiates if possible due to addiction, abuse, tolerance risks.
  • Justified for refractory pain if other modalities fail.
  • Patients previously prescribed opiates: Encourage transition to other medications.
  • Prescribing clinician (gastroenterologist/PCP/pain management/palliative care): Knowledgeable about disease natural history, monitors symptoms/response, adheres to universal precautions for safe prescribing (screening for opiate use disorder, patient-provider agreement, annual urine drug screen, annual prescription system check, addiction counseling, wrap-around services).
Recommendation 9: Pancreatic Enzyme Supplements for Pain
  • We do not suggest the use of pancreatic enzyme supplements to improve pain in CP.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation, low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • PERT established for symptomatic EPI, but role for pain less clear.
  • Plausible for EPI-related pain (cramping, diarrhea), but unclear for primary pancreatic pain (inflammation, ductal stones).
  • Older study (26 CP patients): Enteric-coated enzymes vs. placebo (4-month cross-over).
  • No difference found; enzymes not beneficial for pain.
  • Cochrane review (2009, 10 RCTs, 361 patients): Equivocal result for enzymes treating pain; studies of less than ideal quality.
  • Meta-analysis (5 randomized studies): Failed to show meaningful effect on pain relief; 1 study showed benefit for non-enteric-coated enzymes.
  • Meta-analysis (6 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials): Failed to show significant benefit on CP-associated pain.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Do not use pancreatic enzyme therapy for pain control due to expense & general lack of efficacy.
  • Patients w/ EPI: Usually derive benefit from PERT for discomfort (abdominal cramping, etc.).
  • If patients feel pain improved on PERT (especially non-enteric-coated formulations w/ biologic plausibility): Reasonable to continue due to low risk & lack of other low-risk analgesic alternatives.
Recommendation 10: Celiac Plexus Block (CPB) for Pain
  • We suggest considering celiac plexus block for treatment of pain in CP.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation, very low quality of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • CPB: Injection of pharmaceuticals (local anesthetic + steroid, e.g., bupivacaine + triamcinolone) into/around celiac ganglia.
  • Performed via endoscopy, interventional radiology, or surgery.
  • Advantages: Single treatment → pain reduction/relief for 3-6 months, may reduce/eliminate oral analgesia, quick, repeatable.
  • Disadvantages: Procedure risks (bleeding, allergic reaction), sedation risks/needs.
  • Generally similar efficacy regardless of physician type (endoscopist, radiologist, surgeon); decision based on experience/availability.
  • Meta-analysis (efficacy of CPB): Favored EUS-guided injections over percutaneous, but percutaneous data limited.
  • Reported pain relief range: 25-96%; median response rate: EUS-guided 68%, percutaneous 61%.
  • Overall: CPB effective at relieving CP pain.
  • Rationale for recommendation: Numerous studies, but most compared EUS vs. percutaneous; clinical success varied significantly. 'Selected patients' highlights that many studies show no significant benefit in some CP patients.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • CPB: Relatively low-risk, opioid-free method to reduce refractory pain in certain CP patients.
  • Meaningful symptom reduction possible for some, but unclear which patients benefit most.
  • Repeatable on 'as-needed' basis, generally ≥3 months apart if initial intervention was beneficial.
Key Concept 8: Total Pancreatectomy with Islet Autotransplantation (TPIAT)
  • TPIAT should be reserved for highly selected patients w/ refractory chronic pain in whom all other symptom control measures have failed.
Summary of Evidence
  • Increasingly used for refractory painful CP.
  • Offered at selected centers.
  • Outcomes/effectiveness not subjected to RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses.
  • Data: Exclusively case series & cohort studies → limited efficacy statements.
  • Critical need for comparative effectiveness studies on clinical outcomes.
  • Patients considering TPIAT: Evaluate at expert center w/ multidisciplinary evaluation.
  • Apprise patients of risks: Type 3c DM, potential lifelong intestinal dysmotility disorders.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Consider only after all medical treatment options exhausted.
  • Resection procedures for painful CP should include discussion of islet cell replacement therapy.
Key Concept 9: Experimental Treatment Modalities
  • Experimental treatment modalities should be limited to use in the context of a clinical research trial.
Summary of Evidence
  • For refractory CP pain: Spinal cord nerve stimulation, transmagnetic brain stimulation, direct radiation therapy to pancreatic bed.
  • Data: Limited to small case series & retrospective cohort studies.
  • Recommendation: Limit use to clinical research trials.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Patients w/ debilitating CP where medical/surgical interventions failed: Consider referral to expert center offering experimental therapies.
Management of Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI) in CP
  • EPI is a common complication of CP, leading to malnutrition and other issues.
Recommendation 11: Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT)
  • We suggest PERT in patients w/ CP and EPI to improve the complications of malnutrition.
  • Strength: Conditional recommendation, low level of evidence.
Summary of Evidence
  • Benefits of PERT: Symptom reduction, weight gain, fat absorption improvement, fat-soluble vitamin/trace element level improvement, reduction in maldigestion consequences (e.g., bone fracture risk), QoL improvement, mortality reduction.
  • Primary outcome for RCTs/FDA approval: Coefficient of fat absorption (CFA) over 72 hours.
  • FDA guidance: Clinically meaningful decrease in stool fat ≥30% (for CFA <40%), CFA must ↑ ≥10%; studies ≥200 patients over 6 months (or 100 over 1 year).
  • Normal CFA: >93% (≤7% dietary fat lost in stool).
  • 17 randomized trials on PERT in CP (12 cross-over, 5 parallel).
  • Varied: Enteric-coated, non-enteric-coated, microtablets, granules; daily dosage (lipase USP/day) <50,000 to >700,000.
  • Some compared PERT products/dosages, others PERT vs. placebo.
  • Timing of ingestion varied (before/during meals); some added H2 inhibitor/PPI.
  • Cochrane review (2009, 10 RCTs, 361 patients): No identifiable improvement in pain or analgesic use.
  • Significantly reduced fecal fat; non-significant trend to weight/QoL improvement.
  • Conclusion: Data equivocal, urged additional studies.
  • Recent meta-analysis (17 trials): 14 trials on CFA.
  • PERT improved CFA vs. baseline significantly (83.7% ± 6.0 on PERT vs. 63.1 ± 15.0 baseline, P < 0.00001).
  • Fecal nitrogen excretion & stool weight also reduced (P < 0.001).
  • Some studies: PERT improved flatulence & fecal consistency.
  • 7 studies compared PERT w/ placebo: PERT ↑ CFA vs. placebo (83.2 ± 5.5 vs. 67.4 ± 7.0, P = 0.0001, high heterogeneity I2 = 86%).
  • 4 studies analyzed high-dose vs. low-dose PERT: Slightly higher CFA w/ lipase >60,000 units daily (not significant).
  • Conclusion: PERT improved fat & protein absorption vs. baseline or placebo.
  • Original studies: Max follow-up 2 months.
  • Long-term follow-up studies: Significant improvement in nutritional parameters, weight, symptoms, QoL.
  • No data on PERT preventing bone fracture.
  • Retrospective studies: EPI may have detrimental effect on mortality, but no data on PERT improving this condition.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Suspect EPI: Long-standing CP, weight loss, malnutrition, diarrhea, steatorrhea, osteoporosis, osteopenia.
  • Clinical suspicion often sufficient for diagnosis w/o formal fecal fat measurement; titrate enzymes to improve symptoms.
  • Abnormal fecal elastase: Most easily available diagnostic test.
  • Therapy: Adequate dosage (≥40,000-50,000 USP units of lipase w/ each meal) administered during meal.
  • Baseline & periodic measurement of fat-soluble vitamin levels & bone density are appropriate.
Key Concept 10: Periodic Malnutrition Evaluation
  • Patients w/ CP should have periodic evaluation for malnutrition, including tests for osteoporosis and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.
Summary of Evidence
  • Testing for malnutrition consequences frequently performed, but no RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses provide evidence.
  • Single-center studies: ↑ risk of osteoporosis & subsequent fracture risk in CP patients.
  • 1 RCT: Intramuscular vs. oral vitamin D for sufficiency in CP; intramuscular more efficacious.
  • No studies on most appropriate test for bone loss.
  • CP patients at risk for fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K), zinc, magnesium deficiency, regardless of proven EPI.
  • Degree of abnormality likely corresponds to EPI degree.
  • No RCTs, systematic reviews, or meta-analyses on testing for these deficiencies or dietary interventions in CP.
Practical Clinical Approach
  • Despite low-quality evidence, periodic evaluation of fat-soluble vitamin & zinc deficiency should be considered due to higher fracture risk & ↑ malnutrition incidence.
  • Advise small, frequent meals w/o fat restriction.
