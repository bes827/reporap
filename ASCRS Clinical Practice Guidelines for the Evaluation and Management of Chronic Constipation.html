ASCRS Clinical Practice Guidelines for the Evaluation and Management of Chronic Constipation

  • - Publication Date: 2024; 67: 1244–1257

Overview of Chronic Constipation
  • - One of the most common GI disorders in ambulatory medicine clinics.
  • - Worldwide prevalence of 15%.
  • - More frequently diagnosed in North America and Europe compared to Asia.
Risk Factors
  • - Age > 65 years
  • - Female sex
  • - Inactivity
  • - Low socioeconomic status
  • - Low-fiber diet
  • - Non-White race

Primary Constipation Subtypes
  • - Characterized by dysfunctional colonic motility and/or outlet dysfunction.
Normal Transit Constipation
  • - Also known as functional constipation.
  • - Meets Rome IV criteria.
Rome IV Criteria for Functional Constipation
  • - Must include ≥2 of the following for >25% of defecations:
  • - Straining
  • - Lumpy/hard stools
  • - Sensation of incomplete evacuation
  • - Sensation of anorectal blockage
  • - Requiring manual maneuvers
  • - Fewer than 3 spontaneous defecations per week.
  • - Symptoms must be present for 3 to 6 months before diagnosis.


Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C)
  • - Overlaps with normal transit constipation.
  • - Distinguishing symptom: Abdominal pain more than once per week that resolves with flatulence or a bowel movement.

Delayed Transit Time Constipation
  • - Also known as slow-transit constipation.

Outlet Dysfunction Constipation
  • - Difficulty expelling stool from the rectum.


Secondary Constipation
  • - Cause is multifactorial.
  • - Can include diet, medications, metabolic or neurological disorders, and psychosocial issues.


Initial Evaluation and Management
1. History and Physical Examination
  • - Recommendation: Strong (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - Goal: Ensure a serious or life-threatening disease is not the underlying cause.
History: Red Flag Symptoms
  • - Rectal bleeding
  • - Change in caliber of stools
  • - Blood in the stool
  • - Weight loss
  • - Anemia
  • - Family history of colorectal cancer
  • - These symptoms warrant endoscopic evaluation.

History: Medication Review
  • - Common contributing medications:
  • - Opioids
  • - Antidepressants
  • - Anticholinergics
  • - Calcium channel blockers
  • - Calcium supplements

Physical Examination Focus
  • - Abdominal: Check for distention or palpable masses.
  • - Anorectal: Inspect for distorted anatomy, neoplasms.
  • - Digital Rectal Exam: Can reveal anal hypertonia, paradoxical puborectalis contraction, rectocele, mass, stricture, or fecal impaction.
  • - Valsalva Maneuver: May help determine degree of pelvic descent or presence of puborectalis dysfunction.


Diagnostic Workup
  • - Laboratory testing: Can identify hypothyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, or diabetes mellitus but is nonspecific.
  • - Imaging (CT): Can demonstrate colonic dilation or fecal loading but is unlikely to show an anatomic abnormality unless symptoms are suspicious.
Role of Colonoscopy
  • - Constipation alone is not associated with an increased risk of colorectal cancer.
  • - A colonoscopy should be recommended if patients meet criteria for general screening or present with concerning symptoms (hematochezia, weight loss, etc.).


2. Objective Measures & Quality of Life
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - Useful for directing treatment and assessing response over time.
  • - Recommended tools by the Pelvic Floor Disorders Consortium:
  • - Patient Assessment of Constipation Symptom Questionnaire
  • - Constipation Severity Instrument

3. Initial Management: Diet & Fiber
  • - Recommendation: Strong (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - Considered first-line treatment.
  • - Involves dietary modifications, ensuring adequate fluid intake, and fiber supplementation.
Fiber Supplementation Efficacy
  • - Daily oat bran supplementation allowed discontinuation of laxatives in 59% to 80% of elderly patients.
  • - Soluble fiber led to improvements in global constipation symptoms (86.5% vs 47.4% with placebo) and straining (55.6% vs 28.6% with placebo).

Limitations of Fiber Therapy
  • - 80% of patients with slow colonic transit may not respond adequately.
  • - 63% of patients with constipation due to outlet obstruction may not respond adequately.
  • - 85% of patients without an underlying pathological finding may see symptomatic improvement.


4. Medical Therapy: Laxatives
  • - Recommendation: Strong (based on moderate-quality evidence).
First-Line: Osmotic Laxatives
  • - Preferred agents include polyethylene glycol (PEG) and magnesium salt–based products.
  • - A meta-analysis showed PEG is superior to lactulose in improving stool frequency, consistency, and relieving abdominal pain.

Second-Line/Rescue: Stimulant Laxatives
  • - e.g., bisacodyl, sodium picosulfate.
  • - Can be considered for rescue therapy or as second-line therapy.
  • - Most frequent side effect is abdominal pain.
  • - There is a lack of RCTs for anthraquinone stimulants like senna, cascara, and aloe.



Management of Refractory Constipation & Defecatory Disorders
5. Evaluation for Outlet Dysfunction
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - Should be performed in patients who fail dietary changes, fiber, and osmotic laxatives.
  • - Outlet dysfunction is the most common cause of medically refractory chronic constipation.
Balloon Expulsion Test (BET)
  • - A direct test of evacuation (expelling a 50mL water-filled balloon).
  • - Has low sensitivity (50%) but is highly specific and can predict response to biofeedback therapy.

Anorectal Manometry & Sensation Testing
  • - Indirect tests to clarify functional causes.
  • - Evaluates rectoanal inhibitory reflex, which is absent in Hirschsprung disease.

Defecography
  • - The only direct test of evacuation that evaluates both anatomical and functional causes.
  • - Can be fluoroscopic (seated) or MRI (supine).
  • - Fluoroscopic defecography is considered to have the most construct validity.
Common Pathological Findings
  • - High-grade (Oxford III/IV) intussusception: 23.7%
  • - Large (>4 cm) rectoceles: 15.9%
  • - Enterocele descent: 16.8%
  • - Perineal descent: 44.4%



6. Colonic Motility Testing
  • - Recommendation: Strong (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - Must be measured before surgical intervention is considered.
  • - The most common modality is the transit time of radiopaque markers.

7. Biofeedback Therapy
  • - Recommendation: Strong (based on moderate-quality evidence).
  • - Considered a first-line treatment for patients with symptomatic pelvic floor dyssynergia.
Efficacy
  • - Electromyography (EMG) biofeedback is superior to non-EMG biofeedback (OR 6.74).
  • - A Cochrane Review showed biofeedback was successful in 80% of patients compared to only a 22% success rate with laxatives and dietary modifications.


8. Botulinum Toxin (BTX) Injection
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - May be considered for outlet dysfunction related to a nonrelaxing puborectalis muscle.
  • - Symptomatic relief is typically temporary, with efficacy decreasing within 3 months post-injection.
Study Example
  • - In a study of patients unresponsive to biofeedback, an injection of 100 units of BTX-A was used.
  • - 74.2% of patients who had previously failed the BET were able to successfully expel the balloon after the injection.


9. Surgical Repair of Rectocele
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on moderate-quality evidence).
  • - May be considered for significant outlet dysfunction from a rectocele.
  • - Prerequisite: Any concomitant functional causes (e.g., nonrelaxing puborectalis) must be addressed first.
Surgical Approaches & Outcomes
  • - Approaches include transanal, transvaginal, transperineal, and abdominal.
  • - Success rates are variable, ranging from 50% to 100%.
  • - The transvaginal approach may be associated with a higher incidence of dyspareunia.


10. Stapled Transanal Rectal Resection (STARR)
  • - Recommendation: Strongly NOT recommended for repair of a rectocele or internal rectal intussusception.
  • - Reason: High complication rates associated with the procedure.
Reported Complications
  • - Postoperative complication rates range from 10% to 78%.
  • - Specific complications include rectovaginal fistula, pelvic sepsis (1.3%), fecal urgency (27.8%), and staple line dehiscence (4.2%).


11. Repair of Rectal Intussusception
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - May be considered in patients with severe obstructed defecation when nonoperative treatments are unsuccessful.
  • - Surgery may restore anatomy but does not guarantee functional improvement.
Delorme Procedure
  • - A mucosal proctectomy that excises redundant mucosa and plicates the underlying muscularis.
  • - A study of 100 patients reported significant improvement in constipation scores at 6 months (from 18.9 to 5).
  • - Recurrence rate was 6% with an excellent safety profile.

Ventral Mesh Rectopexy
  • - Associated with improvement in constipation in 80% to 95% of patients.
  • - Features minimal new-onset constipation and a 5% recurrence rate.
  • - Long-term follow-up (median 60 months) showed no mesh-related complications and sustained improvement in symptoms at 7 years.


12. Total Abdominal Colectomy with Ileorectal Anastomosis (TAC-IRA)
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - May benefit patients with isolated refractory colonic slow-transit constipation.
  • - In contrast, segmental colectomy for this condition can have a failure rate as high as 100%.
Outcomes & Morbidity
  • - Associated with clinical improvement in 50% to 100% of patients.
  • - Potential morbidity includes anastomotic leak, bowel obstruction, and prolonged ileus.
  • - Post-operative symptoms can include diarrhea, abdominal pain, fecal incontinence, or recurrent constipation.
15-Year Follow-Up Study
  • - 50% of patients had fewer than 4 BMs per day.
  • - 21% experienced severe incontinence.
  • - 19% of patients eventually required a permanent ileostomy.
  • - Despite this, overall patient satisfaction was high.

Patient Satisfaction
  • - In one survey, despite diarrhea in 46% of patients, more than 90% reported they would undergo TAC-IRA again.


Alternative: Jinling Procedure
  • - A subtotal colectomy with side-to-side cecorectal anastomosis.
  • - Theoretically preserves the ileocecal valve to reduce diarrhea risk.
  • - Efficacy compared with TAC-IRA remains unclear.
Comparative Data
  • - A retrospective comparison showed cecorectal anastomosis was more often associated with persistent constipation and lower patient satisfaction (73%) compared to ileosigmoid anastomosis (93%).



13. Fecal Diversion (Ostomy)
  • - Recommendation: Conditional (based on low-quality evidence).
  • - Considered a last resort for patients with intractable constipation refractory to other treatments.
Ileostomy
  • - Successful in alleviating constipation in 96% of patients in one analysis.
  • - Must be weighed against risks of dehydration, parastomal hernia, and retraction.
  • - In one series, 55% of patients required additional stoma-related procedures.

Colostomy
  • - Less evidence available.
  • - One series showed 100% improvement in patients with rectosigmoid dilation, but no benefit for those with pancolonic dilation.

Quality of Life
  • - Despite a reoperation rate of 20% in one study, more than 70% of patients were satisfied with their quality of life post-ostomy.
