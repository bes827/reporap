Pharmacological Strategies for the Management of Severe Alcohol-associated Hepatitis: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis
  • Authors: Alvi H. Islam, Claudia Alvizuri, et al.
  • Journal: Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology
  • Publication Date: May 4, 2025
  • This is a systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs examining pharmacological treatments for severe AH.
Knowledge Test
  • 1. What is the reported short-term mortality rate (within three months) for severe alcohol-associated hepatitis (AH)?
  • 2. According to the guidelines, what MDF score is typically used as a cutoff for considering corticosteroid treatment in AH?
  • 3. Which pharmacological agent, when combined with corticosteroids, showed a statistically significant reduction in 28-day mortality compared to corticosteroids alone?
  • 4. What is the primary mechanism of liver injury in AH?
  • 5. Name one therapy that showed a 90-day mortality benefit compared to corticosteroids.
Background
  • AH is a severe form of ALD.
  • Short-term mortality can reach 50% within 3 months.
  • Optimal pharmacological treatment for severe AH is uncertain.
ALD Burden
  • ALD is a leading cause of liver-related mortality worldwide.
  • It's a common indication for liver transplantation.
  • ALD accounts for 41.7% of cirrhosis-related deaths.
AH Characteristics
  • AH involves sudden jaundice onset.
  • Progressive inflammatory liver injury occurs.
  • Risk of multiorgan failure ↑.
Need for Effective Therapies
  • Current AH treatment options often fail to alter long-term prognosis.
  • There's an urgent need for more effective therapies.
  • AH is associated w/ serious adverse events: acute kidney injury, hepatorenal syndrome, infections.
Mechanisms of Liver Injury in AH
  • Severe inflammatory response is central.
  • Direct hepatocyte injury occurs via oxidative stress.
  • Indirect ethanol-induced liver damage happens via microbial dysbiosis & loss of intestinal barrier integrity.
Consequences
  • Impaired hepatocyte regeneration.
  • Liver synthetic dysfunction.
  • Ultimately, liver failure.
Pharmacotherapies
  • Target mechanisms of alcohol-induced liver injury.
  • Include anti-inflammatory drugs (corticosteroids), pro-regeneration drugs (G-CSF), therapies targeting gut-liver axis.
  • No significant improvement in mortality & morbidity outcomes despite advancements.
Current Treatment Recommendations
  • Achieving & maintaining alcohol abstinence + supportive care (nutritional support) are most effective.
  • Guidelines recommend corticosteroids if MDF score > 32 or MELD score > 20.
  • MELD scores between 25 & 39 provide maximum benefit.
Corticosteroids
  • Prednisolone associated w/ ↓ in 28-day mortality.
  • Survival benefit after 28 days not proven.
  • Heterogeneous use of corticosteroids in eligible patients.
Systematic Review Aim
  • Identify optimal pharmacological treatment for severe AH to improve survival outcomes.
  • Primary outcome: mortality @ 28 days.
  • Secondary outcomes: mortality @ 90 days, adverse events, serious adverse events (infections, acute kidney injury, hepatorenal syndrome), withdrawal due to adverse events, liver transplantation rates.
Methods
  • MEDLINE, EMBASE, CENTRAL searched to Feb 16, 2025.
  • RCTs of medical therapy for adults w/ severe AH included.
  • Primary outcome: mortality @ 28 days.
Eligibility Criteria
  • RCTs of adults (≥18 years) w/ severe AH (MDF ≥ 32 or MELD >20) receiving drug treatment during hospitalization.
  • Any pharmacological therapy eligible.
  • Active-comparator or placebo-controlled trials eligible; English only.
Outcomes
  • Primary: Overall mortality @ 28 days (30-day/1-month data substituted if unavailable).
  • Secondary: Mortality @ 90 days, adverse events, serious adverse events, withdrawal due to adverse events, incidence of hepatorenal syndrome, acute renal injury/infections, liver transplantation rates.
Data Analysis
  • Risk ratio (RR) & 95% confidence interval (CI) calculated for dichotomous outcomes.
  • Random-effects model used to pool data.
  • Heterogeneity assessed using chi-square test & I2 statistic.
I2 Interpretation
  • >75% = high heterogeneity; 50-75% = moderate; 25-50% = low; <25% = minimal.
  • Sensitivity analysis conducted to explore heterogeneity.
  • Post hoc Bayesian network meta-analysis for mortality @ 28 & 90 days.
Results
  • 52 RCTs (5,121 participants) included.
  • 39 different comparisons made.
  • 39 of 52 studies had unclear risk of bias for at least one item.
Corticosteroids vs. Placebo
  • 10 studies (918 participants).
  • No statistically significant difference in mortality rates @ 28 days (RR 0.69, 95% CI 0.46-1.03, I2 = 61%, GRADE low certainty).
  • Sensitivity analysis (excluding outlier) showed statistically significant difference in mortality rates @ 28 days.
Mortality Rates
  • 19% (84/449) of corticosteroids group died @ or before 28 days vs. 26% (116/441) of placebo (RR 0.62, 95% CI 0.41-0.95, I2 = 63%, GRADE low certainty).
  • No statistically significant difference in adverse events, serious adverse events, infections, acute kidney injury, or progression to hepatorenal syndrome.
Pentoxifylline vs. Corticosteroids
  • 7 studies (994 participants).
  • No statistically significant difference in mortality rates @ 28 days (RR 0.99, 95% CI 0.68-1.45, I2 = 60%, GRADE low certainty).
  • No statistically significant difference in mortality @ 90 days, adverse events, serious adverse events, withdrawal due to adverse events, infections, acute kidney injury, or progression to hepatorenal syndrome.
Pentoxifylline vs. Placebo
  • 3 studies (686 participants).
  • No statistically significant difference in mortality rates @ 28 days.
  • 20% (67/332) of pentoxifylline group died @ or before 28 days vs. 23% (79/346) of placebo (RR 0.73, 95% CI 0.39-1.34, I2 = 70%, GRADE low certainty).
Hepatorenal Syndrome
  • Pentoxifylline patients significantly less likely to progress to hepatorenal syndrome.
  • 8% (4/49) of pentoxifylline patients progressed vs. 35% (18/52) of placebo (RR 0.24, 95% CI 0.09-0.65, GRADE low certainty).
Metadoxine & Corticosteroids vs. Corticosteroids
  • 1 study (70 participants).
  • Metadoxine + corticosteroids had significantly lower mortality rates @ 28 days than corticosteroids alone.
  • 26% (9/35) vs. 54% (19/35) (RR 0.47, 95% CI 0.25-0.90, GRADE low certainty).
90-Day Mortality
  • Metadoxine + corticosteroids had significantly lower mortality rates @ 90 days.
  • 31% (11/35) vs. 80% (28/35) (RR 0.39, 95% CI 0.23-0.66, GRADE moderate certainty).
Metadoxine & Corticosteroids vs. Pentoxifylline
  • 1 study (68 participants).
  • Metadoxine + corticosteroids had significantly lower mortality rates @ 28 days than pentoxifylline.
  • 26% (9/35) vs. 52% (17/33) (RR 0.50, 95% CI 0.26-0.96, GRADE low certainty).
90-Day Mortality
  • Metadoxine + corticosteroids had significantly lower mortality rates @ 90 days.
  • 31% (11/35) vs. 67% (22/33) (RR 0.47, 95% CI 0.27-0.81, GRADE low certainty).
G-CSF & Pentoxifylline vs. Pentoxifylline
  • 3 studies (129 participants).
  • Combination therapy had significantly lower mortality rates @ 28 days.
  • 17% (7/41) vs. 63% (27/43) (RR 0.27, 95% CI 0.13-0.54, I2 = 0%, GRADE low certainty).
90-Day Mortality
  • Combination therapy had significantly lower mortality rates @ 90 days.
  • 17% (7/41) vs. 74% (32/43) (RR 0.32, 95% CI 0.16-0.63, I2 = 29%, GRADE moderate certainty).
Hepatorenal Syndrome
  • Combination therapy had significantly lower rates of progression to hepatorenal syndrome.
  • 4% (1/23) vs. 35% (8/23) (RR 0.13, 95% CI 0.02-0.92, GRADE low certainty).
Corticosteroids & N-acetylcysteine vs. Corticosteroids
  • 1 study (174 participants).
  • Combination therapy had significantly lower mortality rate @ 28 days.
  • 8% (7/85) vs. 24% (RR 0.35, 95% CI 0.16-0.78, GRADE low certainty).
Infection and Hepatorenal Syndrome
  • Combination therapy had significantly lower rates of infection (RR 0.45, 95% CI 0.27-0.75, GRADE moderate certainty) & progression to hepatorenal syndrome (RR 0.48, 95% CI 0.24-0.94, GRADE low certainty).
Fecal Microbiota Transplant vs. Corticosteroids
  • 1 study (120 participants).
  • No statistically significant difference in mortality rates @ 28 days (RR 0.54, 95% CI 0.23-1.26, GRADE low certainty).
  • Fecal transplant group had significantly lower mortality rate @ 90 days (RR 0.58, 95% CI 0.34-0.98, GRADE moderate certainty).
Anakinra & Zinc vs. Corticosteroids
  • 1 study (147 participants).
  • Combination therapy had significantly higher mortality rates @ 28 days.
  • 19% (14/74) vs. 3% (2/73) (RR 6.91, 95% CI 1.63-29.32, GRADE low certainty).
90-Day Mortality and Acute Kidney Injury
  • Combination therapy had significantly higher mortality rates @ 90 days.
  • 26% (19/74) vs. 10% (7/73) (RR 2.68, 95% CI 1.20-5.98, GRADE low certainty).
  • Combination therapy had significantly lower rates of acute kidney injury (RR 2.03, 95% CI 1.23-3.36, GRADE moderate certainty).
Bayesian Network Meta-analysis
  • Treatment w/ corticosteroids, pentoxifylline, & combination of NAC & prednisolone significantly reduced risk of mortality @ 28 days compared to placebo.
  • Treatment w/ corticosteroids, combination therapy w/ G-CSF & corticosteroids, & combination therapy w/ metadoxine & corticosteroids significantly reduced risk of mortality @ 90 days compared to placebo.
Discussion
  • Corticosteroid use associated w/ 28-day mortality benefit compared to placebo.
  • Benefits seen w/ combination of N-acetylcysteine & corticosteroids, pentoxifylline compared to placebo, G-CSF combined w/ pentoxifylline, & metadoxine combined w/ corticosteroids.
  • No benefit found when pentoxifylline & corticosteroids used in combination.
Corticosteroids
  • Aligns w/ previous studies showing short-term benefits in reducing 28-day mortality.
  • Benefit doesn't extend to long-term mortality @ 90 days.
  • No differences observed between corticosteroids & placebo for adverse events, serious adverse events, infections, acute kidney injury, or progression to hepatorenal syndrome.
Pentoxifylline
  • No benefit observed w/ pentoxifylline vs. corticosteroids.
  • No differences in 28-day mortality when comparing pentoxifylline to placebo.
  • Pentoxifylline group less likely to progress to hepatorenal syndrome.
G-CSF
  • Combination of G-CSF & pentoxifylline significantly reduced 28-day & 90-day mortality compared to pentoxifylline alone.
  • Combination therapy showed lower rates of progression to hepatorenal syndrome.
  • Benefit of G-CSF in patients w/ AH should be viewed w/ caution.
Other Therapies
  • Metadoxine combined w/ corticosteroids demonstrated reduction in mortality @ both 28 & 90 days compared to pentoxifylline or corticosteroids alone.
  • Combination of corticosteroids & NAC associated w/ reduced 28-day mortality, but w/o differences in 90-day mortality.
  • Fecal microbiota transplant vs. corticosteroids showed significantly lower mortality rate @ 90 days compared to corticosteroids.
Limitations
  • Supportive care may have improved over time.
  • Lack of solid evidence-based treatments.
  • Heterogeneity among studies may introduce variability.
  • Treatment-related adverse events were generally poorly reported.
Conclusion
  • Low certainty evidence supports efficacy of corticosteroids as first-line therapy for eligible patients w/ severe AH.
  • Benefits noted for combination of NAC & corticosteroids vs. corticosteroids, G-CSF combined w/ pentoxifylline vs. pentoxifylline, & metadoxine combined w/ corticosteroids vs. corticosteroids or pentoxifylline.
  • Further studies needed to confirm these benefits.
What You Need to Know
  • AH is a severe form of ALD, w/ short-term mortality up to 50% within 3 months.
  • To date, there is no consensus on the best therapeutic approach for severe AH.
  • A survival benefit at 28 days was observed for corticosteroids over placebo, for corticosteroids plus N-acetylcysteine over corticosteroids alone, for granulocyte colony-stimulating factor combined with pentoxifylline over pentoxifylline alone, and metadoxine combined with corticosteroids over corticosteroids alone or pentoxifylline.
Implications for Patient Care
  • Low certainty evidence supports efficacy of corticosteroids as first line therapy for eligible patients w/ severe AH.
  • The certainty of the evidence supporting other therapies ranges from low to moderate, indicating the need for further studies to confirm these benefits.
